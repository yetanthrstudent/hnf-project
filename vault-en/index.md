# Hadith Network Forensics

## What we do

This project is a critical investigation into hadith transmission chains. We use the **same sources** as the classical tradition — Bukhārī, Muslim, Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, ʿIlal of al-Dāraquṭnī, Tahdhīb al-Kamāl, Tārīkh Dimashq — but we **dig differently**.

The classical approach of jarḥ wa-l-taʿdīl often reduces a transmitter to a binary verdict: *thiqa* or *ḍaʿīf*. We believe this framework is insufficient — in both directions. A transmitter can be *thiqa* while relying on fragile material, and his books may have been altered without his knowledge. A chain can be "authentic" by formal criteria and carry fabricated content. A collection can be "ṣaḥīḥ" and contain traditions that the classical critics themselves identified as problematic.

But the reverse is equally true. A *ḍaʿīf* chain, where there is no incentive for falsification, can on the contrary be very valuable — it transmits material that nobody had any reason to invent. And a transmitter can be unjustly excluded from the patrimony: [[Persons/Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā|Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]], al-Shāfiʿī's principal teacher, was called a liar, a heretic, a *rāfiḍī* — yet the empirical examination of his traditions (Ibn ʿAdī) reveals no *munkar*. His crime was not transmitting badly, but transmitting the wrong things to the wrong people.

Our method is **multidimensional**: we cross-reference multiple angles of analysis on each tradition, each transmitter, each concept. None of these angles is new in itself — each exists in the classical muṣṭalaḥ literature. What is new is applying them **together**, systematically, and drawing the consequences.

This work touches **everything** — including what many consider unthinkable to question. We analyze the chains of the [[Concepts/Arkān al-Islām|pillars of Islam]], those of the [[Traditions/Ḥadīth Jibrīl|ḥadīth Jibrīl]] that founds the pillars of faith, the [[Traditions/Assassination of Kaʿb ibn al-Ashraf|assassination of Kaʿb]], the [[Traditions/Massacre of Banū Qurayẓa|massacre of Banū Qurayẓa]], and the Sīra itself. Nothing is sanctuarized. We know this can be unsettling — some readers are not ready, and that is their right. But it is not a reason to refrain from working.

## Our approaches

### 1. The isnād as network — not as list

We treat transmission chains as mappable **graphs**, not as linear lists. When a master has 40 students and only one reports a given tradition, this **tafarrud** is a signal — even if that transmitter is *thiqa*. When all paths of a tradition converge to a single node (bottleneck), the "plurality of routes" is a mirage.

We look for **absences** as much as presences: who does NOT transmit a narrative, and why?

> See [[Concepts/Tafarrud]], [[Concepts/Silsilat al-Dhahab]], [[Persons/Mālik ibn Saʿṣaʿa]]

### 2. The era of popularity of a tradition

A hadith does not appear out of nowhere. We identify **when** a tradition enters circulation, and we compare this dating with the legal, theological and political debates of the era. A tradition about rajm (stoning) that only appears when the Umayyads are applying it with the help of Ka'b al-Aḥbār does not hold the same value as a tradition attested from the first generation.

Meritorious ḥadīths about the Ḍuḥā prayer only appear in the ʿAbbāsid era. Traditions about qabd are attested only in al-Shām. Late multiplication is a classic indicator of retrospective fabrication.

> See [[Concepts/Qadar]], [[Concepts/Naskh]]

### 3. The geographic anchoring of traditions

A Prophetic "Sunna" should be universal. When a tradition exists only in one region — al-Shām, Kūfa, the Ḥijāz — and the scholars of other regions do not know it, the question arises: is it a Prophetic sunna or a local practice retrojected?

Al-Zuhrī himself admits regarding the prohibition of carnivores: "I never heard about it from our scholars in the Ḥijāz." When the official scholar of the Umayyad regime acknowledges not knowing a tradition in his own city, it is an admission.

> See [[Concepts/Kūfa vs al-Shām]], [[Traditions/Prohibition of carnivores]], [[Concepts/Ighrāb]]

### 4. Comparison with the ʿamal of the Companions

If the Prophet taught something, his Companions practiced it. When Ibn ʿUmar calls the Ḍuḥā prayer a *bidʿa*, when ʿĀʾisha says "I never saw the Prophet pray it," when Ibn Masʿūd did not pray any daytime supererogatory prayers — these negative testimonies are as informative as positive traditions.

The ʿamal (actual practice) is a test that many canonical traditions fail.

> See [[Concepts/Nabīdh, ṭilā', khamr]]

### 5. ʿIlm al-rijāl beyond the binary verdict

A transmitter is not a point on a thiqa/ḍaʿīf scale — he is an **individual** with a geography, employers, interests, a network. Qatāda is *thiqa* by consensus — but he is also a notorious mudallis, a qāṣṣ (court storyteller) paid by the Umayyad governor, a blind man who depended on a phantom reader absent from the isnāds, and the inventor of the "muʿtazila" label.

We build **prosopographic profiles** that illuminate what the verdict alone conceals.

> See [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma]], [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar]], [[Persons/al-Zuhrī]], [[Concepts/Quṣṣāṣ]]

### 6. The circulation of books and falsification

Hadith does not circulate only orally. Ṣaḥīfa (folios), nusakh (copies), compilations circulate — and are falsified. The scribes Khālid ibn Qāsim, Khālid ibn Najīḥ and Ḥabīb ibn Abī Ḥabīb are forgers identified by the tradition itself. Mālik's Muwaṭṭaʾ was subject to documented falsifications.

When a transmitter practices wijāda (finding in a book) and presents it as samāʿ (direct audition), the system produces inauthenticity in full formal legality.

> See [[Concepts/Falsification of nusakh]], [[Concepts/Al-Ziyāda fī al-kutub]], [[Concepts/Taḥammul al-ḥadīth]]

### 7. The nature of the chain AND the nature of what it propagates

A chain is not neutral with respect to its content. When the **sole** vector of isrāʾīlī material is a former student of Ka'b al-Aḥbār, and that material echoes the biblical texts that Ka'b taught, the chain and the content illuminate each other.

We track the circuits through which non-Prophetic material — isrāʾīliyyāt from Ka'b, qaṣaṣ from Qatāda, Umayyad practices — was **elevated** to the rank of canonical ḥadīth. The mawqūf → marfūʿ pattern is recurrent: Bukhārī systematically selects the minority variant that attributes a statement to the Prophet, when the majority of transmitters report it as a Companion's words.

> See [[Concepts/Isrā'īliyyāt]]

### 8. The system turned against itself

We do not use sources external to the Islamic tradition. Our arguments come from Bukhārī, Muslim, al-Dāraquṭnī, Ibn Ḥajar, Ibn Taymiyya, al-Bayhaqī. When al-Dāraquṭnī identifies 78 weak ḥadīths in Bukhārī and 100 in Muslim, it is a **classical** critic speaking, not an orientalist.

The paradox is that the system **documented its own flaws** — in the ʿIlal, the Ḍuʿafāʾ, the muṣṭalaḥ works — but chose not to draw the consequences. We draw the consequences.

> See [[Concepts/Tafarrud]], [[Concepts/Kafaytukum tadlīs thalātha]], [[Concepts/ʿAdālat al-Ṣaḥāba]]

## The limits of the analysis — and what we do not do

This work has a fundamental limit: we analyze how the patrimony speaks about itself. Our sources **are** the patrimony. When al-Dāraquṭnī identifies a weak hadith in Bukhārī, when Ibn ʿAdī examines the traditions of a rejected transmitter and finds no *munkar*, when Shuʿba denounces a *mudallis* — it is the system diagnosing itself. We merely take these diagnoses seriously.

But even this internal self-criticism has its limits. The judgments of the Companions about each other — ʿĀʾisha correcting Abū Hurayra, Ibn ʿUmar contradicting a tradition — reach us only through the same fragile chains. The "corrections" may be downstream transmitters settling scores under the cover of authority. We have no direct access to the voices of the 1st century — only to what the 2nd and 3rd centuries chose to preserve, transform, or fabricate.

The conclusion we reach is sober: **nothing is certain.** Where the classical patrimony deploys intellectual gymnastics — abrogation (*naskh*), conciliation (*al-jamʿ*), preponderance (*tarjīḥ*) — to maintain the appearance of coherence, we simply **observe the chaos** and show that the system did not achieve the goal it set for itself: to preserve the *dīn* with certainty. And *tarjīḥ* is perhaps the most vertiginous of these mechanisms: when two traditions contradict each other, one "prefers" the stronger chain and discards the other. A chain can be considered "reliable" for lack of a better option, then be relegated when a chain deemed stronger surpasses it. The "truth" of the *dīn* becomes a function of the power balance between isnāds — not an established fact. What was *ṣaḥīḥ* yesterday can become *marjūḥ* tomorrow, not because a fraud was discovered, but simply because a better competitor appeared.

**We do not promote anything.** We do not point toward anything — not Sunnism, not Shiism, not Muʿtazilism, not the exit. Each of these paths rests on a series of **axioms** — accepting such transmitter, rejecting such other, considering such consensus as binding — which necessarily entail closing one's eyes to part of the dossier. Everyone is free to follow what they wish, but **with full knowledge of the facts**.

We do not claim to hold absolute truth. This work is laborious, imperfect, and certainly debatable on this or that point. But every claim is **documented** and **sourced** — with references, pages, Arabic quotations. You can disagree with our conclusions: that is not the problem. The problem would be to ignore them without examining them.

And if, after reading these dossiers, you are still not convinced — if on the contrary it strengthens your convictions — then it will already be a service rendered. You will know **why** you practice what you practice. You will know the objections before they are raised against you, and you will already have your answers. A believer who has traversed criticism and maintains his faith is infinitely more solid than one who never asked the question.

The real problem is not that people follow this or that movement. It is that it is presented as **bulletproof**, as a methodological certainty, as an edifice whose foundations would be unassailable — when it is built on a tangle of *tadlīs*, contradictions, phantom transmitters, *ṣaḥīfa* presented as *samāʿ*, *mawqūf* traditions elevated to *marfūʿ*, and *jarḥ* judgments motivated by theological quarrels rather than actual deficiencies. Showing these flaws is not an attack — it is a service rendered to anyone who wants to understand what they base their practice upon.
