# Muḥammad ibn Kaʿb al-Quraẓī

**Exegete and maghāzī transmitter**, based first in **Kūfa** then settled in **Medina**. Unanimously declared **thiqa** by the classical critics. He is the **common informant** of Ibn Isḥāq and al-Wāqidī for the detailed account of the [[Traditions/Massacre of the Banū Qurayẓa|massacre of the Banū Qurayẓa]] — the transmission bottleneck through which passes the entirety of the mass narrative (numbers, trenches, individual episodes).

The problem is not reliability in the technical sense — nobody declares him weak. The problem is **identity**: he claims descent from a tribe allegedly exterminated, and he is the one who provides the account of that extermination. The circularity is total.

## The nisba "al-Quraẓī" — where does it come from?

### What the sources say

All classical sources repeat the same formula:

> كان أبوه كعب من سبي بني قريظة

> "His father Kaʿb was among the **captives** of Banū Qurayẓa."

The usual detail: Kaʿb was supposedly among those who **لم ينبت** (had no pubic hair), and was therefore spared under the criterion described in the hadith of ʿAṭiyya (Abū Dāwud [no. 4404](https://shamela.ws/book/1726/6171)). He then became *mawlā* (client) of an Anṣārī or "ally of the Aws" (من حلفاء الأوس).

### What the sources do not say

1. **The father Kaʿb has no riwāya.** Not a single hadith is preserved from him. His existence is attested only by his son's biography — that is, by the very network he is supposed to found.

2. **The nasab stops at Sulaym** (or Sulaym ibn Asad). No genealogy connects him to a known branch of the Banū Qurayẓa in pre-Islamic sources or tribal dictionaries.

3. **No independent isnād** establishes Kaʿb's filiation with the historical Banū Qurayẓa. The only proof is the **nisba** itself — "al-Quraẓī" — and the assertion that his father "was among the captives".

4. The mention **من حلفاء الأوس** ("among the allies of the Aws") could explain the nisba without requiring a blood link to the tribe. The Banū Qurayẓa were indeed allies of the Aws — but a client of the Aws could bear the tribal nisba without being biologically of the tribe.

### The circularity

The reasoning is circular:
- He is called "al-Quraẓī" --> therefore he descends from the Qurayẓa --> therefore his father was a captive from the massacre --> therefore the massacre took place as described --> and he is **the one** who provides the detailed account.

The massacre narrative **presupposes** the transmitter's identity, and the transmitter's identity **presupposes** the massacre narrative.

## The father — a ghost

Kaʿb ibn Sulaym (or Kaʿb ibn Ḥayyān ibn Sulaym):

| Question | Answer |
|----------|---------|
| Did he transmit a hadith? | **No** — لا أحفظ له رواية |
| Who attests his existence? | Only his son's biography |
| Who attests he was a Quraẓī captive? | Only his son (via the nisba) |
| Is there external testimony? | **None** |

A father without riwāya, without external attestation, whose existence rests entirely on the son who bears his nisba — and whose son is the source of the narrative that justifies this nisba.

## The Companions — whom did he actually meet?

The list of his shuyūkh in the Tahdhīb al-Kamāl is impressive: Anas, Jābir, Ibn ʿAbbās, Ibn ʿUmar, Ibn Masʿūd, Abū Hurayra, ʿAlī, al-ʿAbbās, Abū Dharr, Abū al-Dardāʾ, ʿĀʾisha, Muʿāwiya, and others.

But **Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal** dismantles this list:

> قيل: محمد بن كعب عمن روى من أصحاب النبي ﷺ ؟ قال : يقولون : **لم يرو إلا عن معاوية**

> "He was asked: from whom among the Companions did Muḥammad ibn Kaʿb transmit? He replied: they say he **transmitted only from Muʿāwiya**."

And for his transmission from ʿAlī:

> قال أبي : **وهذا وهم** ، محمد بن كعب يحدث عن عبد الله بن شداد ، عن علي

> "My father said: **this is an error** — Muḥammad ibn Kaʿb transmits in fact via ʿAbd Allāh ibn Shaddād, not directly from ʿAlī."

### The chronological problem

If he was born around **40H**:
- **Al-ʿAbbās** died in 32H --> **impossible**
- **Abū Dharr** died in 32H --> **impossible**
- **Abū al-Dardāʾ** died in 32H --> **impossible**
- **Ibn Masʿūd** died in 32H --> **impossible**
- **ʿAlī** died in 40H --> at best an infant

The vast majority of his transmissions from Companions are **undisclosed mursals**. Aḥmad recognises him as transmitting only from **Muʿāwiya** — the sole Companion whose dates (d. 60H) are compatible with actual audition.

| Abū Zurʿa | ثقة | idem |

| Al-Dhahabī | الإمام العلامة الصادق | Siyar |

**No jarḥ** is recorded. This is a case of total unanimity.

But this unanimity concerns his **reliability as a transmitter** — not the reality of his encounters with the Companions (which Aḥmad reduces to a single one), nor the truthfulness of his nisba (which nobody verifies), nor the quality of his Qurayẓa narrative (which is a composite *talfīq* in Ibn Isḥāq, not a hadith with a verifiable isnād).

## The "Quraẓī" network

Among the students of Muḥammad ibn Kaʿb, one finds:
- **ʿUthmān ibn Kaʿb al-Quraẓī** — his own brother
- **Muḥammad ibn Rifāʿa al-Quraẓī** — another "Quraẓī"

And among al-Wāqidī's informants on Qurayẓa:
- **ʿAṭiyya al-Quraẓī** (the survivor from the Abū Dāwud hadith)
- **Al-Mithwar al-Quraẓī** via Thaʿlaba al-Quraẓī

This is a **family/clan network** — the "Quraẓīs" transmit among themselves the account of their own tribe. The pattern is that of a closed family memory that feeds itself, not of open and verifiable transmission.

## What he provides on Qurayẓa

Muḥammad ibn Kaʿb is explicitly named by **Ibn Isḥāq** in the composite isnād of the Qurayẓa narrative:

> حدثني عاصم بن عمر بن قتادة وعبد الله بن أبي بكر **ومحمد بن كعب القرظي** والزهري...

And he is one of the main informants of **al-Wāqidī** for the same account.

What does he transmit? The composite isnād does not allow us to know — this is the structural blind spot. But he is the only informant common to both channels (Ibn Isḥāq and al-Wāqidī), and he is a **presumed descendant** of the tribe. There is thus a strong probability that he is the source of the narrative details that the other informants (al-Zuhrī, ʿĀṣim ibn ʿUmar, etc.) do not provide — notably the **number** and the **individual episodes** (Ḥuyayy ibn Akhṭab, Rayḥāna, the woman with the millstone).

He also transmits the account of **Rayḥāna** (Ibn Saʿd, Ṭabaqāt) — the Quraẓī captive whom the Prophet allegedly took.

## Qutayba's confusion — a clue

Al-Tirmidhī reports that **Qutayba ibn Saʿīd** claimed Muḥammad ibn Kaʿb "was born during the Prophet's lifetime". **Ibn Ḥajar** corrects in al-Iṣāba:

> وهو وهم من قتيبة وإنما ورد ذلك في حق كعب والد محمد

> "This is an error by Qutayba — this information concerns **Kaʿb, the father** of Muḥammad, not Muḥammad himself."

The confusion between father and son — who share a confused biography and an unverifiable nisba — is itself a symptom. When critics themselves mix up generations, the solidity of the filiation is in question.

The gap of **12 years** between 108 and 120H is abnormally wide for a transmitter of this calibre. Circumstances: collapse of a mosque roof.
