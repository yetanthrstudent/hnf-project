# Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī

**Central figure of the Basran network**, considered "sayyid al-tābi'īn" (master of the Successors). Born in Medina in 21 H, his mother Khayra was the freedwoman of Umm Salama — al-Ḥasan grew up in the entourage of the prophetic household. He settled in Basra, where he became the intellectual pole of an entire generation.

Classified as **thiqa** (reliable), but Ibn Ḥajar describes him unambiguously: **"كان يرسل كثيراً ويدلس"** — "he practiced massive irsāl and tadlīs" (Taqrīb al-Tahdhīb). Placed in the **2nd ṭabaqa** of the mudallis (Ṭabaqāt al-Mudallisīn of Ibn Ḥajar): tolerated by the imams because of his prestige, but the disconnection in his chains is a documented fact.

## Irsāl — the systematic disconnection

### The scope of the problem

Al-Ḥasan claimed to have "met 300 Companions" and "70 veterans of Badr." But the samāʿ confirmed by the critics covers only a small fraction of those from whom he transmits.

**Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal** confirms the samāʿ of: **[[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]], Ibn 'Umar, 'Abd al-Raḥmān ibn Samura, 'Amr ibn Taghlib, al-Mughīra ibn Shu'ba, Abū Barza al-Aslamī.**

### The proven disconnections

**[[Persons/Abū Hurayra|Abū Hurayra]]**: [[Persons/Shu'ba ibn al-Ḥajjāj|Shuʿba]] asked Yūnus ibn 'Ubayd: "Did al-Ḥasan hear from Abū Hurayra?" Yūnus replied: **"ما رآه قط"** — "He never even saw him." Ibn al-Madīnī, Abū Ḥātim and Abū Zur'a confirm: **no samāʿ from Abū Hurayra**.

**'Alī ibn Abī Ṭālib**: Ibn al-Madīnī denies the samāʿ, while conceding a possible childhood encounter in Medina.

**Abū Mūsā al-Ash'arī, Jābir, Ibn 'Abbās**: transmissions without confirmed samāʿ.

Al-Dāraquṭnī summarizes: **"مراسيله فيها ضعف"** — "His mursal contain weakness."

Al-Ḥasan himself reportedly said: **"ما كل ما نفتي به سمعناه، بل رأيناه خيراً لهم"** — "We did not hear everything on which we give fatwa; we simply considered it good for the people." — an admission that content takes precedence over the chain.

## The books of al-Ḥasan — a written corpus

Al-Ḥasan was not a purely oral transmitter. He possessed **kutub** (books/notebooks):

**The testimony of [[Persons/Ḥammād ibn Salama|Ḥammād ibn Salama]]** (al-'Uqaylī, [p. 770](https://shamela.ws/book/13041/770); al-'Ilal of Aḥmad, no. 3831):

> « أخذ حميد كتب الحسن فنسخها ثم ردها عليه »

> "[[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl|Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]] took the **books of al-Ḥasan**, copied them, then returned them to him."

**His son 'Abd Allāh** reportedly burned his father's books after his death, except for one ṣaḥīfa — which confirms the existence of **several written documents**.

**His function as kātib**: al-Ḥasan served as **secretary** (kātib) to al-Rabī' ibn Ziyād al-Ḥārithī, Umayyad governor of Khurāsān, from approximately 43 H for about ten years. The man knew how to write, administer, and produce documents.

**The Risāla on qadar**: a letter addressed to caliph 'Abd al-Malik ibn Marwān on the question of free will — the oldest known theological document in Islam (see Qadar section below).

The existence of these books is crucial: [[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl|Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]] had access to a written reservoir of traditions that he could then present as orally heard from Anas.

## The qāṣṣ — the preacher-storyteller

Al-Ḥasan practiced **qaṣṣ** (narrative public preaching), as did [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] after him.

**Ṭabaqāt Ibn Sa'd**: his own mother would preach to women — **"رأيت أم الحسن تقص على النساء"**. It was a family practice.

**'Alī ibn al-Ḥusayn** (Zayn al-'Ābidīn) found him near the Ka'ba **"يقص"** (doing qaṣṣ) during the Ḥajj and confronted him. Al-Ḥasan reportedly ceased his public qaṣṣ after this encounter.

Qaṣṣ blends Quranic narratives, isrā'īliyyāt, moral exhortations and prophetic traditions into a free oral flow. It is the same genre practiced by [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar|Ka'b al-Ahbar]], [[Persons/Nawf al-Bikālī|Nawf al-Bikālī]] and [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]]. The difference between a qāṣṣ and a muḥaddith is that the qāṣṣ does not adhere to the rigor of the isnād — he transmits for **effect**, not for the chain.

## The relay of Ka'b al-Ahbar's isrā'īliyyāt

Al-Ḥasan is a link in the transmission of [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar|Ka'b al-Ahbar]]'s traditions into Quranic exegesis.

**Tafsīr al-Tha'labī** ([p. 12081](https://shamela.ws/book/18686/12081)): the chain **Qatāda ← al-Ḥasan ← Ka'b** for the story of David.

Ka'b died around 32-34 H. Al-Ḥasan, born in 21 H, was approximately 11-13 years old — a direct meeting is chronologically possible but not confirmed by the critics. The chain is very likely yet another **irsāl**: al-Ḥasan says "Ka'b said" without having heard him, consistent with his usual practice.

The circuit:

```
Ka'b al-Ahbar (d. ~32 H)
        │
        ? (probable irsāl)
        ↓
  AL-ḤASAN AL-BAṢRĪ (books + qaṣṣ)
        │
        ├── QATĀDA (tadlīs: "al-Ḥasan said")
        │       │
        │   Hammām / Sa'īd / Shu'ba
        │       ↓
        │   "PROPHETIC" HADITHS
        │
        └── ḤUMAYD (copies al-Ḥasan's books)
                │
            "Anas said" (tadlīs)
```

## Political connections

### Kātib of the governor of Khurāsān

Al-Ḥasan was **secretary** to al-Rabī' ibn Ziyād al-Ḥārithī, Umayyad governor of Khurāsān, for about ten years (from ~43 H). He was an official of the Umayyad apparatus.

### Al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf

The relationship is described as tense. Al-Ḥasan reportedly criticized him publicly. During the revolt of Ibn al-Ash'ath (82-83 H), al-Ḥasan refused to engage militarily — neither with the authorities nor with the rebellion: **"لا تكن مع هؤلاء ولا مع هؤلاء"** ("Be neither with these nor with those").

### Qāḍī of Basra

He was appointed **judge** (qāḍī) of Basra around 102 H — eight years before his death. A state function.

### The classification of four regional scholars

[[Persons/Sulaymān ibn Mūsā|Sulaymān ibn Mūsā]] classifies him as **the scholar of Iraq** in his classification of the four regional poles: al-Zuhrī (Ḥijāz), **al-Ḥasan** (Iraq), [[Persons/Maymūn ibn Mahrān|Maymūn ibn Mahrān]] (Jazīra), [[Persons/Makhūl al-Shāmī|Makhūl al-Shāmī]] (Shām).

## The question of qadar

Al-Ḥasan is associated with **qadarism** (the doctrine of human free will). His **Risāla** addressed to 'Abd al-Malik ibn Marwān is considered the oldest Islamic theological document on the subject — he defends human responsibility and denies that God is the author of evil.

His students **Wāṣil ibn 'Aṭā'** (d. 131 H) and **'Amr ibn 'Ubayd** (d. 144 H), founders of Mu'tazilism, claim him as a precursor.

Al-Shahrastānī (al-Milal wa al-Niḥal) suggests that the Risāla might be from Wāṣil rather than al-Ḥasan. Later Sunni sources affirm that al-Ḥasan **retracted** (raja'a) from this position.

The same pattern as [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]], accused of qadarism — the two central figures of the Basran network share this theological inclination.

## Synthesis

Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī is the **central node** of the Basran transmission network. His prestige as "sayyid al-tābi'īn" masks a structurally problematic profile: massive irsāl, documented tadlīs, oral qaṣṣ blending genres, written books serving as an unattributed reservoir, political connections with the Umayyad apparatus. He is the source (often mursal) through which Ka'b's isrā'īliyyāt flow into tafsīr, and the master from whom [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] and [[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl|Ḥumayd]] draw their material — one through verbal tadlīs, the other through copying books.
