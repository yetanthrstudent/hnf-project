# Qatāda ibn Di'āma al-Sadūsī

Great exegete and transmitter of [[Basra]], **blind from birth**, endowed with an exceptional memory. Qatāda is not a simple muḥaddith: he is a **[[Concepts/Quṣṣāṣ|qāṣṣ]]** — preacher-storyteller — whose narratives were then transformed into hadiths by his students. **Exegetical relay** of the [[Concepts/Isrā'īliyyāt]] of [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar]] toward Quranic tafsīr.

## Profile

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Blindness | Blind from birth — all his knowledge is oral |
| Memory | Reputed phenomenal — but without work of critical selection |
| Tadlīs | **Famous for tadlīs** from Anas and al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī (al-Ḥākim, p. 5997/19) |
| Qadar | Accused of qadarism (free will) |
| Qaṣṣ | « كان قتادة يقص » — he practised qaṣṣ (Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal, p. 3617/96) |
| Death | 117 H at **Wāsiṭ**, at the residence of governor [[Persons/Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid al-Qasrī]] (Tahdhīb al-Kamāl [p. 26743/25](https://shamela.ws/book/26743/25)) |

## Relations

- **Transmits from** [[Persons/Shahr ibn Ḥawshab]] the hadith of the [[Traditions/Hijra to the Shām|hijra]] (Dimashq p. 28829)
- **Transmits from** al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī and Ibn Sam'ān traditions going back to [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar]] (Tha'labī p. 12081)
- **Transmits from** [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar]] directly: the brain through the nostrils (Ḥilyat al-Awliyā' p. 7997)
- **Receives provisions from** [[Persons/Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid al-Qasrī]] at Wāsiṭ — dies in debt at his residence (Tahdhīb al-Kamāl [p. 26743/25](https://shamela.ws/book/26743/25))
- **Transmitted by** Sa'īd ibn Bashīr (declared weak, "transmits the munkarat of Qatāda")
- **Transmitted by** Hammām ibn Yaḥyā, Sa'īd ibn Abī 'Arūba, [[Persons/Shu'ba ibn al-Ḥajjāj|Shuʿba]], [[Persons/Ḥammād ibn Salama]] and others
- **Never met by** [[Persons/Ḥammād ibn Zayd]] — who had prepared the sheets to write from him, but Qatāda died before the meeting
- **Contemporary of** [[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]] and [[Persons/Thābit al-Bunānī]] — the three form the Basran core of transmitters from Anas, all linked to [[Persons/Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid al-Qasrī]]
- **Hidden source of Ḥumayd** — Ḥumayd confused what he had heard from Qatāda and Thābit with what he had heard from Anas (al-'Uqaylī, [p. 770](https://shamela.ws/book/13041/770))
- **Memorises the ṣaḥīfa of** Sulaymān ibn Qays al-Yashkurī — without having heard him (died in the fitna of Ibn al-Zubayr). See [[Persons/Jābir ibn 'Abd Allāh]]
- **Sole vector of** [[Mālik ibn Saʿṣaʿa]] — presumed Companion, known only through the Miʿrāj hadith via Qatāda ← Anas
- **Grafts onto** the chain of the [[Traditions/Ḥadīth Jibrīl|hadith of Jibrīl]] via ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda — **without samāʿ** (al-Tirmidhī)

## The qāṣṣ, not the muḥaddith

### Direct evidence: Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal (al-'Ilal, p. 3617/96-97)

1. **كان قتادة يقص** — "Qatāda practised qaṣṣ" (p. 96). Hammām confirms: **كان قتادة يقص علينا** — "Qatāda used to tell us stories (qaṣṣ)".

2. **سمعت قتادة يقول في قصصه** — "I heard Qatāda say in his tales (qaṣaṣ)" regarding the Jahannamiyyūn, the people who exit Hell (p. 97).

Qaṣṣ is an oral genre distinct from hadith: the storyteller freely mixes Quranic narratives, isrā'īliyyāt, moral exhortations and prophetic traditions in a narrative flow intended for an audience. Qatāda's **students** then extracted fragments from this flow and presented them as hadiths with chains of transmission.

### Systemic tadlīs

Al-Ḥākim al-Naysābūrī (Ma'rifat 'Ulūm al-Ḥadīth, p. 5997/19):
> "Qatāda ibn Di'āma, imam of the people of insight and tafsīr... **مشهور بالتدليس عنهما** — famous for tadlīs from both of them" (= [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik]] and al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī).

Qatāda's tadlīs is **structural**: he says "Anas said" or "al-Ḥasan said" without having actually heard from them. His real sources are often unnamed intermediaries — or his own oral qaṣaṣ.

### Shu'ba and the "protection" against tadlīs

Shu'ba asserts: « **كفيتكم تدليس ثلاثة: الأعمش، وأبي إسحاق، وقتادة** » — "I have spared you the tadlīs of three." See [[Concepts/Kafaytukum tadlīs thalātha]].

His method: **watching Qatāda's mouth** (كنت أتفطن إلى فم قتادة, al-ʿIlal, [p. 1390](https://shamela.ws/book/2331/1390)) to distinguish "ḥaddathanā" (heard) from "ḥaddatha" (not heard). When Qatāda said "**ḥaddathanā** Anas / al-Ḥasan / Sa'īd / Muṭarrif", Shu'ba considered the transmission as direct. When he said "**ḥaddatha** Sulaymān ibn Yasār" or "**qāla** Abū Qilāba", it was tadlīs (al-'Ilal, [p. 1390](https://shamela.ws/book/2331/1390)).

**The Abū al-'Āliya case**: Shu'ba asserts that Qatāda heard from Abū al-'Āliya only **3 things** (Tirmidhī, [p. 318](https://shamela.ws/book/7895/318)). Yet the Isrā' hadith (Qatāda <-- Abū al-'Āliya <-- Ibn 'Abbās), which **is not among the three**, is in Bukhārī (no. 3239, 3396) and Muslim (no. 165) — muttafaq 'alayh (unanimously recognised as authentic). In Muslim, **Shu'ba himself** transmits this hadith, and Qatāda declares **« سمعت أبا العالية »** ("I heard Abū al-'Āliya"). Shu'ba thus transmits a 4th hadith where Qatāda claims direct samāʿ, contradicting his own tally. See [[Concepts/Kafaytukum tadlīs thalātha]].

## Death at Wāsiṭ — the political link

Tahdhīb al-Kamāl (p. 26743/25): Qatāda dies at **Wāsiṭ** at the residence of governor **Khālid al-Qasrī**. He was in debt and received provisions (أرزاق) from Khālid. Ibn al-Jaʿd (Musnad, p. 166 no. 1073) confirms independently: "مات قتادة بواسط كان عند خالد بن عبد الله القسري".

Recurring pattern: like [[Persons/Nawf al-Bikālī]] (qāṣṣ and Umayyad soldier), like [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar]] (authorised by Mu'āwiya), Qatāda is a scholar **maintained by the authorities**. The independence of the preacher from the Umayyad state is a fiction — the quṣṣāṣ are **functionaries of the sacred**.

### The relationship with Khālid al-Qasrī — more than a patron

Khālid is not a simple host. The link is a **reciprocal intellectual dependence**.

**1. Consultant on qirā'āt — extending to the Quranic text** — al-Muḥtasib of Ibn Jinnī 1/82 ([book/8660](https://shamela.ws/book/8660)):

> أرسلني خالد بن عبد الله القسري إلى قتادة أسأله عن حروف من القرآن

> "Khālid sent me to Qatāda to ask him about **variant readings of the Quran**."

Among the readings discussed: verse 2:54 (Sūrat al-Baqara), **"fa-qtulū anfusakum"** (فاقتلوا أنفسكم — "kill one another"). Qatāda proposes the alternative reading **"fa-qtalū anfusakum"** (فاقتَلوا — from iftiqāl/istiqtāl, mutual combat), which modifies the verse's meaning: no longer an order for expiatory suicide, but a battle between two groups.

The case is extreme. Umayyad patronage does not only concern hadith — it extends to the **reading of the Quran itself**. The qāṣṣ-mudallis is also a **consultant on qirā'āt** for the governor. When a prefect sends an emissary to a scholar to ask him how to read the Book, and the scholar lives at that same prefect's expense, the boundary between scholarly authority and political service disappears. Qatāda does not merely transmit hadith for the benefit of power — he **adjusts the Quranic text** at the request of power.

**2. Provisions and redistribution** — al-Ziyādāt fī Kitāb al-Jūd wal-Sakhāʾ lil-Ṭabarānī ([p. 25](https://shamela.ws/book/26743/25)):

> قدم علينا قتادة واسط على خالد بن عبد الله القسري وكان يجري عليه نزلا له قيمة

> "Qatāda came to us in Wāsiṭ at Khālid's, and the latter paid him **provisions of value**."

**3. The collusion on the brotherhood hadith** — Bukhārī no. 13:

The hadith "لا يؤمن أحدكم حتى يحب لأخيه ما يحب لنفسه" is transmitted by Shuʿba ← Qatāda ← Anas. But Khālid al-Qasrī reports **the same hadith** from his own father — claiming that his grandfather Yazīd ibn Asad was a Companion (Juzʾ al-Alf Dīnār lil-Qaṭīʿī p. 368 no. 234). Yet the claim of Ṣuḥba is **denied** in Tārīkh Dimashq 65/105:

> قالوا لا [لصحبة يزيد بن أسد جد خالد] قال من يقول ذاك ولده

> "They said: [Did Yazīd ibn Asad, Khālid's grandfather, have Ṣuḥba?] No. — Who claims it? His own descendants."

The governor and the qāṣṣ transmit the same hadith through two apparently independent chains — but the convergence is suspect when one knows the first is the patron of the second.

### Sources without selection

Ibn Abī Khaythama (al-Tārīkh al-Kabīr 2/240 no. 2674):

> كان قتادة وعمرو بن شعيب لا يغث عليهما شيء يأخذان من كل أحد

> "Qatāda and ʿAmr ibn Shuʿayb — **nothing repelled them**: they took from **anyone**."

And Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal via Abū Dāwūd (al-Jāmiʿ li-ʿUlūm al-Imām Aḥmad — al-Rijāl 18/463):

> أحاديث قتادة عن سعيد بن المسيب ما أدري كيف هي؟ قد أدخل بينه وبين سعيد نحوًا من عشرة رجال لا يعرفون

> "Qatāda's hadiths from Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab — I don't know **what to make of them**. He has inserted between himself and Saʿīd **about ten unknown men**."

The problem is not just tadlīs (omitting an intermediary) — it is the opposite: Qatāda **interposes** phantom sources between himself and a master he probably never heard directly. Aḥmad does not understand the logic of these insertions, and for good reason: there is none. They are the traces of a qaṣṣ who draws from heterogeneous sources without traceability.

### The Daghfal network — the masked Umayyad chain

Tārīkh Abī Zurʿa al-Dimashqī p. 151:

> حدثنا قتادة عن الحسن عن دغفل بن حنظلة أن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم توُفِّيَ وهو ابن خمس وستين

Qatāda ← al-Ḥasan ← **Daghfal ibn Ḥanẓala**: the Prophet supposedly died at 65 years old. Daghfal is the **tutor of Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiya** — a chronicler and genealogist in the direct service of the first Umayyad heir. The chain shows Qatāda relaying material from the immediate entourage of power, with al-Ḥasan as an intermediary masked by tadlīs.

### Shuʿba investigates on the ground

ʿIlal wa-Maʿrifat al-Rijāl li-Aḥmad (riwāyat ʿAbdallāh) 1/270:

> حدثني أبي قال حدثنا محمد بن سواء قال جاء شعبة إلى عوف فجعل يسأله رأيت قتادة عند خلاس

Shuʿba goes to ʿAwf and questions him: "Did you see Qatāda **at Khilās** [ibn ʿAmr]'s?" Khilās is a direct transmitter of **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib** — his material is therefore Kūfan and pro-ʿAlid. Shuʿba's question is precise: did Qatāda have **personal access** to Khilās, or was he transmitting from his **manuscripts** (ṣaḥīfas) without having frequented him?

The verdict is known through other channels. **Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Qaṭṭān** — the most rigorous of Basran critics — **refused to transmit anything from the chain Qatāda ← Khilās**:

> يحيى بن سعيد لا يحدث عن قتادة عن خلاس بن عمرو شيئا — يعني **كأنه لم يسمع منه**

> "Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd transmitted nothing from Qatāda from Khilās ibn ʿAmr — **meaning he had not heard [directly] from him**." — Musnad Aḥmad 8/4171 (ḥ. 18631), reprised in Tuḥfat al-Taḥṣīl fī al-Marāsīl 1/417.

Aḥmad confirms the mechanism: Qatāda **inserted Khilās and al-Ḥasan** as intermediaries between himself and sources to which he had no direct access (e.g. Abū Rāfiʿ). Shuʿba investigates, Yaḥyā rules: the chain Qatāda ← Khilās is **mursal**. Qatāda never frequented Khilās.

This is not simple tadlīs — it is a deeper problem. Classical tadlīs consists of hiding a weak intermediary in an oral chain. What Qatāda does with Khilās is something else: he **absorbs an entire written corpus** — the ṣaḥīfas of a man he never met — and **injects it into the oral transmission system** as though it were samāʿ. This is **[[Concepts/Taḥammul al-ḥadīth|wijāda]] disguised as audition**. The credibility of the entire system rests on the principle that the transmitter **heard** from his source. Qatāda short-circuits this principle: he reads manuscripts and reproduces them as if he had received them orally. And the material of Khilās — a direct transmitter of ʿAlī — thus enters the Basran circuit without anyone being able to verify what truly comes from ʿAlī and what is Qatāda's reading.

## The Qatāda-Anas problem: tadlīs and real sources

When a hadith is reported **only by Qatāda** or **only by [[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]]**, the declared source is almost always either **al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī** (often without explicit source), or **[[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik]]**. Yet the chain al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī --> Anas is considered **unreliable** — al-Ḥasan did not truly hear from Anas most of what he transmits.

This creates a methodological necessity: to verify whether a hadith attributed to Anas is authentic, one must **study the other reporters from Anas** — those who are not Basran mudallisīn — and check whether they know the same tradition. When a hadith is known only through the circuit Qatāda/Ḥumayd --> Anas, without corroboration from other students of Anas, the probability that it is a late projection is high.

## The sole vector — major accounts without corroboration

Tadlīs and wijāda are only part of the problem. The other is **tafarrud**: Qatāda is, for several major accounts, the **sole transmitter** — without any independent corroboration.

### Mālik ibn Saʿṣaʿa and the Miʿrāj

The hadith of the **Miʿrāj** (the Prophet's nocturnal ascension through the seven heavens) is one of the most important accounts in the Islamic tradition. Its chain of transmission poses a fundamental problem: the hadith is reported by **[[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik]]** from a certain **[[Persons/Mālik ibn Saʿṣaʿa]]**, described as a Companion. Yet:

1. **No one reports this hadith from Anas except Qatāda.** The dozens of other students of Anas do not know this tradition.
2. **Al-Dāraquṭnī confirms** that Qatāda is the sole vector of this character (ʿIlal al-Dāraquṭnī, book/9082 -- *ref. to verify*).
3. **Mālik ibn Saʿṣaʿa is attested by no independent source** outside this single hadith. No autonomous biography, no other tradition, no mention in Medinan or Basran sources outside this chain.

The pattern is identical to Khilās, Abū al-ʿĀliya and the others: Qatāda as **sole entry point** for a major account into the canonical corpus. The "Companion" status of Mālik ibn Saʿṣaʿa rests entirely on a single transmitter known for systematic tadlīs. See the entry **[[Persons/Mālik ibn Saʿṣaʿa]]**.

### The hadith of Jibrīl — Qatāda grafts onto the chain

Qatāda transmits the **[[Traditions/Ḥadīth Jibrīl|hadith of Jibrīl]]** via **ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda** — the 2nd link in the main chain (Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur → Ibn Burayda). Yet **al-Tirmidhī** declares:

> لا نعرف لقتادة سماعا من عبد الله بن بريدة

> "We know of no audition by Qatāda from ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda."

Qatāda is not the main vector of this hadith — it is the chain Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur ← Ibn Burayda that is. But the fact that Qatāda attempts to graft onto this chain **without samāʿ** reveals the mechanism: a hadith circulates in the Basran milieu, and Qatāda — the qāṣṣ who transmits everything that reaches him — appropriates it by declaring a source he never heard.

### The Ṣaḥīfat Jābir — open wijāda

The case of the **ṣaḥīfa of [[Persons/Jābir ibn 'Abd Allāh]]** is documented by two independent sources:

1. **Bukhārī** states: "*lam yasmaʿ Qatāda min Sulaymān ibn Qays al-Yashkurī*" — Qatāda did not hear Sulaymān al-Yashkurī, the author of the ṣaḥīfa.

2. **Sulaymān al-Taymī** confirms the mechanism:

> ذهبوا بصحيفة جابر إلى قتادة فرواها

> "They brought the ṣaḥīfa of Jābir to Qatāda and he transmitted it."

This is pure **[[Concepts/Taḥammul al-ḥadīth|wijāda]]**: a manuscript brought to a blind man, who memorises it and integrates it into his oral repertoire as if he had received it through samāʿ. The same pattern as for Khilās ibn ʿAmr — entire written corpora absorbed and reproduced as direct oral transmission.

Qatāda claims it himself:

> لأنا لصحيفة جابر بن عبد الله أحفظ مني لسورة البقرة

> "I know the ṣaḥīfa of Jābir **better than Sūrat al-Baqara**." — Maʿmar, via ʿAbd al-Razzāq, via Aḥmad.

And Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna (Tahdhīb al-Kamāl) specifies the function: **كان قتادة يقص بصحيفة جابر** — "Qatāda **used to do qaṣṣ** with the ṣaḥīfa of Jābir." The manuscript is not an archival document — it is a **preaching tool**.

### The phantom reader — who reads to the blind man?

Qatāda is **blind from birth**. He physically cannot read a manuscript. Someone had to read the ṣaḥīfa of Jābir, the notebooks of Khilās, and every other written document he integrated into his repertoire. This reader is a **real intermediary** in the chain of transmission — but he **never** appears in the isnāds.

The sources systematically use the **passive voice** to hide the agent:

- Aḥmad: **قُرِئَتْ عَلَيْهِ** صحيفة جابر مرة واحدة فحفظها — "the ṣaḥīfa of Jābir **was read to him** once and he memorised it"
- Sulaymān al-Taymī: **ذَهَبُوا** بصحيفة جابر إلى الحسن فرواها، **وذهبوا** بها إلى قتادة فأخذها، **وأتوني** بها فلم أردها — "**They** brought the ṣaḥīfa to al-Ḥasan, he transmitted it; **they** brought it to Qatāda, he took it; **they** brought it to me, I refused it."

Sulaymān al-Taymī's full testimony is revealing: the same anonymous persons (**ذهبوا**) circulate the manuscript among Basra's scholars — al-Ḥasan accepts it, Qatāda accepts it, Sulaymān refuses. The manuscript carriers are **real intermediaries** whose names and reliability are unknown.

The only case where a reader is **named** in Qatāda's entourage is **Saʿīd ibn Abī ʿArūba**:

> قال قتادة لسعيد بن أبي عروبة: يا أبا النضر، **خذ المصحف**

> "Qatāda said to Saʿīd ibn Abī ʿArūba: O Abū al-Naḍr, **take the muṣḥaf**!"

Saʿīd recites al-Baqara to him as a memory test. He is also the **most prolific transmitter** of Qatāda — "the most retentive of his companions" (Abū Dāwūd al-Ṭayālisī). He served as secretary-reader before being a transmitter.

The flaw is structural: in every isnād where Qatāda transmits from a written source, the real schema is:

```
What the isnād says:       Qatāda ← Khilās ← ʿAlī
What actually happened:    Qatāda ← [ANONYMOUS READER] ← [MANUSCRIPT] ← Khilās ← ʿAlī
```

The reader is a **necessary** but **invisible** link. His identity and reliability are never evaluated by the critics. The treatises of muṣṭalaḥ al-ḥadīth discuss at length the hierarchy of reception methods (samāʿ, ʿarḍ, ijāza, wijāda) — but never raise the question: when a blind man receives a written text, **who is the reader, and why does he have no status in the isnād?**

## Case study: the flogging of the wine drinker

Four versions of the same hadith passing through Qatāda <-- Anas produce **contradictory** accounts regarding the instrument, the number of blows and the actors. Four direct students of Qatāda (Shuʿba, Hishām, Ibn Abī ʿArūba, Hammām) give incompatible versions. The intermediary Shabbāba ibn Sawwār, confirmed as very reliable by Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn, **adds al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī** between Qatāda and Anas in the isnād — a link that all other transmitters omit. This is the tadlīs described by al-Ḥākim in action.

For the complete analysis of the versions, the ziyāda of al-Ḥasan, and what the divergence among the fuqahāʾ reveals about the hadith's dating, see **[[Traditions/Flogging the wine drinker]]**.

## The question of real sources

### Methodology

Study Qatāda as one studies any transmitter in the network: not what he says he heard (declared isnād), but **from whom he actually learned** and in what context:

1. **His real masters**: Did Qatāda truly have sufficient contact with Anas? Al-Ḥasan? Or do his traditions pass through unmentioned intermediaries?
2. **His life**: blind, Basran, oral memory — his knowledge comes from **qaṣṣ assemblies**, not from dictation sessions (imlā').
3. **His ideology**: qadarī — a theological position that influences the transmitted content.
4. **The process of transformation**: Qatāda tells stories (qaṣaṣ) --> his students (Hammām, Sa'īd, Shu'ba) cut these narratives into individual hadiths with formal isnāds.

## Summary

Qatāda is the **conversion point** where oral qaṣṣ (a free genre, mixing scriptural, Quranic and invented sources) transforms into formal hadith (a codified genre, with isnād). His prestige as a blind exegete with a prodigious memory masked this process. The result: traditions of [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar]] circulating in the canonical collections under the guise of "Qatāda <-- Anas <-- the Prophet" chains — whereas the real source is an oral flow of Basran qaṣaṣ fed by isrā'īliyyāt.
