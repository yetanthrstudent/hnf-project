# Ibn al-Ashʿath — the revolt that decimated the Iraqi transmitters

**ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Muḥammad ibn al-Ashʿath al-Kindī** (d. 85H / 704), grandson of **al-Ashʿath ibn Qays** — a Companion who fought at Ṣiffīn on ʿAlī's side then submitted to Muʿāwiya. Noble of the tribe of **Kinda**, a royal Yemeni lineage. Military commander appointed by **al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf** to lead the **Sijistān** campaign (modern Afghanistan/Baluchistan), he mutinied in 81H and triggered the largest revolt against the Umayyad regime in Iraq.

For the HNF project, this revolt is a **structuring event in the transmission network**: it physically decimates a generation of Iraqi transmitters, deepens the Kufa/Basra vs. Shām fracture, and conditions the survival or disappearance of entire chains.

## Chronology

| Date | Event |
|------|-----------|
| 78-80 H | Al-Ḥajjāj appoints Ibn al-Ashʿath commander of the Sijistān army |
| **81 H** | **Mutiny** — Ibn al-Ashʿath refuses the order for a reckless advance, turns against al-Ḥajjāj |
| 81-82 H | Series of victories — sources speak of **80 battles** won against Syrian forces |
| **82 H** | **Kufa joins the revolt** — the fuqahāʾ and qurrāʾ form a battalion (katībat al-qurrāʾ) |
| **83 H (702)** | **Battle of Dayr al-Jamājim** — decisive defeat near Kufa |
| 83-85 H | Flight to Sijistān, then handed over by Rutbīl (king of Zābulistān) |
| **85 H (704)** | Death — thrown from a roof (or suicide) |

---

## The causes — what the scholars reproached al-Ḥajjāj for

The revolt was not a simple military putsch. The **fuqahāʾ and muḥaddithūn** of Iraq participated massively, and the sources document their grievances:

1. **Arbitrary violence** — executions without trial, massacre of prisoners
2. **Humiliation of the Companions** — al-Ḥajjāj brands [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]] with a **seal** (ختم) on the hand, a gesture reserved for slaves and dhimmīs. He calls Anas a "**fitna vagrant**" (جوّال في الفتنة — Ṭabarānī [p. 823](https://shamela.ws/book/1733/823))
3. **Delaying prayers** — al-Ḥajjāj systematically delayed the congregational prayers, which constituted a major religious grievance
4. **Fiscal tyranny** — heavy taxation of the mawālī and Iraqis

Al-Shaʿbī summarizes the call to arms:

> **« قاتلوهم على جورهم واستذلالهم الضعفاء وإماتتهم الصلاة »**

> "Fight them for their **tyranny**, their **enslavement of the weak**, and their **killing of the prayer**."

## The katībat al-qurrāʾ — the scholars' battalion

The central fact for the HNF project: **hundreds of scholars** took up arms. Khalīfa ibn Khayyāṭ reports **500 scholars** in Ibn al-Ashʿath's army. Among them, names that populate the isnāds of canonical collections.

| Scholar | Status in hadith | Fate after defeat |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** | Tābiʿī, direct student of Ibn ʿAbbās, pillar of tafsīr | Fled, hid ~12 years, arrested by [[Persons/Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī\|Khālid al-Qasrī]], **executed by al-Ḥajjāj in 95 H** |
| **ʿĀmir al-Shaʿbī** (d. 106 H) | Great jurist and muḥaddith of Kufa | Captured, brought before al-Ḥajjāj, made a sincere confession → **pardoned** |

## The battle of Dayr al-Jamājim (83 H / 702)

Decisive battle near Kufa. The **katībat al-qurrāʾ** (battalion of reciters/scholars) was on the **front line**. Al-Ḥajjāj's Syrian army, better equipped and better organized, crushed the revolt. ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Abī Laylā drowned in the Euphrates. Muḥammad ibn Saʿd ibn Abī Waqqāṣ was captured and executed there.

Ibn al-Ashʿath fled eastward. He was eventually handed over by **Rutbīl**, king of Zābulistān, under pressure from al-Ḥajjāj — and died in 85H.

## Consequences for the transmission network — the HNF angle

### 1. Physical decimation of Iraqi transmitters

Hundreds of muḥaddithūn killed, drowned, crucified, or dispersed. Al-Ḥajjāj pardoned the Qurayshites and Syrians but **massacred the mawālī** and Iraqi tribes. The Iraqi transmission network (Kufa + Basra) emerges physically broken from the episode.

### 2. The Iraq vs. Shām fracture — reinforced

The revolt deepened the split between Iraqi networks (oppositional) and Syro-Hijazi networks (pro-regime). The surviving Iraqi scholars are marked as **political suspects**. The Syrians — [[Persons/Al-Awzāʿī|al-Awzāʿī]], [[Persons/Makhūl al-Shāmī|Makhūl]], the Damascus circles — remain in the orbit of power. This fracture is the same one documented in [[Concepts/Kūfa contre le Chām]].

### 3. Saʿīd ibn Jubayr — a break in the chain

His execution in **95 H** — more than **12 years** after the defeat — shows a long-term repression. A direct student of **Ibn ʿAbbās**, Saʿīd was a pillar of tafsīr and the transmission of Quranic scholarship. His death **forced the redistribution** of this transmission toward other channels — channels controlled by transmitters more accommodating to the regime.

### 4. "Regret" as a tool of delegitimation

Al-Shaʿbī — who had issued the call to arms — is pardoned by al-Ḥajjāj after a public confession. Sources then insist on the **regret** of surviving participants. Ayyūb al-Sakhtiyānī: "I know no one among them who does not regret his fate." This motif of regret functions as a **mechanism of retroactive delegitimation**: the revolt is retrospectively presented as an error, not as a legitimate act of resistance.

### 5. The qadar — revolt link

As documented in [[Concepts/Qadar]], the Qadarites (Maʿbad, then Ghaylān) were executed in the aftermath. The doctrine of **free will** was directly subversive: if God did not decree Umayyad rule, it is legitimate to overthrow it. Theological repression (anti-qadar) and political repression (anti-revolt) are **one and the same movement**.

### 6. Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī — the survivor

His abstention protected him. He subsequently became **qāḍī of Basra** under ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz (~102 H). Sources note that his prestige was too great to kill him — which explains his survival in the network. But this survival comes at a price: **accommodation**. The al-Ḥasan who refuses to fight alongside the Iraqi scholars is the same al-Ḥasan who then transmits within the institutional framework of power. See the entry [[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]].

## The schema — before and after Dayr al-Jamājim

```
BEFORE (81 H)                         AFTER (83-95 H)

KUFA                                   KUFA — decimated
  Saʿīd ibn Jubayr ─── in hiding 12 years → EXECUTED (95 H)
  al-Shaʿbī ────────── confession, pardon → ACCOMMODATED
  Ibn Abī Laylā ────── DROWNED at Dayr al-Jamājim
  Kumayl ibn Ziyād ─── EXECUTED

BASRA                                  BASRA — fractured
  Maʿbad al-Juhanī ─── CRUCIFIED
  al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī ── ABSTENTION → survives, qāḍī
  Anas ibn Mālik ────── HUMILIATED (ختم) → survives

SHĀM                                   SHĀM — intact
  Al-Awzāʿī, Makhūl ── not involved → PRESERVED
  Pro-regime network ── reinforced
```

The revolt of Ibn al-Ashʿath is a **selective filter**: it eliminates transmitters who resist the regime and preserves those who accommodate it. The canonical corpus that has come down to us was transmitted by the **survivors** — those who, like al-Ḥasan, al-Shaʿbī, or Anas, survived the ordeal through abstention, confession, or submission. The voices of the dead — Saʿīd, Ibn Abī Laylā, Maʿbad — are absent from the corpus, except through indirect channels.
