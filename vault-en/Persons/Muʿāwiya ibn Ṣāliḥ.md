# Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ al-Ḥaḍramī

A Ḥimṣī transmitter who became **qāḍī of al-Andalus** under the Umayyads of the West. Having departed very early for the Maghreb (~125H), he returned to the East around 154H with a corpus of **~200 unique hadiths** (*ifrādāt*) that nobody in the hadith centres knew — the paradigmatic case of **[[Concepts/Ighrāb|ighrāb]]**.

The majority of classical critics declare him **thiqa** or **ṣadūq**, but his profile raises structural questions: geographical isolation of ~30 years, unverifiable material, broken tafsīr chain, and organic link to Umayyad power.

**Yaḥyā al-Qaṭṭān's** verdict is remarkable: this Iraqi critic, known for his strictness but also his rigour, rejected Muʿāwiya **wholesale**. Abū Ḥātim adopts an intermediate position — one records the material, but it does not constitute proof (*ḥujja*).

## The ~200 unique hadiths — ighrāb

The founding narrative comes from **Ḥamīd ibn Zunjawaih** quoting **ʿAlī ibn al-Madīnī**: Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ transmitted approximately **200 hadiths** that nobody else reports. Ibn ʿAdī uses the term ***ifrādāt*** — isolated traditions, without *mutābaʿāt* (corroboration by other chains).

**The structural problem**: Muʿāwiya lived **~30 years** far from any hadith centre — the Ḥijāz, Iraq, Egypt. For three decades, the cross-referencing system that underpins hadith criticism could not be applied to his material. When he returns to the East (~154H), he brings a corpus that **nobody can verify by cross-referencing**.

The content of these *ifrādāt* also raises questions:
- **Shāmī material** — traditions circulating in Syrian circles, potentially pro-Umayyad
- **Ṣifāt hadiths** (divine attributes) — theologically sensitive material

See [[Concepts/Ighrāb]] for analysis of the mechanism.

## The Andalusian context

The chronology is decisive:

```
125H — Muʿāwiya departs for the West
  │
132H — Abbasid revolution: the Umayyads fall in the East
  │
138H — ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Dākhil founds the Umayyad emirate of al-Andalus
  │       └── Muʿāwiya is appointed qāḍī
  │
154H — Muʿāwiya returns to the East for the ḥajj
  │       └── A former Umayyad qāḍī in the midst of an Abbasid regime
  │
158H — Death of Muʿāwiya
```

One must appreciate what the **return** of a former Umayyad qāḍī from al-Andalus into an Abbasid-ruled East signifies. He returns with material from Umayyad circles, in a context where the Umayyads of the West are the declared enemies of the Abbasid caliphate.

**The loss of manuscripts**: **Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad ibn Abī Khaytha** reports that Muʿāwiya's manuscripts remaining in al-Andalus after his departure were **lost**. We therefore have only what he reported from memory — or at least what he declared he had memorised. No verification by returning to written sources is possible.

## The malāhī anecdote

**Mūsā ibn Salama** recounts that upon going to Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ's home to write hadiths, he saw **musical instruments** (*malāhī*) there. Muʿāwiya excused himself: "These are **gifts for the emir of al-Andalus**" (*hadāyā li-amīr al-Andalus*).

Mūsā left **without writing anything**.

Source: Tahdhīb al-Kamāl (al-Mizzī), Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ (al-Dhahabī, shamela.ws/book/10906).

This anecdote is significant on two levels:
1. A hadith transmitter who possesses musical instruments — objects of prohibition in the very tradition he transmits
2. His **material link** to Umayyad power in al-Andalus: he carries gifts for the emir

## The chain of the tafsīr of Ibn ʿAbbās

Muʿāwiya is the central link in one of the four major chains of the tafsīr attributed to Ibn ʿAbbās:

```
Ibn ʿAbbās (d. 68H)
    ╳ (break — unanimous)
ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa (d. ~143H)
    │
Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ
    │
ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ṣāliḥ (kātib al-Layth)
    │
al-Bukhārī (taʿlīqāt only)
```

**[[Persons/ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa]]** ([shamela.ws/narrator/4501](https://shamela.ws/narrator/4501)) **never met** Ibn ʿAbbās — this is a point of **consensus**. The probable intermediaries are **Mujāhid** and **ʿIkrima**, but the chain omits them. This is a **ṣaḥīfa** (written document) transmitted without *samāʿ* (direct audition), making it a classic *munqaṭiʿ*.

Upstream, **ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ṣāliḥ** — the *kātib al-Layth* (secretary of al-Layth ibn Saʿd) — is himself **controversial**: accused of negligence with his manuscripts, his neighbour Abū Ṣāliḥ allegedly inserted material into them.

Al-Bukhārī is aware of these weaknesses: he uses this ṣaḥīfa only in the **taʿlīqāt** (suspended citations, without complete isnād) — never in the body of the Ṣaḥīḥ with a connected chain.

See [[Concepts/Chains of the Tafsīr of Ibn ʿAbbās]] for the complete analysis of the four chains.

## Shuyūkh (masters)

Muʿāwiya transmitted from approximately **60 shuyūkh**, almost exclusively **shāmiyyūn** (Syrians):

- The Ḥimṣī circles: local Shāmī traditions
- **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa**: the tafsīr ṣaḥīfa
- Transmitters from the generation of Syrian Tābiʿīn

## Summary

Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ is a transmitter whom the **majority of critics** accept — between *thiqa* and *ṣadūq*. But his profile raises **structural** questions that individual evaluations fail to capture:

1. **Geographical isolation**: ~30 years far from any verification centre
2. **Unverifiable material**: ~200 *ifrādāt* without *mutābaʿāt*, manuscripts lost in al-Andalus
3. **Broken tafsīr chain**: the ṣaḥīfa of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa is a recognised *munqaṭiʿ*
4. **Organic link to power**: qāḍī of the Umayyads of al-Andalus, bearer of gifts for the emir
5. **The *malāhī* anecdote**: a transmitter whose own lifestyle contradicts the traditions he reports

The classical system of **jarḥ wa-taʿdīl** evaluates individuals — their probity (*ʿadāla*) and their precision (*ḍabṭ*). But it has no tools to evaluate a **structural context**: a man may be sincere and reliable, and yet carry material that has been shaped, selected or fabricated by the milieu from which he comes.
