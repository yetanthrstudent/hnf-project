# Al-Zuhrī — the convergence node

Al-Zuhrī stands at the crossroads of three networks: the **Ḥijāzī-academic** network ([[Persons/ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr|ʿUrwa]]), the **Umayyad-political** network (Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik), and the network of **classical criticism** (his mursals unanimously rejected). A fundamental paradox: the most canonized source AND the most structurally criticized transmitter.

It is he who transforms the family memory of ʿĀʾisha — transmitted by ʿUrwa in mursal letters to governors — into hadiths with systematic isnād. The "father of the isnād" is also a man of power: kātib of Hishām, tutor to the caliph's sons, and yet an open critic of al-Walīd. His material is the most abundant in the Ṣaḥīḥayn, but his mursals are, according to al-Qaṭṭān, "like wind."

## The formalizer — ʿUrwa → al-Zuhrī → the canon

The canonical circuit unfolds in four stages:

1. **Family memory**: ʿĀʾisha recounts memories to her nephew ʿUrwa
2. **Correspondence**: ʿUrwa writes these memories in letters to governors (without formal isnād)
3. **Formalization**: al-Zuhrī, student of ʿUrwa and official of the same state, integrates the content into systematic chains of transmission
4. **Canonization**: Mālik → Bukhārī/Muslim incorporate them as authentic prophetic traditions

```
ʿĀʾisha (family memory)
    │
    ▼
ʿUrwa (mursal letters to governors)
    │
    ▼
AL-ZUHRĪ (Umayyad official, formalizes into isnād)
    │
    ├──→ Mālik (Muwaṭṭaʾ)
    ├──→ Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna (problems: majarra, wijāda)
    ├──→ Maʿmar, Mūsā ibn ʿUqba (mursals)
    │
    ▼
Bukhārī / Muslim (CANON)
```

The link between ʿUrwa and al-Zuhrī is not merely pedagogical — it is an **institutional** link. Both men orbit the Umayyad sphere, and the accounts pass from one context (official correspondence) to another (canonical hadith).

### The majarra — the chain that attracts

The chain al-Zuhrī→ʿUrwa is so **famous and dominant** that it induces errors in [[Persons/Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna|Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna]]. Al-Shāfiʿī diagnoses: Sufyān followed the Milky Way (*ittibāʿ al-majarra*) — he took the path al-Zuhrī→ʿUrwa instead of the correct chain al-Zuhrī→**Ḥumayd ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān**. Mālik is right, Sufyān is wrong.

For the full analysis: [[Concepts/Ittibāʿ al-Majarra]]

## The mursals — the unanimous verdict

The classical critics consider al-Zuhrī's mursals to be **among the weakest** in all hadith literature.

### The verdicts

**Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Qaṭṭān** (d. 198H) — Jāmiʿ al-Taḥṣīl ([pp. 89-90](https://shamela.ws/book/25864/75)):

> « مرسل الزهري شر من مرسل غيره ، لأنه حافظ وكلما قدر أن يسمّي سمّى ، وإنما يترك من لا يستجيز أن يسمّيه »

> "The mursal of al-Zuhrī is **worse than any other mursal** — because he is a ḥāfiẓ: whenever he can name his source, he does. If he omits, it is because he does not allow himself to name it."

And further:

> « مرسل الزهري شبه لا شيء »

> "The mursal of al-Zuhrī is virtually nothing."

And on the mursals of al-Zuhrī and Qatāda:

> « هو بمنزلة الريح »

> "It is **like wind**."

**Al-Shāfiʿī** (d. 204H) — Siyar ([5/339](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/4111)):

> « إرسال الزهري ليس بشيء ، ذلك أنا نجده يروي عن سليمان بن أرقم »

> "The mursal of al-Zuhrī **is nothing** — because we find him transmitting from [[Persons/Sulaymān ibn Arqam|Sulaymān ibn Arqam]]."

**Ibn Maʿīn** (d. 233H) — al-Marāsīl of Ibn Abī Ḥātim (p. 3):

> « مراسيل الزهري ليست بشيء »

> "The mursals of al-Zuhrī **are nothing**."

**Al-Dhahabī** (d. 748H) — Siyar ([5/339](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/4111)):

> « مراسيل الزهري كالمعضل ؛ لأنه يكون قد سقط منه اثنان »

> "The mursals of al-Zuhrī are **like muʿḍal** — because there are probably **two** missing links, not just one."

### Al-Shāfiʿī's structural argument

If al-Zuhrī — a master of transmission — transmits by name from [[Persons/Sulaymān ibn Arqam|Sulaymān ibn Arqam]] (unanimously matrūk), what does he hide in his mursals? When he omits his source's name, it is perhaps because that source is as weak as Sulaymān — or worse.

### The admission on carnivores

Al-Zuhrī acknowledges in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim (no. 1932, [p. 5956](https://shamela.ws/book/711/5956)):

> « وَلَمْ أَسْمَعْ ذَلِكَ مِنْ عُلَمَائِنَا بِالْحِجَازِ حَتَّى حَدَّثَنِي أَبُو إِدْرِيسَ »

> "**I never heard that from our scholars in the Ḥijāz**, until Abū Idrīs reported it to me."

This hadith that everyone believes is "Ḥijāzī" actually comes from Syria: [[Traditions/Ban on carnivores]]

## The relationship with power — instrumentalization

Al-Zuhrī is neither a docile collaborator nor a heroic resister. His relationship with Umayyad power is **ambivalent** — he is at once constrained, critical, and integrated into the system.

### What he endures

Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik compels him to put hadith in writing. Al-Zuhrī complains (Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ, [p. 1131](https://shamela.ws/book/10495/1131)):

> « كنا نكره الكتب حتى أكرهنا عليه السلطان فكرهنا أن نمنعه الناس »

> "We used to detest the writing [of hadith] — until **the authorities compelled us**. Then we hated to withhold it from the people."

And in the same source:

> « أكره هشام الزهري فكتب لبنيه فكتب الناس الحديث »

> "Hishām compelled al-Zuhrī [to write], and **he wrote for his sons** — then the people [began to] write hadith."

The detail is significant: al-Zuhrī is the **tutor** of the caliph's sons. He is not a simple scribe — he educates the dynasty in hadith. It is the most ambivalent position imaginable: integrated at the heart of power, but by coercion; training future rulers, but dispossessed of his own material as soon as it is committed to paper.

### What he resists

Al-Zuhrī corrects al-Walīd ibn ʿAbd al-Malik **to his face** when the caliph claims that the verse "والذي تولى كبره" targets ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Al-Zuhrī replies (Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ, [p. 1137](https://shamela.ws/book/10495/1137)):

> « أصلح الله الأمير، ليس كذا »

> "May God reform the emir, **it is not so**."

He cites ʿUrwa from ʿĀʾisha: the verse targets ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ubayy, not ʿAlī.

More seriously: al-Zuhrī transmits the hadith (via Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab ← ʿUmar) about a man from Banū Umayya named al-Walīd (Musnad Aḥmad):

> « أشد على هذه الأمة من فرعون على قومه »

> "Harsher upon this community than **Pharaoh** upon his people."

He dies in 124H — one year before the accession of al-Walīd ibn Yazīd (125H), the libertine caliph killed in 126H.

### What he loses

The structural problem is not al-Zuhrī's submission, it is the **loss of control** over his material. Once written for the caliph's sons, the hadith enters the **dīwān** — the Umayyad administrative machinery. Al-Zuhrī no longer controls the circulation, copying, or use of what he produced. The codification of hadith is born from political coercion, and the material immediately escapes its producer.

For the systemic analysis: [[Concepts/ʿAmal al-Sulṭān]]

## The Syrian → Ḥijāzī vector

Al-Zuhrī absorbs **official Syrian material** — via [[Persons/Abū Idrīs al-Khawlānī|Abū Idrīs al-Khawlānī]], [[Persons/Khālid ibn Ma'dān|Khālid ibn Ma'dān]], Ḥumayd ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān — and canonizes it as "Ḥijāzī." His position in the formula of four regional scholars (according to [[Persons/Sulaymān ibn Mūsā|Sulaymān ibn Mūsā]]) is revealing:

- **Al-Zuhrī** = Ḥijāz
- **[[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|Al-Ḥasan]]** = Basra
- **[[Persons/Maymūn ibn Mahrān|Maymūn]]** = Jazīra
- **[[Persons/Makhūl al-Shāmī|Makhūl]]** = Syria

The irony: the "scholar of the Ḥijāz" is the one whose material comes most from **Syria**. He did not know the ban on carnivores from the scholars of the Ḥijāz — he learned it from a Syrian qāḍī. He frequents the court of Damascus, visits [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas]] in Damascus, writes for the caliph in Damascus. The "Ḥijāzī" label masks a Syrian reality.
