# ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr — between family knowledge and Umayyad power

Nephew of ʿĀʾisha, brother of the rebel caliph ʿAbd Allāh ibn al-Zubayr, one of the "seven fuqahāʾ of Medina." The tradition unanimously recognizes him as thiqa and as one of the greatest scholars of his generation. He is also the author of the **oldest preserved correspondence on the life of the Prophet** — letters addressed to caliph ʿAbd al-Malik and governor al-Walīd. These letters are almost all **mursal** (without an isnād going back to the Prophet) — the question is how they became, through the intermediary of al-Zuhrī and Hishām, formal hadiths integrated into the Ṣaḥīḥayn.

## Classical assessments

| Critic | Judgment |
|---|---|
| **Al-ʿIjlī** | "thiqa, rajul ṣāliḥ, **lam yadkhul fī shayʾ min al-fitan**" — reliable, virtuous, did not participate in any discord |
| **Al-Zuhrī** (his student) | « بحر لا تكدره الدلاء » — "an ocean that buckets do not disturb" |
| **ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz** | "There was no one more learned than ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr" |
| **Ibn Ḥajar** | thiqa, faqīh |
| **Ibn Ḥibbān** | Mentioned in the Thiqāt |

The classical tradition does not criticize him. Al-ʿIjlī even emphasizes his **abstention from fitna** — contrasting with the tumultuous career of his brother ʿAbd Allāh. The unanimous judgment of the muḥaddithīn is positive. What follows is therefore not a critique of ʿUrwa as a transmitter, but an analysis of the **context** in which his traditions circulated and the **form** they took.

## The seven fuqahāʾ — the intellectual milieu

ʿUrwa does not work alone. He belongs to a circle of seven Medinan scholars recognized as the authorities of their generation:

1. **Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab** (d. ~94H) — the greatest in fiqh
2. **ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr** (d. ~94H) — the greatest in hadith and sīra
3. **Al-Qāsim ibn Muḥammad ibn Abī Bakr** (d. ~106H) — ʿUrwa's cousin (both grandsons of Abū Bakr through different lines)
4. ʿUbayd Allāh ibn ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿUtba (d. ~98H)
5. Khārija ibn Zayd (d. ~99H)
6. Sulaymān ibn Yasār (d. ~107H)
7. Abū Bakr ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān (or Sālim ibn ʿAbd Allāh — sources diverge)

Al-Zuhrī, their principal common student, distinguishes specialties: Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab had the most fiqh, ʿUrwa the most hadith. **Al-Qāsim ibn Muḥammad** shares with ʿUrwa access to ʿĀʾisha — both are her grand-nephews. This redundancy is important: when only ʿUrwa reports a narrative that al-Qāsim does not know, it is an indicator worth noting.

## The family — between revolt and accommodation

ʿUrwa is caught between two worlds:

- His **brother** ʿAbd Allāh proclaims himself rival caliph in Mecca/Medina (64-73H)
- Al-ʿIjlī specifies that ʿUrwa himself **did not participate** in his brother's revolt
- After the defeat and death of ʿAbd Allāh (73H, killed by al-Ḥajjāj), ʿUrwa accommodates Umayyad power
- He corresponds with ʿAbd al-Malik ibn Marwān then with his son al-Walīd

This trajectory — from a family in rebellion toward a relationship with the ruling power — is not unique. Many Medinans accommodate the Umayyads after 73H. But the context remains relevant for understanding the nature of his writings on the sīra.

## The destruction of books — al-Ḥarra (63H)

Before his letters to the caliph, ʿUrwa already possessed **kutub** (writings) of fiqh and hadith. In Dhū l-Ḥijja 63H, when Yazīd I's army sacks Medina during the **battle of al-Ḥarra**, ʿUrwa destroys his own books.

The reported motive is **pious**: he feared they might become "books alongside the Book of Allah." Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr reports (Jāmiʿ Bayān al-ʿIlm, via ʿAbd al-Razzāq → Maʿmar → Hishām → ʿUrwa) that he regretted this act for the rest of his life:

> « وددت لو أن عندي كتبي بأهلي ومالي »
>
> "I wish I had my books — rather than my family and my wealth, doubled."

The episode reveals two things: (1) ʿUrwa had a writing activity **from before 63H**, well before the letters to ʿAbd al-Malik (post-73H); (2) the loss of these books means that what he transmitted thereafter relied more on his **memory** — making the mursal question more complex than a simple voluntary omission of isnād.

## The letters to the caliph — the sīra as official correspondence

Four texts in the Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī with the **same isnād**:

> Abān al-ʿAṭṭār → Hishām ibn ʿUrwa → ʿUrwa **« أنه كتب إلى عبد الملك »** ("he wrote to ʿAbd al-Malik")

| Letter | Subject | Ṭabarī ref. |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Beginning of revelation | [p. 1575](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/1575) |
| 2 | The hijra to Medina | [p. 1597](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/1597) |
| 3 | The battle of Badr | [p. 1638](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/1638) |
| 4 | Al-Ḥudaybiyya | [p. 1653](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/1653) |

**Al-Mustadrak**: ʿUrwa also writes to the son, **al-Walīd ibn ʿAbd al-Malik** ([p. 8430](https://shamela.ws/book/5765/8430)).

**Ibn Saʿd**: al-Zuhrī **sees** ʿUrwa writing to a governor ([p. 7752](https://shamela.ws/book/9351/7752)).

**Observations**:
- The format is that of **correspondence**, not a ḥalqa — ʿUrwa does not teach in a circle but writes to a specific addressee
- The content is narrative sīra (biography of the Prophet), not juridical hadith
- None of these letters contains a formal isnād — it is **mursal**: "ʿĀʾisha told me that such-and-such happened"
- It is unknown whether these letters respond to a **request from the caliph** or whether ʿUrwa sends them on his own initiative — the sources do not specify

## The academic debate — are the letters authentic?

The question of these letters' authenticity is an active field of research:

**Gorke & Schoeler** (2005, *Der Islam* 82): by comparing the different recensions (Hishām, al-Zuhrī, etc.) like a manuscript stemma, they conclude that a large part of the corpus does go back to ʿUrwa. These letters would be the **oldest nucleus of the sīra**. Summary book: *The Earliest Writings on the Life of Muḥammad* (Gerlach Press).

**Harald Motzki** (*isnād-cum-matn* method): if text (*matn*) variations systematically correspond to chain (*isnād*) variations, this confirms real transmission. The ICMA has been applied to ʿUrwa's letters with generally positive results. Synthesis: Gorke, Motzki & Schoeler, *Der Islam* 89 (2012).

**Stephen Shoemaker** (*Der Islam* 85, 2011): skeptical position. One can go back to al-Zuhrī with confidence, but the leap from al-Zuhrī to ʿUrwa is "not convincing" — the transmission networks are not dense enough for a reliable *common link* analysis before the beginning of the 2nd century. Letters transmitted only by al-Ṭabarī have too limited attestation.

This article does not settle this debate. But in **all cases** — whether the letters are from ʿUrwa or from al-Zuhrī — the content is mursal and the question of formalization into marfūʿ hadith remains open.

## The mursal — a generational phenomenon

ʿUrwa transmits from ʿĀʾisha — but without an isnād systematically going back to the Prophet. Most of his traditions are:
- **Mursal**: ʿUrwa → ʿĀʾisha → [no attribution to the Prophet]
- Or **historical narratives**: ʿĀʾisha relates an event, without the hadith formula

This phenomenon must be placed in context: the mursal is **not unique to ʿUrwa**. It is a characteristic of the entire generation of senior tābiʿīn. Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab, [[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]], al-Qāsim ibn Muḥammad — all transmit mursal. The classical tradition does not consider the mursal of senior tābiʿīn as a defect: al-Shāfiʿī accepts it when corroborated; Abū Ḥanīfa and Mālik accept it more broadly.

What is **specific** to ʿUrwa is the **transformation** of his mursal into marfūʿ by the following generation:
1. ʿUrwa narrates: "ʿĀʾisha told me that such-and-such happened"
2. Al-Zuhrī formalizes: "ʿUrwa reported to me from ʿĀʾisha that the Prophet said/did..."
3. Bukhārī/Muslim integrate al-Zuhrī's version as marfūʿ hadith (going back to the Prophet)

The transition from mursal to marfūʿ occurs through **added attribution**: ʿĀʾisha's historical narrative (family memory) becomes a prophetic statement (normative sunna).

**Concrete case — the death of Khadīja**:
- **Mursal** version (al-Ṭabarānī, [p. 5004](https://shamela.ws/book/14452/5004)): Khadīja dies **3 years before the hijra** — **no attribution to ʿĀʾisha**
- **Marfūʿ** version (Bukhārī/Muslim): the same narrative with "ʿUrwa reports from ʿĀʾisha"
- The coexistence of both versions raises the question: which is earlier?

## ʿUrwa reports FROM Marwān — an unexpected source

Sunan Abī Dāwūd ([p. 7162](https://shamela.ws/book/3645/7162)): ʿUrwa reports a hadith **from Marwān ibn al-Ḥakam** — the Umayyad governor of Medina, future caliph.

The fact is noteworthy: Marwān is not a hadith transmitter in the classical sense — he is a **man of power**. When ʿUrwa transmits from him, the circuit is no longer the canonical scheme (Prophet → Companion → tābiʿī) but one that passes through the Umayyad apparatus. This is not necessarily a disqualification — Marwān was also technically a ṣaḥābī — but it illustrates the interweaving of knowledge and power in ʿUrwa's environment.

## The criticism of Ibn ʿAbbās — a tābiʿī performing jarḥ on a ṣaḥābī

Already in [[Concepts/ʿAdālat al-Ṣaḥāba]] — summarized here. ʿUrwa identifies the **actual source** of Ibn ʿAbbās (a poet, not the Prophet) on the duration of the Meccan period. Text in the Istīʿāb ([p. 33](https://shamela.ws/book/12288/33)).

> « إنما أخذه من قول الشاعر »
>
> "He merely took it from the words of a poet."

ʿUrwa does not say: "Ibn ʿAbbās was wrong." He says: his **source** is a poem, not a prophetic tradition. The tābiʿī performs jarḥ on the ṣaḥābī — and a methodological jarḥ, not a simple difference of opinion. This episode also reveals ʿUrwa's intellectual rigor: he does not merely contradict, he **identifies the alternative source**.

## Hishām ibn ʿUrwa — the son who transmits in Iraq

[[Persons/Hishām ibn ʿUrwa|Hishām ibn ʿUrwa]] (d. 146H) is the **son** and principal transmitter of ʿUrwa. Two phases:

**Medinan phase** (reliable): transmissions in Medina, recognized by Mālik and the Medinans.

**Iraqi phase** (problematic): Hishām settles in Iraq toward the end of his life. **Mālik ibn Anas refuses** Hishām's Iraqi transmissions — considering that he changed/deteriorated his material.

Abū al-Ḥasan ibn al-Qaṭṭān: "his transmissions to the Iraqis are weak" (munkar).

This pattern is recurring in our research: **regional** transmissions are reliable, transmissions **exported** to Iraq are suspect — the same pattern as for Nāfiʿ (reliable in the Ḥijāz, but al-Awzāʿī is the only one to report from him in the Shām → [[Concepts/Tafarrud]]).

## Al-Zuhrī — the student who formalizes

Al-Zuhrī (d. 124H) is ʿUrwa's principal student and **the father of systematic isnād**. He is the one who formalizes ʿUrwa's narratives into hadiths with complete transmission chains.

Al-Zuhrī is also an Umayyad official (kātib of Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik, qāḍī). The emerging circuit:
- ʿUrwa corresponds with the caliph, transmits mursal
- Al-Zuhrī, an official of the same state and ʿUrwa's student, formalizes these narratives into hadiths with isnād
- Mālik ibn Anas integrates al-Zuhrī's hadiths into the Muwaṭṭaʾ
- Bukhārī/Muslim canonize the whole

The link between ʿUrwa and al-Zuhrī is not only pedagogical — it is also **institutional**. Both men orbit Umayyad power, and the narratives pass from one context (official correspondence) to another (canonical hadith).

## The normalization — from mursal to marfūʿ

The process observable in the sources:
1. **Family memory**: ʿĀʾisha recounts memories to her nephew
2. **Correspondence**: ʿUrwa writes these memories in letters (without formal isnād)
3. **Formalization**: al-Zuhrī and Hishām integrate the content into transmission chains
4. **Canonization**: Bukhārī/Muslim integrate as authentic prophetic traditions

This process does not necessarily imply deliberate falsification. It may be an **evolution of norms**: what was acceptable as historical narrative (mursal) in ʿUrwa's time was reformatted according to the demands of systematic isnād in al-Zuhrī's era. But the result is the same: private memories, transmitted within an official correspondence framework, become normative sunna — and the transition passes through men linked to Umayyad power.

## The trials — amputation and loss of a son

The classical tradition preserves a striking episode. ʿUrwa travels to Damascus to visit caliph al-Walīd, accompanied by his son **Muḥammad**. Two misfortunes strike:

- **Muḥammad** falls into the caliph's stable, trampled by horses
- **ʿUrwa** develops gangrene in his leg; al-Walīd's physicians amputate

The accounts report that he refuses anesthesia and endures the operation repeating "Lā ilāha illā Allāh." Then:

> "Lord, You gave me four limbs and took only one; You gave me seven sons and took only one."

The episode, reported in al-Dhahabī's Siyar, is important for the overall portrait: the classical tradition does not present ʿUrwa as a political agent but as a model of **ṣabr** (patience). The image of the caliph's correspondent and that of the enduring scholar coexist in the sources — and it is this tension that makes the character interesting.

## Summary

ʿUrwa is unanimously recognized as thiqa by the classical tradition. Al-ʿIjlī emphasizes that he did not participate in any fitna; al-Zuhrī compares him to an ocean. His erudition and probity are not in question.

What merits attention is the **trajectory of his traditions**: family memories of ʿĀʾisha, transmitted in letters to Umayyad caliphs and governors, then formalized into hadiths with complete isnāds by al-Zuhrī (himself an Umayyad official) and Hishām. The content passes from one register (official correspondence, mursal) to another (canonical hadith, marfūʿ) — and this transition occurs within a precise political environment.

This normalization process is not unique to ʿUrwa — the mursal is the dominant mode of transmission of his generation. But with him, the documentation is exceptionally clear: the letters to the caliph are preserved, the link with the Umayyad apparatus is explicit, and the coexistence of mursal and marfūʿ versions of the same narrative is observable. It is this transparency of the sources that makes ʿUrwa a privileged case study.
