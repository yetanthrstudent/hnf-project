# ʿAmr ibn Maymūn al-Awdī

A Kūfite tābiʿī, mukhaḍram (lived through both the Jāhiliyya and Islam without having seen the Prophet). A transmitter considered thiqa by consensus. Known for his piety — he is chiefly famous for an extraordinary account that al-Bukhārī chose to include in his Ṣaḥīḥ.

## The hadith of the stoned she-monkey — Bukhārī no. 3849

Kitāb Manāqib al-Anṣār, bāb al-Qasāma fī al-Jāhiliyya ([p. 5824](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/5824)):

Chain: Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād ← Hushaym ← Ḥuṣayn ← ʿAmr ibn Maymūn

> رَأَيْتُ فِي الْجَاهِلِيَّةِ قِرْدَةً اجْتَمَعَ عَلَيْهَا قِرَدَةٌ، قَدْ زَنَتْ، **فَرَجَمُوهَا، فَرَجَمْتُهَا مَعَهُمْ**

> « In the pre-Islamic era I saw a she-monkey upon which other monkeys had gathered — she had committed adultery — **they stoned her, and I stoned her with them.** »

## The analysis

### What the text says

A man claims to have seen, before Islam, a group of monkeys identify a she-monkey guilty of adultery and stone her collectively — and to have participated himself. The account attributes to the monkeys:
- The **notion of zinā** (adultery) — a human legal concept
- The **organization of a collective punishment** — rajm (stoning)
- A **judicial behavior** that ʿAmr ibn Maymūn recognizes and joins in

### What it is not

This is **not a prophetic hadith** (marfūʿ). The Prophet is not mentioned, does not speak, does not validate anything. It is the personal account of a tābiʿī about an event he claims to have experienced before Islam.

### Why al-Bukhārī includes it

Al-Bukhārī places it in the bāb al-Qasāma fī al-Jāhiliyya — a chapter on pre-Islamic practices. The inclusion serves to show that stoning (rajm) was a recognized practice **even before Islam**, including in the animal kingdom. It is a **reinforcement argument**: if nature itself applies rajm, the Islamic prescription is in the natural order of things.

This choice reveals al-Bukhārī's method: the Ṣaḥīḥ is not a simple collection of prophetic hadiths — it is an **argumentative construction** where the chapter classification (tarājum al-abwāb) carries the thesis, and where non-prophetic materials are mobilized when they serve the demonstration.

### The chain

| Link | Status | Remarks |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| **Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād** (shaykh of Bukhārī) | Controversial | Al-Nasāʾī: « laysa bi-thiqat ». Known for his errors and singularities (tafarrudāt) |
| **Hushaym ibn Bashīr** | Notorious mudallis | Transmits here via "ʿan" — an ambiguous formula that does not guarantee direct audition |
| **Ḥuṣayn ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān** | Thiqa | No problem |
| **ʿAmr ibn Maymūn al-Awdī** | Thiqa by consensus | Mukhaḍram |

Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād is the weak link — a transmitter that Bukhārī uses here but whom other critics have rejected. Hushaym is a mudallis and transmits via ʿanʿana. If this chain carried content embarrassing to the tradition, it would be disqualified.

## The stakes for al-Bukhārī's methodology

The inclusion of this account in the Ṣaḥīḥ has a direct consequence for evaluating his editorial choices: **al-Bukhārī does not limit himself to prophetic hadiths when the material serves his argumentative construction**. When he excludes a khabar — such as the tradition of Ibn Yāmīn contesting the [[Traditions/Assassination of Kaʿb ibn al-Ashraf|assassination of Kaʿb ibn al-Ashraf]] — the argument "it is not a marfūʿ hadith, it does not fall within the Ṣaḥīḥ's scope" does not hold. The she-monkey account proves the contrary.
