# Ibn Mas'ūd — the founder of the Kūfī school

**'Abd Allāh ibn Mas'ūd** — one of the earliest converts to Islam, present at Badr, sent by the caliph 'Umar to Kūfa to teach the Quran and fiqh to the Iraqis. **Founder of the Kūfī tradition** — his Quranic reading, his fiqh, and his students structure the Islam of Kūfa for centuries.

His position in the [[Concepts/Kūfa contre le Chām|Kūfa vs. Shām]] dossier is decisive: it is question 5 — **which Quran?** His reading stands against that of Zayd b. Thābit, the official muṣḥaf imposed by 'Uthmān and adopted by the Shām.

## Profile

Companion of the Prophet from the very first hour. He is among those who "knew the Quran best among the Prophet's people" — the hadith is reported in the Ṣaḥīḥayn. 'Umar sends him to Kūfa with an explicit mandate:

> « قد آثرتكم بعبد الله على نفسي »
>
> "I have preferred 'Abd Allāh to you over myself."

His students form the lineage: 'Alqama ibn Qays, al-Aswad ibn Yazīd, Ibrāhīm al-Nakha'ī — and, at the end of the chain, [[Persons/Abū Ḥanīfa|Abū Ḥanīfa]]. The Kūfī school of fiqh and qirā'a is the school of Ibn Mas'ūd.

## The reading of Ibn Mas'ūd vs. Zayd b. Thābit

The Shām adopts the reading of Zayd b. Thābit — this is the 'Uthmānī muṣḥaf, imposed under 'Uthmān and made the sole official text.

Kūfa prefers the reading of Ibn Mas'ūd. Ibn 'Abbās explicitly defends it (Bazzār 4923; Sa'īd b. Manṣūr, Sunan, no. 58-59):

> « كان القرآن يُعرض على النبي في كل رمضان مرة، وعُرض عليه في آخر عمره مرتين، وكان ابن مسعود حاضرا في العرضة الأخيرة »
>
> "The Quran was presented to the Prophet every Ramadan once. In his last year, it was presented to him **twice** — and **Ibn Mas'ūd was present** at the last presentation."

The argument is decisive within the framework of the tradition: the "last presentation" ('arḍa akhīra) is supposed to be the definitive version of the Quran. If Ibn Mas'ūd was present and not Zayd, his reading has greater authority.

## The divergences of reading — concrete examples

The divergences between the two readings are not mere phonetic variants. They produce **doctrinal divergences**:

### Mālik / Malik (Quran 1.4)

> مَالِكِ يوم الدين (Ibn Mas'ūd — Kūfa) vs مَلِكِ يوم الدين (Zayd — Shām)

**Mālik** = Owner of the Day of Judgment. **Malik** = King of the Day of Judgment. The difference is political: "King" (malik) magnifies **kingship** — the vocabulary of Umayyad power. "Owner" (mālik) is a term of possession, not of reign.

### Ḥadab / jadath (Quran 21.96)

> حَدَبٍ (ḥadab — Zayd) vs جَدَثٍ (jadath — Ibn Mas'ūd)

The verse speaks of Gog and Magog who "pour forth from every ḥadab/jadath." **Ḥadab** = hill. **Jadath** = tomb. Ibn Mas'ūd's version has Gog and Magog emerging from **tombs** — a radically different eschatological image.

### Active/passive marriage (Quran 2.221)

The reading variant changes the status of the woman in the marriage contract — active subject or passive object of the union.

### The sun: assigned resting place or not (Quran 36.38)

> لِمُسْتَقَرٍّ لها (Zayd) — the sun runs toward an assigned resting place
>
> لا مُسْتَقَرَّ لها (Ibn Mas'ūd) — the sun runs **with no assigned resting place**

Zayd's version presupposes a resting place for the sun — compatible with Jewish traditions about the setting of the sun. Ibn Mas'ūd's version denies this resting place — the sun runs endlessly.

## The politics of diacritical dots

Primitive Arabic script does not distinguish certain letters (b/t/th, j/ḥ/kh, d/dh, etc.) — only context and oral tradition allow reading. When Marwān and 'Abd al-Malik impose the **naqṭ** (diacritical dots), they **fix** the readings — and with them, the doctrines.

The divergences between Zayd's and Ibn Mas'ūd's readings are often divergences of **dots**: the same skeletal letters (rasm), but different dots. Fixing the dots means fixing the meaning. The naqṭ is not a neutral calligraphic act: it is an act of **textual sovereignty**.

The Kūfites recite differently to preserve their reading from Ibn Mas'ūd. But the Umayyad standardization ultimately prevails — the 'Uthmānī muṣḥaf, with its dots fixed by the Shām, becomes the sole Quran.

## The Kūfī chain

```
IBN MAS'ŪD (d. 32H, Medina)
    │
    ├── 'Alqama ibn Qays (d. ~62H, Kūfa)
    │       │
    │       └── Ibrāhīm al-Nakha'ī (d. 96H, Kūfa)
    │               │
    │               ├── Ḥammād ibn Abī Sulaymān (d. 120H)
    │               │       │
    │               │       └── ABŪ ḤANĪFA (d. 150H)
    │               │
    │               └── al-Aʿmash (student of Ibrāhīm)
    │
    └── al-Aswad ibn Yazīd (d. ~75H, Kūfa)
```

The entire Kūfī school descends from Ibn Mas'ūd. Ḥanafī fiqh, Kūfī qirā'a, and resistance to the Sunna of the Shām — all trace back to him.
