# 'Abd Allāh ibn 'Amr ibn al-'Āṣ

Companion of the Prophet, son of the conqueror of Egypt ('Amr ibn al-'Āṣ). **Central node** in the transmission network: the Companion to whom traditions from [[Ka'b al-Ahbar]], [[Nawf al-Bikālī]], and [[Wahb ibn Munabbih]] converge.

## The zamālatain of Yarmūk

A fact attested even by defenders of the Sunna: Ibn 'Amr obtained at the battle of **Yarmūk** (15 H / 636 CE) **two zamālatain** (camel-loads) of books from the People of the Book. He would then draw stories from them.

- **Ibn Kathīr** (al-Bidāya 2/107): « أصاب يوم اليرموك زاملتين من كتب المتقدمين، فكان يحدث منهما »
- **Bishr al-Marīsī** (d. 218 H) accuses Ibn 'Amr of having presented these accounts as prophetic
- The Tafsīr al-Ṭabarī explicitly notes: « وكان يقرأ الكتب » — "he read the Books"
- **Muṣannaf Ibn Abī Shayba** ([p. 42550](https://shamela.ws/book/9944/42550)): « إنا لنجد **في كتاب الله المنزل** صنفين في النار » — he claims to find **in the revealed Book of God** content absent from the Quran (two categories in Hell: whips/cow tails, women dressed-yet-naked). See [[Concepts/Formule - dans certains Livres|the formula "in certain Books"]]

## Scriptural teacher: Sarj al-Yarmūkī

Three sources confirm that Ibn 'Amr **learned from [[Sarj al-Yarmūkī]]**, a convert from the People of the Book:
- « وكان عبد الله بن عمرو يتعلم من سرج هذا » (al-Iṣāba, Kitāb al-Fitan, Tārīkh Dimashq)

Ibn 'Amr was therefore **doubly fed**: by the physical books (zamālatain) and by an oral teacher (Sarj).

## The hadith of the Qahtanite king and Mu'āwiya's anger

Ibn 'Amr reported that a **king would come from Qaḥṭān** — a tradition of scriptural origin (cf. his zamālatain and his link with [[Ka'b al-Ahbar]]). Mu'āwiya, learning of this, became angry and mounted the pulpit (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī no. 6720, [p. 10728](https://shamela.ws/book/735/10728)):

> « بلغني أن رجالا منكم يحدثون أحاديث **ليست في كتاب الله ولا تؤثر عن رسول الله**، وأولئك **جهالكم** »

> "It has reached me that some men among you report traditions **that are not in the Book of God and do not come from the Messenger of God** — and those are **your ignorant ones**."

Mu'āwiya counters with a prophetic hadith: « هذا الأمر في قريش » ("authority belongs to Quraysh") — against the prediction of a Yemeni king. This is a remarkable case where **Mu'āwiya himself** denounces the extra-Quranic and extra-prophetic traditions of Ibn 'Amr — while having patronized him as a transmitter. The tradition of the Qahtanite king reflects the content of the **zamālatain**: scriptural eschatological prophecies that Mu'āwiya cannot tolerate when they threaten Quraysh's legitimacy.

## Relationships

- **Meets** [[Ka'b al-Ahbar]] at Mecca (Tabari p. 398) — documented physical contact
- **Source** of [[Nawf al-Bikālī]] (listed in Nawf's biography, p. 28828)
- **Met by** [[Wahb ibn Munabbih]] (listed in Wahb's biography, p. 29324)
- **Learns from** [[Sarj al-Yarmūkī]] — scriptural convert from the era of the conquests
- **Son of** 'Amr ibn al-'Āṣ (conqueror of Egypt, ally of [[Mu'āwiya ibn Abī Sufyān|Mu'āwiya]])
- **Possessor** of the Ṣuḥuf Ṣādiqa ("truthful pages") — personal collection of traditions
- **Transmitted by** [[Abū Hurayra]] who says he received from him the hadith of the [[Traditions/Hijra vers le Shām|hijra to the Shām]]

## Pivotal role

Ibn 'Amr is the **convergence point** of two transmission routes for the same hadith:
- Syrian route: [[Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] ← [[Shahr ibn Ḥawshab|Shahr]] ← [[Nawf al-Bikālī|Nawf]] ← **Ibn 'Amr**
- Egyptian route: Mūsā ibn 'Alī ← [[Abū Hurayra]] ← **Ibn 'Amr**

This double convergence toward a single Companion is the signature of a tradition fabricated and then injected into multiple circuits.
