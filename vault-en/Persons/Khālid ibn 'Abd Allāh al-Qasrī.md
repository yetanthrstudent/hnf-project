# Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī

**Umayyad governor of Mecca then of Iraq**, a major figure of Umayyad power. Son of a **Christian woman** (Dimashq [p. 7028](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7028): « الأشراف من أبناء النصرانيات : خالد بن عبد الله »). Great-grandson of the Companion **Asad ibn Kurz** (Ma'rifat al-Ṣaḥāba, [p. 1037](https://shamela.ws/book/10490/1037)), counted among the Syrians. Khālid transmits from him a hadith: « يا أسد بن كرز، لا تدخل الجنة بعمل ولكن برحمة الله » ([p. 1039](https://shamela.ws/book/10490/1037)). Brother of **Asad ibn 'Abd Allāh**, governor of Khurāsān. Sons: **Yazīd** and **Muḥammad** (both active in late Umayyad politics).

## The sacrifice of al-Ja'd ibn Dirham

On the day of **Eid al-Aḍḥā**, Khālid mounted the minbar and declared (Dimashq [p. 7025](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7025), Baghdad [p. 8074](https://shamela.ws/book/736/8074)):

> « إني مضح بالجعد بن درهم — زعم أن الله لم يكلم موسى تكليما ولم يتخذ إبراهيم خليلا — ثم نزل فذبحه »

> "I sacrifice al-Ja'd ibn Dirham. He claims that Allah did not speak to Mūsā directly, and did not take Ibrāhīm as an intimate friend." Then he descended from the minbar **and slaughtered him**.

A theological execution **in public, before all the Muslims present at the Eid prayer** (على رؤوس من شهد العيد معه من المسلمين — al-Dārimī) — a heretic treated as a sacrificial animal. Al-Ja'd denied the divine attributes (kalām and khulla), a doctrine precursory to Mu'tazilism.

## The Christian mother

Ibn 'Ayyāsh (via al-Haytham ibn 'Adī) classifies Khālid among the "ashraf min abnā' al-naṣrāniyyāt" — nobles born of Christian mothers (Dimashq [p. 7028](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7028)). The recurring insult **"ibn al-naṣrāniyya"** (son of the Christian woman) follows him — a Kharijite rebel calls him thus (Ṭabarī [p. 3834](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3834)).

His mother never converted. He **built her a church** (بيعة) near the mosque of Kufa. Al-Farazdaq attacks him in a famous verse:

> بنى بيعة فيها الصليب لأمه … ويهدم من بغض منار المساجد

> "He built a **church with a crucifix for his mother** / and out of hatred destroys **the minarets of mosques**."

## Governor of Mecca — the provocative sermons

In Mecca, Khālid **publicly disparages Zamzam**, calling it "umm al-khanāfis" (mother of beetles), and boasting that the water channel he built was superior (Dimashq [p. 7048-7049](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7048), Ṭabarī [p. 3520](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3520)).

An even more provocative sermon (Ṭabarī [p. 3520](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3520)): "Which is greater — the **khalīfa** (representative) of a man among his family, or his **messenger** to them?" — implying that the caliph is superior to the Prophet.

After the arrest of **Sa'īd ibn Jubayr** and Ṭalq ibn Ḥabīb (Dimashq [p. 7049](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7049)): "If the Commander of the Faithful had written it to me, I would have **demolished the Ka'ba stone by stone**."

### The chain of qiṣāṣ — the governor as God's link

Sermon from the minbar reported by Shubayb ibn Shayba (Dimashq [p. 7049](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7049)):

> « يسومونني أن أقيد من كاتبي، ولئن أقدت منه لقد أقدت من نفسي، ولئن أقدت من نفسي لقد أقاد أمير المؤمنين من نفسه، ولئن أقاد ليقيدن — **هاه هاه!** »
> (ويومئ بيده إلى فوق)

> "They want me to apply the law of retaliation against my secretary! If I apply it against him, it is against myself. If against myself, the Commander of the Faithful applies it against himself. And if he applies it — **hah, hah!**" (pointing his hand toward the sky.)

An ascending chain: secretary --> governor --> caliph --> Prophet --> God. Khālid creates a **chain of immunity** reaching up to God to protect a functionary from retaliation.

## Governor of Iraq — patron of Basran scholars

Based at **Wāsiṭ**. Administered Iraq and the entire East, with his brother Asad in Khurāsān (Ṭabarī [p. 3735](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3735)).

Khālid was surrounded by **Basran scholars** gravitating around his power:

| Basran scholar            | Link with Khālid                                                                             | Source                                                        |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **[[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma]]** | Dies at his residence in Wāsiṭ (117 H), in debt, sustained by his provisions                        | Tahdhīb al-Kamāl [p. 25](https://shamela.ws/book/26743/25)    |
| **[[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]]**   | **Transmits directly from Khālid** — criticised for "عمل السلطان" (working for the authorities) | Tārīkh al-Islām [p. 2468](https://shamela.ws/book/22771/2468) |
| **[[Persons/Thābit al-Bunānī]]**  | **Dies under Khālid's governorship** — Ibn Sa'd dates his death by Khālid's reign       | Tahdhīb al-Tahdhīb [p. 257](https://shamela.ws/book/1293/257) |

Al-Dhahabī lists Khālid's transmitters: « وعنه **حميد الطويل** وإسماعيل بن أبي خالد وسيار أبو الحكم » (Tārīkh al-Islām, [p. 2468](https://shamela.ws/book/22771/2468)). Ḥumayd is thus the **governor's man** in the hadith heritage.

The three Basrans (Qatāda, Ḥumayd, Thābit) form the core of transmitters from **[[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik]]** — and all three are linked to the power of Khālid al-Qasrī. Khālid is the **patron** of this network, the same pattern as Mu'āwiya patronising Ka'b al-Ahbar.

Executes **al-Mughīra ibn Sa'd** (false prophet) and his followers by fire (Dimashq [p. 7030-7031](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7030)). Crucifies another man who claimed prophethood in Kufa.

## "Rajul sū'" — the condemnation of Yaḥyā ibn Ma'īn

Dimashq [p. 7048](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7048):

> **Yaḥyā ibn Ma'īn**: « خالد بن عبد الله القسري كان واليا لبني أمية وكان **رجل سوء** وكان **يقع في علي بن أبي طالب** »

> "Khālid al-Qasrī was a governor of the Umayyads, he was a **bad man**, and he **insulted 'Alī ibn Abī Ṭālib**."

Al-Faḍl ibn al-Zubayr adds: "I heard Khālid al-Qasrī mention 'Alī, and he said words **that it is not permissible to repeat**" (same page).

## Fall and death

### The accumulation of grievances (Ṭabarī, "ذكر سبب عزل هشام خالدا", [p. 3840-3844](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3840))

Khālid publicly insulted a relative of Hishām (Ṭabarī [p. 3840](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3840)) and called the caliph **"ibn al-ḥamqā'"** (son of the simpleton) — Hishām's mother being reputed feeble-minded (Ṭabarī [p. 3842](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3842)). Hishām wrote to him:

> « يا بن أم خالد... يابن اللخناء، كيف لا تكون إمرة العراق لك شرفا، وأنت من بجيلة القليلة الذليلة! »

> "O son of Umm Khālid... **son of the foul-smelling one**! How could the governorship of Iraq not be an honour for you, **you who are from Bajīla, the small and humiliated tribe**!"

Al-Ṭabarī concludes: « فلم يزل يبلغه عنه ما يكره حتى تغير له » — "Reports of detestable things kept reaching him until [Hishām] turned against him."

### The qulfa incident — the final trigger

Reported by al-Aṣma'ī via Shubayb ibn Shayba (Dimashq [p. 7049](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7049), Siyar) — cited as **سبب عزله** (the direct cause of his dismissal):

A woman came before Khālid to report being raped by a Mazdean (مجوسي) slave of his administration. Khālid's response:

> « كيف وجدت **قلفته** ؟ »

> "How did you find **his foreskin**?" (the Mazdean slave being uncircumcised)

An obscene question posed to a rape victim. **Ḥassān al-Nabaṭī** reported the incident in writing to Hishām, who dismissed him and appointed Yūsuf ibn 'Umar.

### The fall

1. **Hishām** dismisses him in 120 H — Yūsuf ibn 'Umar arrives secretly in Kufa with 30 men, leads the dawn prayer and arrests Khālid (Ṭabarī [p. 3843-3844](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3843))
2. Imprisoned at Wāsiṭ for 18 months, then transferred to **Damascus** (Ṭabarī [p. 3950-3953](https://shamela.ws/book/9783/3950))
3. **Al-Walīd II** asks him to name the Yemeni conspirators — Khālid refuses and is handed over to Yūsuf ibn 'Umar (Dimashq [p. 7050](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7050))
4. **Tortured** — the **miḍrassa** (compression instrument) is placed on his chest — killed at night, buried at al-Ḥīra in his 'abāya, **Muḥarram 126 H** (Dimashq [p. 7050](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7050))
5. A man sacrifices his horse on Khālid's grave — Yūsuf has him flogged **700 lashes** (same page)
6. "When Khālid was killed, no Arab composed an elegy for him despite the abundance of his generosity" — except Abū al-Shaghb al-'Absī (Dimashq [p. 7051](https://shamela.ws/book/71/7051))

## "Qaṣṣāb al-zanādiqa" — the butcher of heretics

Later scholars forged for Khālid the nickname **"qaṣṣāb al-zanādiqa"** (قصاب الزنادقة — the butcher of heretics), reported as coming "from the Salaf" (Sharḥ al-'Aqīda al-Ṭaḥāwiyya, [p. 602](https://shamela.ws/book/37433/602)):

> « ولذلك سماه السلف: **قصاب الزنادقة** »

This nickname is remarkable: it does not merely excuse the violence — it **consecrates** it with an honorific title. A governor who slaughters a theologian like a sheep receives a trade title, as if the act were a necessary function. The epithet implies a **retrospective endorsement**: Khālid accomplished a task that others dared not, and the tradition is grateful.

## Absolution by the scholars — the sacrifice as "good deed"

Despite all the above, later scholars **absolved** Khālid for the sacrifice of al-Ja'd, and made it an exemplary act of piety:

### Al-Dārimī (d. 280 H) — al-Radd 'alā al-Jahmiyya ([p. 18089/27](https://shamela.ws/book/18089/27))

> « فذبحه ذبحاً بواسط في يوم أضحى على رؤوس من شهد العيد معه من المسلمين، **لا يعيبه به عائب، ولا يطعن عليه طاعن. بل استحسنوا ذلك من فعله، وصوّبوه من رأيه** »

> "He slaughtered him at Wāsiṭ on the day of Eid before the Muslims. **Nobody reproached him for it, nobody criticised him. On the contrary, all approved his act and judged his opinion correct.**"

### Al-Dhahabī (d. 748 H) — Siyar a'lām al-nubalā' ([p. 8322/45718](https://shamela.ws/book/8322/45718))

> « **هذه من حسناته** هي وقتله مغيرة الكذاب »

> "**This is one of his good deeds**, along with the killing of al-Mughīra the liar."

### Ibn Taymiyya (d. 728 H) — Majmū' al-Fatāwā ([p. 7289/6393](https://shamela.ws/book/7289/6393))

> « فضحّى بالجعد خالد بن عبد الله القسري بواسط **على عهد علماء التابعين** وغيرهم من علماء المسلمين وهم بقايا التابعين في وقته مثل **الحسن البصري** وغيره **الذين حمدوه على ما فعل وشكروا ذلك** »

> "Khālid sacrificed al-Ja'd at Wāsiṭ **in the time of the scholars among the Tābi'ūn** and other Muslim scholars, the last of the Tābi'ūn of his time such as **al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī** and others, **who praised him for what he had done and were grateful for it.**"

And in another passage (Fatāwā 13/177): « **برضا علماء الإسلام** » — "with the approval of the scholars of Islam".

### Ibn al-Qayyim (d. 751 H) — al-Qāṣīda al-Nūniyya (cited in [p. 30017/172](https://shamela.ws/book/30017/172))

> ولأجل ذا ضحّى بجعد خالد الـ … ـقسري يوم ذبائح القربان
> شكرا لضحية كل صاحب سنة … **لله درّك من أخي قربان**

> "It is for this that Khālid al-Qasrī sacrificed al-Ja'd on the day of offerings. Every adherent of the Sunna gives thanks for this sacrifice — **may God bless you, what a brother of offering you are!**"

### Abū Muḥammad al-Yamanī — 'Aqā'id al-73 firqa (cited in [p. 12079/135](https://shamela.ws/book/12079/135))

> « فاستحسن الناس منه ذلك وقالوا **نفى الغلّ عن الإسلام جزاه الله خيرا** »

> "The people approved this and said: **he purged Islam of rancour, may God reward him.**"

## The contrasting portrait — two traditions, two Khālids

The same person is the subject of two **radically opposed** biographical constructions, carried by two competing traditions. Their juxtaposition reveals less the "truth" about Khālid than the **mechanism of fabrication of historical figures** by doctrinal traditions.

### The Sunni tradition: the defender of orthodoxy

The Sunni sources (al-Dārimī, al-Dhahabī, Ibn Taymiyya, Ibn al-Qayyim) primarily retain the sacrifice of al-Ja'd ibn Dirham. Khālid is the **qaṣṣāb al-zanādiqa** — the man who dared to strike heresy in the name of the community. His excesses (Zamzam, Ka'ba, qulfa, naṣb) are mentioned but **absorbed** into an overall positive assessment: al-Dhahabī classifies the sacrifice among his "good deeds" (ḥasanāt). The Sunni doctrinal framework retains from Khālid what serves the cause of Ash'arite/Atharite orthodoxy against the Jahmites.

### The al-Aghānī tradition: the accursed debauchee

The [[Sources/Kitāb al-Aghānī|Kitāb al-Aghānī]] of Abū al-Faraj al-Iṣfahānī (d. 356 H) — a **Shiite** author of Umayyad descent — devotes to Khālid an entirely hostile portrait, belonging to the genre of ***mathālib*** (scandalous anecdotes for polemical purposes).

**The takhannuth** (section "تخنثه في حداثته"): Khālid allegedly grew up in Medina in an effeminate milieu — "في حداثته يتخنث ويتتبع المغنين والمخنثين" (in his youth, he was effeminate and frequented singers and effeminate men). He served as a **go-between** (خريت) between the poet 'Umar ibn Abī Rabī'a and women — "يمشي بين عمر بن أبي ربيعة وبين النساء في رسائلهن إليه وفي رسائله إليهن". Two women call him "يا خريت" in a narrative at Wādī al-'Aqīq where he is described as "غلام مؤنث يصحب المغنين والمخنثين" (an effeminate youth who accompanies singers and effeminate men).

**The contested origins**: Al-Aghānī reports (via al-Madā'inī and Abū 'Ubayda) that the ancestor Kurz ibn 'Āmir was "عبد كان آبقا عن مواليه عبد القيس من هجر ويقال إن أصله من يهود تيماء" (a runaway slave from his masters 'Abd al-Qays of Hajar, whose origins are said to go back to the Jews of Taymā'). This narrative of servile and Jewish ancestry directly contradicts Khālid's claim of a Companion ancestor (Asad ibn Kurz).

**The closing**: the al-Aghānī text concludes with an explicit curse — "انقضت أخبار خالد لعنة الله عليه أبدا" (end of the accounts of Khālid, may God's curse be upon him forever).

### What the contrast reveals

| Dimension | Sunni tradition | Al-Aghānī |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Ancestor | Asad ibn Kurz, **Companion** | Kurz, **runaway slave**, possibly Jewish |
| Youth | Undocumented | **Takhannuth**, go-between |
| Central act | Sacrifice of al-Ja'd = **ḥasana** | Not mentioned or minimised |
| Closing | "Qaṣṣāb al-zanādiqa" — title of honour | "May God's curse be upon him" |

The gap is **maximal**: the same man is a hero of orthodoxy for one side and a depraved, accursed figure for the other. Both traditions select, omit and amplify according to their doctrinal needs. The Sunni portrait needs a defender of the faith against the Jahmites; the Shiite portrait needs a morally disqualified Umayyad. Neither produces a historical portrait — they produce **arguments**.

**Source**: Kitāb al-Aghānī, OpenITI text (0362AbuFarajIsbahani.Aghani), vol. 22 of the Cairo editions. The Sunni reception of the work is unanimously critical: see the entry [[Sources/Kitāb al-Aghānī]].

## Summary

A governor who:
- **disparages Zamzam** and places the caliph above the Prophet
- **publicly slaughters a theological opponent** like an Eid animal, before all the faithful
- **threatens to demolish the Ka'ba** if the caliph orders it
- **insults 'Alī ibn Abī Ṭālib** with words "that it is not permissible to repeat"
- **builds a church** for his Christian mother beside the mosque of Kufa
- **creates a chain of immunity** (secretary --> governor --> caliph --> Prophet --> God)
- **responds with an obscenity** to a rape victim
- **patronises Basran scholars** — Qatāda dies at his residence, Ḥumayd transmits from him, Thābit dies under his rule
- calls Hishām "son of the simpleton"

Yaḥyā ibn Ma'īn qualifies him as **rajul sū'** (رجل سوء — bad man). And yet al-Dārimī, al-Dhahabī, Ibn Taymiyya and Ibn al-Qayyim **absolve and celebrate** him because he killed a theological opponent. The act of slaughtering a human being like a sheep on the day of Eid becomes a **ḥasana** (good deed) and a cause for **shukr** (gratitude). The nickname "**qaṣṣāb al-zanādiqa**" (the butcher of heretics) completes this rehabilitation — violence receives a title of nobility.

Al-Dhahabī (Siyar): « وكان جوادا ممدحا معظما عالي الرتبة من نبلاء الرجال، **لكنه فيه نصب معروف** ». Note: the only grievance al-Dhahabī explicitly raises is the naṣb (hostility toward 'Alī). The other facts — Zamzam, the Ka'ba, the church, the qulfa, the chain of immunity — are not mentioned as reservations. This reflects the doctrinal framework of his era (8th c. H): after the Abbasid rehabilitation of 'Alī and the consolidation of the doctrine of **'adālat al-ṣaḥāba**, insulting a Companion became the primary criterion for disqualification, while other acts are incorporated into the overall assessment without negative comment.

This is Umayyad absolutism incarnate — political power does not merely use the sacred, it **publicly subordinates** it. And quṣṣāṣ like Qatāda are its dependants.
