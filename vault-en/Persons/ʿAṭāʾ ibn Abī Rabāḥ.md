# ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ — the witness of the first century

Nubian, former freed slave, one-eyed, lame, paralyzed in one arm, then blind — and yet **undisputed muftī of Mecca** for decades. Under the Umayyads, a herald would cry during the Ḥajj: **"No one gives fatwa except ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ!"** Abū Ḥanīfa said he had never met anyone better. Abū Jaʿfar al-Bāqir (the Shīʿī imam) sends his own disciples to him: "He is better for you than I am."

And yet, when one reads his legal responses in the Muṣannaf of Ibn Abī Shayba and ʿAbd al-Razzāq, one discovers an astounding phenomenon: **ʿAṭāʾ gives legal opinions in total ignorance of hadiths that would later be certified as authentic in the Ṣaḥīḥayn.** He — the muftī of Mecca, the direct student of Ibn ʿAbbās, the key witness to what happens in the holy city during the first century — **has not heard** traditions that are supposed to have circulated widely.

It is not his legal opinion per se that interests us. It is the **context** of that opinion: if he could have invoked a prophetic hadith to support his position, he would have. The fact that he does not — and that he explicitly says **لم أسمع** ("I have not heard of it") — is proof by absence that these hadiths **were not circulating** in the first-century Ḥijāz.

### Physical description (al-Dhahabī)

> كَانَ عَطَاءٌ أَسْوَدَ، أَعْوَرَ، أَفْطَسَ، أَشَلَّ، أَعْرَجَ، ثُمَّ عَمِيَ، وَكَانَ ثِقَةً، فَقِيْهاً، عَالِماً، كَثِيْرَ الحَدِيْثِ.

> "ʿAṭāʾ was **black, one-eyed, snub-nosed, paralyzed in one arm, lame**, then he became blind. He was thiqa, faqīh, learned, abundant in hadith."

— Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ ([p. 3852](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3852))

The accumulation of physical traits by al-Dhahabī is not gratuitous: it underscores that his authority rests entirely on **knowledge**, not on birth, ethnicity, or appearance.

## The status — an overwhelming consensus

The dossier of praise is so massive that it must be measured to understand how central the figure was — and therefore how significant his silence on certain hadiths is.

### Ibn ʿAbbās and Ibn ʿUmar — both refer to him

> يَا أَهْلَ مَكَّةَ! **تَجْتَمِعُوْنَ عَلَيَّ وَعِنْدَكُم عَطَاءٌ!**

> "O Meccans! You gather around me when **you have ʿAṭāʾ**!"

— Ibn ʿAbbās, Siyar ([p. 3853](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3853))

The same anecdote is attributed to **Ibn ʿUmar** in a variant (Qabīṣa via Sufyān; Tahdhīb al-Kamāl [p. 10208](https://shamela.ws/book/3722/10208)):

> **أتجمعون لي يا أهل مكة المسائل وفيكم ابن أبي رباح؟!**

Whether Ibn ʿAbbās or Ibn ʿUmar, the message is the same: the two greatest living Companions in Mecca declare ʿAṭāʾ as **their successor**.

### The Umayyad herald — monopoly on fatwa

> أَذْكُرُهُم فِي زَمَانِ بَنِي أُمَيَّةَ يَأْمُرُوْنَ فِي الحَجِّ مُنَادِياً يَصِيْحُ: **لاَ يُفْتِي النَّاسَ إِلاَّ عَطَاءُ بنُ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ**، فَإِنْ لَمْ يَكُنْ عَطَاءٌ، فَعَبْدُ اللهِ بنُ أَبِي نَجِيْحٍ.

> "Under the Umayyads, a herald would cry during the Ḥajj: **'No one gives fatwa except ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ!'** And if he is not there, ʿAbd Allāh ibn Abī Najīḥ."

— Siyar ([p. 3854](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3854))

The Umayyad authorities grant him the **official monopoly** on fatwa during the Ḥajj. He is not a scholar among others — he is the **only one authorized** by the caliphate.

### Abū Ḥanīfa, Abū Jaʿfar al-Bāqir, Mālik, Saʿīd ibn Jubayr

> **مَا رَأَيْتُ فِيْمَنْ لَقِيْتُ أَفْضَلَ مِنْ عَطَاءِ بنِ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ**

> "I have met **no one better** than ʿAṭāʾ." — **Abū Ḥanīfa**, Siyar ([p. 3855](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3855))

> **عَلَيْكُم بِعَطَاءٍ، هُوَ -وَاللهِ- خَيْرٌ لَكُم مِنِّي** [...] **خُذُوا مِنْ عَطَاءٍ مَا اسْتَطَعْتُم**

> "Go to ʿAṭāʾ, he is better for you than I am. [...] **Take from ʿAṭāʾ everything you can.**" — **Abū Jaʿfar al-Bāqir**, Siyar ([p. 3853](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3853))

> لَمْ يَزَالُوا مُتَنَاظِرِيْنَ حَتَّى خَرَجَ عَطَاءُ بنُ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ إِلَى المَدِيْنَةِ، فَلَمَّا رَجَعَ إِلَيْنَا، **اسْتَبَانَ فَضْلُهُ عَلَيْنَا**

> "[The scholars of Mecca] were evenly matched, then ʿAṭāʾ went to Medina. When he returned, **his superiority over us became evident.**" — **Mālik**, Siyar ([p. 3854](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3854))

> **مَا لَنَا هَا هُنَا مَعَ عَطَاءٍ شَيْءٌ**

> "**We are nothing next to ʿAṭāʾ.**" — **Saʿīd ibn Jubayr**, Siyar ([p. 3855](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3855))

### Al-Zuhrī before the caliph — "through piety and transmission"

> قُلْتُ: **عَطَاءٌ** [يسود مكة]. قَالَ: أَمِنَ العَرَبِ أَمْ مِنَ المَوَالِي؟ قُلْتُ: مِنَ المَوَالِي. قَالَ: فِيْمَ سَادَهُم؟ قُلْتُ: **بِالدِّيَانَةِ وَالرِّوَايَةِ**.

> "I said [to ʿAbd al-Malik]: ʿAṭāʾ [dominates Mecca]. Arab or mawlā? — Mawlā. — By what does he dominate them? — **Through piety and transmission.**"

The caliph is impressed — he exclaims: **هذا والله الشرف ! هذا والله السؤدد !** — "That, by God, is nobility! That is sovereignty!"

— Siyar ([p. 3857](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3857))

### The four imams of the provinces

Qatāda — and the most senior scholar of the peninsula:

> الحَسَنُ، وَابْنُ المُسَيِّبِ، وَإِبْرَاهِيْمُ، وَعَطَاءٌ، **هَؤُلاَءِ أَئِمَّةُ الأَمْصَارِ**

— Siyar ([p. 3855](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3855))

And to Sulaymān ibn Hishām:

> هَلْ بِالبَلَدِ أَحَدٌ؟ قُلْتُ: نَعَمْ، **أَقْدَمُ رَجُلٍ فِي جَزِيْرَةِ العَرَبِ عِلْماً** [...] عَطَاءُ بنُ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ

> "Is there anyone here? — Yes, **the most senior man in knowledge in all the Arabian peninsula**: ʿAṭāʾ."

— Siyar ([p. 3855](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3855))

### The majlis — eight or nine people

Al-Awzāʿī:

> مَاتَ عَطَاءُ بنُ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ يَوْمَ مَاتَ وَهُوَ **أَرْضَى أَهْلِ الأَرْضِ عِنْدَ النَّاسِ**، وَمَا كَانَ يَشْهَدُ مَجْلِسَهُ إِلاَّ **تِسْعَةٌ أَوْ ثَمَانِيَةٌ**

> "ʿAṭāʾ died on the day he was **the most approved man on earth** — and yet his majlis had only **eight or nine people.**"

— Siyar ([p. 3856](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3856))

This detail is crucial: the leading scholar of Mecca, in the last years of the first century, has only **eight students**. Transmission circles are **tiny**. The probability that a hadith circulates "widely" in this environment is low — and yet, that is what the system assumes when it declares "authentic" a hadith that ʿAṭāʾ does not know.

### 20 years in the mosque, 70+ pilgrimages

Ibn Jurayj — Siyar ([p. 3856](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3856)):

> **كَانَ المَسْجِدُ فِرَاشَ عَطَاءٍ عِشْرِيْنَ سَنَةً**، وَكَانَ مِنْ أَحْسَنِ النَّاسِ صَلاَةً

> "The mosque was ʿAṭāʾ's **bed** for **twenty years**, and he had the most beautiful prayer."

And Ibn Abī Laylā — Siyar ([p. 3854](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3854)):

> قَدْ حَجَّ زِيَادَةً عَلَى **سَبْعِيْنَ حَجَّةً**

> "He completed more than **seventy pilgrimages.**"

## The phenomenon: fatwas without hadiths

The Muṣannaf of ʿAbd al-Razzāq and Ibn Abī Shayba preserve his legal responses in extenso — and in many cases, he answers **"لم أسمع فيه ذلك"** ("I have not heard of that") to questions for which "authentic" prophetic hadiths are supposed to exist. On the meaning and scope of this formula among the ancients, see [[Concepts/Lam asmaʿ]].

For each case, we place alongside it the corresponding "authenticated" hadith, **with its isnād**.

### Case 1: The duʿāʾ on al-Ṣafā and al-Marwa — a hadith from his teacher Jābir

**ʿAṭāʾ says** — Muṣannaf Ibn Abī Shayba, no. 29643 ([p. 33591](https://shamela.ws/book/9944/33591)):

> **«لَمْ أَسْمَعْ أَنَّ عَلَى الصَّفَا، وَالْمَرْوَةِ دُعَاءً مُوَقَّتًا»**

> "**I have not heard** that there is a fixed invocation for al-Ṣafā and al-Marwa."

**The "authenticated" hadith** — the long hadith of **Jābir** (teacher of ʿAṭāʾ) in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, no. 147/1218 ([p. 2876](https://shamela.ws/book/1727/2876)):

> حَدَّثَنَا **حَاتِمُ بْنُ إِسْمَاعِيل** عَنْ **جَعْفَرِ بْنِ مُحَمَّدٍ** [al-Ṣādiq]، عَنْ **أَبِيهِ** [al-Bāqir] [...] عَنْ **جَابِرِ بْنِ عَبْدِ اللَّهِ** (…) فَلَمَّا دَنَا مِنَ الصَّفَا [...] فَوَحَّدَ اللَّهَ، وَكَبَّرَهُ. وَقَالَ: **«لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ وَحْدَهُ لَا شَرِيكَ لَهُ. لَهُ الْمُلْكُ وَلَهُ الْحَمْدُ وَهُوَ عَلَى كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ [...]»** ثُمَّ دَعَا بَيْنَ ذَلِكَ. قَالَ مِثْلَ هَذَا **ثَلَاثَ مَرَّاتٍ**.

The greatest specialist in Ḥajj rites (70+ pilgrimages), student of Jābir, knows **no prescribed duʿāʾ** for the two central hills of the saʿy. Yet the hadith of Jābir — one of the most famous texts in the corpus — describes in detail what the Prophet said on the Ṣafā, with a precise dhikr **repeated three times**. If Jābir had actually transmitted it during his lifetime, ʿAṭāʾ would have heard it.

The chain of the hadith passes exclusively through the **ahl al-bayt** (Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq → al-Bāqir → Jābir) — a distinct transmission circuit, possibly later.

### Case 2: "Al-ṣalāt khayr min al-nawm" — the muʾadhdhin of Mecca

**ʿAṭāʾ says** — Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, no. 1828 ([p. 1998](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/1998)):

> Ibn Jurayj asks: "When was 'prayer is better than sleep' first said?" ʿAṭāʾ: **«لَا أَدْرِي»** — "**I don't know.**"

**The "authenticated" hadith** — Sunan al-Nasāʾī, no. 1623 ([p. 2495](https://shamela.ws/book/8361/2495)):

> عَنْ **سُفْيَانَ**، عَنْ **أَبِي جَعْفَرٍ**، عَنْ **أَبِي سَلْمَانَ**، عَنْ **أَبِي مَحْذُورَةَ** : كُنْتُ أُؤَذِّنُ لِلنَّبِيِّ ﷺ، فَكُنْتُ أَقُولُ فِي أَذَانِ الْفَجْرِ الْأَوَّلِ: **الصَّلَاةُ خَيْرٌ مِنَ النَّوْمِ**

> "From **Abū Maḥdhūra**: I used to make the call to prayer for the Prophet, and I would say in the first adhān of fajr: **'Prayer is better than sleep.'**"

The Companion who transmits this hadith is **Abū Maḥdhūra** — the **muʾadhdhin of Mecca** designated by the Prophet. The formula comes from the muezzin of the mosque where ʿAṭāʾ lives — and ʿAṭāʾ does not know when it was instituted. A **Meccan** chain.

### Case 3: Taḥiyyat al-masjid — the prayer that the mosque resident does not know

**ʿAṭāʾ says** — Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, no. 1676 ([p. 1824](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/1824)):

> **«لَمْ أَسْمَعْ فِيهِ ذَلِكَ، وَذَلِكَ حَسَنٌ»**

> "**I have not heard of it, but it is a good thing.**"

**The "authenticated" hadith** — Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, no. 444 ([p. 747](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/747)):

> عَنْ **مَالِكٍ**، عَنْ **عَامِرِ بْنِ عَبْدِ اللهِ بْنِ الزُّبَيْرِ**، عَنْ **عَمْرِو بْنِ سُلَيْمٍ الزُّرَقِيّ**، عَنْ **أَبِي قَتَادَةَ** : أَنَّ رَسُولَ اللهِ ﷺ قَالَ: **«إِذَا دَخَلَ أَحَدُكُمُ الْمَسْجِدَ فَلْيَرْكَعْ رَكْعَتَيْنِ قَبْلَ أَنْ يَجْلِسَ»**

The chain is **entirely Medinan** — every link lives in Medina:

| Transmitter | City |
|---|---|
| Mālik ibn Anas | **Medina** |
| ʿĀmir ibn ʿAbd Allāh ibn al-Zubayr | **Medina** |
| ʿAmr ibn Sulaym al-Zuraqī | **Medina** (Zuraq clan, Anṣār) |
| Abū Qatāda al-Salamī | **Medina** (Anṣārī Companion) |

And the **variant via Jābir** (teacher of ʿAṭāʾ) in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, no. 71/715 ([p. 1589](https://shamela.ws/book/1727/1589)):

> عَنْ **سُفْيَانَ**، عَنْ **مُحَارِبِ بْنِ دِثَارٍ**، عَنْ **جَابِرِ بْنِ عَبْدِ اللَّهِ** : دَخَلْتُ عَلَيْهِ الْمَسْجِدَ. فَقَالَ لِي **«صَلِّ رَكْعَتَيْنِ»**

> "Jābir: I entered the mosque to see [the Prophet]. He said to me: **'Pray two rak'āt.'**"

But the most damning is the variant **Muslim 875** (hadith of Sulayk al-Ghaṭafānī, same prescription: pray two rak'āt upon entering the mosque, even during the khuṭba). This variant passes through a **Meccan** chain:

> عَنْ **ابْنِ جُرَيْجٍ**، عَنْ **عَمْرِو بْنِ دِينَارٍ**، عَنْ **جَابِرٍ** [...]

| Transmitter | City |
|---|---|
| Ibn Jurayj | **Mecca** |
| ʿAmr ibn Dīnār | **Mecca** |
| Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh | **Medina** (Companion) |

It is **Ibn Jurayj himself** — ʿAṭāʾ's student — who transmits a Meccan variant of this hadith, via **ʿAmr ibn Dīnār** — ʿAṭāʾ's Meccan colleague. The hadith circulated **in ʿAṭāʾ's immediate circle** in Mecca, and he does not know it. The man who **sleeps** in the sacred mosque for twenty years does not know the hadith about praying upon entering the mosque.

### Case 4: The tanning of skin — ʿAṭāʾ is in the chain of the hadith he does not know

**ʿAṭāʾ says** — Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, no. 207 ([p. 225](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/225)):

> عَبْدُ الرَّزَّاقِ، عَنِ ابْنِ جُرَيْجٍ قَالَ: سَأَلَ إِنْسَانٌ عَطَاءً عَنْ صُوفِ الْمَيْتَةِ فَكَرِهَهُ، وَقَالَ: **«إِنِّي لَمْ أَسْمَعْ أَنَّهُ يُرَخِّصُ إِلَّا فِي إِهَابِهَا إِذَا دُبِغَ»**

> "Someone asked ʿAṭāʾ about the wool of a dead animal. He disapproved and said: **'I have only heard of a dispensation for its skin, once tanned.'**"

ʿAṭāʾ knows the **practical rule** (you tan, you use) but cites no prophetic hadith, mentions neither Ibn ʿAbbās nor Maymūna.

**The "authenticated" hadith** — Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, no. 103/364 ([p. 753](https://shamela.ws/book/1727/753)):

> حَدَّثَنَا **ابْنُ جُرَيْجٍ**. أَخْبَرَنِي **عَمْرُو بْنُ دِينَارٍ**. أَخْبَرَنِي **عَطَاءٌ** مُنْذُ حِينٍ. قَالَ: أَخْبَرَنِي **ابْنُ عَبَّاسٍ**; أَنَّ **مَيْمُونَةَ** أَخْبَرَتْهُ; أَنَّ دَاجِنَةً كَانَتْ لِبَعْضِ نِسَاءِ رَسُولِ اللَّهِ ﷺ. فَمَاتَتْ. فَقَالَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ ﷺ **«أَلَّا أَخَذْتُمْ إِهَابَهَا فَاسْتَمْتَعْتُمْ بِهِ؟»**

> "**Ibn Jurayj**: **ʿAmr ibn Dīnār** informed me: **ʿAṭāʾ** informed me some time ago: **Ibn ʿAbbās** informed me that **Maymūna** informed him that a domestic sheep belonging to one of the Prophet's wives died. The Prophet said: **'Why didn't you take its skin and use it?'**"

The chain is **entirely Meccan**:

| Transmitter | City | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Ibn Jurayj | **Mecca** | |
| ʿAmr ibn Dīnār | **Mecca** | |
| **ʿAṬĀʾ** | **Mecca** | **HE HIMSELF IS IN THE CHAIN** |
| Ibn ʿAbbās | **Mecca** | |
| Maymūna | — | wife of the Prophet |

This is the most grotesque case in the dossier. According to Muslim, **ʿAṭāʾ himself** is the one who transmitted this hadith from Ibn ʿAbbās to ʿAmr ibn Dīnār, who transmitted it to **Ibn Jurayj** — the same Ibn Jurayj who, in the Muṣannaf, records ʿAṭāʾ's "لم أسمع." ʿAṭāʾ gives a faqīh's answer without ever invoking a prophetic hadith — on a subject for which **he is supposed to be the transmitter**.

Two hypotheses:
1. ʿAṭāʾ knew the **practice** but not the **structured hadith** (matn + isnād). The formal hadith was crystallized after him by the networks of Ibn Jurayj and ʿAmr ibn Dīnār.
2. The Muslim 103 chain passing through ʿAṭāʾ is a **retrospective projection** — a hadith he never transmitted in that form was attributed to him.

In both cases: the **hadith as a structured text** is a product posterior to ʿAṭāʾ.

### Summary table

| # | ʿAṭāʾ says | "Authenticated" hadith | Companion | Chain | Impact |
|---|-----------|----------------------|-----------|--------|--------|
| **1** | لم أسمع دعاء موقتا | Muslim 147/1218 | **Jābir** (his teacher) | Ahl al-bayt | ★★★★ |
| **2** | لا أدري | Nasāʾī 1623 | **Abū Maḥdhūra** (muezzin of Mecca) | **Meccan** | ★★★★ |
| **3** | لم أسمع فيه ذلك | Bukhārī 444, Muslim 875 | Abū Qatāda, **Jābir** | **Medinan** + **Meccan** (Ibn Jurayj ← ʿAmr) | ★★★★★ |
| **4** | لم أسمع إلا في إهابها إذا دبغ | Muslim 103/364 | **Ibn ʿAbbās** via **ʿAṬĀʾ HIMSELF** | **Entirely Meccan** | ★★★★★★ |

The geographic proximity between ʿAṭāʾ and the chains of these hadiths increases from case to case: distinct circuit (ahl al-bayt), same city (Abū Maḥdhūra), same circle (Ibn Jurayj transmits the Meccan variant), and finally the same transmitter — Muslim 103/364 makes ʿAṭāʾ the transmitter of a hadith from Ibn ʿAbbās on tanning — while in the Muṣannaf, ʿAṭāʾ responds to Ibn Jurayj on the same subject without ever mentioning a prophetic hadith.

## Fasting — three versions of the same event

ʿAṭāʾ is a key witness to the ancient practice of fasting. He transmits that the original fast was **three days per month** — not a full month (Ṭabarī, [p. 1550](https://shamela.ws/book/43/1550#p6)). And he himself **ate or drank during Ramaḍān**, invoking the opinion of Ibn ʿAbbās that the verse of the fidya (2:184) was not abrogated for the elderly (Siyar, [p. 3854](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3854)).

### The three versions

**Version 1 — Siyar** (al-Dhahabī, [p. 3854](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3854)), via Ibn Abī Laylā:

> رَأَيْتُهُ **يَشْرَبُ المَاءَ** فِي رَمَضَانَ، وَيَقُوْلُ: قَالَ ابْنُ عَبَّاسٍ: {وَعَلَى الَّذِيْنَ يُطِيْقُوْنَهُ فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِيْنٍ}: إِنِّي أُطْعِمُ أَكْثَرَ مِنْ مِسْكِيْنٍ.

> "I saw him **drinking water** in Ramaḍān, saying: Ibn ʿAbbās said: 'And upon those who can do it, a compensation' — and I feed more than one poor person."

The shortest version. No mention of old age. ʿAṭāʾ invokes the verse and the fidya without explicitly placing himself in a category of exception.

**Version 2 — Wakīʿ / ʿAbd ibn Ḥumayd** (Durr al-Manthūr, Suyūṭī, on 2:184):

> عن ابن أبي ليلى قال: دخلت على عطاء في رمضان وهو يأكل، فقال: قال ابن عباس: نزلت هذه الآية... لم تنسخ، هي **لمثلي**: للكبير الذي لا يستطيع الصوم.

> "Ibn Abī Laylā said: I visited ʿAṭāʾ during Ramaḍān while he was eating. He said: Ibn ʿAbbās said — this verse was revealed... it was not abrogated, it is **for someone like me**: for the elderly person who cannot fast."

This is the decisive version. The word **مثلي** ("like me") explicitly places ʿAṭāʾ in the category of the **shaykh al-kabīr al-fānī** — the decrepit elder exempted by consensus. It is no longer a rejection of the general obligation to fast; it is the application of a recognized exception.

**Version 3 — Ibn Mardawayh** (via Ibn Kathīr, Tafsīr 1/215 = Shamela [book/8473](https://shamela.ws/book/8473)):

> دَخَلْتُ عَلَى عَطَاءٍ فِي رَمَضَانَ **وَهُوَ يَأْكُلُ**، فَقَالَ: قَالَ ابْنُ عَبَّاسٍ: نَزَلَتْ هَذِهِ الْآيَةُ {وَعَلَى الَّذِينَ يُطِيقُونَهُ فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِينٍ} فَكَانَ مَنْ شَاءَ صَامَ وَمَنْ شَاءَ أَفْطَرَ وَأَطْعَمَ مِسْكِينًا، ثُمَّ نَزَلَتْ هَذِهِ الْآيَةُ فَنَسَخَتِ الْأُولَى، إِلَّا **الْكَبِيرَ الْفَانِيَ** إِنْ شَاءَ أَطْعَمَ عَنْ كُلِّ يَوْمٍ مِسْكِينًا وَأَفْطَرَ.

> "I visited ʿAṭāʾ during Ramaḍān while he was **eating**. He said: Ibn ʿAbbās said: this verse was revealed — whoever wished fasted, whoever wished broke his fast and fed a poor person — then the next verse was revealed and abrogated the first, **except for the decrepit elder**: if he wishes, he feeds a poor person per day and breaks the fast."

The isnād for versions 2 and 3: **Khālid ibn ʿAbd Allāh** ← **Ibn Abī Laylā** (Muḥammad ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān, contested: sayyi' al-ḥifẓ) ← ʿAṭāʾ.

### What "مثلي" changes — and what it does not

The Wakīʿ version with "مثلي" explains why **no classical scholar condemns the practice**: ʿAṭāʾ does not reject the general obligation to fast — he places himself in a recognized category of exception. At ~100 years old, he is the shaykh al-kabīr par excellence. It is not a scandal; it is the application of a rule.

| Source | Treatment | Criticism? |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Al-Dhahabī** (Siyar, [pp. 3853-3860](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3853)) | Reports the scene in an 8-page notice of praise | **None** |
| **Ibn Kathīr** (Tafsīr, 1/215 = [book/8473](https://shamela.ws/book/8473)) | Cites the Ibn Mardawayh version in the exegesis of 2:184 | **None** |
| **Al-Qurṭubī** (Tafsīr, on 2:184) | Cites ʿAṭāʾ among the scholars extending the fidya to pregnant and nursing women | **None** |

The silence is explained: there is nothing to condemn. The only criticism in the tarājim concerns his **mursalāt** (Aḥmad, Yaḥyā al-Qaṭṭān) — not his legal positions.

### What remains significant despite "مثلي"

1. **Ibn Abī Laylā's surprise** — "أتأكل؟!". If eating during Ramaḍān for an elderly person were banal, the question would not be asked. The scene shows that the **concrete application** of the exemption remained **remarkable**, even if the principle was accepted.

2. **The legal foundation** — ʿAṭāʾ does not say "I am old, I cannot." He invokes **Ibn ʿAbbās** and the **non-abrogation of the verse**. It is an argument from tafsīr, not a medical excuse. The old-age exemption is the **vehicle**; the substance of the argument is that verse 2:184 remains operative.

3. **The Siyar version** (version 1) is **shorter** and contains **neither "مثلي" nor "الكبير الفاني."** The divergence between the versions suggests that the clarification "for someone like me" may be a **clarificatory addition** by the transmitters — or that the Siyar version was abbreviated. The ambiguity persists.

4. **The overall profile remains laxist** — fidya instead of fasting, taḥiyyat al-masjid unknown, duʿāʾ of ṣafā not heard. ʿAṭāʾ testifies to a practice **more flexible** than what the canonical collections present as the norm.

### Conclusion

ʿAṭāʾ does not prove that Ramaḍān fasting was optional in the first century — the Wakīʿ version shows that he placed himself within the exemption for the elderly. But he remains a **witness to normative flexibility**: the muftī of Mecca openly applied the fidya by invoking a non-abrogated reading of the verse, and no one criticized him. This flexibility contrasts with the later rigidity of the consensus on strict obligation.

For the full analysis — the verses, Companion practice, the panorama of kaffārāt, and what the divergences reveal about the dating of the consensus — see **[[Traditions/Ṣawm Ramaḍān]]**.

## The zakat al-fiṭr episode — al-ʿilj

Sunan al-Bayhaqī al-Kubrā, hadith 7736 ([p. 8968](https://shamela.ws/book/7861/8968)):

> سَمِعْتُ ابْنَ أَبِي مُلَيْكَةَ، وَرَجُلٌ يَقُولُ لَهُ: إِنَّ عَطَاءً أَمَرَنِي أَنْ أَطْرَحَ زَكَاةَ الْفِطْرِ فِي الْمَسْجِدِ، فَقَالَ ابْنُ أَبِي مُلَيْكَةَ: **أَفْتَاكَ الْعِلْجُ بِغَيْرِ رَأْيَهِ اقْسِمْهَا فَإِنَّمَا يُعْطِيهَا ابْنُ هِشَامٍ أَحْرَاسَهُ وَمَنْ شَاءَ.**

> "I heard Ibn Abī Mulayka, while a man was saying to him: ʿAṭāʾ ordered me to deposit the zakat al-fiṭr in the mosque. Ibn Abī Mulayka said: **'The barbarian (al-ʿilj) gave you an opinion contrary to his own [= contrary to the people's interest]. Divide it yourself, for Ibn Hishām will only give it to his guards and whomever he pleases.'**"

The term **العلج** (al-ʿilj) is an ethnic slur targeting non-Arabs — directed at ʿAṭāʾ because of his Nubian origin. The isnād passes through **al-Shāfiʿī** himself.

The episode is triply revealing:
1. ʿAṭāʾ gives a **naive** opinion (deposit at the mosque = give it to the governor) — Ibn Abī Mulayka corrects him by saying that governor Ibn Hishām will divert the money to his guards
2. The **ethnic** dimension of the rejection: ʿAṭāʾ is a Nubian mawlā in an Arab Qurashite milieu — and he is reminded of it when he displeases
3. ʿAṭāʾ's opinion is not "wrong" per se — it is Ibn Abī Mulayka who is **politically lucid** about the governor's corruption. ʿAṭāʾ thinks in terms of norms; Ibn Abī Mulayka thinks in terms of power

## The confusion ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ / ʿAṭāʾ al-Khurāsānī

A textbook case of the blurring generated by book-based transmissions.

In Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, tafsīr of Sūrat Nūḥ (hadith 4920, [p. 7296](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/7296)):

> حَدَّثَنَا إِبْرَاهِيمُ بْنُ مُوسَى، أَخْبَرَنَا هِشَامٌ، عَنِ ابْنِ جُرَيْجٍ **وَقَالَ عَطَاءٌ** : عَنِ ابْنِ عَبَّاسٍ: «صَارَتِ الْأَوْثَانُ الَّتِي كَانَتْ فِي قَوْمِ نُوحٍ فِي الْعَرَبِ بَعْدُ...»

The hadith identifies the Quranic idols (Wadd, Suwāʿ, Yaghūth, Yaʿūq, Nasr) as pious men of Noah deified after their death, and attributes them to the Yemeni tribes. Is the ʿAṭāʾ in the isnād **Ibn Abī Rabāḥ** (the muftī of Mecca, thiqa) or **al-Khurāsānī** (weak transmitter, d. ~135H, عامة أحاديثه مقلوبة — al-Bukhārī in al-Ḍuʿafāʾ)?

Al-Dhahabī in the Siyar ([p. 4391](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/4391)) rules in favor of Ibn Abī Rabāḥ:

> قِيْلَ: إِنَّ الَّذِي فِي تَفْسِيْرِ سُوْرَةِ نُوْحٍ مِنْ (صَحِيْحِ البُخَارِيِّ) هُوَ عَطَاءٌ الخُرَاسَانِيُّ، وَلَيْسَ بِجَيِّدٍ، **بَلْ هُوَ عَطَاءُ بنُ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ**

But the editors correct al-Dhahabī: **it is indeed al-Khurāsānī**, in accordance with the tafsīr of Ibn Jurayj via ʿAbd al-Razzāq. [[Persons/Ibn Jurayj|Ibn Jurayj]] did not actually hear the tafsīr directly from al-Khurāsānī — he received it from his son ʿUthmān, yet says "akhbaranā." See [[Persons/ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa]] and [[Concepts/Chains of the Tafsīr of Ibn ʿAbbās]] for the full dossier.

The confusion illustrates the problem of **opaque ʿan** developed in [[Concepts/Taḥammul al-ḥadīth]]: when the isnād simply says "عطاء عن ابن عباس," one does not even know **which ʿAṭāʾ** is speaking.

## Marginalization — when the ahl al-ḥadīth take power

ʿAṭāʾ represents a model of **pre-hadith** knowledge: the faqīh who reasons from his knowledge of the Quran, Meccan practice, and what he received from Ibn ʿAbbās — without the systematic filter of the prophetic hadith. When the ahl al-ḥadīth impose the model of hadith as the primary source of law, ʿAṭāʾ is progressively **discredited**.

### Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal — the weakest mursals

> **لَيْسَ فِي المُرْسَلاَتِ شَيْءٌ أَضْعَفُ مِنْ مُرْسَلاَتِ الحَسَنِ، وَعَطَاءِ بنِ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ، كَانَا يَأْخُذَانِ عَنْ كُلِّ أَحَدٍ**

> "There are no mursals weaker than those of al-Ḥasan and **ʿAṭāʾ** — they took from **anyone.**"

— Siyar ([p. 3858](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3858))

### Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Qaṭṭān — Mujāhid's mursals are far better

> **مُرْسَلاَتُ مُجَاهِدٍ أَحَبُّ إِلَيَّ مِنْ مُرْسَلاَتِ عَطَاءٍ بِكَثِيْرٍ، كَانَ عَطَاءٌ يَأْخُذُ عَنْ كُلِّ ضَرْبٍ**

> "Mujāhid's mursals please me **far more** than ʿAṭāʾ's — ʿAṭāʾ took from **every kind of people.**"

— Siyar ([p. 3858](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3858))

### Abū Dāwūd — the hierarchy of mursals

> مراسيل مجاهد أو عطاء؟ قال: **مجاهد**، كان عطاء يحمل عن كل ضرب. مراسيل الحسن أو مراسيل عطاء؟ قال: **عطاء**

> "Mujāhid's mursals or ʿAṭāʾ's? — Mujāhid [is better]. Al-Ḥasan's or ʿAṭāʾ's? — **ʿAṭāʾ** [is better than al-Ḥasan]."

— Suʾālāt al-Ājurrī, via Tahdhīb al-Kamāl ([p. 10216](https://shamela.ws/book/3722/10216))

### ʿAlī ibn al-Madīnī — the ikhtilāṭ

> كَانَ عَطَاءٌ **اخْتَلَطَ بِأَخَرَةٍ**، تَرَكَهُ: ابْنُ جُرَيْجٍ، وَقَيْسُ بنُ سَعْدٍ.

> "ʿAṭāʾ **lost his faculties at the end**. Ibn Jurayj and Qays ibn Saʿd abandoned him."

— Siyar ([p. 3859](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3859))

Al-Dhahabī tempers: Ibn al-Madīnī does not mean the technical "abandonment" (which invalidates a transmitter), but simply that they had already taken everything from him.

### Shuʿba — his traditions from ʿAlī come from a book

> عَطَاءِ بْنِ أَبي رَبَاحٍ عن علي **إنما هي من كتاب**

> "What ʿAṭāʾ [transmits] from ʿAlī **comes only from a book.**"

— Shuʿba, via Muqaddima al-Jarḥ wa al-Taʿdīl ([p. 130](https://shamela.ws/book/2170/130)); confirmed in Tahdhīb al-Kamāl ([p. 10216](https://shamela.ws/book/3722/10216))

So ʿAṭāʾ himself practiced **wijāda** — transmission from a book, the weakest form of [[Concepts/Taḥammul al-ḥadīth|taḥammul]]. The same phenomenon documented in [[Persons/Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna|Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna]] with the book of Ayyūb ibn Mūsā.

### "I am ashamed to give my opinion"

Paradoxically, ʿAṭāʾ himself rejects ra'y:

> سُئِلَ عَطَاءٌ عَنْ شَيْءٍ، فَقَالَ: لاَ أَدْرِي. قِيْلَ: أَلاَ تَقُوْلُ بِرَأْيِكَ؟ قَالَ: **إِنِّي أَسْتَحْيِي مِنَ اللهِ أَنْ يُدَانَ فِي الأَرْضِ بِرَأْيِي**.

> "Someone asked ʿAṭāʾ a question. He said: I don't know. They said: won't you give your opinion? He said: **I am ashamed before God that people should practice religion on earth according to my opinion.**"

— Siyar ([p. 3858](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3858))

The contradiction is instructive: the same man is accused of "too much ra'y" by later critics, and explicitly refuses ra'y when it is proposed to him. What his detractors call "ra'y" is in reality the **first-century Meccan juridical practice** — a practice anterior to the systematization of hadith as a source of law.

## Possible objections

### Objection 1: "His mursals are weak — he took from anyone"

This is the criticism of Aḥmad and Yaḥyā al-Qaṭṭān. But it **reinforces** our argument rather than undermining it.

The criticism says: when ʿAṭāʾ attributes a hadith to the Prophet as a mursal, it could come from anyone — so it is weak as *positive* proof of authenticity. But our argument is **negative**: when ʿAṭāʾ says "لم أسمع," it means that **even a man who took from all sources** has not heard this hadith. If someone who accepts traditions from "every kind of people" (كل ضرب) has not heard a hadith, then that hadith **was truly not circulating**.

The more open ʿAṭāʾ is to sources, the more significant his silence.

### Objection 2: "He perhaps knew the hadith but preferred to reason"

The text itself refutes this objection. When ʿAṭāʾ says "لم أسمع فيه ذلك، وذلك حسن" (taḥiyyat al-masjid), he does not say "I know a hadith but I prefer my own opinion." He **explicitly** says he has not heard — and he **approves** the practice. A muftī who knows a prophetic hadith and approves the practice it prescribes would **cite it** — that is exactly his role.

And above all — ʿAṭāʾ himself says he is **ashamed** to give opinions by ra'y (إني أستحيي من الله أن يدان في الأرض برأيي). This is not a man who chooses not to cite hadiths — it is a man who **would prefer** to have a prophetic text.

Moreover, when he knows a hadith, he cites it. The sources describe him as "كثير الحديث" (abundant in hadith). He regularly transmits from Ibn ʿAbbās with chains. His silence elsewhere is not a methodological choice — it is a statement of absence.

### Objection 3: "He learned the hadith later — he didn't know it yet"

This is the most natural response from the muḥaddithīn: ʿAṭāʾ said "لم أسمع" at a given moment, then later heard the hadith. The "لم أسمع" would date from the beginning, the transmitted hadith from the end.

Except that **Case 4** (tanning) destroys this explanation. The chain of Muslim (103/364) says:

> **Ibn Jurayj** ← **ʿAmr ibn Dīnār** ← **ʿAṭāʾ** ← Ibn ʿAbbās ← Maymūna

Now **Ibn Jurayj** is **ʿAṭāʾ's direct student** for 18 years — and it is **the same Ibn Jurayj** who records the "لم أسمع" in the Muṣannaf. One would therefore have to believe that:
1. ʿAṭāʾ tells Ibn Jurayj: "I have never heard a prophetic hadith on tanning"
2. Then ʿAṭāʾ learns the hadith from Ibn ʿAbbās
3. Then ʿAṭāʾ transmits it to ʿAmr ibn Dīnār (Meccan colleague of Ibn Jurayj)
4. But he never **corrects** his answer to Ibn Jurayj, his own student

This is incoherent. A muftī who discovers a prophetic hadith on a question he was asked about would transmit it — **especially** to the student who asked the question.

Worse still: if ʿAṭāʾ had "learned the hadith later," the muḥaddithīn should have produced **two traditions** from him — an old one (the ra'y) and a new one (the hadith) — with mention of his retraction (رجع عن ذلك). Yet this retraction **does not exist**. The ra'y tradition and the hadith coexist in the sources without anyone connecting them — a sign that they come from **different circuits** that do not know each other.

And the problem recurs: for **Case 1** (duʿāʾ Ṣafā/Marwa), ʿAṭāʾ says "لم أسمع دعاء موقتا" about a ritual detailed in the long hadith of **Jābir** — **his own teacher** with whom he studied. The undisputed specialist in Ḥajj rites (70+ pilgrimages) has "not yet heard" what his shaykh supposedly prescribed on the two central hills of the saʿy? That is exactly what our argument demonstrates: **the hadith did not exist in that form during his lifetime**.

### Objection 4: "لم أسمع does not prove the hadith didn't exist"

In the strict sense, this is true. But the question is not the metaphysical existence of the hadith — it is its **circulation**. Let us measure the weight of the silence:

- ʿAṭāʾ is the muftī of the sacred mosque — **20 years** as a resident
- He met **200 Companions**
- He takes from "every kind of people"
- The Umayyads forbid anyone else from giving fatwas during the Ḥajj
- He completed **70+** pilgrimages
- He is the direct student of Ibn ʿAbbās
- And he says "لم أسمع" about the prayer upon entering **the mosque where he sleeps**

The only coherent explanation is that these hadiths **were not circulating** in the Ḥijāz during his lifetime.

### Objection 5: "There is a chronological gap — the Muṣannaf is compiled ~200H"

The chain of transmission is short and solid:

```
ʿAṬĀʾ (d. ~114H)
    │
    │  ← 18 years of companionship (لزمت عطاء ثمان عشرة سنة)
    │
IBN JURAYJ (d. 150H)
    │  First compiler by subject (muṣannif)
    │  ʿAṭāʾ's responses are recorded during his lifetime
    │
ʿABD AL-RAZZĀQ (d. 211H)
    │  Direct student of Ibn Jurayj
    │
MUṢANNAF (~200H)
```

Ibn Jurayj is a **compiler**, not a purely oral transmitter — he is considered one of the first to write books. ʿAṭāʾ's responses are recorded during his lifetime. And above all: when ʿAṭāʾ says "لم أسمع," it is his **own testimony** — there is no isnād problem, no mursal, no chain to verify. He says "I have not heard," and Ibn Jurayj hears him say that, and records it.

### Objection 6: "The ikhtilāṭ may have altered his responses"

ʿAlī ibn al-Madīnī says he "lost his faculties at the end." But:

1. Al-Dhahabī tempers — it is not the technical abandonment
2. The "لم أسمع" statements are transmitted by **Ibn Jurayj**, who accompanied ʿAṭāʾ for **18 years** and who precisely "left him" at the end (the ikhtilāṭ). Ibn Jurayj's records date from the **pre-ikhtilāṭ** period
3. Ikhtilāṭ affects **memory** — it could make one forget a known hadith. But ʿAṭāʾ does not say "I don't remember." He says "لم أسمع" — I have **never** heard. This is not forgetfulness; it is a declaration of ignorance.

## The analysis: the witness who invalidates by his silence

The case of ʿAṭāʾ is structurally different from the isnād problems treated in the rest of the vault. Here, it is not the **chain** that is suspect — it is the **absence** of a chain that speaks.

The elements converge:

- ʿAṭāʾ is the muftī of Mecca for ~40 years, resident of the sacred mosque, 200 Companions met, 70+ pilgrimages
- He answers hundreds of legal questions in the two Muṣannaf, via Ibn Jurayj
- For some of these questions, prophetic hadiths exist in the canonical collections (Bukhārī, Muslim)
- On these same questions, ʿAṭāʾ answers as a faqīh: he reasons, approves or disapproves ("وذلك حسن"), but never cites a prophetic text and explicitly says "لم أسمع"
- When he knows a hadith, he cites it (كثير الحديث); he says he is ashamed to resort to ra'y ("أستحيي من الله")

If these hadiths had circulated in the first-century Ḥijāz, the muftī of Mecca would have known them.

### What the grotesque cases reveal about the subtle ones

The cases presented here are **spectacular** — and that is precisely why they are visible. When the muftī of Mecca is unaware of a hadith that passes through himself (Case 4) or that describes the Ḥajj rites he has practiced for 70 pilgrimages (Case 1), the contradiction is **too glaring** to go unnoticed.

But the process of retrospective projection — attributing to a Companion or Successor a hadith crystallized after him — is not limited to these extreme cases. It operates on a much larger scale, across hundreds of traditions where the contradiction is **subtle**, where the gap between the transmitter's fatwa and the hadith attributed to him is not immediately visible, where the transmission circuits overlap enough to seem coherent.

ʿAṭāʾ's cases are the **gaping fissures** that make the mechanism visible. They prove that the isnād system could attribute a hadith to a man who had never heard it — and make it pass as authentic. If the system fails this flagrantly on the most exposed muftī of his era, one must assume it fails **systematically** on less documented figures, where no one can verify.
