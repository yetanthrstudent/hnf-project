# Ḥammād ibn Salama — the compromised witness

Imam of Basra, the most reliable transmitter of [[Thābit al-Bunānī]] and [[Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]]. Unanimously thiqa for his early traditions — but his **books** are suspected of having been infiltrated by his stepson **Ibn Abī al-ʿAwjāʾ**, a zindīq executed under al-Mahdī. Bukhārī takes from him only a single hadith, in the form of a taʿlīq. The result: an **abnormal** proportion of anthropomorphist hadiths (ṣifāt) in his corpus.

## Evaluation — the cracked consensus

Four levels of judgment:

### Unanimously positive (early hadiths)

- **Aḥmad**: « أثبت الناس في ثابت البناني » — the most reliable for Thābit
- **Ibn Maʿīn**: thiqa
- **Ibn Ḥibbān**: defends Ḥammād **against Bukhārī** — reproaches Bukhārī for refusing Ḥammād while accepting weaker transmitters

### Serious criticism

- **Al-Dhahabī** (Mīzān, [p. 590-593](https://shamela.ws/book/1692/590)): acknowledges late ikhtilāṭ
- **Muslim** (al-Tamyīz, [p. 50](https://shamela.ws/book/6048/50)): specifically criticizes the chains Ḥammād ← Qatāda, Ḥammād ← Ayyūb, Ḥammād ← Yūnus — **then uses these same chains in the Ṣaḥīḥ**

### Bukhārī — near-total refusal

Only one taʿlīq (see dedicated section below).

## The zindīq stepson — Ibn Abī al-ʿAwjāʾ

Ibn al-Thaljī reports (Mīzān, [p. 593](https://shamela.ws/book/1692/593)):

> « ابن أبي العوجاء ربيب حماد بن سلمة، فكان يدس في كتبه »

> "Ibn Abī al-ʿAwjāʾ was the **stepson** (rabīb) of Ḥammād ibn Salama, and he **inserted** (yuduss) into his books."

**Ibn Abī al-ʿAwjāʾ**: declared zindīq (atheist/heretic), executed by the governor of Basra under the caliph al-Mahdī. The accusation is one of **dassasa** — the physical insertion of material into Ḥammād's kutub (manuscripts). The mechanism is simple: Ḥammād owned manuscripts; his stepson had physical access to them within the household. The insertion required neither complicity nor negligence — just domestic proximity.

The link with the ṣifāt hadiths is structural: anthropomorphist traditions — God seen as a young man, God on a bed, the divine thumb — are exactly the kind of content a zindīq seeking to **discredit Islam from within** would have inserted. Hadiths shocking enough to be noticed, but "technical" enough to pass through the transmission system.

## Bukhārī — a single taʿlīq

Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, hadith **6440** ([p. 9661-9662](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/9661)):

> « قال لنا أبو الوليد... حدثنا حماد بن سلمة... »

Form **"qāla lanā"** = taʿlīq — Bukhārī does not say "ḥaddathanā" (direct audition) but "he told us." This is the signal of **maximum distance** in the Ṣaḥīḥ. Out of the ~7,000 hadiths of Bukhārī, **only one** passes through Ḥammād, and in this degraded form.

Ibn Ḥibbān protests against this refusal, but Bukhārī maintains his silent stance.

Hadith **6439** (just before) also passes through Ḥammād ← Thābit ← Anas — same actors. Both hadiths are in the same bāb, but only 6440 is retained, and in the weakest possible form.

## The Muslim paradox — al-Tamyīz

Al-Tamyīz ([p. 50](https://shamela.ws/book/6048/50)):

Muslim explicitly criticizes Ḥammād for his transmissions from **[[Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]]**, **Ayyūb**, **Yūnus** and others. He considers Ḥammād unreliable for these chains.

**However**: Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim contains hadiths transmitted through these **same chains** criticized in al-Tamyīz. Muslim documents the problem in his methodological book, then ignores it in his canonical collection.

Same pattern as [[Shu'ba ibn al-Ḥajjāj|Shuʿba]] with Abū Isḥāq (see [[Concepts/Kafaytukum tadlīs thalātha]]): the system records its own failures without resolving them.

## The ṣifāt hadiths — the anthropomorphist charge

Al-Dhahabī notes that Ḥammād has **many ṣifāt hadiths** (anthropomorphic divine attributes). Three documented examples:

### The divine thumb — Tirmidhī 3074

Tirmidhī ([p. 5285](https://shamela.ws/book/7895/5285)) — chain via Ḥammād, **[[Concepts/Tafarrud|tafarrud]]** (isolated transmission). The Prophet says that a Jew comes and describes the heavens on a finger, the earths on a finger, water on a finger, etc. The Prophet laughs and confirms.

The hadith is a tafarrud of Ḥammād — no other reliable route transmits it independently.

### God seen as a young man — al-Kāmil

Al-Kāmil of Ibn ʿAdī ([p. 1053](https://shamela.ws/book/12579/1053)): via Ḥammād ← [[Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] ← ʿIkrima ← Ibn ʿAbbās — a vision of God in the form of a **beardless young man** (شاب أمرد).

The chain passes through **Qatāda** — exactly the channel that Muslim criticizes in al-Tamyīz.

### God on His throne/bed

Sunan al-Bayhaqī ([p. 8415](https://shamela.ws/book/7861/8415)) and Ibn Khuzayma, al-Tawḥīd ([p. 179](https://shamela.ws/book/13011/179)): « أرى ربي على سريره / كرسيه » — "I shall see my Lord on His bed/throne."

Also: ʿUthmān al-Dārimī, al-Radd ʿalā al-Jahmiyya ([p. 57](https://shamela.ws/book/18091/57)).

### The pattern

These hadiths are **bizarre**, systematically transmitted by Ḥammād, and correspond exactly to the type of material a satirical zindīq would insert to ridicule religion. Ḥammād's anthropomorphist corpus is not a transmission accident — it is a **converging body of evidence** pointing to the insertion documented by Ibn al-Thaljī.

## The Ka'b al-Aḥbār connection

Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ ([p. 7988](https://shamela.ws/book/10495/7988)): passage on Ka'b and the tyrants, transmitted by Ḥammād. Same circuit as for [[Ka'b al-Ahbar]] — the [[Concepts/Isrā'īliyyāt]] also transit through Ḥammād, adding a layer of exogenous material to the already compromised corpus.

## The confusion of the two Ḥammāds

A classic trap in hadith criticism: **[[Ḥammād ibn Zayd]]** and **Ḥammād ibn Salama** are both Basran, both from the same generation, both thiqāt. Frequent attribution errors in manuscripts.

Critical distinction:
- **Ibn Zayd**: **never** met [[Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] — he had prepared the pages, but Qatāda died at Wāsiṭ before the meeting
- **Ibn Salama**: transmitted from Qatāda — and this is the chain that Muslim criticizes in al-Tamyīz

The case study of Ibn Ḥazm (Muḥallā 1/90) — where he erroneously attributes a hadith to Ibn Zayd instead of Ibn Salama — is detailed in [[Ḥammād ibn Zayd]].

## The witness to Ḥumayd's tadlīs

Ḥammād is the one through whom [[Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]]'s confession is transmitted:

> « عامة ما يروي حميد عن أنس لم يسمعه منه، إنما سمعه من ثابت »

> "The majority of what Ḥumayd reports from Anas, he did not hear from him — he heard it from Thābit."

The witness to the confusion is himself a compromised transmitter. The diagnosis of Ḥumayd's tadlīs rests on a man whose own books were infiltrated — a mise en abyme of the system.

## Summary

Ḥammād ibn Salama is the convergence point of three problems: books physically accessible to a heretic, an abnormal proportion of anthropomorphist traditions, and a refusal by Bukhārī that the system has tried to minimize. The Muslim paradox — criticize in al-Tamyīz then use in the Ṣaḥīḥ — is the same mechanism as for [[Shu'ba ibn al-Ḥajjāj|Shuʿba]]: the system documents its failures without resolving them. The "thiqa" consensus for Ḥammād protects only his early hadiths — the rest of the corpus bears the mark of an infiltration that no one was willing to settle.
