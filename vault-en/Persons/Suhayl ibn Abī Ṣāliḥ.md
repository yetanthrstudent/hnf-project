# Suhayl ibn Abī Ṣāliḥ — the son, the ṣaḥīfa, and the memory

Son of [[Persons/Dhakwān Abū Ṣāliḥ al-Sammān|Dhakwān Abū Ṣāliḥ al-Sammān]], the **silent pivot** of the most frequent chain in the Ṣaḥīḥayn leading to [[Persons/Abū Hurayra|Abū Hurayra]]. Suhayl inherits from his father a written corpus — nusakh (manuscripts) of hadiths — and transmits massively through this channel. The classical critics are **divided**: some declare him thiqa, others ḍaʿīf. What is documented: (1) his **audition** from his father was contested — "they said: it is a ṣaḥīfa"; (2) an illness impaired his memory; (3) al-Bukhārī avoids him in the Ṣaḥīḥ, Muslim uses him — and it is in Muslim that Suhayl is the **only** one to elevate certain hadiths to the Prophet where all other transmitters from his father keep them mawqūf.

## Classical assessments — the disagreement

Suhayl's case divides the critics like few transmitters do. The table below shows the extent of the disagreement:

| Critic | Judgment |
|---|---|
| **Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna** | « كنا نعد سهيلا ثبتا في الحديث » — "We counted Suhayl as reliable (thabit) in hadith" |
| **Al-ʿIjlī** | "thiqa" (as well as his brother ʿAbbād) |
| **Al-Nasāʾī** | "laysa bihi baʾs" — no objection |
| **Ibn ʿAdī** | "thabit lā baʾs bihi, maqbūl al-akhbār" — reliable, acceptable |
| **Abū ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-Barr** | "thiqa" |
| **Al-Ḥākim** | "one of the pillars of hadith — his memory deteriorated at the end" |
| **Al-Dhahabī** | "a great memorizer, but an illness changed his memory" |
| **Aḥmad** | Contradictory: "mā aṣlaḥa ḥadīthahu!" (how defective his hadith is!) — but also: "Suhayl is more reliable than Muḥammad ibn ʿAmr" |
| **Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn** | Contradictory: sometimes "ḍaʿīf," sometimes "laysa bi-dhāk," sometimes "thiqa," sometimes "the hadith people never stopped avoiding his hadith" |
| **Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Qaṭṭān** | "ikhtalata wa-taghayara" — he became confused and changed |
| **Abū Ḥātim al-Rāzī** | "yuktab ḥadīthuhu wa-lā yuḥtajj bihi" — his hadith is recorded but not used as proof |
| **Ibn Ḥajar** (Taqrīb) | "ṣadūq taghayara ḥifẓuhu bi-akhara" — truthful, memory changed at the end. 5th martaba |
| **Ibn al-Qaṭṭān** | Classifies him among the **mukhtalitūn** (transmitters whose condition changed) |
| **Al-Dāraquṭnī** | Defends Suhayl: "I know of no argument justifying his exclusion by al-Bukhārī" |

The emerging picture: a man who was **reliable in his early period**, then whose memory deteriorated — and the contradictory judgments reflect the **period** to which each critic refers. Those who knew him early (Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna) praise him; those who evaluate the entire corpus (Abū Ḥātim) are reserved.

## The inherited ṣaḥīfa — samāʿ or wijāda?

This is the central question. Abū Ṣāliḥ Dhakwān possessed manuscripts (kutub) containing hadiths from [[Persons/Abū Hurayra|Abū Hurayra]]. At his death (101H), these manuscripts passed to his son Suhayl.

Classical criticism formulates the problem:

> « تُكلِّم في سماعه من أبيه فقيل صحيفة »
>
> "His **audition** from his father was **contested** — they said: it is a ṣaḥīfa."

The question is precise: did Suhayl **hear** (samiʿa) all these hadiths from his father's mouth, or did he **find them in a book** (wijāda) after his death? If it is a wijāda, the chain "Suhayl ← Abū Ṣāliḥ" is formally **broken** according to strict hadith criteria — one transmits from a manuscript, not from a teacher.

### The physical nuskha

The material left a concrete trace: a manuscript of **48-49 hadiths**, all through the chain Suhayl → Abū Ṣāliḥ → Abū Hurayra → the Prophet, is preserved at the **al-Ẓāhiriyya library** in Damascus (6th century of the hijra copy). Muṣṭafā al-Aʿẓamī produced a critical edition in *Studies in Early Hadith Literature* (Cambridge, 1967).

Suhayl reportedly sent this nuskha to **Wuhayb ibn Khālid** (d. 165H), which explains why several hadiths from this collection also appear transmitted via Wuhayb in the canonical collections. All hadiths from this nuskha appear in the Musnad of Aḥmad. Muslim reports **all but six**.

### The parallel with al-Aʿmash

Suhayl's father had two main transmission channels:
- **Suhayl** (biological son, Medina) — contested for the ṣaḥīfa
- **[[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|Al-Aʿmash]]** (Kūfa) — who "**wrote** from Abū Ṣāliḥ 1,000 hadiths" (verb kataba, not samiʿa)

The ṣaḥīfa problem is therefore not unique to Suhayl. Both channels — the son in Medina and the student in Kūfa — show converging evidence of manuscript transmission rather than audition. But the vulnerabilities differ: for Suhayl, it is ikhtilāṭ (memory); for al-Aʿmash, it is tadlīs.

## The ikhtilāṭ — the changing memory

Two classical accounts document the deterioration:

**ʿAlī ibn al-Madīnī** reports:

> « مات أخ لسهيل فوجد عليه فنسي كثيرا من الحديث »
>
> "A brother of Suhayl died; he was stricken with grief, and he **forgot many hadiths**."

The brother is generally identified as **ʿAbbād ibn Abī Ṣāliḥ** (whom al-ʿIjlī also declared thiqa).

**ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz al-Dārawardī** reports:

> « أصابت سهيلا علة أذهبت بعض عقله ونسي بعض حديثه »
>
> "An illness struck Suhayl, which took away part of his reason, and he forgot some of his hadiths."

Al-Dārawardī adds a revealing detail: after this deterioration, Suhayl no longer remembered a certain hadith, and it was **Rabīʿa al-Raʾy** who reminded him. Suhayl was now transmitting hadiths that others **gave back** to him — a classic sign of ikhtilāṭ.

### Periodization

Scholars distinguish **two periods**:
- **Before ikhtilāṭ**: Mālik ibn Anas, Sufyān al-Thawrī, and early transmitters are considered reliable — "Mālik took from him **before the change**"
- **After ikhtilāṭ**: late transmissions are suspect and have value only when corroborated by other routes

The question this periodization does not resolve: when Suhayl, after the ikhtilāṭ, no longer remembers a hadith heard from his father, **does he go back to the ṣaḥīfa** to refresh his memory? And if so, is he transmitting samāʿ or wijāda?

## Bukhārī avoids him, Muslim uses him

**Al-Bukhārī** uses Suhayl only as **maqrūnan** (paired with another transmitter) and **taʿlīqan** (suspended chain) — never as the primary source for a hadith. Ibn Ḥajar notes this explicitly: « روى له البخاري مقرونا وتعليقا ». There are approximately **3 hadiths** from Suhayl in Bukhārī, all in these categories.

**Muslim** uses him abundantly, but al-Ḥākim specifies: "most are in the shawāhid" (corroborating testimonies — the 3rd level of reliability in Muslim). He also sometimes uses him in the uṣūl (primary proofs), showing that Muslim had a more favorable opinion than Bukhārī.

**Al-Dāraquṭnī** criticizes this exclusion:

> « لا أعلم لأبي عبد الله (البخاري) حجة في تركه »
>
> "I know of no argument for al-Bukhārī justifying his abandonment [of Suhayl]."

The Bukhārī/Muslim divergence on Suhayl is one of the sharpest between the two compilers. It illustrates a real methodological disagreement on the threshold of reliability.

## The tuʿraḍ al-aʿmāl hadith — the textbook case

Already detailed in [[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|Al-Aʿmash]] — summarized here. The hadith "the deeds of the servants are presented [to God] every Monday and Thursday" (Muslim 2565a) is a perfect test.

Five transmitters report this hadith from Abū Ṣāliḥ. Four maintain it as **mawqūf** (stopped at Abū Ṣāliḥ or Abū Hurayra, without attribution to the Prophet). Only one elevates it to the Prophet (**marfūʿ**) — and that is Suhayl:

| Transmitter from Abū Ṣāliḥ | Status |
|---|---|
| **Al-Aʿmash** | Mawqūf — with doubt Abū Hurayra/Kaʿb |
| **Muslim ibn Abī Maryam** | Mawqūf |
| **Al-Ḥakam** | Mawqūf |
| **Al-Musayyab** | Mawqūf |
| **Suhayl** | **Marfūʿ** (alone) |

Al-Dāraquṭnī rules (ʿIlal no. 1884): "those who stopped it [mawqūf] are **more reliable** than those who elevated it [marfūʿ]."

The fact that the only transmitter to elevate this hadith to the Prophet is the one whose audition is contested and whose memory deteriorated is not an isolated coincidence — it is a **pattern**: the ṣaḥīfa contains material whose status (prophetic or not) is not always clear, and the ikhtilāṭ further blurs the attributions.

## Ibn ʿAdī's argument — transparency

Ibn ʿAdī (al-Kāmil fī Ḍuʿafāʾ al-Rijāl, [p. 2099](https://shamela.ws/book/12579/2099)) offers the best defense of Suhayl:

> « ولسهيل نسخ، روى عنه الأئمة، وهو عندي ثبت لا بأس به مقبول الأخبار »
>
> "Suhayl possesses nusakh [manuscripts]; the imams transmitted from him; he is, in my view, reliable, without reproach, and his reports are acceptable."

Most importantly, Ibn ʿAdī notes that Suhayl **clearly distinguished** between what he had heard directly from his father and what he had received through intermediaries. When a hadith reached him via al-Aʿmash or via Sumayy, he specifies: "Suhayl ← al-Aʿmash ← Abū Ṣāliḥ" or "Suhayl ← Sumayy ← Abū Ṣāliḥ" — instead of claiming to have heard everything directly.

This transparency (tamyīz) is a strong argument for his integrity: a man who specifies his intermediaries is not trying to deceive. But integrity does not protect against ikhtilāṭ — after the deterioration of his memory, even an honest man can confuse what he **heard** with what he **read**.

The diagram shows the **two inputs** into Abū Ṣāliḥ's ṣaḥīfa: on one side Abū Hurayra (prophetic hadiths), on the other [[Persons/ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ḍamra al-Salūlī|ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ḍamra al-Salūlī]] (isrāʾīliyyāt from Kaʿb). Abū Zurʿa (Q.2022) proves that the two sources mix in the ṣaḥīfa.

The material then transits through two independent channels with **complementary** problems:
- **Suhayl**: the inherited ṣaḥīfa + ikhtilāṭ → risk of confusing samāʿ/wijāda, and confusing Ka'b/the Prophet
- **Al-Aʿmash**: the 1,000 "written" hadiths + tadlīs → risk of confusing audition/copying

Both channels pose the same question in different forms: **where exactly does this material come from** — from the audition of Abū Hurayra, or from a manuscript that mixes Abū Hurayra and [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar|Kaʿb al-Aḥbār]]? Abū Zurʿa's answer (next section) is unambiguous: part of the material is from Kaʿb, passing through al-Salūlī — and the attribution to Abū Hurayra is an **error** (خطأ).

## Abū Zurʿa's answer — al-Salūlī and Kaʿb

The ʿilal books directly answer this question. When Abū Zurʿa examines the chain Suhayl → Abū Ṣāliḥ → Abū Hurayra, he corrects — on at least two occasions: the source is not Abū Hurayra, and the hadith is not prophetic.

**ʿIlal Ibn Abī Ḥātim**, Q.2022 ([p. 3002](https://shamela.ws/book/1350/3002)):

> سألتُ أبا زرعة عن حديث رواه عبد الرحمن بن أبي الرجال، عن سهيل بن أبي صالح، عن أبيه، عن أبي هريرة ... قال أبو زرعة: **هذا خطأ؛ إنما هو: سهيل، عن أبيه، عن السلولي، عن كعب؛ قوله.**
>
> "I asked Abū Zurʿa [...]. Abū Zurʿa said: **This is an error. The correct [chain] is: Suhayl, from his father, from al-Salūlī, from Kaʿb — his own words.**"

**Suʾālāt al-Bardhaʿī**, Q.453 ([p. 449](https://shamela.ws/book/812/449)) — same correction:

> **وإنما هو عن سهيل، عن أبيه، عن السلولي، عن كعب.**

**Al-Salūlī** is **ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ḍamra al-Salūlī** — tābiʿī of the 3rd ṭabaqa, thiqa (al-ʿIjlī), transmitter of **[[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar|Kaʿb al-Aḥbār]]** and of Abū Hurayra (Tahdhīb al-Kamāl no. 3345). And the rijāl confirm the link: **Abū Ṣāliḥ al-Sammān transmits from ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ḍamra** (Tahdhīb al-Kamāl, [p. 7614](https://shamela.ws/book/3722/7614)).

Al-Dāraquṭnī confirms the same pattern. **ʿIlal Q.1979** ([p. 3490](https://shamela.ws/book/9082/3490)), on the hadith of the takbīr at the Ḥajj:

> **وخالفه سليمان بن بلال، رواه، عن سهيل، عن أبيه، عن مرداس، عن كعب، وهو الصحيح.**
>
> "Sulaymān ibn Bilāl contradicted him [...] **from Mirdās, from Kaʿb — and this is the ṣaḥīḥ.**"

The pattern is recurring: behind "Abū Ṣāliḥ → Abū Hurayra → the Prophet (marfūʿ)" lies "Abū Ṣāliḥ → **al-Salūlī → Kaʿb** (mawqūf)." The material consists of **isrāʾīliyyāt** elevated to the status of prophetic hadith.

### Suhayl also transmits from ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār

The link with the Ibn ʿUmar network is direct. Al-Dāraquṭnī, **ʿIlal Q.1507** ([pp. 2624-2626](https://shamela.ws/book/9082/2624)), for the hadith "al-Īmān bidʿ wa-sabʿūn shuʿba":

> **والصحيح قول من قال: عن سهيل، عن عبد الله بن دينار، عن أبي صالح، عن أبي هريرة**

Suhayl transmits from **[[Persons/ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār|ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār]]** — the same ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār whose canonical chain leads to Ibn ʿUmar. The two networks (Ibn ʿUmar and Abū Hurayra) are not parallel: they share a common node. See the entry [[Persons/ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār|ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār]] for the full circuit analysis.

## Summary

Suhayl ibn Abī Ṣāliḥ is a textbook case in hadith sciences. The classical critics are genuinely divided — Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna praises him, Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn hesitates, Abū Ḥātim refuses to use him as proof. Ibn ʿAdī highlights his transparency about intermediaries — a man who distinguishes his sources is not trying to deceive. Al-Dāraquṭnī cannot understand why Bukhārī avoids him.

But the documented body of evidence goes beyond the question of Suhayl's probity. Three facts converge:

1. **The ṣaḥīfa mixes two sources** — Abū Ṣāliḥ transmits from Abū Hurayra (hadiths) and from [[Persons/ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ḍamra al-Salūlī|ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ḍamra al-Salūlī]] (isrāʾīliyyāt from Kaʿb). Abū Zurʿa proves this twice: Q.2022 (هذا خطأ — the correct chain is al-Salūlī → Kaʿb, not Abū Hurayra → the Prophet) and Q.453 (same correction). Al-Dāraquṭnī confirms (Q.1979: Mirdās → Kaʿb, وهو الصحيح). This is not a hypothesis — it is the explicit verdict of the greatest ʿilal critics.

2. **The ikhtilāṭ erases attributions** — After the death of his brother and his illness, Suhayl no longer distinguishes what he heard from what he read, nor what comes from Abū Hurayra from what comes from al-Salūlī. The observable pattern confirms it: when five transmitters from Abū Ṣāliḥ report the same hadith, four keep it mawqūf and the fifth elevates it to the Prophet — and that fifth is Suhayl (tuʿraḍ al-aʿmāl, ʿIlal no. 1884).

3. **The shared node** — Suhayl also transmits from [[Persons/ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār|ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār]] (Q.1507), the pivot of the Medinan network of Ibn ʿUmar. Behind him lies al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī in mursal (Q.3086: والمرسل أشبه). Both networks — that of Ibn ʿUmar and that of Abū Hurayra — converge toward non-prophetic sources (al-Ḥasan mursal, Kaʿb mawqūf) that the system elevated to marfūʿ.

The parallel with [[Persons/Makhrama ibn Bukayr|Makhrama ibn Bukayr]] (who also transmits from an inherited ṣaḥīfa without samāʿ, and whose material ends up in the Muwaṭṭaʾ) shows that the mechanism is not isolated. But Suhayl's case is the most documented: the classical critics **themselves** identify by name the hidden source ([[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar|Kaʿb al-Aḥbār]]), the erased link ([[Persons/ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ḍamra al-Salūlī|al-Salūlī]]), and the diagnosis (خطأ). The system documents its own flaw without resolving it.
