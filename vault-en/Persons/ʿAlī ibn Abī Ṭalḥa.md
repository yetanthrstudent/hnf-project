# ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa

A Ḥimṣī transmitter whose **real name** is **Sālim ibn al-Mukhāriq** — "ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa" is a substitute name. **Mawlā** of the Banū Hāshim, more precisely of **al-ʿAbbās ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib** — a client of the ʿAbbāsid family.

He is known for one thing only: carrying a **ṣaḥīfa of tafsīr** attributed to **Ibn ʿAbbās** whom he **never met**. Yet this ṣaḥīfa became one of the main sources of tafsīr in the Sunni tradition — al-Bukhārī uses it in his taʿlīqāt.

## The problem in one sentence

ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa arrives with a **complete tafsīr** of Ibn ʿAbbās, but when Ṣāliḥ Jazara is asked from whom he heard this tafsīr, the answer is blunt:

> « مِن لا أَحَد »

> « From nobody. »

— Ṣāliḥ ibn Muḥammad Jazara, reported in Tahdhīb al-Kamāl (20/490)

## Profile

| Data | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Real name | **Sālim ibn al-Mukhāriq** |
| Common name | ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa |
| Nisba | al-Hāshimī, al-ʿAbbāsī mawlāhum, al-Jazarī al-aṣl, al-Ḥimṣī |
| Kunya | Abū al-Ḥasan (or Abū Muḥammad, or Abū Ṭalḥa) |
| Death | **120H** or **143H** (two divergent dates) |
| Ṭabaqa | 6th (Ibn Ḥajar) |
| Residence | Ḥimṣ (originally from the Jazīra) |
| Ideological position | **Kāna yarā al-sayf** — advocated armed revolt |

The discrepancy in the death date is considerable: **23 years** apart. If 120H, he is contemporary with the last generation of Tābiʿīn. If 143H, he is significantly later. In both cases, he did **not meet** Ibn ʿAbbās (d. 68H) — the consensus is explicit.

| al-Fasawī | **Ḍaʿīf al-ḥadīth, laysa bi-maḥmūd al-madhhab** — then: **laysa bi-matrūk wa-lā huwa ḥujja** | Tahdhīb al-Kamāl (20/490) |
| al-ʿUqaylī | Cited **among the weak** | Ikmāl (9/347) |

The verdict of **Abū Dāwūd** is revealing: the hadith is "straight" (*mustaqīm*), but the man has a "bad opinion" (*raʾy sawʾ*) — he **advocated armed revolt** (*kāna yarā al-sayf*). Al-Fasawī formulates a double-edged judgment: neither abandoned (*matrūk*) nor probative argument (*ḥujja*) — an in-between that sums up the figure well.

## The consensus on not meeting Ibn ʿAbbās

This is the decisive point. **All** critics who address the question agree:

| Critic | Formulation | Source |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Duḥaym** | لم يسمع من ابن عباس التفسير — "he did not hear the tafsīr from Ibn ʿAbbās" | al-Jarḥ wa-l-Taʿdīl (6/188) |
| **Ibn Ḥibbān** | روى عن ابن عباس الناسخ والمنسوخ ولم يره — "he transmitted the nāsikh wa-l-mansūkh of Ibn ʿAbbās without having seen him" | al-Thiqāt (7/211) |
| **Abū Ḥātim** | عن ابن عباس مرسل — "from Ibn ʿAbbās: mursal" | al-Jarḥ wa-l-Taʿdīl (6/188) |
| **al-Mizzī** | مرسل بينهما مجاهد — "mursal, between them is Mujāhid" | Tahdhīb al-Kamāl (20/490) |
| **Ibn Ḥajar** | ولم يسمع منه بينهما مجاهد — "he did not hear from him, between them is Mujāhid" | Tahdhīb al-Tahdhīb (3/171) |
| **Ṣāliḥ Jazara** | ممن سمع التفسير ؟ قال : من لا أحد — "From whom did he hear the tafsīr? — From nobody." | Tahdhīb al-Kamāl (20/490) |
| **al-Fasawī** | روى عن ابن عباس الناسخ والمنسوخ ولم يره — same as Ibn Ḥibbān | Tuḥfat al-Taḥṣīl (1/362) |

The **key testimony** is that of **Duḥaym**, reported by Abū Ḥātim in the Jarḥ: after stating that ʿAlī did not hear the tafsīr from Ibn ʿAbbās, he follows up with an alternative chain — **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa ← Mujāhid** — revealing the actual intermediary.

## ʿAlī compared to the real students of Ibn ʿAbbās

The contrast with the **actual** students of Ibn ʿAbbās is striking:

| Transmitter | Relationship with Ibn ʿAbbās | Attestation | Transmission status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mujāhid ibn Jabr** (d. ~104H) | Direct student — **recited the Quran** before Ibn ʿAbbās **three times**, stopping at each verse to ask for the tafsīr | Multiple — confirmed by numerous students | **Samāʿ** solid, the best source |
| **ʿIkrima** (d. ~105H) | **Mawlā** (freed servant) of Ibn ʿAbbās — lived in his house, trained by him for years | Organic attestation — he is *of the household* | **Samāʿ** prolonged, though ʿIkrima is controversial (accusations of lying, Khārijite ties) |
| **Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** (d. 95H) | Direct student — "the most learned of people" according to Ibn ʿAbbās himself | Killed by al-Ḥajjāj — his martyrdom confirms his independence | **Samāʿ** solid |
| **Ṭāwūs ibn Kaysān** (d. ~106H) | Direct student in Yemen, among the *fuqahāʾ* of the generation | Confirmed by multiple chains | **Samāʿ** solid |
| **[[Persons/ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ]]** (d. ~114H) | Direct student in Mecca | Confirmed by multiple chains | **Samāʿ** solid |
| **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa** (d. 120/143H) | **Never met** Ibn ʿAbbās — unanimous | No attestation of contact | **Ṣaḥīfa** without *samāʿ* — **munqaṭiʿ** |

Ibn ʿAbbās had a **dense circle** of direct students: Mujāhid, ʿIkrima, Saʿīd ibn Jubayr, Ṭāwūs, ʿAṭāʾ — men who attended him for years, whose transmission is attested by dozens of cross-referencing chains. ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa does not belong to this circle. He transmits a complete tafsīr from a master he **never saw**, via an intermediary (**Mujāhid**) whom he **omits** from the chain.

## The mechanism of the ṣaḥīfa

The transmission functions as a **ṣaḥīfa** (written document) and not as *samāʿ* (audition):

```
Ibn ʿAbbās (d. 68H)
    │
    ├── Mujāhid (direct samāʿ — 3 complete recitations)
    │       │
    │       └──→ ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa (took from Mujāhid — Duḥaym, Abū Ḥātim)
    │               │
    │               └── BUT the chain omits Mujāhid and says:
    │                   "ʿAlī ← Ibn ʿAbbās" directly
    │
    ╳ Actual break (munqaṭiʿ — unanimous)
```

ʿAlī thus took the tafsīr from **Mujāhid** (and probably also from **al-Qāsim ibn Muḥammad** and others — Abū Ḥātim lists them), but the chain as it circulates **skips the intermediary** and connects directly to Ibn ʿAbbās. This is a case of **taswiya** (chain smoothing) or, more charitably, transmission by **wijāda** (finding in a manuscript attributed to Ibn ʿAbbās, without chain verification).

## The complete circuit of the ṣaḥīfa

```
Ibn ʿAbbās (d. 68H, Ṭāʾif)
    ╳ (break — unanimous)
ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa (d. 120/143H, Ḥimṣ)
    │
Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ (d. ~158H, Ḥimṣ → al-Andalus → return)
    │                   └── case of ighrāb: ~30 years outside the centers
    │
ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ṣāliḥ (kātib al-Layth, Egypt)
    │                   └── controversial: infiltrated manuscripts?
    │
al-Bukhārī (taʿlīqāt ONLY — not in the body of the Ṣaḥīḥ)
```

Every link is problematic:
1. **ʿAlī → Ibn ʿAbbās**: break (*munqaṭiʿ*)
2. **Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ**: [[Concepts/Ighrāb|ighrāb]] — unverifiable material brought from al-Andalus
3. **ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ṣāliḥ**: accused of negligence, potentially altered manuscripts
4. **Al-Bukhārī**: uses the ṣaḥīfa, but refuses to certify it formally — taʿlīqāt only

## The content of the ṣaḥīfa — volume and nature

### Volume

The published ṣaḥīfa (ed. Rāshid al-Rijāl, Muʾassasat al-Kutub al-Thaqāfiyya, Beirut 1991) spans **560 pages**, with numbered entries exceeding **1,100**. In al-Ṭabarī's Tafsīr, approximately **1,000 texts** from Ibn ʿAbbās via this chain are compiled — roughly **one quarter** of all rivāyāt from Ibn ʿAbbās in al-Ṭabarī.

The typical chain in al-Ṭabarī is:

> حدثني المثنى قال حدثنا عبد الله بن صالح قال حدثني معاوية عن علي بن أبي طلحة عن ابن عباس

### An overwhelming tafsīr specialization

The data from the Maktaba al-Shāmila reveal a unique profile:

| Transmitter | Total references | Of which tafsīr | % tafsīr | Of which kutub al-sunna |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa** | 6,746 | **4,458** | **66%** | 588 |
| **Mujāhid** | 8,143 | 1,268 | 16% | 2,632 |
| **ʿIkrima** | 34,449 | 4,176 | 12% | 12,440 |
| **Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** | 22,972 | 4,167 | 18% | 8,732 |
| **al-Ḍaḥḥāk** | 4,372 | 2,408 | 55% | 392 |
| **Ṭāwūs** | 5,551 | 226 | 4% | 2,272 |

The contrast is striking:

1. **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa never met Ibn ʿAbbās** — yet he **surpasses** him in tafsīr references (4,458) compared to ʿIkrima (4,176) and Saʿīd ibn Jubayr (4,167), two direct students who attended him for years.
2. **66% of his transmissions are tafsīr** — he is an almost exclusively exegetical transmitter. ʿIkrima, who lived in Ibn ʿAbbās's house, is at 12% tafsīr — the rest is prophetic hadith, fiqh, historical narrative. A real student transmits **everything**; a ṣaḥīfa carrier transmits only the **document**.
3. **Mujāhid** — the actual intermediary — is at only **1,268** tafsīr references. If ʿAlī took everything from Mujāhid, how does he have **more than three times as much** tafsīr material as his source?

### Nature of content: a Quranic glossary

The content is essentially **lexical**: the explanation of rare Quranic terms (*gharīb al-Qurʾān*). Muḥammad Fuʾād ʿAbd al-Bāqī in fact extracted the ṣaḥīfa citations from al-Bukhārī under the revealing title: **Muʿjam gharīb al-Qurʾān mustakhraj min Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī** — a *dictionary*, not a narrative tafsīr.

This lexical profile is consistent with the concerns of **grammarians of the 2nd century hijrī** — the era of gharīb codification. The question arises whether the content reflects traditions from the 1st century or a **later systematization** retrospectively attributed to Ibn ʿAbbās.

### The Mujāhid paradox

This is the crux of the problem. If ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa took everything from Mujāhid (as stated by Duḥaym, Abū Ḥātim, al-Mizzī, and Ibn Ḥajar), then there should be strong correspondence between the two corpora. Yet:

- The published **Tafsīr Mujāhid** (recension Warqāʾ ← Ibn Abī Najīḥ ← Mujāhid) contains **few explicit references to Ibn ʿAbbās**. Mujāhid reports essentially his **own** interpretations — which is expected from an independent scholar who studied the Quran with his master then developed his own thought.
- The ṣaḥīfa of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa attributes **everything** to Ibn ʿAbbās — every entry is formulated "qāla Ibn ʿAbbās." If ʿAlī took from Mujāhid, why attribute to Ibn ʿAbbās what Mujāhid himself does not attribute to him?
- **Content divergences** are documented between the ṣaḥīfa and the interpretations of Mujāhid, ʿIkrima, and Saʿīd ibn Jubayr on certain verses — weakening the thesis of simple transmission via Mujāhid.

Apologists respond that he could have taken from Mujāhid **and** from Saʿīd ibn Jubayr simultaneously (Ibn Ḥajar's position). But this does not resolve the fundamental problem: why don't these intermediaries themselves transmit the same content under Ibn ʿAbbās's name?

### The nāsikh wa-l-mansūkh

Ibn Ḥibbān does not say that ʿAlī transmitted "the tafsīr" of Ibn ʿAbbās, but specifically **al-nāsikh wa-l-mansūkh** (the abrogating and the abrogated). This literary genre — treatises on Quranic abrogation — **proliferated in the 2nd century hijrī**, at a time when the need to systematize the apparent contradictions of the Quran became pressing. It is possible that the ṣaḥīfa was originally an abrogation treatise, gradually expanded into a general tafsīr and retrospectively attributed to Ibn ʿAbbās for authority.

## The man behind the ṣaḥīfa

Two elements of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa's profile deserve attention:

### 1. Mawlā of the ʿAbbāsids

He is **mawlā of al-ʿAbbās ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib** — a client of the family that would give its name to the ʿAbbāsid dynasty. His tafsīr is attributed to **Ibn ʿAbbās** — ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbbās ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, the Prophet's cousin and eponymous ancestor of the ʿAbbāsids. An ʿAbbāsid client carrying an ʿAbbāsid tafsīr: the coincidence deserves noting.

### 2. Kāna yarā al-sayf

Abū Dāwūd reports that he **advocated armed revolt** (*kāna yarā al-sayf* — "he saw the sword"). Al-Fasawī adds: **laysa bi-maḥmūd al-madhhab** ("his orientation is not praiseworthy"). This is not an apolitical scholar transmitting neutral material — this is a militant carrying an ideologically marked tafsīr.

## Synthesis: a tafsīr without foundation

The case of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa concentrates several anomalies:

1. **No meeting** with Ibn ʿAbbās — unanimous among critics
2. **The intermediary is known** (Mujāhid) but **omitted** from the chain
3. An **ʿAbbāsid mawlā** carrying a tafsīr attributed to the ʿAbbāsid founder
4. A man who **advocates armed revolt** — not a neutral transmitter
5. The ṣaḥīfa then passes through **two weak links**: [[Persons/Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ]] (ighrāb) and ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ṣāliḥ (compromised manuscripts)
6. Al-Bukhārī **uses** the content but **refuses** to formally certify it

Al-Fasawī sums it up perfectly: **laysa bi-matrūk, wa-lā huwa ḥujja** — "he is not abandoned, but he does not constitute proof." This is the status of the entire ṣaḥīfa: too important to ignore, too weak to guarantee.
