# Sufyān al-Thawrī — the imam on the run

**One of the greatest muḥaddithīn in the history of Islam.** Kūfian par excellence, part of the dissident lineage of [[Concepts/Kūfa vs. al-Shām|Kūfa vs. al-Shām]]. Founder of a school of fiqh now extinct. Amīr al-Muʾminīn fī l-Ḥadīth alongside [[Persons/Shu'ba ibn al-Ḥajjāj|Shuʿba]]. Author of the Jāmiʿ, among the earliest hadith compilations. Dies in hiding in Basra, fleeing caliph al-Mahdī who wants to appoint him qāḍī. Three tensions run through his file: (1) he is classified as **mudallis** but his tadlīs is considered acceptable, (2) he hides the name of [[Persons/Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā|Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]] — the same one hidden by [[Persons/Ibn Jurayj|Ibn Jurayj]] and al-Shāfiʿī, (3) al-Nasāʾī establishes that he did **not hear** from [[Persons/Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī|Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī]] — the same diagnosis as for Ibn Jurayj.

The consensus has no apparent fissure — but the classification as mudallis opens a breach that the critics chose not to explore.

## The tadlīs — "from a man"

Ibn Ḥajar places him in the **2nd ṭabaqa** of the Ṭabaqāt al-Mudallisīn (entry no. 51, [p. 29](https://shamela.ws/book/1186/29)):

> « سفيان بن سعيد الثوري، الإمام المشهور، الفقيه العابد الحافظ الكبير. وصفه النسائي وغيره بالتدليس. وقال البخاري : **ما أقل تدليسه** »
>
> "Sufyān ibn Saʿīd al-Thawrī, the famous imam, the devout faqīh, the great ḥāfiẓ. Al-Nasāʾī and others described him as mudallis. Al-Bukhārī said: **'How rare his tadlīs is!'**"

Al-Bukhārī's formula is an admission wrapped in a compliment: the tadlīs exists but it is "rare." The 2nd ṭabaqa is a **comfort** category: everything is accepted unless proven otherwise.

But the case of Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā is not benign.

### The third man — Ibrāhīm hidden

Three major scholars transmit from [[Persons/Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā|Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]] while concealing his identity:

| Scholar | Cover name |
|---------|------------|
| **[[Persons/Ibn Jurayj|Ibn Jurayj]]** | "Ibrāhīm ibn Abī ʿAṭāʾ" or "Abū al-Dhiʾb" |
| **Sufyān al-Thawrī** | **« عن رجل »** — "from a man" |
| **Al-Shāfiʿī** | « أخبرني من لا أتهم » — "one whom I do not suspect" |

Marwān ibn Muʿāwiya, a fourth, uses "ʿAbd al-Wahhāb."

When Sufyān says "ʿan rajul" — "from a man" — and that man is Ibrāhīm, he does exactly what Ibn Jurayj does with his pseudonyms: he transmits content he deems valuable from a man whose name is socially toxic. Ibn ʿAdī confirms this (al-Kāmil, [pp. 275-289](https://shamela.ws/book/12579/275)): after fifteen pages of analysis, no munkar is attributable to Ibrāhīm. One does not hide the name of a worthless fabricator — one hides the name of a man whose identity has become an obstacle. See [[Persons/Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā|Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]] for the full dossier.

### The Abū al-Zubayr channel — same diagnosis as Ibn Jurayj

Al-Nasāʾī, in al-Sunan al-Kubrā (Kitāb al-Sariqa, nos. 7461-7466, [Shamela](https://shamela.ws/book/8361)), examines a hadith transmitted by Sufyān via [[Persons/Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī|Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī]]. The diagnosis:

> **لم يسمعه سفيان من أبي الزبير**

> "Sufyān did **not hear** it from Abū al-Zubayr."

This is the **same diagnosis** as for [[Persons/Ibn Jurayj|Ibn Jurayj]] — who, for his part, goes through the forger **Yāsīn al-Zayyāt** (see [[Persons/Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī|Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī]]). Al-Nasāʾī does not specify the hidden intermediary for Sufyān, but the verdict is identical: the "samāʿ" is fictitious. Two of the greatest muḥaddithīn of their generation — al-Thawrī and Ibn Jurayj — claim to transmit directly from Abū al-Zubayr without having actually heard him.

## The flight — systematic refusal of power

Sufyān is the **fugitive** of the hadith heritage. Caliph al-Mahdī wants to appoint him qāḍī — he refuses and flees. His life is a succession of movements to escape summons from the authorities: Kūfa, Mecca, Medina, Yemen, and finally Basra, where he dies in hiding.

His condemnation is sweeping:

> « أفسد الناس صنفان : علماء السوء وعُبَّاد الجهل »
>
> "Two categories have corrupted the people: **evil scholars** and **ignorant devotees**."

And more specifically, on scholars who associate with power:

> « إذا رأيت العالم يلوذ بالسلطان فاعلم أنه لص »
>
> "If you see the scholar seeking refuge with power, know that he is a **thief**."

### The political spectrum

```
TOTAL REFUSAL ←───────────────────────────────────────→ EMBODIMENT OF POWER

Abū Ḥanīfa   SUFYĀN   Mālik         al-Aʿmash       Qatāda      al-Zuhrī      Anas
(prison)     (flight)  (torture)   (5 dinars/month)  (patronized) (coerced)  (governor)
```

Sufyān's position is radical even among the resisters: Abū Ḥanīfa remains in prison, Mālik endures torture — Sufyān **flees**. His refusal is not an act of frontal resistance but a systematic withdrawal from the political field. Al-Aʿmash, his own teacher, accepts a small salary from the bayt al-māl — Sufyān takes nothing.

The Thawrī madhhab goes extinct in the 4th century, for lack of political support. The four surviving schools all found a patron — his did not. The correlation between patronage and institutional survival is no coincidence.

## The Kūfa triangle — al-Aʿmash, Shuʿba, Sufyān

The three greatest muḥaddithīn of Kūfa/Basra in the 2nd century form a tight network:

```
                AL-AʿMASH (d. 148H, Kūfa)
              mudallis / ṣaḥīfa of Abū Ṣāliḥ
                /                    \
               /                      \
    SHUʿBA (d. 160H, Basra)    SUFYĀN AL-THAWRĪ (d. 161H, Basra)
    anti-tadlīs guarantor             mudallis / fugitive
    ~500 h. from al-Aʿmash           the most knowledgeable of his hadiths
              \                      /
               \                    /
        « Amīr al-Muʾminīn fī l-Ḥadīth »
              (shared title)
```

**Al-Aʿmash** is the common teacher — mudallis of the 2nd-3rd ṭabaqa, whose chain to [[Persons/Abū Hurayra|Abū Hurayra]] via [[Persons/Dhakwān Abū Ṣāliḥ al-Sammān|Dhakwān Abū Ṣāliḥ al-Sammān]] is the most frequent in the Ṣaḥīḥayn. Sufyān is declared "the most knowledgeable of his hadiths" (أعلم الناس بحديث الأعمش). Shuʿba claims to protect the corpora of the three major mudallis (including al-Aʿmash) — see [[Concepts/Kafaytukum tadlīs thalātha]].

The question the triangle poses: the three pillars of Kūfian transmission are **all** linked to tadlīs — al-Aʿmash practices it, Shuʿba claims to detect it (but fails — see his entry), and Sufyān also practices it (2nd ṭabaqa). The Kūfian corpus rests on a system where mudallis and verifier are the same people.

## The Thawrī family and the Ka'b chain

The al-Thawrī family is a **Kūfian transmission nucleus**. Sufyān's father and brother appear in the chain of the moral contestation of the [[Traditions/Assassination of Kaʿb ibn al-Ashraf|assassination of Kaʿb ibn al-Ashraf]]:

```
ʿAbāya ibn Rifāʿa ibn Rāfiʿ al-Zuraqī (tābiʿī, Medina) — thiqa
    │
Saʿīd ibn Masrūq al-Thawrī (d. ~126H, Kūfa) — thiqa (Sufyān's father)
    │
ʿUmar ibn Saʿīd al-Thawrī (Kūfa) — ثقة (Taqrīb no. 4906, Sufyān's brother)
    │
SUFYĀN IBN ʿUYAYNA (d. 198H) ◄── not Sufyān al-Thawrī, but the hadith circulates in the same milieu
```

The father Saʿīd ibn Masrūq is a Kūfian tābiʿī thiqa, a leading teacher. The fact that the Thawrī family transmits the narration of Ibn Yāmīn — where a Companion accuses the assassins of **ghadr** (treachery) — is not trivial: a family of independent muḥaddithīn preserves a narrative that the canonical collections do not include.

## The Jāmiʿ — the lost compilation

Sufyān authored two compilations:

| Work | Type | Survival |
|------|------|----------|
| **al-Jāmiʿ al-Kabīr** | Muṣannaf arranged by topics | Partially preserved in citations |
| **al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaghīr** | Abridgement | Fragmentary |

These compilations are among the **oldest** in hadith — alongside those of Ibn Jurayj and Mālik. But unlike Mālik's Muwaṭṭaʾ, Sufyān's Jāmiʿ did not benefit from a state's patronage to ensure its copying and dissemination. The Muwaṭṭaʾ survives because the Abbasid empire adopted it; the Jāmiʿ survives in fragments because its author fled that empire.

The fate of Sufyān's Jāmiʿ mirrors that of [[Persons/Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā|Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]]'s Muwaṭṭaʾ — which was "several times the size of Mālik's" and was **physically destroyed** (books torn up by Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād). The compilations that survive are not necessarily the best — they are the ones that found a patron.
