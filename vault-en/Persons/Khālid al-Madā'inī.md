# Khālid al-Madā'inī — the forger who corrupted al-Layth

**Khālid ibn al-Qāsim al-Madā'inī** (Abū al-Haytham) is a forger who systematically **interpolated the books of al-Layth ibn Sa'd** — the same al-Layth who is the **only** person to have verified the samā' of [[Persons/Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī]] from Jābir. The irony is structural: the verifier of the Jābir corpus is himself the victim of a forger.

## The mechanism of the fraud

Khālid al-Madā'inī practised a specific form of falsification: he took the **mursal isnāds** (disconnected chains) of al-Layth and transformed them into **musnad** (connected chains) by **inserting names** of reliable transmitters.

### The technique

1. **The original**: al-Layth transmits a mursal hadith (without mention of the Companion or the intermediate tābi'ī)
2. **The falsification**: Khālid inserts names like **Sālim** (ibn 'Abd Allāh ibn 'Umar), **'Urwa** (ibn al-Zubayr), **Nāfi'** — classic Medinan transmitters — to "connect" the chain
3. **The result**: the hadith appears muttaṣil (connected) and marfū' (attributed to the Prophet), whereas al-Layth's original was munqaṭi' (disconnected)

### The evidence — Tārīkh Baghdād

Al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī (Tārīkh Baghdād, [ed. 'Ilmiyya p. 3413](https://shamela.ws/book/23764/3413)):

> « خالد بن القاسم المدائني، **أجمع أهل الحديث على ترك حديثه، كان يعمد إلى الحديث المنقطع فيسنده** »
>
> "Khālid ibn al-Qāsim al-Madā'inī — the people of hadith are **unanimous in abandoning** his traditions. He **deliberately took disconnected (munqaṭi') hadiths and connected them (fa-yusniduh)**."

And via al-Bukhārī ([ed. Bashshār p. 5143](https://shamela.ws/book/736/5143)):

> « خالد بن القاسم أبو الهيثم المدائني **متروك**، تركه علي والناس »
>
> "Khālid ibn al-Qāsim Abū al-Haytham al-Madā'inī — **abandoned** (matrūk). 'Alī [ibn al-Madīnī] and everyone else abandoned him."

## The structural irony — al-Layth the verifier, victimised

Al-Layth ibn Sa'd is the **only transmitter** to have verified the contents of the books of [[Persons/Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī]]:

> "I went back and asked him [whether he had heard all of that from Jābir]. He said: **منه ما سمعت ومنه ما حدثت عنه**. I said: **mark for me what you heard**."

This sorting — samā' vs. non-samā' — is the **only guarantee** distinguishing reliable transmissions of Abū al-Zubayr from the rest. Yet al-Layth's own books were **interpolated by Khālid al-Madā'inī**.

The verification circuit:

```
Jābir ibn 'Abd Allāh
        │
   Abū al-Zubayr (partial samā' + ṣaḥīfa)
        │
   AL-LAYTH IBN SA'D
   (verifies: "mark what you heard")
        │
   AL-LAYTH'S BOOKS
        │
   KHĀLID AL-MADĀ'INĪ (inserts names, transforms mursal → musnad)
        │
   CORRUPTED BOOKS circulating under al-Layth's name
```

**The verifier is himself the victim of falsification.** The chain of trust is broken at two levels: Abū al-Zubayr mixes samā' and ṣaḥīfa, and al-Layth — the only one to have sorted them — sees his own books corrupted.

## The case in the history of falsification

Khālid al-Madā'inī is not an isolated case. The falsification of nusakh (copies) is a documented phenomenon. See [[Concepts/Falsification of nusakh]] for the parallel with Ma'mar's nephew and the books of 'Abd al-Razzāq.

## Relations

- **Falsifies the books of** al-Layth ibn Sa'd
- **Method**: insertion of names (Sālim, 'Urwa, Nāfi') into mursal isnāds
- **Consequence**: copies of al-Layth's books in circulation contain artificially "connected" chains
