# Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā al-Aslamī — the erased teacher

**The principal teacher of al-Shāfiʿī in Medina.** Author of a Muwaṭṭaʾ several times larger than Mālik's. Transmitter of **Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq**. Rejected by nearly all of the Sunni heritage: called a liar, a heretic, an insulter of the Companions. His books were torn up, his hadiths struck from notebooks. And yet: three great scholars — **al-Thawrī**, **Ibn Jurayj**, and **al-Shāfiʿī** — transmitted from him **while concealing his name**.

## The indictment — the most violent jarḥ in the heritage

No transmitter has been as extensively insulted in the biographical dictionaries. The accumulation of charges is **theologically incoherent** — one cannot simultaneously be qadarī, jahmī, muʿtazilī, and rāfiḍī — which reveals a rejection motivated by something other than critical rigor.

### Mālik ibn Anas

> « إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى كذاب »
>
> "Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā is a **liar**."

When Bishr ibn ʿUmar asks if it is because of the qadar, Mālik replies:

> « لا، ولا ثقة في دينه »
>
> "No, and he is not even reliable in his religion."

### Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Qaṭṭān

Testimony under oath:

> « أشهد على إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى أنه يكذب »
>
> "I **testify** that Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā lies."

And the crucial precision:

> « لم يُترك إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى للقدر، إنما تُرك للكذب »
>
> "Ibrāhīm was not abandoned for the qadar — he was abandoned **for lying**."

### Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn — the triple charge

> « كان فيه ثلاث خصال: كان كذّابًا، وكان قدريًّا، وكان رافضيًّا »
>
> "He had three defects: he was a **liar**, a **qadarī**, and a **rāfiḍī**."

And on his family — the contrast is stark:

> « سحبل وأنيس ومحمد وإبراهيم بنو أبي يحيى كلهم ثقات، إلا إبراهيم فإنه ليس بثقة »
>
> "The sons of Abū Yaḥyā are all reliable — **except Ibrāhīm**."

### Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal

> « قدريّ جهميّ، كلّ بلاء فيه »
>
> "Qadarī, jahmī — **every calamity is in him**."

### Al-Bukhārī

> « قدريّ جهميّ، تركه ابن المبارك والناس »
>
> "Qadarī, jahmī — Ibn al-Mubārak and the people **abandoned** him."

### Abū Dāwud — the sexual slur

> « كان قدريًّا رافضيًّا مأبونًا »

The term **maʾbūn** (مأبون) is an accusation of sexual passivity — this goes beyond methodological jarḥ into pure defamation.

### Yazīd ibn Zurayʿ — upon seeing him teach

> « لو ظهر لهم الشيطان لكتبوا عنه »
>
> "If the **Devil** appeared to them, they would write from him."

### Ibrāhīm ibn Saʿd — the nickname

> « كنّا نسمّي إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى — ونحن نطلب الحديث — خُرَافَة »
>
> "When we were seeking hadith, we called him **Khurāfa** (fairy tale)."

### Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal — the detail

Beyond "every calamity," Aḥmad specifies the mechanism:

> « كان يروي أحاديث منكرة ليس لها أصل، وكان يأخذ أحاديث الناس يضعها في كتبه »
>
> "He reported munkar hadiths with no basis, and he **took other people's hadiths and put them in his books**."

### Al-ʿIjlī — the paradox of memory

> « كان من أحفظ الناس … وهو غير ثقة »
>
> "He was among the people **with the best memory** ... and he is not reliable."

Al-ʿIjlī acknowledges his prodigious memory while rejecting him — an admission that the problem is not technical.

### ʿAbd al-Razzāq — the aborted debate

> « ناظرته فإذا هو معتزلي فلم أكتب عنه »
>
> "I debated him: he is a Muʿtazilī. I did not write from him."

### Abū Hammām al-Sakūnī — insulting the Companions

Beyond "يشتم بعض السلف," Abū Hammām reports an explicit statement:

> « غلامك خير من أبي بكر وعمر »
>
> "**Your slave** is worth more than Abū Bakr and ʿUmar."

If the report is authentic, this is pure rafḍ. But it is also the kind of statement one **attributes** to someone to justify their abandonment.

### Bishr ibn al-Mufaḍḍal — the survey of the jurists

> « سألت فقهاء أهل المدينة عن إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى فكلهم يقول كذاب »
>
> "I asked the jurists of Medina about Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā: **all** of them said liar."

## The destruction of the books

### Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād — purchase and destruction

Nuʿaym spends **fifty dinars** to buy his books, then discovers a work on qadar and another on Jahmism:

> « فقلت له: هذا رأيك؟ قال: نعم، فمزّقت بعض كتبه وطرحتها »
>
> "I said to him: is this your opinion? He said yes. So I **tore up** some of his books and threw them away."

### ʿĪsā ibn Yūnus — systematic erasure

When ʿĪsā found hadiths of Ibrāhīm in his notebooks:

> « خُطّ عليه! اضرب عليه! فإن سفيان بن عيينة نهاني أن أحدث عنه »
>
> "**Cross it out! Strike it out!** Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna forbade me to transmit from him."

### Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna — the public prohibition

> « احذروا إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى، لا تُجالسوه »
>
> "Beware of Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā, **do not sit with him**."

## The defense — those who resisted

### Al-Shāfiʿī — the only great name

Al-Shāfiʿī acknowledges that Ibrāhīm is a qadarī, but declares him reliable in hadith (al-Rabīʿ ibn Sulaymān reports):

> « لأن يخرّ إبراهيم من بُعد أحبّ إليه من أن يكذب، وكان ثقة في الحديث »
>
> "That Ibrāhīm should fall from a height would be **dearer** to him than to lie. He was **thiqa** in hadith."

Al-Shāfiʿī goes further — he judges him superior to an accepted transmitter:

> « ابن أبي يحيى أحفظ من الدراوردي »
>
> "Ibn Abī Yaḥyā has a **better memory** than al-Darāwardī."

Al-Dhahabī notes however the nuance:

> « فتجد الشافعي لا يوثّقه، وإنما هو عنده ليس بمتّهم بالكذب »
>
> "Al-Shāfiʿī does not declare him reliable [in the strong sense] — he only says he does not suspect him of lying."

And the mechanism explained by Ibn Ḥibbān:

> « كان الشافعي يجالس ابن أبي يحيى في حداثته، ويحفظ عنه حفظ الصبي، فلما دخل مصر في آخر عمره، أخذ يصنف، واحتاج إلى الأخبار، ولم تكن معه كتبه، فأكثر ما أودع الكتب من حفظه، وربما كنّى عن إبراهيم، ولا يسمّيه »
>
> "Al-Shāfiʿī frequented Ibn Abī Yaḥyā in his youth and memorized from him with a **child's memory**. When he entered Egypt at the end of his life, he began to compile and needed traditions, but he did not have his books. Most of what he set down came from memory, and sometimes he gave a kunya to Ibrāhīm **without naming him**."

#### The fa's anecdote — the paradoxical defense

Al-Shāfiʿī himself recounts (via Ibn Khuzayma ← Ibn ʿAbd al-Ḥakam, Siyar Risāla ed.):

> « كان ابن أبي يحيى أحمق — أو قال: أبله — كان لا يمكنه الجماع، فأخبرني من رآه، معه فأس، فقال: بلغني أنه من بال في ثقب فأس، أمكنه الجماع، فدخل خُربة، فبال في الفأس »
>
> "Ibn Abī Yaḥyā was a **fool** — or he said: a simpleton. He could not have intercourse. Someone told me they saw him carrying an **axe**. He said: 'I was told that whoever urinates in the hole of an axe will be able to have intercourse.' So he entered a ruin and urinated in the axe."

The anecdote seems to sabotage the defense. But in the logic of jarḥ, it **reinforces** it: a man so naive is incapable of **fabricating** hadiths. Al-Shāfiʿī pleads innocence through stupidity.

### Ismāʿīl ibn ʿĪsā al-ʿAbbāsī

> « وكان من أورع من رأيت »
>
> "He was one of the **most pious** men I have ever seen."

### Ibn ʿAdī — the empirical examination (al-Kāmil, [pp. 275-289](https://shamela.ws/book/12579/275))

The most detailed entry in the entire heritage. Ibn ʿAdī methodically examines Ibrāhīm's hadiths one by one — via Ibn Jurayj, al-Thawrī, ʿAbbād ibn Manṣūr, Mundal, Yaḥyā ibn Ayyūb. The final verdict, after fifteen pages of analysis:

> « نظرت في أحاديثه وتبحّرتها وفتّشت الكل منها فليس فيها حديث منكر، وإنما يروي المنكر إذا كان العهدة من قبل الراوي عنه، أو من قبل من يروي إبراهيم عنه، وكأنه أتى من قبل شيخه لا من قبله، وهو في جملة من يُكتب حديثه »
>
> "I examined his hadiths, **explored them in depth** and **scrutinized each one of them**: there is no munkar among them. When munkar is found, the responsibility lies with the transmitter downstream or the teacher upstream — the problem comes from his shaykh, **not from him**. He is in the category of **those whose hadith can be written**."

And the conclusion:

> « وقد وثّقه الشافعي وابن الأصبهاني وغيرهما »
>
> "Al-Shāfiʿī declared him reliable, as did Ibn al-Aṣbahānī and others."

### Ibn ʿUqda

> « نظرت في حديث إبراهيم كثيرا، وليس بمنكر الحديث »
>
> "I examined his hadiths at length: **he does not transmit munkar**."

### Ḥamdān ibn al-Aṣbahānī

When asked if he accepts Ibrāhīm's hadiths as religious proof:

> « أتدين بحديث إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى؟ قال: نعم »
>
> "Do you accept his hadiths for your religious practice? — **Yes**."

## The triple tadlīs — three scholars who conceal his name

A remarkable fact: three major transmitters report from Ibrāhīm while **concealing his identity** — proof that they judged his traditions valuable but that his name had become socially toxic.

| Scholar | Cover name used |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Ibn Jurayj** | "Ibrāhīm ibn Muḥammad ibn Abī ʿAṭāʾ" or "Abū al-Dhiʾb" |
| **Al-Shāfiʿī** | « أخبرني من لا أتهم » — "the one I do not suspect informed me" |
| **Al-Thawrī** | « عن رجل » — "from a man" |
| **Marwān ibn Muʿāwiya** | "ʿAbd al-Wahhāb" |

Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn unmasks the device: « إبراهيم بن أبي عطاء، هو إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى » — the two names designate the same man.

Al-Rabīʿ ibn Sulaymān confirms the key to reading the entire Shāfiʿī corpus (Sharḥ Musnad, [p. 981](https://shamela.ws/book/13241/981)):

> « كان الشافعي إذا قال أخبرني من لا أتهم يريد به إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى، وإذا قال أخبرني الثقة يريد به يحيى بن حسّان »
>
> "When al-Shāfiʿī says 'the one I do not suspect' = **Ibrāhīm**. When he says 'the reliable one' = **Yaḥyā ibn Ḥassān**."

Tadlīs is usually a vice in the transmitter. Here, it is the **receiver** who practices tadlīs — not to conceal a weak link, but to protect a link that is **politically unacceptable**. The tradition has a term for concealing a weak transmitter (tadlīs al-taswiya), but no term for concealing a transmitter one judges reliable but whom the community rejects.

## The janāba case and the Rishdīn scene

Rishdīn ibn Saʿd, himself contested, comes with Ibrāhīm's books and asks if he can transmit them from him. Ibrāhīm accepts. Then Rishdīn attacks:

> « بلغني أنك رجل سوء فاتّق الله عزّ وجلّ وتُب إليه »
>
> "I've been told you are an **evil man** — fear God and repent to Him."

Ibrāhīm logically replies: if I am evil, why do you take my hadiths? Rishdīn:

> « ألم يبلغك أنه يذهب العلم ويبقى منه في أوعية سوء؟ فأنت من الأوعية السوء! »
>
> "Knowledge disappears and subsists only in **evil vessels** — you are one of those evil vessels!"

The formula is brutal but revealing: even those who insult him **take his hadiths**. The content is worth something — it is the man who is rejected.

## What al-Shāfiʿī transmits from him — the assessment

At least **18 traditions** identified in the Musnad al-Shāfiʿī (~9) and al-Umm (~9), some of which deal with questions of **obligatory fiqh** — not just the faḍāʾil:

| Subject | Via | Named or pseudonym |
|-------|-----|---------------------|
| Hadith of ʿUkl — no saml (mutilation) | Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq ← al-Bāqir | **Named** |
| Ḥadd for intoxicating wine/nabīdh | Jaʿfar ← his father ← ʿAlī | **Named** |
| Liability of artisans | Jaʿfar ← his father | **Named** |
| Carrying weapons in iḥrām (ʿumrat al-qaḍiyya) | ʿAbd Allāh ibn Abī Bakr | **Named** |
| Istisqāʾ prayer | Ṣāliḥ mawlā al-Tawʾama ← Ibn ʿAbbās | Pseudonym |
| Al-kharāj bi-l-ḍamān (warranty of defects) | Ibn Abī Dhiʾb ← Makhlad | Pseudonym |
| The divorced woman (al-mabūtta) | ʿAmr ibn Maymūn ← his father | **Named** |

The traditions via Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq — all named — deal with criminal law and punishments. Al-Shāfiʿī does not hesitate to name Ibrāhīm when the chain passes through Ahl al-Bayt.

**Isḥāq ibn Rāhawayh**, astonished, challenges al-Shāfiʿī:

> « وفي الدنيا أحد يحتج بإبراهيم بن أبي يحيى؟ »
>
> "Is there **anyone in the world** who uses Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā as proof?"

## Ibrāhīm and Ahl al-Bayt — the real reason?

Ibrāhīm is a direct transmitter of **Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq** (d. 148 H) and **Muḥammad al-Bāqir**. He figures among the **rijāl al-Ṣādiq** in Shia sources (al-Ṭūsī). Through him passes the chain:

```
Al-Shāfiʿī ← Ibrāhīm ← Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq ← Muḥammad al-Bāqir ← ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn
```

This chain transmits notably the **rejection of the hadith of ʿUkl** by ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn (see [[Traditions/Hadith de ʿUkl-ʿUraniyya]]) — an Imam of Ahl al-Bayt who categorically denies a hadith of Anas canonized by Bukhārī.

The accusation of **rafḍ** (Shiism) that recurs in Ibn Maʿīn, al-ʿIjlī, and Abū Dāwud is directly linked to this association with the ʿAlid Imams. Abū Hammām al-Sakūnī reports:

> « سمعت إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى يشتم بعض السلف »
>
> "I heard Ibrāhīm insulting certain predecessors."

The accumulation **qadarī + jahmī + rāfiḍī** is theologically contradictory. But it is politically coherent: each label is a ground for rejection in a different camp, and the ensemble guarantees **unanimous** rejection.

## The structural paradox

The Sunni tradition:
1. **Canonizes al-Shāfiʿī** as the founder of one of the four schools
2. **Rejects his principal teacher** as a liar, heretic, and deviant
3. Al-Shāfiʿī learned from Ibrāhīm in his youth in Medina and transmitted **from memory** in Egypt at the end of his life (Ibn Ḥibbān)
4. An unknown portion of Shāfiʿī fiqh rests on traditions that passed through a man the tradition considers a fabricator

Al-Bazzār summarizes the tension — and deepens it:

> « كان يضع الحديث وكان يوضع له مسائل فيضع لها إسنادًا وكان قدريًّا وهو من أستاذي الشافعي، وعزّ علينا »
>
> "He **fabricated** hadiths; questions were submitted to him and he would fabricate isnāds for them. He was a qadarī. He is one of al-Shāfiʿī's teachers — and **this weighs on us**."

Al-Sājī attempts to minimize by saying that al-Shāfiʿī only transmitted from him in the faḍāʾil (merits). Ibn Ḥajar dryly refutes:

> « هذا خلاف الموجود المشهود »
>
> "That is **contrary** to what is found in the facts."

Al-Dhahabī, for his part, remains open:

> « لا يُرتاب في ضعفه. بقي: هل يُترك أم لا؟ »
>
> "There is no doubt about his weakness. The question remains: **should he be abandoned [completely] or not?**"

And he notes that Ibrāhīm's hadiths still reach him — « يقع لي حديثه في مسند الشافعي » — they circulate, integrated into the Shāfiʿī corpus, despite the official rejection of their transmitter.

## Relationships

- **Teacher of** al-Shāfiʿī (~18 identified traditions) — his greatest student, who defends him alone against everyone
- **Student of** Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, Muḥammad al-Bāqir, al-Zuhrī, Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Anṣārī, Muḥammad ibn al-Munkadir, ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ
- **Also transmitted from (under tadlīs) by** Ibn Jurayj ("Ibn Abī ʿAṭāʾ"), al-Thawrī ("a man"), Marwān ibn Muʿāwiya ("ʿAbd al-Wahhāb")
- **Rejected by** Mālik, Yaḥyā al-Qaṭṭān, Ibn Maʿīn, Aḥmad, al-Bukhārī, al-Nasāʾī, Abū Dāwud, Abū Ḥātim, Abū Zurʿa, al-Dāraquṭnī, Ibn Ḥibbān, al-Bazzār
- **Defended by** al-Shāfiʿī ("thiqa fī l-ḥadīth"), Ibn ʿAdī (empirical examination over 15 pages), Ibn ʿUqda, Ḥamdān ibn al-Aṣbahānī, Ismāʿīl al-ʿAbbāsī ("the most pious")
- **Books destroyed by** Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād (torn up), ʿĪsā ibn Yūnus (struck out on Ibn ʿUyayna's orders)
- **Contemporary of** Mālik ibn Anas (d. 179 H), whose Muwaṭṭaʾ survived — Ibrāhīm's did not

Four major scholars transmit from him while concealing his name — one does not conceal the name of a fabricator; one conceals the name of a man whose content is precious but whose identity is toxic.
>
> The accumulation qadarī + jahmī + muʿtazilī + rāfiḍī is theologically impossible (qadar is the opposite of Jahmite jabr). This is not a diagnosis: it is a lockout — each label closes a door in a different camp.
>
> But — and this is important — one should not make it into a conspiracy. Mālik is not an agent of the regime. He is a man who was tortured for a rebellious fatwa. His rejection of Ibrāhīm is probably sincere: local rivalry between two Medinan Muwaṭṭaʾs, theological rejection of qadar, perhaps personal contempt. The real mechanism is not conspiracy, it is the **cascade**: a "kadhdhāb" from Mālik in Medina propagates to Iraq, radicalizes in Yaḥyā al-Qaṭṭān (oath), gets loaded with labels in Ibn Maʿīn (triple charge), hardens in Aḥmad ("every calamity"), transforms into physical destruction in Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād (book tearing), and ends in Abū Dāwud as a sexual slur (maʾbūn). Each picks up from the previous and adds more. Nobody re-examines the hadiths.
>
> What was lost in the cascade: an alternative Medinan Muwaṭṭaʾ several times the size of Mālik's, and the bridge between nascent Sunnism and the legal tradition of Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq. Jarḥ wa-l-taʿdīl is a system where the verdict of one critic is transmitted like a hadith, strengthened with each repetition, and ultimately crushes empirical examination under the weight of accumulated authority. Ibn ʿAdī has the evidence. But he has twenty names against him. The names win.
