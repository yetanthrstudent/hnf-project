# ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz — the pious Umayyad

The "fifth rightly-guided caliph." The one the Sunni tradition contrasts with the bad Umayyads — Yazīd, al-Ḥajjāj, al-Walīd — as proof that the dynasty could produce a just man. But ʿUmar II is not an accident of the system — he **is** the system. Grandson of Marwān ibn al-Ḥakam, nephew of ʿAbd al-Malik, governor appointed by al-Walīd, installed as caliph by a palace coup. His reforms die with him. His own family kills him.

---

## Genealogy — a thoroughbred Umayyad

```
Marwān ibn al-Ḥakam (caliph)
    │
    ├── ʿAbd al-Malik (caliph) ──→ al-Walīd, Sulaymān, Yazīd II, Hishām
    │
    └── ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz (governor of Egypt, 20 years)
            │
            └── ʿUMAR ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz
                    (mother: Umm ʿĀṣim bint ʿĀṣim ibn ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb)
```

The mother is the **label**: Umm ʿĀṣim descends from ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb — it is this maternal lineage that founds the legend of the "second ʿUmar." But the father, grandfather, and uncles are Umayyads of the Marwānid branch — the one that seized power through civil war, executed ʿAbd Allāh ibn al-Zubayr, and entrusted Iraq to al-Ḥajjāj.

---

## Governor of Medina (87-93H) — the laboratory

Al-Walīd ibn ʿAbd al-Malik appoints him governor of Medina and the Ḥijāz. ʿUmar establishes a **council of ten fuqahāʾ** — among them [[Persons/ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr|ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr]], al-Qāsim ibn Muḥammad, Sālim ibn ʿAbd Allāh, Khārija ibn Zayd — and declares:

> **إِنِّي لَا أُرِيدُ أَنْ أَقْطَعَ أَمْراً إِلَّا بِرَأْيِكُمْ أَوْ بِرَأْيِ مَنْ حَضَرَ مِنْكُمْ**

> "I do not wish to settle any matter without your counsel, or that of whichever of you is present."

The gesture is political as much as pious: an Umayyad governor appointed by Damascus associates the local scholarly elites with power by offering them an advisory role. The mechanism creates a **mutual dependency**: the fuqahāʾ gain influence, the governor gains legitimacy.

### The prohibition of ṭilāʾ — proof by absence

Ibn Sīrīn, asked about ṭilāʾ (Siyar, [p. 3902](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3902)):

> **نَهَى عَنْهُ إِمَامُ هُدًى — يَعْنِي عُمَرَ بْنَ عَبْدِ العَزِيزِ**

> "An imam of guidance prohibited it — meaning ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz."

The implication is devastating for the narrative of an absolute alcohol prohibition: **before ʿUmar, ṭilāʾ (grape must cooked down by two-thirds) was not prohibited in the Shām**. His own great-uncle ʿAbd al-Malik ibn Marwān drank it openly — and when Umm al-Dardāʾ reproaches him, he replies: "**Yes, by God — and blood too**" (see [[Concepts/Nabīdh, ṭilā', khamr]]).

The primary sources show that the prohibition is linked to a **devastating earthquake in Syria**. Ibn Abī Shayba (hadith 8328, [p. 9252](https://shamela.ws/book/9944/9252)), via Wakīʿ ← Jaʿfar ibn Burqān:

> كَتَبَ إِلَيْنَا عُمَرُ بْنُ عَبْدِ العَزِيزِ **فِي زَلْزَلَةٍ كَانَتْ بِالشَّامِ**: أَنِ اخْرُجُوا يَوْمَ الِاثْنَيْنِ مِنْ شَهْرِ كَذَا وَكَذَا، وَمَنِ اسْتَطَاعَ مِنْكُمْ أَنْ يُخْرِجَ صَدَقَةً فَلْيَفْعَلْ

> "ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz wrote to us **on the occasion of an earthquake in Syria**: 'Go out on Monday of such-and-such month. Let whoever among you can give a charitable offering do so.'"

Al-Suyūṭī (Tārīkh al-Khulafāʾ) dates the earthquake to **97H** — "a great tremor struck Egypt, the Shām, and the Jazīra, destroying many places and fortresses, and **swallowing a village in the district of Buṣrā**." Theophanes the Confessor (Chronographia, AM 6210 = 717-18) explicitly links the earthquake to ʿUmar's prohibition of wine.

The rescript prohibiting ṭilāʾ is preserved in al-Nasāʾī (Sunan al-Kubrā, hadith 5090, [p. 7115](https://shamela.ws/book/8361/7115)), via Ibn al-Mubārak ← ʿAbd al-Malik ibn al-Ṭufayl al-Jazarī:

> كَتَبَ إِلَيْنَا عُمَرُ بْنُ عَبْدِ العَزِيزِ: **أَلَا تَشْرَبُوا مِنَ الطِّلَاءِ حَتَّى يَذْهَبَ ثُلُثَاهُ، وَيَبْقَى ثُلُثُهُ وَكُلُّ مُسْكِرٍ حَرَامٌ**

> "ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz wrote to us: **Do not drink ṭilāʾ until two-thirds have evaporated and only one-third remains. And every intoxicant is ḥarām.**"

The formula is revealing: ʿUmar does not say "ṭilāʾ is khamr" — he says "do not drink it before cooking to two-thirds." He **regulates** more than he prohibits. And even this regulation is not consensual: **Sālim ibn ʿAbd Allāh** (son of the Companion Ibn ʿUmar) drinks ṭilāʾ in the Shām in ʿUmar II's presence, and when the latter expresses surprise, Sālim replies (Muwaṭṭaʾ Ibn Wahb, [p. 142](https://shamela.ws/book/916/142)):

> **نَعَمْ، قَدْ كَانَ أَبِي يَشْرَبُهُ**

> "**Yes, my father used to drink it.**"

The son of Ibn ʿUmar invokes the Sunna of his father — a major Companion — against the caliph's rescript. The prohibition is a **circumstantial eschatological act** (fear of divine punishment after the earthquake), not the application of an obvious Quranic norm.

*(Harvey, "ʿUmar II and the Prohibition of Ṭilāʾ and Nabīdh", Islamic Law and Society 30/4, 2023, DOI: 10.1163/15685195-bja10041 — academic synthesis outside the heritage, useful for context.)*

### Dismissal

Al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf pressures al-Walīd to dismiss him — ʿUmar had been sheltering refugees fleeing Iraq. He leaves Medina in tears.

## Accession to the caliphate — a palace coup

Caliph **Sulaymān ibn ʿAbd al-Malik** is dying at Dābiq (Ṣafar 99H). His designated son Ayyūb is already dead. **[[Persons/Rajā' ibn Ḥaywa|Rajāʾ ibn Ḥaywa]]** — the religious advisor — convinces Sulaymān to designate ʿUmar in a sealed testament. Rajāʾ summons the Umayyad princes, makes them swear allegiance to a document whose content is **secret**. Only after the bayʿa is ʿUmar's name revealed. **Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik** protests — he is threatened with violence.

The "pious caliph" is installed through a **stratagem** that violates ʿAbd al-Malik's will (succession reserved for his direct sons) and relies on a **blind** oath from the princes.

## The quṣṣāṣ — paid preachers

ʿUmar does not abolish the [[Concepts/Quṣṣāṣ|quṣṣāṣ]] — he **institutionalizes and pays them**: **2 dinars per month** (Tārīkh al-Madīna, p. 26). Under Hishām, the pay drops to 6 dinars per year.

The quṣṣāṣ are the official preaching agents of the caliphate — the very ones that classical criticism (Ibn al-Jawzī, *Kitāb al-Quṣṣāṣ*) considers a source of hadith corruption and fabrication of [[Concepts/Isrā'īliyyāt|isrāʾīliyyāt]]. The pious caliph finances them. The qaṣṣ is an Umayyad state office: the quṣṣāṣ preach in congregational mosques with the governor's authorization, and their pay appears in the fiscal registers.

## The codification of hadith — a state project

The letter to **Abū Bakr ibn Ḥazm** (governor of Medina) is the founding document of official hadith codification:

> **انْظُرْ مَا كَانَ مِنْ حَدِيثِ رَسُولِ اللهِ ﷺ فَاكْتُبْهُ، فَإِنِّي خِفْتُ دُرُوسَ العِلْمِ وَذَهَابَ العُلَمَاءِ، وَلَا تَقْبَلْ إِلَّا حَدِيثَ النَّبِيِّ ﷺ، وَلْتُفْشُوا العِلْمَ**

> "Seek out whatever exists of the hadith of the Messenger of God and write it down, for I fear the disappearance of knowledge and the death of scholars. Accept only the hadith of the Prophet. Spread knowledge."

— Al-Bukhārī, Kitāb al-ʿIlm, bāb "kayfa yuqbaḍ al-ʿilm"

[[Persons/al-Zuhrī|Al-Zuhrī]] carries out the order:

> **أَمَرَنَا عُمَرُ بْنُ عَبْدِ العَزِيزِ بِجَمْعِ السُّنَّةِ فَكَتَبْنَاهَا دَفْتَراً دَفْتَراً، فَبَعَثَ إِلَى كُلِّ أَرْضٍ لَهُ عَلَيْهَا سُلْطَانٌ دَفْتَراً**

> "ʿUmar ordered us to gather the Sunna. We wrote it notebook by notebook, and he sent a notebook **to every territory under his authority**."

The caliph decides **when**, **how**, and **by whom** hadith will be written. The copies are sent to the provinces as official documents — hadith enters the **dīwān** (the administrative machinery). The initiative comes not from the ʿulamāʾ — it comes from the authorities. The "science of hadith" is born from an **order of the sultanate**.

As the [[Persons/al-Zuhrī|al-Zuhrī]] entry shows: a few years later, **Hishām** forces al-Zuhrī to teach his sons, effectively confiscating his material. Codification is a continuum of **state control**, from ʿUmar II to Hishām.

## Ghaylān al-Dimashqī — the embarrassing collaborator

The relationship with **Ghaylān** (the Qadarite — see [[Concepts/Qadar]]) is the most awkward for the hagiographic narrative.

ʿUmar does not merely tolerate Ghaylān — he **appoints him to oversee the sale of confiscated Umayyad princely property**. Ghaylān cries out in the markets of Damascus:

> **هَلُمُّوا إِلَى مَتَاعِ الظَّلَمَةِ، هَلُمُّوا إِلَى مَتَاعِ الخَوَنَةِ**

> "Come get the goods of the **tyrants**! Come get the goods of the **traitors**!"

The "fifth rightly-guided caliph" entrusts a major political mission to the founder of the Qadarite "heresy." Van Ess (*IJMES*) has documented how later biographers systematically obscured this link.

**Why ʿUmar tolerates the qadar**: the doctrine of free will (qadar) serves his anti-Umayyad reform policy. If men are responsible for their actions, corrupt caliphs are **guilty** — not instruments of divine will. Jabr protects the Umayyads; qadar threatens them. ʿUmar needs qadar to delegitimize his predecessors.

And yet, the same ʿUmar **uses qadar as an excuse** when it suits him. Letter to ʿAdī ibn Arṭāʾa (Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, [p. 744](https://shamela.ws/book/20879/744)):

> **فَإِنَّ اسْتِعْمَالَكَ سَعْدَ بْنَ مَسْعُودٍ عَلَى عُمَانَ مِنَ الخَطَايَا الَّتِي قَدَّرَ اللهُ عَزَّ وَجَلَّ عَلَيْكَ**

> "Your appointment of Saʿd ibn Masʿūd as governor of Oman is among **the sins that God decreed for you**."

The "reformist" caliph excuses a governor's crimes through predestination — exactly the jabriyya logic he is supposed to be fighting.

And this is no obscure official. ʿAdī ibn Arṭāʾa al-Fazārī is a Damascene, former chief of the shurṭa under al-Walīd, whom ʿUmar II **himself** appointed governor of Basra in 99H. The sources document his abuses — Ibn Sīrīn interprets his dream as **illicit tax collection** (Dimashq [p. 17985](https://shamela.ws/book/71/17985): "you have exceeded what God allowed you toward what He forbade you"). ʿUmar II eventually denounces him in the famous **black turban letter** (Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq via Maʿmar, [p. 5387](https://shamela.ws/book/84/5387); Dimashq [p. 17986](https://shamela.ws/book/71/17986)):

> إِنَّكَ **غَرَرْتَنِي بِعِمَامَتِكَ السَّوْدَاءِ** وَمُجَالَسَتِكَ القُرَّاءَ [...] وَقَدْ **أَظْهَرَنَا اللهُ عَلَى كَثِيرٍ مِمَّا كُنْتُمْ تَكْتُمُونَ**

> "You **deceived me with your black turban** and your association with the reciters [...] God has **revealed to us much of what you were concealing**."

The paradox is complete: in the same correspondence, ʿUmar II (a) denounces ʿAdī as an impostor, and (b) excuses his appointment of a corrupt governor through divine decree. And ʿAdī himself openly contests the caliph's reforms: « **يرد أعمال الخلفاء قبله ويسميها المظالم** » — "he reverses the decisions of the caliphs before him and calls them *injustices*" (Dimashq [p. 17987](https://shamela.ws/book/71/17987)). The qadar letter is attested by two independent chains: Maʿmar (ʿAbd al-Razzāq / Sunna of ʿAbd Allāh, [p. 744](https://shamela.ws/book/20879/744)) and al-Awzāʿī (Dimashq [p. 9195](https://shamela.ws/book/71/9195)). ʿAdī is executed in 102H by Muʿāwiya ibn Yazīd ibn al-Muhallab (Dimashq [p. 17988](https://shamela.ws/book/71/17988)).

## The reforms — and their fragility

| Reform | Impact | Survival after his death |
|--------|--------|--------------------------|
| Dismissal of corrupt governors | Immediate | **Annulled** — Hishām reappoints the old ones |
| Tax equality Arabs/mawālī | Significant | **Annulled** — return to discriminatory jizya |
| Restitution of maẓālim (confiscated property) | Spectacular | **Annulled** — the princes recover their property |
| Removal of the cursing of ʿAlī from the sermon | Symbolic | **Maintained** (only lasting reform) |
| Codification of hadith | Fundamental | **Continued** — but diverted by Hishām |
| Funding of the quṣṣāṣ | Structural | **Continued** (reduced by Hishām) |

None of the substantive reforms survives ʿUmar. They depended entirely on the **personal will of the caliph**, without institutional foundations. The Umayyad system absorbs the reform, eliminates the reformer, and resumes its course.

## Death — poisoned by his own

The sources converge: a servant is bribed with **1,000 dinars and the promise of manumission** to pour poison into the caliph's drink. ʿUmar agonizes for **20 days**. He dies in **Rajab 101H** (February 720), at ~40 years old. Buried at **Dayr Simʿān** (Syria). **Maslama ibn ʿAbd al-Malik** — one of the princes most aggrieved by the confiscations — leads his funeral prayer.

Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal summarizes: **كَانَ أَشَدَّ عَلَى بَنِي أُمَيَّةَ** — "He was the harshest on the Banū Umayya."

The chronology speaks: ʿUmar restores the maẓālim, the princes lose their property, ʿUmar dies 29 months after his accession. Hishām annuls the restitutions. None of his fiscal reforms survives him — only the removal of the cursing of ʿAlī from the sermon is maintained.

## What ʿUmar II reveals about the system

### 1. Ṭilāʾ was not prohibited

If the "pious caliph" must prohibit ṭilāʾ by rescript after an earthquake, then **the norm of total alcohol prohibition did not exist** in the first century. The consensus that "every alcoholic drink is ḥarām" is a **progressive construction** — not a Quranic given. See [[Concepts/Nabīdh, ṭilā', khamr]].

### 2. The codification of hadith is an act of power

Hadith does not arise from a scholarly initiative — it arises from a **caliphal order**. The authorities decide what will be written, by whom, and how it will be disseminated. The "science of hadith" is from its inception **dependent on the sultanate**.

### 3. The quṣṣāṣ are officials

The preacher-storytellers are not marginals — they are **paid from the public treasury**. The same state that produces hadith produces the qaṣṣ. The two streams — codified hadith and oral qaṣṣ — coexist and feed each other.

### 4. Reforms without structures are parentheses

The ʿUmar II experiment shows that a virtuous individual in a vicious system does not change the system — he is **absorbed or eliminated**. Reforms die with the reformer. The system perpetuates itself through its structures, not its individuals.

### 5. "Consensus" is produced by power

The removal of the cursing of ʿAlī — the only lasting reform — shows that Sunni "consensus" is not a free intellectual agreement: it is the result of **successive caliphal decisions**. Muʿāwiya imposes it, ʿUmar II removes it, the Abbasids ratify the removal. Each stage of "consensus" is an **act of power**.
