# Ibn Jurayj — the seal that hides the forgers

First author of **muṣannafāt** (works classified by subject) in the history of hadith. Unanimously recognized as thiqa when he says "akhbaranī" or "sami'tu," but his tadlīs is the most dangerous in the corpus: **he only conceals discredited sources** (majrūḥīn). When he says "qāla" or "'an," expect the worst.

## The "horrible" tadlīs — Dāraquṭnī

Al-Ḥākim asks Dāraquṭnī his opinion on Ibn Jurayj's tadlīs (Suʾālāt al-Ḥākim, p. 174):

> « يتجنب تدليسه، فإنه وحش التدليس، لا يدلس إلا فيما سمعه من مجروح، مثل إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى، وموسى بن عبيدة، وغيرهما »
>
> "**Avoid** his tadlīs, for it is a **horrible tadlīs** (waḥsh). He only practices tadlīs **for what he heard from discredited transmitters** (majrūḥ) — like [[Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]], Mūsā ibn 'Ubayda, and others."

This is the most dangerous form of tadlīs according to Dāraquṭnī: the ordinary mudallis conceals a weak link. Ibn Jurayj conceals transmitters that **the majority declares discredited** (majrūḥīn). His silence is not a gap — it is a **signal**.

## Aḥmad's rule

Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal formulates the binary rule (Tārīkh Baghdād 10/405; Tahdhīb al-Kamāl 18/348):

> « إذا قال ابن جريج: قال فلان وقال فلان وأخبرت — جاء بمناكير. فإذا قال أخبرني وسمعت فحسبك به »
>
> "When Ibn Jurayj says 'so-and-so said' or 'I was told' — he produces **munkarāt**. But when he says 'he informed me' (akhbaranī) or 'I heard' (sami'tu) — he suffices."

Al-Maymūnī reports from Aḥmad the more radical variant:

> « إذا قال ابن جريج: قال — فاحذروه »
>
> "When Ibn Jurayj says 'qāla' — **beware**."

## The three hidden sources

Ibn Jurayj's tadlīs targets three principal channels:

### 1. [[Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]] — the toxic name

'Alī ibn al-Madīnī (via al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī, al-Kifāya p. 511):

> « كل ما في كتب ابن جريج "أخبرت عن داود بن حصين" و"أخبرت عن صالح مولى التوأمة" — فهو من كتب إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى »
>
> "Everything in the books of Ibn Jurayj under 'I was told from Dāwūd ibn Ḥuṣayn' and 'I was told from Ṣāliḥ mawlā al-Taw'ama' — it comes from the **books of Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā**."

Ibn Jurayj does not merely conceal Ibrāhīm: he invents **pseudonyms** for him. Yaḥyā ibn Ma'īn unmasks the device: « إبراهيم بن أبي عطاء هو إبراهيم بن أبي يحيى » — the two names designate the same man.

Dāraquṭnī classifies him among the majrūḥīn, and twenty critics declare him kadhdhāb. But empirical examination tells a different story: **Ibn ʿAdī**, after fifteen pages of hadith-by-hadith analysis (al-Kāmil), concludes: « **ليس فيها حديث منكر** » — no munkar attributable to Ibrāhīm. And Ibn Jurayj is not the only one to conceal his name: **al-Thawrī** and **al-Shāfiʿī** do the same — one does not conceal the name of a fabricator; one conceals the name of a man whose content is precious but whose identity is **socially unacceptable**. See [[Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā]] for the full dossier.

### 2. Yāsīn al-Zayyāt — the forger behind Abū al-Zubayr

Ibn Ḥibbān (al-Majrūḥīn 3/142, cited in the Mu'jam al-Mudallisīn [p. 309](https://shamela.ws/book/794/309)):

> « ياسين بن معاذ الزيات، كنيته أبو خلف، من أهل الكوفة، انتقل إلى اليمامة وأقام بها، ثم سكن الحجاز، يروي عن أبي الزبير والزهري، روى عنه عبد الرزاق، وكان ممن يروي الموضوعات عن الثقات، وينفرد بالمعضلات عن الأثبات، لا يجوز الاحتجاج به بحال. وكل ما وقع في نسخة ابن جريج عن أبي الزبير من المناكير كان ذلك مما سمعه ابن جريج عن ياسين الزيات عن أبي الزبير، فدلس عنه »
>
> "Yāsīn ibn Mu'ādh al-Zayyāt, kunya Abū Khalaf, from Kūfa. He moved to Yamāma then to the Ḥijāz. He transmitted from [[Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī]] and al-Zuhrī. 'Abd al-Razzāq transmitted from him. He **fabricated traditions** (mawḍū'āt) in the name of reliable transmitters, and isolated problematic traditions (mu'ḍalāt) in the name of established authorities. **All the munkar traditions** in Ibn Jurayj's nuskha from Abū al-Zubayr — that is what Ibn Jurayj **heard from Yāsīn al-Zayyāt** from Abū al-Zubayr, and **he concealed this** (dallasa 'anhu)."

The real circuit behind "Ibn Jurayj ← Abū al-Zubayr ← Jābir":

```
Jābir ibn 'Abd Allāh
        │
  Abū al-Zubayr (heard? or ṣaḥīfa?)
        │
  YĀSĪN AL-ZAYYĀT (kadhdhāb — CONCEALED)
        │
    IBN JURAYJ (tadlīs: "Abū al-Zubayr informed me")
        │
  al-Ḍaḥḥāk / Ḥajjāj / etc.
        │
  ṢAḤĪḤ MUSLIM
```

### 3. Abān ibn Abī 'Ayyāsh — the kurāsa exchange

Two versions of the same scene (Masā'il Ḥarb p. 485; Ya'qūb ibn Sufyān):

Ibn Jurayj comes to **Abān ibn Abī 'Ayyāsh** — unanimously declared matrūk — with a **folded notebook** (kurāsa muṭbaqa) and asks: « أروي هذا عنك؟ » — "May I transmit this from you?" Abān replies: "Yes."

Then Hammām tells the rest: Abān gave a hadith to Ibn Jurayj, who then transmitted it **directly from the original source**, **removing** Hammām and Abān from the chain. When Hammām questions him, he replies:

> « هذا أتانا من قبلكم، جاءنا به أبان من عندكم »
>
> "This came to us from your side — Abān brought it to us from you."

## The phantom samāʿ — al-Nasāʾī, Aḥmad, and the collapse of the rule

Aḥmad's rule (above) rests on a postulate: when Ibn Jurayj says "akhbaranī," he genuinely heard. This postulate collapses on the Abū al-Zubayr channel.

**Al-Nasāʾī** delivers the diagnosis in al-Sunan al-Kubrā (Kitāb al-Sariqa, no. 7461-7466, [Shamela](https://shamela.ws/book/8361)). Sufyān al-Thawrī transmits a hadith via Abū al-Zubayr. Al-Nasāʾī notes: « **لم يسمعه سفيان من أبي الزبير** » — Sufyān did not hear it. Then the same chain passes through Ibn Jurayj. Same verdict:

> **ولم يسمعه أيضاً ابن جريج من أبي الزبير**

> "Ibn Jurayj **did not hear it either** from Abū al-Zubayr."

And the sentence:

> **ما صنع شيئاً** ، ابن جريج لم يسمعه من أبي الزبير عندنا والله أعلم

> "**He has done nothing** [of value]."

**Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal** — the very man who formulated the "akhbaranī" rule — identifies the hidden intermediary (via Abū Dāwūd):

> **إنما سمعهما ابن جريج من ياسين الزيات**

> "Ibn Jurayj heard them **only from Yāsīn al-Zayyāt**."

**Abū Zurʿa and Abū Ḥātim** confirm (al-ʿIlal, Ibn Abī Ḥātim, 1/450). The consensus is complete: four of the greatest critics declare that Ibn Jurayj's samāʿ from Abū al-Zubayr is **fictitious**, and that the real intermediary is a proven forger.

### The irony of the rule

It is **Aḥmad** who says: "when Ibn Jurayj says akhbaranī, it is ṣaḥīḥ." And it is the **same Aḥmad** who says: "he only heard them from Yāsīn." The rule and its refutation come from the same man. The "akhbaranī" is not a seal of guarantee — it is the very tool of the tadlīs. Ibn Jurayj says "Abū al-Zubayr informed me" when the true transmitter is a man whom al-Nasāʾī calls **matrūk al-ḥadīth**, al-Bukhārī calls **munkar al-ḥadīth**, and Ibn Ḥibbān calls **يروي الموضوعات عن الثقات** — "he fabricates traditions in the name of reliable transmitters."

### The Muslim paradox — again

Despite this consensus, **Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim** uses "Ibn Jurayj — **akhbaranī** Abū al-Zubayr — Jābir" in several hadiths (tark al-ṣalāt [no. 82](https://shamela.ws/book/711/269), the grave [no. 970], and others). This is the same pattern as for [[Persons/Ḥammād ibn Salama|Ḥammād ibn Salama]]: Muslim documents the problem (in al-Tamyīz for Ḥammād, here through the consensus of his own teachers) — then uses the chain in his Ṣaḥīḥ. The system records its failures without resolving them.

## The case study: the hadith of abandoning prayer (Muslim no. 82)

Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim ([p. 268-269](https://shamela.ws/book/711/268)) transmits the hadith « بين الرجل وبين الشرك والكفر ترك الصلاة » through **two chains**:

| Chain | Source |
|--------|--------|
| Al-A'mash ← **Abū Sufyān** (Ṭalḥa ibn Nāfi') ← Jābir | p. 268 |
| **Ibn Jurayj** ← "akhbaranī" **Abū al-Zubayr** ← Jābir | p. 269 |

In the second chain, Ibn Jurayj uses the formula "akhbaranī" — supposedly guaranteeing direct samā' according to Aḥmad's rule. But **Ibn Ḥibbān states** that everything munkar in Ibn Jurayj's nuskha via Abū al-Zubayr passes through Yāsīn al-Zayyāt.

The question: does the hadith of abandoning prayer belong among the "munkarāt"? Muslim deemed it reliable enough to include. But the double chain (A'mash + Ibn Jurayj) reveals a recurring pattern: the same matn circulates through independent channels that all pass through **ṣaḥīfa transmitters** — Abū Sufyān and Abū al-Zubayr both having access to the written collection of [[Jābir ibn 'Abd Allāh]].
