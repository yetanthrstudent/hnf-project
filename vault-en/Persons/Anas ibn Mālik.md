# Anas ibn Mālik

**A Companion claiming khidma (service) to the Prophet for 9-10 years**, who became the **central node** of Basran transmission. The three Basran transmitters — [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] (tadlīs), [[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl|Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]] (confusion), [[Persons/Thābit al-Bunānī|Thābit al-Bunānī]] — all converge on him. He is credited with **2286 hadiths**, making him the third Companion in volume of traditions after Abū Hurayra and ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿUmar.

His first documented historical anchor is situated **after 75H**, under the governorate of al-Ḥajjāj. His official recognition as "servant of the Prophet" is triggered by a **letter to ʿAbd al-Malik ibn Marwān**. Several classical sources — ʿĀʾisha, ʿUrwa, Abū Khaldah, Zayd ibn ʿAlī — **document doubts about the real extent of his samāʿ** (سماع — direct reception from a master).

## The late appearance

Ibn ʿAsākir notes a crucial textual fact: the first traditions placing Anas in a historical political context situate him under **ʿAbd al-Malik**, not before. A tradition mentions a visit to Muʿāwiya, but Ibn ʿAsākir corrects (Dimashq [p. 3961](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3961), [p. 3963](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3963), [p. 3964](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3964)):

> « المحفوظ على عبد الملك و على الوليد »

> "What is preserved [in reliable chains] is [that it concerns] ʿAbd al-Malik and al-Walīd."

The first documented historical anchor for Anas is situated **after 75H**, under the governorate of al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf in Iraq. Before that date, no source allows placing Anas in a verifiable event.

## The letter to ʿAbd al-Malik — al-Ḥajjāj's ختم

Two related facts mark Anas's entry into documented history:

**The branding**: Al-Ḥajjāj **ختم** (brands/seals) Anas's neck and hands to humiliate him, a treatment reserved for dhimmīs and slaves (Mustadrak [no. 6453](https://shamela.ws/book/2266/7073), Dimashq [p. 4000](https://shamela.ws/book/71/4000)).

**The insult**: Al-Ḥajjāj calls him (Ṭabarānī [1/247 no. 704](https://shamela.ws/book/1733/823)):

> « يا خبثة، جوّال في الفتنة »

> "O vile one! Vagabond from fitna to fitna!"

**The letter**: Anas writes to ʿAbd al-Malik (Dimashq [p. 4001](https://shamela.ws/book/71/4001)):

> « يا أمير المؤمنين إني قد خدمت محمدا تسع سنين »

> "O Commander of the Believers, I served Muḥammad for nine years."

It is this letter that triggers the **official recognition** of his status as "servant of the Prophet." The caliph intervenes on his behalf with al-Ḥajjāj. The pattern is clear: Anas claims the khidma **in a context of political negotiation** with Umayyad power.

## Wealth and khazz

### The duʿāʾ of prosperity

Anas reports that the Prophet invoked for him: "اللهم أكثر ماله و ولده" — "O God, multiply his wealth and his children" (Muslim [2480](https://shamela.ws/book/1727/6306)-[2481](https://shamela.ws/book/1727/6309), Dimashq [p. 3973](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3973) to [p. 3982](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3982)). This hadith serves as **retroactive legitimation** of considerable wealth:

- **120+ children** (Dimashq [p. 3975](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3975), [p. 4005](https://shamela.ws/book/71/4005))
- **83 sons** dead in the ṭāʿūn al-jārif of 79 H (al-Madāʾinī, al-Taʿāzī p. 118)
- Gardens and properties in Basra

### The khazz — luxury clothing

He is reproached for wearing **khazz** (mixed silk fabric, a sign of luxury) while claiming to forbid it. He replies (Ibn Saʿd [9/23](https://shamela.ws/book/146/3917)):

> « ما لك تنهانا عن الخز و تلبسه أنت ؟ إن أمراءنا يكسونها فنحب أن يروه علينا »

> "Why do you forbid us the khazz when you wear it yourself? Our emirs give it to us and we want them to see it on us."

A remarkable admission: Anas wears the khazz **to please the emirs** who gift it to him — the political dependence is sartorial.

**Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** settles the matter (Qabūl al-Akhbār [p. 192](https://shamela.ws/book/14538/192), Dimashq [36/461](https://shamela.ws/book/71/16416)):

> « لو رآه السلف لأوجعوه »

> "If the early [Muslims] had seen him, they would have **beaten him**."

## Documented doubts about the samāʿ

A bundle of **classical** testimonies — not modern ones — converges on the same finding: Anas's real samāʿ is questioned by his own contemporaries.

### ʿĀʾisha — "they were only children"

Dimashq [20/394](https://shamela.ws/book/71/9185), Qabūl al-Akhbār [1/191](https://shamela.ws/book/14538/191):

> « ما علم أنس و أبو سعيد الخدري بحديث رسول الله ، إنما كانا غلامين صغيرين »

> "**Anas and Abū Saʿīd al-Khudrī knew nothing of the Prophet's hadith** — they were only boys."

### [[Persons/ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr|ʿUrwa]] — the contradiction on the ʿumra

[[Persons/ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr|ʿUrwa]] contradicts Anas on the number of ʿumra (minor pilgrimages) of the Prophet. Anas says four; ʿUrwa, relying on ʿĀʾisha, corrects him (Bukhārī [1778](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/2843)-[1779](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/2844) vs Muwaṭṭaʾ [342](https://shamela.ws/book/1699/1011)). ʿĀʾisha's text is unequivocal: **Anas is wrong**.

### Abū Khaldah — "Did Anas hear from the Prophet?"

Dimashq [p. 3983](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3983):

Abū Khaldah asks Abū al-ʿĀliya:

> « سمع أنس من النبي ؟ »

> "**Did Anas [really] hear from the Prophet?**"

The very question is an act of doubt — one does not ask this question about a Companion whose samāʿ is obvious.

### Zayd ibn ʿAlī — contesting the Umm Ḥarām hadith

Zayd ibn ʿAlī contests the transmission of the Umm Ḥarām hadith by Anas (Ibn Saʿd [10/264](https://shamela.ws/book/146/4684)), casting doubt on the chain.

### Anas's admission — "we don't report everything firsthand"

A man challenges Anas (Ibn Saʿd [4/331](https://shamela.ws/book/146/2116), Dimashq [p. 3995](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3995)):

> « أنت سمعته من رسول الله ؟ »

> "Did **you** hear it from the Messenger of God?"

Anas becomes angry, then admits:

> « ما كل ما نحدثكم سمعناه من رسول الله ، و لكن كان يحدث بعضنا بعضا »

> "**We do not report to you everything [as] what we heard from the Messenger of God** — rather, some of us reported to others."

A crucial admission: Anas acknowledges that his traditions do not all come from direct samāʿ of the Prophet. He transmits at **second or third hand**, which structurally invalidates the claim of a direct isnād "Anas ← the Prophet."

## The claim of Badr

Anas claims to have been present at the **Battle of Badr** (Dimashq [p. 3989](https://shamela.ws/book/71/3989), Ibn Saʿd [5/327](https://shamela.ws/book/146/2112)). But his name is **absent from all lists of Badr combatants** (Tahdhīb al-Kamāl 3/368). The tradition resolves the contradiction with a conciliatory formula: "he was there as a servant, not as a combatant" (Ibn Ḥajar, Fatḥ 7/292).

The problem remains: if Anas was really there, why is he on no list? The answer "he was only a servant" is an *a posteriori* construction designed to reconcile the claim with the documentary absence.

## The Yazīd al-Ḍabbī incident

A revealing fact about Anas's political position (Abū Yaʿlā [2/536 no. 1411](https://shamela.ws/book/12520/1424), Dimashq [15/5](https://shamela.ws/book/71/6489)):

Al-Ḥakam ibn Ayyūb — cousin of al-Ḥajjāj, governor of Basra — delays the Friday prayer. **Yazīd al-Ḍabbī** protests publicly and is beaten by the guards. Anas, **seated on the emir's throne** (على سرير الأمير), does not react. Yazīd calls out to him:

> « يا أنس ، أذكرك الله ، إنك قد صليت مع نبي الله »

> "O Anas, I remind you of God — you prayed with the Prophet of God!"

Al-Ḥakam then asks Anas whether the Prophet's prayer was on time. Anas **confirms** — but said nothing while the violence was being inflicted. He endorses the authorities by his silence, then validates the sunna when the authorities ask him to.

## Governor and tax collector

Anas is not a simple transmitter retired in Basra — he is an **Umayyad official**:

- **Governor (ʿāmil) of Naysābūr** for the Umayyads (Ṭabarānī [1/246 no. 697](https://shamela.ws/book/1733/816))
- **[[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]]** accompanies him **two winters** in his duties (Ibn Abī Shayba 1408 no. 5139, Ṭabarānī [1/243 no. 682](https://shamela.ws/book/1733/801))
- **Qatāda** testifies: "أقام بفارس سنتين يقصر الصلاة" — "he stayed in Fārs two years shortening the prayer" (Tahdhīb al-Ṭabarī 1/257)

**Ibn Sīrīn** summarizes (Ibn Saʿd [9/18](https://shamela.ws/book/146/3912)):

> « كان أنس بن مالك من أحرص أصحاب محمد على المال »

> "Anas ibn Mālik was **the most avid for money** among the Companions of Muḥammad."

## Nabīdh and contradictions

Anas transmits hadiths forbidding certain vessels for nabīdh (fermented drink), but **himself consumes** what he prohibits:

- **Mujāhid** — a tābiʿī who openly despises a ṣaḥābī — says about Anas (Qabūl al-Akhbār [1/191](https://shamela.ws/book/14538/191)): "**دعه فإنه يشرب الطلاء**" — "**Leave him, he drinks ṭilāʾ**" — the gesture is outright dismissal: Anas does not deserve to be listened to
- **Shuʿba**: "رأيت أنس بن مالك يشرب الطلاء على النصف" — "I saw Anas drink ṭilāʾ [cooked wine] at half-concentration" (Tārīkh Wāsiṭ [p. 64](https://shamela.ws/book/11766/60))
- He drinks nabīdh from **jars** (jarr) that he otherwise forbids in his hadiths (Maʿānī al-Āthār [4/226-229](https://shamela.ws/book/1547/3608))

The same man who transmits the prohibition violates it publicly — the contradiction troubles neither his contemporaries nor the later tradition.

## Political servitude

| Period | Allegiance | Context |
|---------|------------|----------|
| ~35 H | ʿUthmān | End of the caliphate |
| ~36-40 H | ʿAlī | Caliphate of ʿAlī |
| ~41-64 H | Muʿāwiya / Yazīd | Umayyad power |
| ~64-73 H | Ibn al-Zubayr | Counter-caliphate |
| ~73-86 H | ʿAbd al-Malik | Return to the Umayyads |
| ~86-96 H | Al-Walīd | Umayyads |

Al-Ḥajjāj summarizes Anas's career in one sentence (Ṭabarānī [1/247 no. 704](https://shamela.ws/book/1733/823)):

> « جوّال في الفتنة مرة مع علي بن أبي طالب ، و مرة مع ابن الزبير ، و مرة مع ابن الأشعث »

> "**Vagabond from fitna to fitna** — now with ʿAlī, now with Ibn al-Zubayr, now with Ibn al-Ashʿath."

Al-Ḥajjāj's diagnosis is brutal but documented: Anas switches sides with every political reversal. His loyalty goes to whoever is in power, regardless.

## The transmission network — Anas's transmitters

### The three Basrans patronized by Khālid al-Qasrī

The core of Anas's transmission consists of three Basrans — all linked to governor [[Persons/Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid al-Qasrī]]:

| Transmitter | Documented problem | Link with Khālid |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]]** | Tadlīs: "مشهور بالتدليس عنهما" — famous for tadlīs from Anas and al-Ḥasan (al-Ḥākim, Ma'rifat [p. 19](https://shamela.ws/book/5997/19)) | Dies at his home in Wāsiṭ, sustained by his provisions |
| **[[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl|Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]]** | Confusion: heard only **5 hadiths** from Anas (Shuʿba), the rest comes from Thābit (al-ʿUqaylī [p. 770](https://shamela.ws/book/13041/770)) | Direct transmitter of Khālid, criticized for ʿamal al-sulṭān |
| **[[Persons/Thābit al-Bunānī|Thābit al-Bunānī]]** | Real source for Ḥumayd: "عامة ما يروي حميد عن أنس لم يسمعه منه، إنما سمعه من ثابت" | Dies under Khālid's governorate |

### Other transmitters

- **[[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]]**: massive irsāl from Anas — "كان يرسل كثيراً ويدلس" (Ibn Ḥajar, Taqrīb). Accompanies him in his governmental duties in Naysābūr.
- **Al-Zuhrī**: "دخلت على أنس بن مالك بدمشق" — "I visited Anas ibn Mālik in Damascus" (Abū Zurʿa 1/412 no. 965). Context: al-Zuhrī is **ʿAbd al-Malik's man**, the official scholar of Umayyad power.

### What the transmitters reveal

A remarkable fact: **no major Companion transmits from Anas**. His transmitters are all Basran Tābiʿūn or Umayyad officials. **Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab** — the greatest Medinan scholar of the generation — has **no known riwāya from Anas** (Tirmidhī [4/411](https://shamela.ws/book/1435/3603)). If Anas had truly been a servant of the Prophet for 9-10 years, one would expect first-rank Companions to transmit from him. Their silence is a fact.

## The hadith of ʿUkl/ʿUraniyya — political use

> Detailed entry: [[Traditions/Hadith of ʿUkl-ʿUraniyya]]

A hadith massively transmitted via Anas (more than 20 transmitters): men from ʿUkl/ʿUraniyya apostatize, steal the ṣadaqa camels, kill the herdsman. The Prophet orders their hands and feet cut off, their eyes gouged out, and them abandoned in the desert.

**Al-Ḥajjāj** explicitly asks Anas for the most severe punishment of the Prophet (Bukhārī no. 5685, [p. 8508](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/8508)). The following Friday, he ascends the minbar and uses the hadith to justify his own mutilations: "أفلا نفعل نحن ذلك في معصية الله ؟" (Fatḥ al-Bārī, [p. 5903](https://shamela.ws/book/1673/5903)).

**[[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]]** blames Anas for arming the tyrant — and calls al-Ḥajjāj a **shayṭān** (ʿAbd al-Razzāq [9/258](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/18538)). **ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn** (Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn) categorically denies the hadith via a chain passing through three Imams of Ahl al-Bayt (al-Bayhaqī, Maʿrifat [p. 6826](https://shamela.ws/book/2863/6826)).

Anas himself **regrets** having transmitted this hadith to al-Ḥajjāj:

> « ما ندمت على شيء ما ندمت على حديث حدثت به الحجاج »
>
> "I have never regretted anything as much as a hadith that I reported to al-Ḥajjāj." (al-Ismāʿīlī via Thābit, Fatḥ al-Bārī [p. 5903](https://shamela.ws/book/1673/5903))

> « فوددت أني مت قبل أن أحدثه »
>
> "I wish I had died before telling him." (Tārīkh Dimashq [p. 1976](https://shamela.ws/book/71/1976))

The regret is **strategic** — Anas does not say the hadith is false, he regrets having armed al-Ḥajjāj. The pattern is familiar: an Anas hadith, transmitted through the Basran network, **serves the interests of the power** in place.

## Relationships

- **Transmitted by** [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] (tadlīs), [[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl|Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]] (confusion), [[Persons/Thābit al-Bunānī|Thābit al-Bunānī]], [[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]] (irsāl), al-Zuhrī
- **Patronized by** the network of [[Persons/Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid al-Qasrī]] (via the three Basrans)
- **Branded by** al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf (ختم العنق)
- **Wrote to** ʿAbd al-Malik ibn Marwān (claiming the khidma)
- **Governor for** the Umayyads (Naysābūr)
- **Refused by** [[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|al-Aʿmash]] — who saw him but refused to hear him: "كان أنس يمر بي فأقول لا أسمع منك حديثا"
- **Contested by** ʿĀʾisha ("غلامين صغيرين"), [[Persons/ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr|ʿUrwa]] (number of ʿumra), Saʿīd ibn Jubayr ("لأوجعوه"), Zayd ibn ʿAlī (Umm Ḥarām), Abū Khaldah (samāʿ)
- **Ignored by** Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab (no riwāya)
- **Described by** Ibn Sīrīn as "أحرص أصحاب محمد على المال" (the most avid)
- **Described by** al-Ḥajjāj as "جوّال في الفتنة" (vagabond from fitna to fitna)
- **Majarra attractor**: the association Thābit → Anas is so automatic that it distorts real chains — see [[Concepts/Ittibāʿ al-Majarra]]

## The circuit

```
ANAS IBN MĀLIK ("خادم النبي")
        │
        ├── QATĀDA (tadlīs: "Anas said")
        ├── THĀBIT AL-BUNĀNĪ ──→ ḤUMAYD (confusion: 5 real hadiths)

        ┌───────┼───────┐

    Common patron: KHĀLID AL-QASRĪ (governor of Iraq, 105-120 H)
```

## Synthesis

Anas ibn Mālik is the Companion whose status is most tightly linked to **post-75H Umayyad power**. His recognition as "servant of the Prophet" is contemporary with his **letter to ʿAbd al-Malik**, written in a context of conflict with al-Ḥajjāj. Before that date, no reliable source anchors him in political history.

His Basran transmitters — Qatāda, Ḥumayd, Thābit — are all **patronized by [[Persons/Khālid ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid al-Qasrī]]**, the Umayyad governor of Iraq. The same pattern as Muʿāwiya patronizing [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar|Ka'b al-Ahbar]] repeats a generation later: a governor maintains a network of scholars who transmit from a "Companion" whose real samāʿ is questioned by his own contemporaries.

The classical sources document a **converging bundle** of problems:
- **ʿĀʾisha** denies his knowledge ("they were only boys")
- **[[Persons/Shu'ba ibn al-Ḥajjāj|Shuʿba]]** reduces Ḥumayd's samāʿ to 5 hadiths from Anas
- **Al-Ḥākim** notes the systemic tadlīs of Qatāda "from Anas"
- **Anas himself** admits not reporting everything firsthand
- **Ibn Sīrīn** calls him the most avid of the Companions
- **Al-Ḥajjāj** calls him an opportunistic vagabond

The result: 2286 hadiths attributed to a man whose contemporaries doubt the samāʿ, whose main transmitter (Qatāda) is a notorious mudallis, whose second (Ḥumayd) admits masking his sources, and whose political recognition is posterior to 75H. The Sunni consensus has ruled in his favor — but the classical sources it invokes carry within them the traces of doubt.
