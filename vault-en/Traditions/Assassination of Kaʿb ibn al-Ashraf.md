# The assassination of Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf

## The text — long version (Bukhārī n° 4037)

> ٤٠٣٧ - حدثنا علي بن عبد الله: حدثنا سفيان: قال عمرو: سمعت جابر بن عبد الله يقول: قال رسول الله ﷺ: « من لكعب بن الأشرف، فإنه قد آذى الله ورسوله... »

> The Prophet said: "**Who will take care of Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf? For he has offended God and His Messenger.**" Muḥammad ibn Maslama rises: "O Messenger of God, do you want me to kill him? — Yes. — Then allow me to say (false things). — Say."
>
> Muḥammad ibn Maslama goes to find Ka'b: "This man (the Prophet) has asked us for alms, and he has put us in difficulty. I have come to borrow from you." Ka'b: "By God, you will end up tiring of him!" — "We have followed him and do not want to leave him before seeing how his affair turns out. We would like you to lend us a wasq or two." Ka'b: "Yes, but give me a pledge." — "What do you want?" — "Pledge your women to me." — "How would we pledge our women to you, you who are the most handsome of the Arabs?" — "Then pledge your sons." — "How would we pledge our sons? People would say of one of them: 'He was pledged for a wasq or two' — that would be a disgrace. But we will pledge the weapons (al-laʾma) to you."
>
> He promises to return. He comes at night, with **Abū Nāʾila** — Ka'b's foster brother. They call him at the fort. He comes down to them.
>
> **His wife says to him: "Where are you going out at this hour?"** He says: "It is only Muḥammad ibn Maslama and my brother Abū Nāʾila." — *(And another than ʿAmr reports:)* **She says: "I hear a voice dripping with blood."** He says: "It is only my brother Muḥammad ibn Maslama and my foster brother Abū Nāʾila — **the noble one, if invited to a lance-thrust by night, would answer.**"
>
> Muḥammad ibn Maslama enters with two men — *(Sufyān was asked: Did ʿAmr name them? — He named some of them.)* — *(Another than ʿAmr says: Abū ʿAbs ibn Jabr, al-Ḥārith ibn Aws and ʿAbbād ibn Bishr.)*
>
> He says (to his companions): "When he comes, I will take his hair and smell it. When you see me holding his head, strike." Ka'b comes down to them, draped, exuding perfume. He says: "I have never smelled a perfume like today's." — *(Another than ʿAmr says: "I have at home the most perfumed of Arab women and the most accomplished of Arab women.")* — "Will you let me smell your head? — Yes." He smells it, then lets his companions smell it. Then: "Will you let me (again)? — Yes." When he has taken good hold of his head, he says: "**At them!**" They kill him, then go to inform the Prophet.

---

The hadith is in al-Bukhārī in two forms: a short version (n° 3031, *Kitāb al-Jihād*, bāb "lying in war") and a long version (n° 4037, *Kitāb al-Maghāzī*). The chain is the same: **Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna → ʿAmr ibn Dīnār → Jābir**. Both versions pass through a single channel — and this channel poses several problems.

The account also circulated in Medina via **al-Zuhrī**, but in **mursal** form (without a chain going back to a Companion). It is Sufyān who gives it a connected isnād — a "fine chain" — via ʿAmr ibn Dīnār.

## The chain

```
Jābir "ibn ʿAbd Allāh" (?)
    │
    ╳ (ʿAmr did not hear from Jābir — Shuʿba)
    │
ʿAmr ibn Dīnār (d. ~126 AH, Mecca)
    │
Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna (d. 198 AH)
    │
al-Bukhārī (n° 3031, 4037)
```

### Problem 1: ʿAmr did not hear from Jābir

**Shuʿba** — the most rigorous critic of his era — asks the question directly to ʿAmr ibn Dīnār (Musnad Aḥmad, [n° 14957](https://shamela.ws/book/25794/11333)):

> قلت لعمرو : أنت سمعته من جابر ؟ قال : **لا**

> "I said to ʿAmr: Is it you who heard it from Jābir? He said: **No**."

Al-Ḥākim drives the point home in Maʿrifat ʿUlūm al-Ḥadīth ([p. 111](https://shamela.ws/book/13179)):

> عامة حديث عمرو بن دينار عن الصحابة غير مسموعة

> "The majority of the hadiths of ʿAmr ibn Dīnār from the Companions **are not heard** [directly]."

Between ʿAmr and Jābir, there are omitted intermediaries: probably **[[Persons/ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ]]** or **Muḥammad ibn ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn** (al-Muʿallimī al-Yamānī, cited in al-Muʿjam al-Mudallisīn, [p. 343](https://shamela.ws/book/794/343#p5)). The chain is a **disguised munqaṭiʿ** — it has the appearance of a connected isnād, but a link is missing.

### Problem 2: which Jābir?

Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna **himself** corrects Abū Ḥanīfa on the identity of the Jābir in ʿAmr ibn Dīnār's chains. Abū Ḥanīfa says "ʿAmr, from Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh." Sufyān corrects (Suʾālāt al-Bardhʿī, [p. 1030](https://shamela.ws/book/812/1030)):

> حدثنا عمرو عن جابر بن عبد الله : في أخباري
>
> فقلت : حدثنا عمرو عن **جابر بن زيد** ، ليس جابر بن عبد الله

> "[Abū Ḥanīfa said:] ʿAmr transmitted to us from Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh. [Sufyān said:] ʿAmr transmitted to us from **Jābir ibn Zayd** — it is not Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh."

Sufyān concludes: **فإذا هو قد أخطأ فيهما جميعا** — "He made a mistake in both cases."

**Jābir ibn Zayd** (d. ~93 AH) is an **Ibāḍite Tābiʿī** from Basra — not a Companion, not an eyewitness.

Sufyān is not speaking here about the hadith of Ka'b in particular, but about the general reflex of attributing to "Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh" what belongs to "Jābir ibn Zayd" in ʿAmr's chains. The question then naturally arises: if ʿAmr ibn Dīnār said "Jābir" without specifying, and if Sufyān himself warns that this "Jābir" is sometimes the wrong one — on what basis do we identify the Jābir of the Ka'b hadith as being the Companion?

### Who adds "ibn ʿAbd Allāh"?

The comparison of transmissions from **Sufyān's different students** reveals a clear pattern:

| Student of Sufyān | Wording in the chain |
|---|---|
| al-Musnadī (Bukhārī 3032) | "ʿan **Jābir**" |
| Qutayba (Bukhārī 3031) | "ʿan **Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh**" |
| ʿAlī al-Madīnī (Bukhārī 4037) | "ʿan **Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh**" |
| Ibn Abī ʿUmar (Muslim 1801) | "ʿan **Jābir**" |
| Aḥmad ibn Ṣāliḥ (Abū Dāwūd) | "ʿan **Jābir**" |
| al-Ḥumaydī (Bayhaqī, ed. ʿIlmiyya) | "ʿan **Jābir**" |
| Ibn Abī al-Wazīr (Tārīkh al-Madīna) | "ʿan **Jābir**" |
| al-Dhahabī (direct citation) | "ʿan **Jābir**" |

The ratio is **6 to 2** in favor of "Jābir" alone. Muslim, Abū Dāwūd, al-Dhahabī — all have only "Jābir." The commentator of Baḥr al-Muḥīṭ al-Thajjāj must even **add on his own** "أي ابن عبد الله" to the Muslim text — proof that the original had only the first name.

**The predictable objection**: it is common practice in hadith to mention only a first name when the Companion's identity is obvious from context. "ʿAmr, from Jābir" could designate Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh without needing to specify — this is an attested usage, and the first-name-only argument would not suffice on its own. But here, the situation is different: **the confusion is documented by the transmitter himself**. Sufyān explicitly warns that the "Jābir" of ʿAmr ibn Dīnār is sometimes **Jābir ibn Zayd** and not Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh. This is therefore not a case where the first name is self-evident — it is a case where the ambiguity is proven.

Sufyān most likely said **"ʿan Jābir"** — period. It is **Qutayba** and **ʿAlī al-Madīnī**, two great muḥaddithūn, who "complete" the identification by presuming it is the famous Companion. This is a case of downstream [[Concepts/Ittibāʿ al-Majarra|ittibāʿ al-majarra]]: the association ʿAmr ibn Dīnār → "Jābir" automatically calls up "ibn ʿAbd Allāh" in the mind of a collector.

### The silence of Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh's students

The ambiguity of the first name would be anecdotal if there were not a way to settle it: checking on the side of **Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh's students**. If the hadith indeed comes from him, others besides ʿAmr ibn Dīnār should have transmitted it — Jābir is one of the most prolific Companions, with dozens of students in Medina and Mecca.

Yet **none** of them reports this account: neither [[Persons/Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī|Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī]] (who possesses an entire *kitāb* of Jābir, however contested), nor Muḥammad al-Bāqir, nor ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ, nor Saʿīd ibn Mīnāʾ, nor any other. The hadith is a **complete [[Concepts/Tafarrud|tafarrud]]** via ʿAmr ibn Dīnār — who, let us recall, did not hear from Jābir.

The verification settles it: the network of Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh does not know this hadith. If it is **Jābir ibn Zayd** (Ibāḍite tābiʿī from Basra), the silence of the Medinans is normal — they have nothing to transmit from him. And the account then joins the category of the mursal: a tābiʿī reporting without having been a witness.

## The omniscient narrator

The long version (n° 4037) contains a dialogue **inside the fort** between Ka'b and his wife — while the assassins are **outside**:

> فقالت له امرأته : أين تخرج هذه الساعة ؟

> "His wife said to him: Where are you going out at this hour?"

> قالت : أسمع صوتا كأنه يقطر منه الدم

> "She said: I hear a voice dripping with blood."

> قال : إنما هو أخي محمد بن مسلمة ، ورضيعي أبو نائلة ، إن الكريم لو دعي إلى طعنة بليل لأجاب

> "He said: It is only my brother Muḥammad ibn Maslama and my foster brother Abū Nāʾila — the noble one, if invited to a lance-thrust by night, would answer."

Who reports this exchange? The assassins are outside, Ka'b and his wife are in the fort. Nobody in Muḥammad ibn Maslama's group witnessed this conversation. Al-Bukhārī himself notes it: these passages come from an **"other than ʿAmr"** (وقال غير عمرو) — an unidentified source, grafted onto the main account.

The result is a text that reads like a **literary narrative** with an omniscient narrator — one sees inside the fort, the wife's premonitions, Ka'b's answers, his perfume, his vanity — as in a book or a film. This register is that of *qiṣaṣ* (popular storytelling), not hadith.

## What the hadith legitimizes

The account is classified by al-Bukhārī in the **bāb al-kadhib fī al-ḥarb** ("lying in war") — a chapter that legally founds the **lawfulness of deception** in contexts of conflict. In a cascade, the hadith is invoked to legitimize:

- Execution for a **strictly religious motive**: Ka'b is not accused of murder, military treason or treaty violation — he "offended God and His Messenger" (*ādhā Allāh wa-rasūlahu*). The grievance is **verbal offense**, not an act of war.
- **Targeted assassination** by order of religious authority — without trial, without confrontation, without possibility of defense
- **Lying** as a lawful tool (Muḥammad ibn Maslama asks for and receives permission to lie)
- The exploitation of **personal trust** (Abū Nāʾila is Ka'b's foster brother — it is this intimate bond that allows them to lure him out of the fort)
- Killing **by night**, by ruse, outside any judicial framework

All of this rests on a chain where the central transmitter did not hear from the Companion, where the identity of the Companion is disputed by the transmitter himself, and where the text contains scenes that no one could have reported.

## The mursal of [[Persons/al-Zuhrī|al-Zuhrī]] — the account before Sufyān

The account of the assassination of Ka'b circulated in Medina **before** Sufyān gave it a connected isnād. Al-Zuhrī transmitted it in his maghāzī, and his students provide versions that are sometimes mursal, sometimes semi-connected — proof that he possessed the account through the Medinan oral tradition without systematically attaching it to a Companion.

The reference work is *Marwiyyāt al-Imām al-Zuhrī fī al-Maghāzī* by al-ʿAwājī ([p. 395](https://shamela.ws/book/2700/357)), which devotes an entire chapter (المبحث الثاني في مقتل كعب بن الأشرف) to this question.

### The pattern

Al-Zuhrī's students give the same account with **divergent chains** — sometimes mursal, sometimes connected via ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Kaʿb. This type of variation is a classic pattern in al-Zuhrī's corpus: his different students (Maʿmar, Shuʿayb, Mūsā ibn ʿUqba, ʿUqayl, al-Awzāʿī...) often transmit the same hadith with non-matching isnāds, which is partly explained by the conditions under which his material was collected and disseminated. The Ka'b case is not isolated — it is a structural phenomenon deserving a separate study.

It is in this context that Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna arrives with an **entirely different** chain — Meccan rather than Medinan — via ʿAmr ibn Dīnār → Jābir. A chain that has the appearance of a proper musnad, but whose every link is contestable.

### The classical verdict on al-Zuhrī's mursals

This is not a detail: the classical critics consider al-Zuhrī's mursals to be **among the weakest** of all hadith literature.

**Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Qaṭṭān** (d. 198 AH) gives three successive verdicts (Jāmiʿ al-Taḥṣīl, [pp. 89-90](https://shamela.ws/book/25864/75)):

> مرسل الزهري شر من مرسل غيره ، لأنه حافظ وكلما قدر أن يسمّي سمّى ، وإنما يترك من لا يستجيز أن يسمّيه

> "The mursal of al-Zuhrī is **worse than the mursal of anyone else** — for he is a ḥāfiẓ: whenever he can name (his source), he names it. If he omits, it is because he does not permit himself to name it."

And further: **مرسل الزهري شبه لا شيء** — "the mursal of al-Zuhrī is almost nothing." And on the mursal of al-Zuhrī and Qatāda in general: **هو بمنزلة الريح** — "it is like wind."

**Al-Shāfiʿī** (d. 204 AH) concurs (Siyar, [5/339](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/4111)):

> إرسال الزهري ليس بشيء ، ذلك أنا نجده يروي عن سليمان بن أرقم

> "The mursal of al-Zuhrī **is nothing** — for we find him transmitting from Sulaymān ibn Arqam (a notoriously weak transmitter)."

The reasoning is structural: when al-Zuhrī sends a mursal, one does not know whether he is concealing a link as weak as Sulaymān ibn Arqam. **Ibn Maʿīn** (d. 233 AH) confirms: **مراسيل الزهري ليست بشيء** — "the mursals of al-Zuhrī are nothing" (al-Marāsīl, Ibn Abī Ḥātim, p. 3).

**Al-Dhahabī** (d. 748 AH) pushes the analysis further (Siyar, [5/339](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/4111)):

> مراسيل الزهري كالمعضل ؛ لأنه يكون قد سقط منه اثنان

> "The mursals of al-Zuhrī are **like muʿḍal** — because there are probably **two** missing links, not just one."

Al-Zuhrī is a "minor tābiʿī" (ṣaghīr al-tābiʿīn): when he omits the isnād, he has probably omitted another tābiʿī *and* the Companion. Al-Dhahabī adds: "whoever puts the mursal of al-Zuhrī on the same level as that of Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab **does not know what he is saying**."

The Ka'b account is therefore a mursal of al-Zuhrī — that is, according to the classical critics themselves, material that could have **two missing links** and whose real source is unknown.

## Why does al-Bukhārī choose the musnad?

Let us recap what al-Bukhārī had before him:

- A Medinan tradition transmitted by **al-Zuhrī** — the greatest hadith master of his generation — in **mursal** form in the majority of its routes
- A musnad chain passing through **ʿAmr ibn Dīnār → Jābir**, of which Shuʿba established that ʿAmr **did not hear** from Jābir
- A confusion about the very identity of "Jābir" — Sufyān correcting elsewhere that it is not Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh
- **None** of the dozens of students of Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh transmitting this account
- A text containing scenes that **no one** could have reported, and which Bukhārī himself attributes to an unidentified *ghayr ʿAmr*

Al-Bukhārī had al-Zuhrī's mursal available — he necessarily knew it, having spent his life in transmission circles. He also had Shuʿba's criticism available. And yet, he retains the only version that gives a connected isnād to a Companion, and places it in a foundational chapter of fiqh (*bāb al-kadhib fī al-ḥarb*).

The choice is not accidental. A mursal cannot found a legal rule in Bukhārī's system — he needed a musnad. And the only available musnad was Sufyān's. Al-Bukhārī thus selects the chain that is **factually the weakest** but **the only one with the required form** to enter a ṣaḥīḥ collection. The content dictates the choice of chain, not the reverse.

## The maghāzī — a fragile genre

The hadith of Ka'b belongs to the genre of **maghāzī** (the Prophet's military expeditions) — a corpus whose fragility is recognized by the classical critics themselves. **Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal** rules (Ibn ʿAdī, al-Kāmil, [1/212](https://shamela.ws/book/12579/136); al-Khaṭīb, al-Jāmiʿ, [n° 1493](https://shamela.ws/book/13012/1723)):

> ثلاثة كتب ليس لها أصول : المغازي والملاحم والتفسير

> "Three types of books **have no foundations**: the maghāzī, the malāḥim and the tafsīr."

The chain (Aḥmad → ʿAbd al-Malik al-Maymūnī → Muḥammad ibn Saʿīd al-Ḥarrānī → Ibn ʿAdī) is judged **ṣaḥīḥ** by al-Judayʿ (Taḥrīr ʿUlūm al-Ḥadīth, [p. 1027](https://shamela.ws/book/1416/1027)). Al-Khaṭīb glosses: Aḥmad targets works whose authors are disqualified, whose transmitters are unreliable, and whose content is inflated by **quṣṣāṣ** (popular storytellers) — precisely the register of the Ka'b account, with its omniscient narrator and its inaccessible scenes.

The Ka'b account comes from al-Zuhrī's maghāzī — material transmitted in mursal form, without grounding in direct testimony. The only available musnad (Sufyān → ʿAmr → Jābir) is a late graft onto an account that was already circulating without a connected isnād.

## The Sufyān question

Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna is the **sole channel** through which this account passes from a simple Medinan mursal (al-Zuhrī) to a musnad hadith in al-Bukhārī. It is he who provides the chain ʿAmr → Jābir. But one must be precise about what is attributable to him and what is not.

**What is from Sufyān**: the chain itself — ʿAmr ibn Dīnār, from Jābir. And the fact that he knows, in another context, that the "Jābir" of ʿAmr is not always Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh (Suʾālāt al-Bardhʿī).

**What is probably not from him**: the identification "ibn ʿAbd Allāh." The majority of his students (6 out of 8) transmit with "Jābir" alone. It is al-Madīnī and Qutayba who complete — by presumption, not by hearing.

Sufyān nonetheless remains a transmitter whose fallibility is documented elsewhere — al-Shāfiʿī caught him in flagrant [[Concepts/Ittibāʿ al-Majarra|majarra]] on al-Zuhrī → ʿUrwa.

## The accusation of perfidy — the same Sufyān transmits the contestation

The same Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna — the one who provides the canonical chain to al-Bukhārī — also transmits the **moral contestation** of the act by a Companion. This narration is preserved with **complete isnād** in three major classical collections: **al-Ṭaḥāwī** (Sharḥ Mushkil al-Āthār, [p. 190](https://shamela.ws/book/22547/212)), **al-Bayhaqī** (Dalāʾil al-Nubuwwa, [3/193](https://shamela.ws/book/7478/1290)), and **Ibn ʿAsākir** (Tārīkh Dimashq, [55/275](https://shamela.ws/book/71/25576)). **Ibn Taymiyya** cites it in al-Ṣārim al-Maslūl ([Shamela](https://shamela.ws/book/7344/205)):

> روى ابن وهب : أخبرني سفيان بن عيينة عن عمر بن سعيد أخي سفيان بن سعيد الثوري عن أبيه عن عباية قال : ذكر قتل ابن الأشرف عند معاوية فقال ابن يامين : **كان قتله غدرا** فقال محمد بن مسلمة : يا معاوية **أيغدر عندك رسول الله** ﷺ **ثم لا تنكر ؟** و الله لا يظلني و إياك سقف بيت أبدا و لا يخلو لي دم هذا إلا قتله

> "The assassination of Ibn al-Ashraf was mentioned in the presence of Muʿāwiya. **Ibn Yāmīn** said: 'His murder was a **غدر** (perfidy).' **Muḥammad ibn Maslama** said: 'O Muʿāwiya! The Prophet is accused of perfidy in your presence and you do not react? By God, no roof shall ever shelter us both! And this man's blood shall not be safe — I will kill him.'"

### The isnād

```
ʿAbāya ibn Rifāʿa ibn Rāfiʿ al-Zuraqī (tābiʿī, Medina) — thiqa
    │
Saʿīd ibn Masrūq al-Thawrī (d. ~126 AH, Kūfa) — thiqa (father of Sufyān al-Thawrī)
    │
ʿUmar ibn Saʿīd al-Thawrī (Kūfa) — ثقة (Taqrīb n° 4906), brother of Sufyān al-Thawrī
    │
SUFYĀN IBN ʿUYAYNA (d. 198 AH) ◄── the SAME as the Bukhārī chain
    │
    ├── Ibn Wahb (d. 197 AH, Egypt) → al-Ṭaḥāwī, al-Bayhaqī
    ├── Muḥammad ibn ʿAbbād al-Makkī → Ibn ʿAsākir
    └── Suwayd ibn Saʿīd → Ibn ʿAsākir
```

Three independent transmitters take from Sufyān — this is not a downstream tafarrud.

The chain passes through the **al-Thawrī family** — Saʿīd ibn Masrūq is thiqa by consensus. ʿAbāya ibn Rifāʿa is a Medinan Anṣārī tābiʿī (grandson of the Companion Rāfiʿ ibn Khadīj), main transmitter of traditions about Muḥammad ibn Maslama in the Siyar. **Yāmīn ibn ʿUmayr ibn Kaʿb ibn ʿAmr ibn Jaḥḥāsh** is identified in al-Istīʿāb ([shamela.ws/book/1499/3635](https://shamela.ws/book/1499/3635)) as a **Companion** from the **Banū al-Naḍīr** — converted to Islam, "حسن إسلامه."

### The analysis

**1. Sufyān transmits both.** The same man provides:
- The "canonical" hadith (ʿAmr → Jābir) — retained by al-Bukhārī in the *bāb al-kadhib fī al-ḥarb*
- The direct contestation — where a Companion qualifies the act as **perfidy**

Al-Bukhārī most likely knew both of Sufyān's transmissions. He retains the one that founds a legal rule.

**2. The response is violent, not theological.** Muḥammad ibn Maslama — the assassin himself according to the canonical account — does not say: "it was not ghadr because Ka'b had offended the Prophet" or "because the Jews had betrayed the treaty." He does not **refute** the qualification. He **forbids** it from being uttered — on pain of death. It is a response of authority, not of reason.

**3. Muʿāwiya (or Marwān) does not react.** Ibn Taymiyya himself notes this and must justify it: **إمساك الأمير إما معاوية أو مروان عن قتل هذا الرجل** — "the fact that the emir — Muʿāwiya **or** Marwān — refrained from killing this man." Even Ibn Taymiyya does not know whether it is the caliph or the governor. And he must **excuse** the silence of power: "his silence does not indicate a doctrinal position... perhaps he did not examine the case... perhaps his motivation was insufficient to apply the penalty..." The cascade of "perhaps" betrays the embarrassment.

**4. Ibn Yāmīn names what the hadith describes.** The canonical account is explicit: Muḥammad ibn Maslama asks permission to **lie**, uses the trust-bond of Abū Nāʾila (foster brother) to lure Ka'b out of the fort, and kills him by night through ruse. The word **غدر** (perfidy — betrayal of trust given) is the exact description of the act. Ibn Yāmīn merely names what the account tells.

**5. The structural irony.** The assassin threatens to **assassinate** someone for calling the **assassination** a betrayal. The cycle is confirmed: the only response to criticism is the threat of death — exactly the motif of the hadith itself (Ka'b is killed for having "offended"). And Ibn Taymiyya cites this tradition to **justify** the murder of anyone who accuses the Prophet of ghadr — meaning the tradition intended to contest the assassination is turned around to legitimize further killings.

**6. Ibn Yāmīn is Muslim.** Ibn Taymiyya specifies: **هذا الرجل إنما كان مسلما فإن المدينة لم يكن بها يومئذ أحد من غير المسلمين** — "this man was Muslim, for there was no one in Medina at that time who was not Muslim." A **Muslim Companion**, descendant of the Banū al-Naḍīr — that is, from the Jewish community of Medina — qualifies as perfidy an act that the canonical account legitimizes. His perspective is that of an internal witness, not an enemy.

### Al-Ṭaḥāwī recognizes the problem — Sharḥ Mushkil al-Āthār

**Al-Ṭaḥāwī** (d. 321 AH) — a first-rank muḥaddith, author of Sharḥ Maʿānī al-Āthār — devotes an **entire chapter** to this contradiction ([shamela.ws/book/22547/212](https://shamela.ws/book/22547/212)):

> بَابُ بَيَانِ مُشْكِلِ مَا رُوِيَ عَنْ رَسُولِ اللهِ ﷺ فِيمَا كَانَ مِنْ بَعْثِهِ مُحَمَّدَ بْنَ مَسْلَمَةَ لِقَتْلِهِ كَعْبَ بْنَ الْأَشْرَفِ **بِمَا يَدْفَعُ التَّضَادَّ** عَنْ مَا تَوَهَّمَ بَعْضُ النَّاسِ أَنَّهُ قَدْ ضَادَّ مَا فِيهِ

> "Chapter explaining the problem of what was reported from the Prophet concerning his sending of Muḥammad ibn Maslama to kill Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf — **in order to remove the contradiction** that some people thought they saw..."

The title is an admission: **people saw a contradiction** (*taḍādd*). Al-Ṭaḥāwī cites the narration of Ibn Yāmīn with complete isnād (Baḥr ibn Naṣr ← Ibn Wahb ← Sufyān ← ʿUmar ibn Saʿīd ← his father ← ʿAbāya), then cites **immediately after** the hadith of **ʿAmr ibn al-Ḥamiq**:

> مَنْ آمَنَ رَجُلًا عَلَى نَفْسِهِ فَقَتَلَهُ **أُعْطِيَ لِوَاءَ غَدْرٍ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ**

> "Whoever grants a man safety then kills him **shall be given a banner of perfidy on the Day of Judgment**."

The juxtaposition is devastating: Ibn Yāmīn says *ghadr*, and the prophetic hadith promises a *liwāʾ ghadr* (banner of perfidy) to whoever kills someone to whom he had given trust. Al-Ṭaḥāwī **must** then argue that Ka'b's trust toward Muḥammad ibn Maslama was "legally null" (كَانَ كَلَا ائْتِمَانٍ) because Ka'b "harmed God and His Messenger." The apologetic effort betrays the strength of the objection.

### Al-Bayhaqī — the faithful compiler despite himself

Al-Bayhaqī cites the narration with complete isnād, then comments:

> قَالَ أَحْمَدُ: مَا ذَكَرْنَا وَمَا نَذْكُرُهُ مِنْ **غَدْرِ كَعْبِ بْنِ الْأَشْرَفِ** وَنَقْضِهِ عَهْدَهُ وَهِجَائِهِ رَسُولَ اللَّه ﷺ وَالْمُسْلِمِينَ وَعَدَاوَتِهِ إِيَّاهُمْ وَتَحْرِيضِهِ عَلَيْهِمْ **يُكَذِّبُ هَذَا الْقَائِلَ**، وَيَدُلُّ عَلَى **سُوءِ رَأْيِهِ وَقُبْحِ قَوْلِهِ**

> "What we have mentioned and shall mention of **Ka'b's perfidy** [...] **gives the lie to this man** and shows the **baseness of his judgment and the ugliness of his words**."

His strategy: reverse the word *ghadr* — it is Ka'b who is the traitor, not the Prophet. Then he accumulates five charges (ghadr, naqḍ al-ʿahd, hijāʾ, ʿadāwa, taḥrīḍ) and concludes that Ka'b "deserved to be killed."

But this accumulation **betrays the problem**. The canonical hadith (Bukhārī) gives only **one motive** for Ka'b's murder: the offense to the Prophet (hijāʾ). No treaty violation, no treason, no military incitement — just poetry. Al-Bayhaqī **adds** four charges absent from the source text. Why? Because the single motive of the hadith — offensive verses — is not sufficient to answer the accusation of *ghadr*. If Ka'b is killed solely for poems, and killed by ruse after having received trust, then Ibn Yāmīn is right to name the act.

The inventory of his own chapter (Dalāʾil, [3/190-200](https://shamela.ws/book/7478/1284)) confirms it:

| Charge | Ṣaḥīḥ source in the *bāb*? | Reality |
|--------|-------------------------------|---------|
| **hijāʾ** (satire) | **Yes** — Bukhārī/Muslim (ādhā Allāh wa-Rasūlahu) | Only charge attested by a ṣaḥīḥ |
| **taḥrīḍ** (incitement) | Partially — Abū Dāwūd 3000: "fī shiʿrih" | Extension of hijāʾ, no diplomatic or military action |
| **ʿadāwa** (hostility) | **No** | Sīra only — Ibn Isḥāq and Mūsā ibn ʿUqba, ḍaʿīf chains |
| **naqḍ al-ʿahd** (treaty violation) | **No** | Editorial commentary by al-Bayhaqī — **no narration** mentions it |
| **ghadr** (Ka'b's perfidy) | **No** | Rhetorical reversal of Ibn Yāmīn's accusation |

Of five charges, **only one** is attested by a ṣaḥīḥ. Two — naqḍ al-ʿahd and ghadr — rest on **no source cited in the chapter**. And a ḍaʿīf narration he himself cites (Jābir via Ibrāhīm ibn Jaʿfar, p. 1291) places the drafting of the treaty (*kitāb*) between the Prophet and the Jews **after** Ka'b's murder — making naqḍ al-ʿahd logically impossible.

If there were no real tension in the heritage, al-Bayhaqī could have contented himself with "the Prophet ordered it, end of discussion." The fact that he must construct a five-charge argument — of which two are invented by the commentator and two rest only on sīra sources with weak chains — proves that the tension is **structural**, and that the narration of Ibn Yāmīn crystallizes it.

### Ibn Taymiyya against al-Bayhaqī — the inadvertent demolition

**Ibn Taymiyya** (d. 728 AH), in the same al-Ṣārim al-Maslūl, adopts a radically different approach — and one that inadvertently destroys al-Bayhaqī's argument. His book is a treatise on **sabb** (insulting the Prophet) as a **unique and sufficient** motive for execution. Ka'b is his **third hadith-proof** (al-ḥadīth al-thālith, [p. 70](https://shamela.ws/book/7344/71)).

He **anticipates and explicitly refutes** the objection that other causes were at play ([pp. 75-76](https://shamela.ws/book/7344/76)):

> فإن قيل: إن ابن الأشرف كان قد أتى بغير السب والهجاء

> "If one says: Ka'b had committed things other than insult and hijāʾ..."

And his response is categorical ([p. 80](https://shamela.ws/book/7344/81)):

> أن النبي ﷺ لم يندب إلى قتله لكونه ذهب إلى مكة وقال ما قال هناك وإنما ندب إلى قتله **لما قدم وهجاه**

> "The Prophet did **NOT** order his assassination because he had gone to Mecca [...]. He ordered it when he **returned and satirized him**."

Then he closes every exit ([p. 82](https://shamela.ws/book/7344/83)):

> أن **جميع** ما أتاه ابن الأشرف إنما هو **أذى باللسان** [...] **ولم يعمل عملا فيه محاربة**

> "**ALL** that Ka'b did was harm **by the tongue**. [...] He committed **no act of war**."

And he concludes ([p. 89](https://shamela.ws/book/7344/90)):

> وإنما قتلوه **لأجل هجائه وأذاه لله ورسوله** ومن حل قتله بهذا الوجه لم يعصم دمه بأمان ولا عهد

> "They killed him **ONLY** for his hijāʾ and his harm to God and His Messenger."

The paradox is total: Ibn Taymiyya reduces **everything** to verbal sabb — and consequently, al-Bayhaqī's four additional charges (ghadr, naqḍ al-ʿahd, ʿadāwa, military taḥrīḍ) are not only undocumented, they are **explicitly refuted** by the greatest Ḥanbalī jurist. Al-Bayhaqī overloads the file; Ibn Taymiyya strips it to the bone — and the bone is that a man was killed by ruse for having written verses.

Al-Bayhaqī is nonetheless consistent in his approach: he is a systematic defender of the Sunna who, once he holds a text with a solid isnād, preserves and defends it — as he does elsewhere with the [[Traditions/Hadith of ʿUkl-ʿUraniyya|hadith of ʿUkl]]. He **does not hide** the embarrassing narration, he transmits it faithfully then neutralizes it through commentary. It is precisely this compiler's honesty that provides us with the strongest proof of the contestation.

### Al-Bukhārī and this narration

Al-Bukhārī did not retain the narration of Ibn Yāmīn. Why? We do not know. Several hypotheses are possible:

1. **Genre argument**: the narration is a historical khabar (post-prophetic event between Companions), not a marfūʿ hadith — it would not fall within the Ṣaḥīḥ's scope. But this argument has a limit: al-Bukhārī includes other non-prophetic materials, such as the account of [[Persons/ʿAmr ibn Maymūn al-Awdī]] (n° 3849, [p. 5824](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/5824)) — the personal observation of a tābiʿī about monkeys stoning an adulterous she-monkey, placed in a normative chapter to reinforce the rajm thesis. The Ṣaḥīḥ is therefore not strictly limited to marfūʿāt.

2. **Chain argument**: al-Bukhārī may have had a technical reservation about a link in the chain (Sufyān ← ʿUmar ibn Saʿīd ← Saʿīd ibn Masrūq ← ʿAbāya) that we are unaware of — an unconfirmed samāʿ, a connection he judged insufficient. Each link is individually thiqa (ʿUmar ibn Saʿīd: ثقة by unanimity — Taqrīb n° 4906; Saʿīd ibn Masrūq: thiqa by consensus; ʿAbāya: thiqa), but this does not mean Bukhārī validated this combination.

3. **Content choice**: al-Bukhārī retained the account that founds a legal rule and set aside the contestation. But one cannot affirm this without proof — it would be imputing intent.

**In summary, we do not claim to know why al-Bukhārī did not retain this narration.** What we observe is that the chain is judged solid by the general criteria of jarḥ wa taʿdīl, and that three first-rank muḥaddithūn — al-Ṭaḥāwī (d. 321 AH), al-Bayhaqī (d. 458 AH) and Ibn ʿAsākir (d. 571 AH) — preserved it with complete isnād. Non-selection by al-Bukhārī is not a verdict of inauthenticity.
