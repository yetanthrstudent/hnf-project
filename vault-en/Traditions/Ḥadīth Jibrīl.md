# The hadith of Jibrīl

## The described event

A man in dazzlingly white clothes and deeply black hair appears among the Companions, with no trace of travel, unknown to all. He sits facing the Prophet, knee against knee, and asks him questions about Islam, faith (īmān), excellence (iḥsān) and the Hour. To each answer, he says: "you have spoken truly" — which astonishes the assembly (a stranger who **validates** the Prophet's answers?). Then he vanishes. The Prophet then reveals: "That was Jibrīl, come to teach you your religion."

This is an event of **cosmic** magnitude within the Islamic framework — comparable to the visit of the angels to Ibrāhīm or to Lūṭ in the Quran. An angel in human form, visible to all, publicly teaching the foundations of religion. If this event took place, it is **virtually unique in the history of Humanity** according to the tradition itself.

## The text — Muslim, Kitāb al-Īmān, n° 8

### The preamble

The hadith does not begin with the angel scene. It begins with a **political context** — and this context is revealing.

Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur relates ([pp. 28-29](https://shamela.ws/book/711/80#p1)):

> كان أول من قال في القدر بالبصرة **معبد الجهني**، فانطلقت أنا و**حميد بن عبد الرحمن الحميري** حاجين أو معتمرين، فقلنا: **لو لقينا أحدا من أصحاب رسول الله ﷺ، فسألناه عما يقول هؤلاء في القدر**

> "The first to speak about qadar in Baṣra was **Maʿbad al-Juhanī**. I set out with **Ḥumayd ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Ḥimyarī** for pilgrimage or ʿumra, and we said to ourselves: **if we could meet a Companion of the Prophet to ask him about what these people say about qadar**..."

Then:

> فوُفِّق لنا عبد الله بن عمر بن الخطاب داخلا المسجد، فاكتنفته أنا وصاحبي، أحدنا عن يمينه، والآخر عن شماله [...] إنه قد **ظهر قبلنا ناس يقرؤون القرآن، ويتقفرون العلم** [...] وأنهم **يزعمون أن لا قدر، وأن الأمر أُنُف**

> "We found ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿUmar entering the mosque. My companion and I positioned ourselves, one to his right, the other to his left [...] People have appeared among us who **recite the Quran and scrutinize knowledge** [...] and who **claim there is no qadar and that things happen spontaneously**."

Ibn ʿUmar's response — even before citing the hadith:

> فإذا لقيت أولئك **فأخبرهم أني بريء منهم، وأنهم برآء مني**، والذي يحلف به عبد الله بن عمر **لو أن لأحدهم مثل أحد ذهبا فأنفقه، ما قبل الله منه حتى يؤمن بالقدر**

> "If you meet them, **tell them I disavow them and they disavow me**. By Him by whom Ibn ʿUmar swears: **even if one of them spent the equivalent of Mount Uḥud in gold, God would not accept it from him until he believes in qadar.**"

**Then** — and only after this political condemnation — Ibn ʿUmar says "حدثني أبي" (my father told me) and launches into the angel scene.

### What the preamble reveals

The hadith is not told as a spontaneous memory. It is **solicited** — by two men who came specifically to ask a question about qadar. And Ibn ʿUmar's response, before even citing the hadith, is a **condemnation** of the Qadariyya.

The hadith of Jibrīl serves as **textual proof**: in the angel scene, qadar features among the **pillars of faith** (أن تؤمن بالقدر خيره وشره). This is not a secondary detail — it is the reason why the hadith is invoked.

The question is: **why does this cosmic hadith only emerge in the context of a specific political debate, in Baṣra, at the turn of the 1st century?**

## The chain — a double tafarrud

### The transmission circuit

```
ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb (witness of the event)
    └── Ibn ʿUmar (ʿAbd Allāh, reports from his father)
            └── Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur  ←  SOLE transmitter from Ibn ʿUmar
                    └── ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda  ←  SOLE transmitter from Yaḥyā
                            ├── Kahmas
                            ├── Maṭar al-Warrāq
                            ├── ʿUthmān ibn Ghiyāth
                            └── Sulaymān al-Taymī
```

This is a **double tafarrud**:
1. Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur is the **only** one to transmit from Ibn ʿUmar
2. ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda is the **only** one to transmit from Yaḥyā

The funnel only opens (four transmitters) at the level of Kahmas and his contemporaries — that is, in the 2nd century.

### What Ibn ʿUmar told no one else

Ibn ʿUmar is one of the most prolific Companions in transmission. His famous students are numerous — and **none** transmits this hadith:

- [[Persons/Nāfiʿ mawlā Ibn ʿUmar|Nāfiʿ]], his freedman and most famous transmitter — **does not transmit it**
- **Sālim ibn ʿAbd Allāh**, his own son — **does not transmit it**
- **Ḥamza ibn ʿAbd Allāh**, his other son — **does not transmit it**
- **Bakr ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Muzanī** — **does not transmit it**
- **Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** — **does not transmit it**

An event of this magnitude — an angel visible in human form, publicly teaching religion before a group of Companions — and Ibn ʿUmar tells it **neither to his sons, nor to his freedman, nor to any of his dozens of known students**. The sole depository is a Basrian who came to ask a political question about qadar.

This silence is not an ordinary argument from silence. It is a silence **structurally incompatible** with the nature of the described event. If ʿUmar had seen an angel and told his son, this memory would have circulated in Medina well before Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur came from Baṣra to solicit it.

## Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur — the sole transmitter

Tahdhīb al-Kamāl, vol. 32, pp. 53-55 ([p. 17168](https://shamela.ws/book/3722/17168)):

| Data | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Name** | Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur al-Baṣrī, Abū Sulaymān |
| **Residence** | Baṣra, then Marw (Khurāsān) |
| **Death** | ~89 AH |
| **Role 1** | **Kātib (secretary) of al-Muhallab ibn Abī Ṣufra** — Umayyad governor in Khurāsān |
| **Role 2** | **Qāḍī (judge) of Marw under Qutayba ibn Muslim** — appointed by the Umayyad government |
| **Skills** | Faqīh, naḥwī, muḥaddith. First to place diacritical marks on the muṣḥaf. |
| **Assessment** | Thiqat (Abū Zurʿa, Abū Ḥātim, al-Nasāʾī). Ibn Ḥajar: "thiqat faṣīḥ, **wa-kāna yursil**" |

Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur is not an ordinary transmitter — he is an **employee of the Umayyad regime**, scribe to a governor and judge appointed by the government. He is based in Baṣra, the epicenter of the qadar debate. And it is in this precise context that he is the **sole** vehicle for a hadith that establishes qadar as a pillar of faith.

Ibn Ḥajar notes that he practiced **irsāl** — that is, he attributed hadiths to people he had not necessarily heard directly.

## The version of Abū Hurayra — Muslim n° 9

Muslim places immediately after a second version, attributed to [[Persons/Abū Hurayra|Abū Hurayra]] ([p. 30](https://shamela.ws/book/711/85#p1)):

> كان رسول الله ﷺ يوما **بارزا للناس**، فأتاه رجل فقال: يا رسول الله، ما الإيمان؟ [...]

> "The Prophet was one day **visible to the public**, and a man came to him and said: O Prophet, what is faith? [...]"

### Differences with the ʿUmar version

| | ʿUmar version | Abū Hurayra version |
|---|---|---|
| **Preamble** | Political context (qadar in Baṣra) | None |
| **Setting** | Intimate (knee against knee) | Public (بارزا للناس) |
| **Description of the angel** | Detailed (white clothes, black hair) | Absent |
| **Disappearance** | The Prophet announces after a delay | People search for the man, cannot find him |
| **Identification** | "Come to teach you **your** religion" | "Come to teach **people** their religion" |

### The chain of Abū Hurayra — same problem

```
Abū Hurayra
    └── Abū Zurʿa ibn ʿAmr ibn Jarīr  ←  SOLE transmitter
            └── Abū Ḥayyān al-Taymī  ←  SOLE transmitter
```

Identical double tafarrud. The two links are individually solid:

- **Abū Zurʿa ibn ʿAmr ibn Jarīr al-Bajalī** (d. ~100 AH) — thiqat by consensus (Ibn Maʿīn, Ibn Ḥajar, al-Dhahabī). Kufan. His real name is disputed (Harim? ʿAmr? ʿAbd Allāh?). Al-Wāqidī: « كان انقطاعه إلى أبي هريرة » (he was entirely devoted to Abū Hurayra). Al-Dhahabī: « جالس أبا هريرة **خمس سنين** » — he sat with Abū Hurayra for **five years**.
- **Abū Ḥayyān al-Taymī** (Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd ibn Ḥayyān, d. ~145 AH) — unanimously thiqat. Al-Dhahabī: "imām thabt". Sufyān al-Thawrī, Shuʿba and al-Aʿmash transmit from him.

Both are thiqat — but the chain presents technical weaknesses:

- The transmission formula is **ʿanʿana throughout**: عن أبي حيان، عن أبي زرعة، عن أبي هريرة. No "سمعت" (I heard) nor "حدثنا" (he narrated to us) — neither in Muslim nor in Bukhārī (n° 50). It is pure ʿanʿana for both lower links.
- **Abū Ḥayyān has documented precedents of irsāl**: al-Tirmidhī confirms that his transmission from ʿĀʾisha is mursala; Abū Ḥātim al-Rāzī says: « لم يسمع من عطاء » (he did not hear from ʿAṭāʾ). This transmitter thus has attested cases of non-direct transmission.
- **Abū Zurʿa has confirmed irsālāt**: he transmits from ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb (mursal) and from Abū Dharr (yuqāl mursal). He "saw" ʿAlī without having heard from him.

None of these elements alone suffices to invalidate the chain — but combined with the tafarrud, they form a convergent set. And it is precisely the **tafarrud** that poses the central problem. Abū Zurʿa spent **five years** with Abū Hurayra. If Abū Hurayra had witnessed an angel in human form, why does **only one** of his dozens of students report this account? The five years of companionship make the transmission plausible — but worsen the tafarrud problem: the closer Abū Zurʿa was to Abū Hurayra, the more inexplicable the silence of all the others.

The absence of massive transmission among the students of Abū Hurayra suggests that this version is **posterior** — an account that was attributed to him after the fact to reinforce the ʿUmar version.

## The absence of al-Zuhrī — the silence of Medina

[[Persons/al-Zuhrī|Al-Zuhrī]] (Ibn Shihāb, d. 124 AH) is the greatest transmission hub of hadith in the early 2nd century. More than 2200 hadiths pass through him. He regularly transmitted from:

- **Sālim ibn ʿAbd Allāh** — son of Ibn ʿUmar, first link of the ʿUmar chain
- **Abū Salama ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān** — who transmitted from Abū Hurayra
- **Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab** — who transmitted from Abū Hurayra and ʿUmar

Al-Zuhrī had direct access to the three Companions concerned by this hadith, via their closest students. Yet he transmits **no version** of the hadith of Jibrīl — neither musnad, nor mursal, nor muʿallaq. Not the slightest trace.

And the problem extends beyond al-Zuhrī. **All the chains of this hadith bypass Medina at the tābiʿīn level**:

| Chain | Tābiʿī transmitter | Location |
|--------|---------------------|------|
| ʿUmar → Ibn ʿUmar | Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur | **Baṣra / Khurāsān** |
| Abū Hurayra | Abū Zurʿa ibn ʿAmr | **Kūfa** |
| Ibn ʿAbbās | Shahr ibn Ḥawshab | **Shām** |

A public event in Medina, before the Prophet and his Companions — and **no Medinan of the following generation** transmits it. The account circulates only in Baṣra, Kūfa and al-Shām. It is as if a miracle had taken place in Paris and the only testimonies came from Marseille, Lyon and Toulouse — but not from Paris.

## The stakes — rewriting the pillars of faith

The hadith of Jibrīl does not merely list the pillars of Islam. It also defines the **pillars of faith** (al-īmān):

> أن تؤمن بالله، وملائكته، وكتبه، ورسله، واليوم الآخر، **وتؤمن بالقدر، خيره وشره**

> "That you believe in God, in His angels, in His books, in His prophets, in the Last Day, **and that you believe in qadar, its good and its evil.**"

Yet the Quran defines faith in several verses — and **qadar is not among them**:

**Quran 2:177**:

> وَلَـٰكِنَّ الْبِرَّ مَنْ آمَنَ **بِاللَّهِ وَالْيَوْمِ الْآخِرِ وَالْمَلَائِكَةِ وَالْكِتَابِ وَالنَّبِيِّينَ**

> "Righteousness is to believe **in God, the Last Day, the angels, the Book and the prophets**."

**Quran 2:285**:

> آمَنَ الرَّسُولُ بِمَا أُنزِلَ إِلَيْهِ مِن رَّبِّهِ وَالْمُؤْمِنُونَ ۚ كُلٌّ آمَنَ **بِاللَّهِ وَمَلَائِكَتِهِ وَكُتُبِهِ وَرُسُلِهِ**

> "The Messenger believed in what was revealed to him from his Lord, and the believers. All believed **in God, His angels, His books and His prophets**."

**Quran 4:136**:

> يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا آمِنُوا **بِاللَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِ وَالْكِتَابِ الَّذِي نَزَّلَ عَلَىٰ رَسُولِهِ وَالْكِتَابِ الَّذِي أَنزَلَ مِن قَبْلُ** ۚ وَمَن يَكْفُرْ **بِاللَّهِ وَمَلَائِكَتِهِ وَكُتُبِهِ وَرُسُلِهِ وَالْيَوْمِ الْآخِرِ** فَقَدْ ضَلَّ ضَلَالًا بَعِيدًا

> "O you who believe, believe **in God, in His Messenger, in the Book** [...] Whoever disbelieves **in God, His angels, His books, His prophets and the Last Day** has gone far astray."

The Quranic pillars of faith are five: **God, the angels, the Books, the prophets, the Last Day.** Qadar is not among them — in none of the verses that define faith. The hadith of Jibrīl **adds** qadar to this Quranic list. And it is precisely qadar that motivates the entire hadith, as the preamble states unambiguously.

Qadar as a pillar of faith is not a Quranic datum — it is a **hadithic** datum, carried by a hadith whose only chain goes back to an Umayyad official from Baṣra, solicited in the context of a polemic about predestination.

## Qatāda's graft — a chain without samāʿ

[[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] also transmits this hadith via **ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda** — the 2nd link in the main chain. Yet **al-Tirmidhī** declares:

> لا نعرف لقتادة سماعا من عبد الله بن بريدة

> "We know of no audition by Qatāda from ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda."

Qatāda is not the vector of this hadith — the chain Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur ← Ibn Burayda is. But the fact that Qatāda attempts to graft onto this chain **without samāʿ** reveals a mechanism documented throughout his entry: a hadith circulates in the Basran milieu, and Qatāda — the qāṣṣ who transmits everything that reaches him — appropriates it by declaring a source he never heard.
