# Hadith of ʿUkl/ʿUraniyya (حديث العرنيين)

A massively transmitted hadith via [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]]: men apostatize, steal the camels of the ṣadaqa, kill the herdsman. The Prophet orders their hands and feet cut off, their eyes gouged out with hot iron, and they are abandoned in the desert without water.

This hadith is an **exceptional case study**: it reveals four radically different approaches to the same narrative, ranging from **categorical rejection** (Ahl al-Bayt) to **unconditional acceptance** (muḥaddithīn). The methodological gap between "judging by content" and "judging by chain" is laid bare.

## The text

The account is reported by more than twenty transmitters from Anas. The version from Bukhārī (n° 5685, [p. 8508](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/8508)) via Sallām ibn Miskīn:

Men from ʿUkl (or ʿUraniyya) come to Medina and fall ill. The Prophet sends them to the ṣadaqa camels to drink their milk and urine. Once healed, they apostatize, kill the herdsman and steal the animals. The Prophet sends men after them. Their hands and feet are cut off, their eyes gouged out with hot iron (saml), and they are abandoned in the ḥarra — they die of thirst.

## The political exploitation — al-Ḥajjāj and the minbar

Al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf — the Umayyad governor of Iraq, known for his brutality — **requests** this hadith from Anas. The exchange is reported by Bukhārī himself (n° 5685, [p. 8508](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/8508)) via Sallām ibn Miskīn ← Thābit ← Anas:

> « حدثني بأشد عقوبة عاقبه النبي »
>
> "Tell me about the most severe punishment the Prophet ever inflicted."

The request is explicit: al-Ḥajjāj is looking for a **prophetic precedent for mutilation**. Anas complies.

The following Friday, al-Ḥajjāj mounts the minbar and uses the hadith before the community (Fatḥ al-Bārī, [p. 5903](https://shamela.ws/book/1673/5903)):

> « أفلا نفعل نحن ذلك في معصية الله ؟ »
>
> "Should we not do the same against those who disobey God?"

The hadith moves from the prophetic corpus to the **political arsenal** in a single week. Al-Ḥajjāj has what he wanted: a prophetic sanction for his own mutilations.

## The four reactions — a complete spectrum

### Ahl al-Bayt: categorical rejection

**ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn** (Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn), the Prophet's great-grandson, **categorically denies** the hadith. Al-Shāfiʿī reports via al-Bayhaqī (Maʿrifat al-Sunan, [p. 6826](https://shamela.ws/book/2863/6826)):

> « وكان علي بن الحسين ينكر حديث أنس في أصحاب اللقاح »
>
> "ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn **denied** the hadith of Anas concerning the people of the milking camels."

**The isnād of the rejection**: al-Shāfiʿī ← Ibrāhīm ibn Abī Yaḥyā ← Jaʿfar ibn Muḥammad (al-Ṣādiq) ← Muḥammad al-Bāqir ← ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn.

Three **consecutive Imams** of Ahl al-Bayt transmit the rejection. The text (Sharḥ Musnad al-Shāfiʿī, [p. 2537](https://shamela.ws/book/13241/2537); Maʿrifat, [p. 6827](https://shamela.ws/book/2863/6827)):

> « لا والله ما سمل رسول الله عينا ، ولا زاد أهل اللقاح على قطع أيديهم وأرجلهم »
>
> "No, by God! The Messenger of God **never gouged out eyes**, and he did nothing more to the people of the milking camels than cut their hands and feet."

The approach is a **judgment by content**: the Prophet would never have done this. Mutilation (saml) is incompatible with his character. The chain of Anas does not change the verdict — the matn is the criterion.

### Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī: lamentation

[[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]] does not contest the matn directly but **blames Anas** for having provided a weapon to the tyrant. Two formulations, increasingly harsh:

**Bukhārī** (n° 5685, [p. 8508](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/8508)):

> « وددت أنه لم يحدثه بهذا »
>
> "I wish he had not told him that."

**ʿAbd al-Razzāq** (n° 17132, [p. 18538](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/18538)) — far more violent:

> « عمد أنس إلى شيطان فحدثه أن النبي قطع وسمل ، يعيب ذلك على أنس »
>
> "Anas went and found a **demon** (shayṭān) and told him that the Prophet had mutilated — **blaming Anas** for it."

Al-Ḥasan calls al-Ḥajjāj a **shayṭān**. The word is deliberate: this is not a casual metaphor. Anas **armed a demon** with a prophetic hadith.

### Anas himself: belated regret

The transmitter of the hadith ends up regretting having transmitted it. Two convergent formulations:

**Al-Ismāʿīlī** via Thābit (Fatḥ al-Bārī, [p. 5903](https://shamela.ws/book/1673/5903)):

> « ما ندمت على شيء ما ندمت على حديث حدثت به الحجاج »
>
> "I have never regretted anything as much as a hadith I narrated to al-Ḥajjāj."

**Tārīkh Dimashq** ([p. 1976](https://shamela.ws/book/71/1976)):

> « فوددت أني مت قبل أن أحدثه »
>
> "I wish I had **died** before telling it to him."

Anas's regret is **strategic**, not doctrinal. He does not say the hadith is false — he regrets having **armed al-Ḥajjāj**. The distinction is crucial: Anas acknowledges the error of putting a tradition into the hands of power, not a content error.

### The muḥaddithīn: "the isnād settles it"

**Al-Bayhaqī** (Maʿrifat al-Sunan, [p. 6827](https://shamela.ws/book/2863/6827)) — after citing the rejection of Ahl al-Bayt — rules:

> « حدثنا أنس حديثا ثابتا صحيحا... فلا معنى للإنكار بعد صحة الإسناد »
>
> "Anas transmitted to us an **established and authentic** hadith... there is **no sense in denying** after the isnād is confirmed."

Content is secondary. The isnād is the criterion. One accepts first, interprets later — through naskh (abrogation), qiṣāṣ (retaliation), or any other legal category that can reconcile the hadith with the norm.

## The methodological gap

| | **Ahl al-Bayt / salaf** | **Muḥaddithīn** |
|---|---|---|
| **Criterion** | The matn (content) | The isnād (chain) |
| **Question** | "Would the Prophet **have done this**?" | "Is the chain **ṣaḥīḥ**?" |
| **Verdict** | No — categorical rejection | Yes — acceptance + interpretation |
| **Facing power** | The hadith is a **tool of tyranny** | The hadith is a **datum to be interpreted** |

The irony is biting: al-Bayhaqī says "لا معنى للإنكار بعد صحة الإسناد" — there is no sense in denying after the isnād is confirmed — facing an **isnād of the rejection** passing through **three Imams of Ahl al-Bayt** (ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn → al-Bāqir → al-Ṣādiq) who **reject the hadith**.

The isnād of the rejection is at least as strong as the isnād of the hadith. But for al-Bayhaqī, the isnād counts only when it **confirms** — never when it **contradicts**.

## The attempts at reconciliation

The muḥaddithīn, having accepted the hadith on the basis of the isnād, must then explain why the Prophet would have ordered mutilation. Two paths are proposed — and they contradict each other.

### Naskh (abrogation)

Ibn Sīrīn and Qatāda assert that the mutilation took place **before the revelation of the ḥudūd** (fixed Quranic penalties). Anas himself reportedly said:

> « فما خطبنا إلا نهى فيها عن المثلة »
>
> "After that, he never gave a sermon without prohibiting mutilation."

The mutilation would thus be **abrogated** — a one-time act, corrected by subsequent revelation.

### Qiṣāṣ (retaliation)

Al-Sulamī al-Taymī reports via Anas (Muslim) that the apostates had **themselves gouged out the herdsmen's eyes**. The prophetic mutilation would then be **qiṣāṣ** — the law of retaliation, a permanent Quranic right.

### The contradiction

The two reconciliations are **mutually exclusive**:

- If it is **naskh** (abrogation), the mutilation was a temporary act, subsequently prohibited — it is **no longer lawful**.
- If it is **qiṣāṣ** (retaliation), it is a permanent right grounded in the Quran — it is **always lawful**.

One cannot simultaneously say that the act was abrogated **and** that it falls under a permanent right. The reconciliation effort betrays the embarrassment: one accepts the hadith first (because the isnād is ṣaḥīḥ), then searches — in opposite directions — for how to make it acceptable.
