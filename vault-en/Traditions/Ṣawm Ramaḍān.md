# Ṣawm Ramaḍān — fasting and the question of abrogation

## The canonical position — unanimity of the four schools

The obligation of fasting during Ramaḍān is one of the most firmly established points in Islamic law. The four legal schools are unanimous, and classical authorities formulate this obligation in virtually identical terms:

**Ibn Qudāma** (d. 620 AH), *al-Mughnī* ([pp. 323-324](https://shamela.ws/book/6910/1969)) — Ḥanbalite:

> وَصَوْمُ رَمَضَانَ وَاجِبٌ، والأصْلُ في وُجُوبِه الكِتابُ، والسُّنَّةُ، والإِجْماعُ [...] وأجْمَعَ المُسْلِمُونَ على وُجُوبِ صِيامِ شَهْرِ رمضانَ

> "The fast of Ramaḍān is obligatory. The basis of its obligation: the Book, the Sunna and the consensus. [...] **The Muslims have reached consensus on the obligation of fasting during the month of Ramaḍān.**"

**Al-Kāsānī** (d. 587 AH), *Badāʾiʿ al-Ṣanāʾiʿ* ([p. 75](https://shamela.ws/book/8183/398)) — Ḥanafite:

> فَإِنَّ الْأُمَّةَ أَجْمَعَتْ عَلَى فَرْضِيَّةِ شَهْرِ رَمَضَانَ، **لَا يَجْحَدُهَا إلَّا كَافِرٌ**

> "The Community has reached consensus on the obligation of the month of Ramaḍān. **None rejects it except a disbeliever.**"

**Ibn Rushd** (d. 595 AH), *Bidāyat al-Mujtahid* ([p. 45](https://shamela.ws/book/21739/291)) — Mālikite:

> فَأَمَّا صَوْمُ شَهْرِ رَمَضَانَ: فَهُوَ وَاجِبٌ بِالْكِتَابِ وَالسُّنَّةِ وَالْإِجْمَاعِ

> "As for the fast of the month of Ramaḍān: it is obligatory by the Book, the Sunna and the consensus."

**Al-Nawawī** (d. 676 AH), *al-Majmūʿ* ([p. 247](https://shamela.ws/book/2186/3153)) — Shāfiʿite:

> لَا يَجِبُ صَوْمُ غَيْرِ رَمَضَانَ بِأَصْلِ الشَّرْعِ بِالْإِجْمَاعِ

> "No fast other than Ramaḍān is obligatory by the foundation of the Law, by consensus."

### The pillars of this position

The obligation rests on three foundations, identical among all four authors:

| Foundation        | Content                                                                                                                                                         |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The Book**     | Q 2:183 (كُتِبَ عَلَيْكُمُ الصِّيَامُ) + Q 2:185 (فَمَن شَهِدَ مِنكُمُ الشَّهْرَ فَلْيَصُمْهُ)                                                                  |
| **The Sunna**     | The hadith of the five pillars (*buniya al-islām ʿalā khams* — see [[Concepts/Arkān al-Islām]] for variants) + the hadith of the Bedouin (Ṭalḥa ibn ʿUbayd Allāh) |
| **The consensus** | Ijmāʿ explicitly affirmed by all four schools — al-Kāsānī adds that denying it is kufr                                                                       |

This is the most solidly established position there is. It belongs to *maʿlūm min al-dīn bi-l-ḍarūra* — what is known of the religion by necessity. None of the four authors mentions the slightest divergence on the **principle** of the obligation.

### What this essay examines

This dossier does not contest the existence of this consensus — it **interrogates its genesis**. The early sources preserve the trace of a practice prior to the consensus, where the fidya was a lawful alternative to fasting for persons capable of fasting. The question is not "is fasting obligatory today?" — it is "was it always so, and does the thesis of the abrogation of 2:184 suffice to establish it?"

## The verses at stake

### Quran 2:184 — the verse of choice

> وَعَلَى الَّذِينَ يُطِيقُونَهُ فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِينٍ ۖ فَمَن تَطَوَّعَ خَيْرًا فَهُوَ خَيْرٌ لَّهُ ۚ **وَأَن تَصُومُوا خَيْرٌ لَّكُمْ** إِن كُنتُمْ تَعْلَمُونَ

> "And upon those who can endure it, a compensation: feeding a poor person. Whoever does more good, it is better for him. **And it is better for you to fast**, if you only knew."

The verse poses an **explicit choice**: fasting is "better" (خَيْرٌ لَّكُمْ), but compensation through the fidya is **lawful**. The text does not say "you must fast" — it says "it is better for you." The comparative structure (خَيْرٌ) implies two valid options, one of which is preferable.

### Quran 2:185 — the verse of obligation

> شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ الَّذِي أُنزِلَ فِيهِ الْقُرْآنُ هُدًى لِّلنَّاسِ وَبَيِّنَاتٍ مِنَ الْهُدَىٰ وَالْفُرْقَانِ ۚ فَمَن شَهِدَ مِنكُمُ الشَّهْرَ فَلْيَصُمْهُ

> "The month of Ramaḍān in which the Quran was revealed as guidance for people and clear proofs of guidance and criterion. Therefore, whoever of you is present during the month, **let him fast**."

The traditional thesis: verse 185 **abrogates** verse 184. The option of the fidya is removed, fasting becomes obligatory for all (except the sick and travelers).

## The practice of the Companions: choice, not obligation

### Bukhārī — the Companions who did not fast

Bukhārī, bāb: {وَعَلَى الَّذِينَ يُطِيقُونَهُ فِدْيَةٌ} ([p. 3110](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/3110)):

> وَقَالَ ابْنُ نُمَيْرٍ حَدَّثَنَا الْأَعْمَشُ حَدَّثَنَا عَمْرُو بْنُ مُرَّةَ حَدَّثَنَا ابْنُ أَبِي لَيْلَى حَدَّثَنَا أَصْحَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ ﷺ: **نَزَلَ رَمَضَانُ فَشَقَّ عَلَيْهِمْ فَكَانَ مَنْ أَطْعَمَ كُلَّ يَوْمٍ مِسْكِينًا تَرَكَ الصَّوْمَ مِمَّنْ يُطِيقُهُ وَرُخِّصَ لَهُمْ فِي ذَلِكَ** فَنَسَخَتْهَا ﴿وَأَنْ تَصُومُوا خَيْرٌ لَكُمْ﴾ فَأُمِرُوا بِالصَّوْمِ

> "The Companions of Muḥammad said: Ramaḍān was prescribed and **it was burdensome for them. Whoever fed a poor person each day could forego fasting among those who were capable of it, and this was permitted for them.** Then [the verse] 'And it is better for you to fast' abrogated it, and they were ordered to fast."

The text is in Bukhārī himself. Companions **capable of fasting** (مِمَّنْ يُطِيقُهُ) chose not to do it and fed a poor person instead. This **was permitted** for them (رُخِّصَ لَهُمْ فِي ذَلِكَ).

The chain: Ibn Numayr ← [[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|al-Aʿmash]] ← ʿAmr ibn Murra ← Ibn Abī Laylā ← the Companions. This is a collective account attributed to the group of Companions, not to an individual.

### Ibn ʿUmar: "this verse is abrogated"

Bukhārī ([p. 3111](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/3111)):

> حَدَّثَنَا عَيَّاشٌ : حَدَّثَنَا عَبْدُ الْأَعْلَى : حَدَّثَنَا عُبَيْدُ اللهِ، عَنْ نَافِعٍ، عَنِ ابْنِ عُمَرَ: **قَرَأَ: ﴿فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مَسَاكِينَ﴾ قَالَ: هِيَ مَنْسُوخَةٌ.**

> "Ibn ʿUmar recited: 'A compensation: feeding the poor' (2:184), then said: **'This verse is abrogated.'**"

Ibn ʿUmar and Salama ibn al-Akwaʿ affirm the abrogation. But their very **affirmation** is the problem: why must one declare that a verse is abrogated if the obligatory practice was self-evident? One announces the abrogation of a license only if **people continue to exercise it**.

### ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb — mimetic practice

Bayhaqī, Sunan al-Kubrā n° 8031 ([p. 4665](https://shamela.ws/book/148486/4665)), via al-Masʿūdī → Hilāl → ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿUkaym:

> كان عُمَرُ رضي الله عنه إذا كانَتِ اللَّيلَةُ التي يُشَكُّ فيها مِن رَمَضانَ قامَ حينَ يُصَلِّى المَغرِبَ، ثُمَّ قال: إنَّ هَذا شَهرٌ **كَتَبَ اللهُ عَلَيكُم صيامَه**، ولَم يَكتُبْ عَلَيكُم قيامَه، فمَنِ استَطاعَ أن يَقومَ فليَقُمْ [...] **ولا يَقُلْ قائلٌ: إن صامَ فُلانٌ صُمتُ، وإِن قامَ فُلانٌ قُمتُ**. فمَن صامَ أو قامَ فليَجعَلْ ذَلِكَ للهِ عَزَّ وجَلَّ

> "ʿUmar, on the night of doubt of Ramaḍān, rose at the Maghrib prayer and said: This month, **God has prescribed you to fast**, but has not prescribed you to pray at night. [...] **And let no one say: I fast because so-and-so fasts, I pray because so-and-so prays.** Whoever fasts or prays, let him do so for God."

ʿUmar's remark is precious. "لا يقل قائل إن صام فلان صمت" — **do not say: I fast because so-and-so fasts**. The warning only makes sense in a context where the practice of fasting is still **mimetic** — people fast by social imitation, not from awareness of an obvious legal obligation. ʿUmar must remind them that the act must be "for God" — which is superfluous if everyone already knows it is an unavoidable farḍ.

## Traces of the early practice

### Pre-Ramaḍān fasting — three days per month and ʿĀshūrāʾ

Several converging sources attest that before Ramaḍān, the practice was **three days per month** + the day of **ʿĀshūrāʾ**.

**Muʿādh ibn Jabal** — Tafsīr Ibn Abī Ḥātim, n° 1622 ([p. 294](https://shamela.ws/book/8658/294)), via al-Masʿūdī ← ʿAmr ibn Murra ← Ibn Abī Laylā ← Muʿādh:

> أَنّ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ ﷺ قَدِمَ الْمَدِينَةَ **فَجَعَلَ يَصُومُ مِنْ كُلِّ شَهْرٍ ثَلاثَةَ أَيَّامٍ وَصَامَ يَوْمَ عَاشُورَاءَ**، فَصَامَ تِسْعَةَ عَشَرَ شَهْرًا مِنْ رَبِيعٍ الأَوَّلِ إِلَى رَمَضَانَ، ثُمَّ قَالَ: **إِنَّ اللَّهَ قَدِ افْتَرَضَ عَلَيْكُمْ شَهْرَ رَمَضَانَ**

> "The Prophet arrived in Medina and **would fast three days of each month + the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ**, for nineteen months — from Rabīʿ al-Awwal to Ramaḍān. Then he said: **God has prescribed the month of Ramaḍān for you.**"

**Ibn Masʿūd** — Ibn al-Jawzī, Nawāsikh al-Qurʾān ([p. 56](https://shamela.ws/book/38121/56)), via al-Nazzāl ibn Sabra:

> **ثَلاثَةُ أَيَّامٍ مِنْ كُلِّ شَهْرٍ، وَيَوْمُ عَاشُورَاءَ**

> "Three days of each month, and the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ."

**Al-Ḍaḥḥāk ibn Muzāḥim** — Tafsīr Ibn Abī Ḥātim, n° 1624 ([p. 294](https://shamela.ws/book/8658/294)):

> كَانَ الصَّوْمُ الأَوَّلُ، **صَامَهُ نُوحٌ فَمَنْ بَعْدَهُ** حَتَّى صَامَهُ نَبِيُّ اللَّهِ ﷺ وَأَصْحَابُهُ، وَكَانَ صَوْمُهُمْ **مِنْ كُلِّ شَهْرٍ ثَلاثَةَ أَيَّامٍ إِلَى الْعِشَاءِ**

> "The first fast — **Noah fasted it, then those after him**, until the Prophet and his Companions fasted it. Their fast was **three days per month, until ʿIshāʾ** [not until Maghrib]."

The convergence is striking: Muʿādh, Ibn Masʿūd, al-Ḍaḥḥāk, ʿAṭāʾ, Qatāda — all attest to a regime of **three days/month** as the earlier norm. Al-Ḍaḥḥāk's version adds a remarkable detail: the fast lasted "until ʿIshāʾ" (إِلَى الْعِشَاءِ) — not until Maghrib as in current practice. This detail cannot be a late invention (it contradicts the norm in force).

### ʿAṭāʾ ate during Ramaḍān — three versions of the same event

The episode is attested by **three independent chains**, all via **Ibn Abī Laylā** (Muḥammad ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān), but with significantly different formulations.

**Version 1 — al-Dhahabī (Siyar, [p. 3854](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3854))**: the most concise.

> رَأَيْتُهُ يَشْرَبُ المَاءَ فِي رَمَضَانَ، وَيَقُوْلُ: قَالَ ابْنُ عَبَّاسٍ: {وَعَلَى الَّذِيْنَ يُطِيْقُوْنَهُ فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِيْنٍ}: إِنِّي أُطْعِمُ أَكْثَرَ مِنْ مِسْكِيْنٍ.

> "I saw him **drinking water** during Ramaḍān, saying: Ibn ʿAbbās said [...] 'I feed more than one poor person.'"

No justification by old age — just Ibn ʿAbbās and the fidya.

**Version 2 — Wakīʿ and ʿAbd ibn Ḥumayd (Durr al-Manthūr, al-Suyūṭī, on 2:184)**: the most complete.

> دَخَلْتُ عَلَى عَطَاءِ بْنِ أَبِي رَبَاحٍ فِي شَهْرِ رَمَضَانَ وَهُوَ **يَأْكُلُ**، فَقُلْتُ لَهُ: **أَتَأْكُلُ؟!** قَالَ: إِنَّ الصَّوْمَ أَوَّلَ مَا نَزَلَ كَانَ مَنْ شَاءَ صَامَ وَمَنْ شَاءَ أَفْطَرَ وَأَطْعَمَ مِسْكِينًا [...] فَلَمَّا نَزَلَتْ {فَمَنْ شَهِدَ مِنْكُمُ الشَّهْرَ فَلْيَصُمْهُ} وَجَبَ الصَّوْمُ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ إِلَّا مَرِيضًا، أَوْ مُسَافِرًا أَوِ **الشَّيْخَ الْكَبِيرَ الْفَانِيَ مِثْلِي**، فَإِنَّهُ يُفْطِرُ وَيُطْعِمُ كُلَّ يَوْمٍ مِسْكِينًا.

> "I entered upon ʿAṭāʾ during Ramaḍān while he was **eating**. I said to him: **You eat?!** He said: Fasting, when it was first revealed — whoever wished fasted, whoever wished broke his fast [...] Then when 'whoever of you is present during the month, let him fast' was revealed, fasting became obligatory for every Muslim — except the sick, the traveler, or **the decrepit elderly man like myself**: he breaks his fast and feeds a poor person each day."

The **"أتأكل؟!"** shows Ibn Abī Laylā's surprise — this is not commonplace. And the **"مثلي"** is decisive: ʿAṭāʾ **explicitly places himself** in the category of the exempt elderly man.

**Version 3 — Ibn Mardawayh (via Ibn Kathīr, Tafsīr 1/215 = Shamela [book/8473](https://shamela.ws/book/8473))**: intermediate.

> دَخَلْتُ عَلَى عَطَاءٍ فِي رَمَضَانَ **وَهُوَ يَأْكُلُ** [...] إِلَّا **الْكَبِيرَ الْفَانِيَ** إِنْ شَاءَ أَطْعَمَ عَنْ كُلِّ يَوْمٍ مِسْكِينًا وَأَفْطَرَ.

Same content as version 2 but without the "مثلي."

### What the three versions show — and do not show

**What is established**: ʿAṭāʾ ate (or at minimum drank) during Ramaḍān, invoked Ibn ʿAbbās and the verse of the fidya, and justified this practice through a structured legal reasoning.

**What the Wakīʿ version adds**: ʿAṭāʾ invokes **his own old age** ("مثلي") as the basis for exemption. He does not claim that fasting is optional for all — he places himself in the classical category of the shaykh al-kabīr al-fānī.

**What this changes for our thesis**: ʿAṭāʾ can no longer serve as direct proof that fasting was optional in the 2nd century. His case falls under the **elderly exemption** — a position that is not scandalous and explains why no scholar condemned him.

**What remains standing**: even in the Wakīʿ version, ʿAṭāʾ provides a **complete historical account** of the normative evolution of fasting — from the initial optionality ("من شاء صام ومن شاء أفطر") to the obligation. The fact that he **knows and presents** this history without rejecting it shows that the memory of the original optionality was still alive. And above all: the **muftī of Mecca** applies the license of Ibn ʿAbbās — proving that this position was **teachable** and **practicable** in the early 2nd century, even if restricted to the elderly.

### Ibn Shihāb al-Zuhrī — thirst as a category of exemption

Tafsīr al-Ṭabarī ([pp. 1815-1816](https://shamela.ws/book/7798/1815)), via al-Layth ← Yūnus ← Ibn Shihāb:

> كتَب اللهُ الصيامَ علينا، فكان من شاءَ افتدَى ممن يُطيقُ الصيامَ من صحيحٍ أو مريضٍ أو مسافرٍ، ولم يكنْ عليه غيرُ ذلك، فلما أوجَب اللهُ على من شهِد الشهرَ الصيامَ [...] **وبقِيت الفديةُ التى كانت تُقبلُ قبلَ ذلك للكبيرِ الذى لا يُطيقُ الصيامَ، وَالَّذِى يَعْرِضُ لَهُ الْعَطَشُ أَوِ الْعِلَّةُ الَّتِى لَا يَسْتَطِيعُ مَعَهَا الصِّيَامَ**

> "God prescribed fasting for us. Whoever wished could redeem himself — whether healthy, sick or traveling — and he had nothing else to do. Then when God made fasting obligatory for whoever is present during the month [...] **the fidya that was accepted before remained in effect for the elderly who cannot fast, for the one who suffers from thirst, or the one who has an illness with which he cannot fast.**"

The detail is remarkable: al-Zuhrī (d. 124 AH), one of the greatest transmitters of the tābiʿīn generation, makes **thirst** (العطش) an **autonomous category** of exemption — alongside the elderly and the chronically ill. This is not "thirst caused by an illness" — it is thirst *as such*, as a sufficient motive for not fasting and paying the fidya instead.

This category has disappeared from later fiqh. No school recognizes "thirst" as an exemption from the Ramaḍān fast. Its presence in al-Zuhrī is a **normative fossil** — the trace of a state of law where fasting was not as constraining as the late consensus imposed.

### Ibn ʿAbbās: "ليست بمنسوخة" — it is not abrogated

#### The key word: يُطِيقُونَهُ — "those who are capable of it"

The word يُطِيقُونَهُ is the battlefield of this entire dossier. It means "those who are **capable** of it" — and the hadith of Ibn ʿAbbās himself (Abū Dāwūd 2318: وَهُمَا يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ) uses the same verb with this plain meaning. Three strategies were mobilized to neutralize this meaning (naskh, qirāʾa, reinterpretation) — none holds. The complete analysis of the root ط-و-ق in the Quran, hadith and classical dictionaries is in the dedicated entry: **[[Concepts/Yuṭīqūnahu]]**.

Ibn ʿAbbās's position on the non-abrogation of verse 2:184 is attested by **five independent routes**. But one must distinguish what supports the current consensus from what serves our thesis.

#### What confirms the modern consensus — insufficient for our thesis

The best-known version is that of **Bukhārī, n° 4505** (Isḥāq ← Rawḥ ← Zakariyyāʾ ibn Isḥāq ← ʿAmr ibn Dīnār ← ʿAṭāʾ ← Ibn ʿAbbās):

> سَمِعَ ابْنَ عَبَّاسٍ يَقْرَأُ: {وَعَلَى الَّذِينَ **يُطَوَّقُونَهُ** فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِينٍ}. قَالَ ابْنُ عَبَّاسٍ: **لَيْسَتْ بِمَنْسُوخَةٍ**، هُوَ الشَّيْخُ الْكَبِيرُ وَالْمَرْأَةُ الْكَبِيرَةُ **لاَ يَسْتَطِيعَانِ أَنْ يَصُومَا**، فَلْيُطْعِمَانِ مَكَانَ كُلِّ يَوْمٍ مِسْكِينًا.

> "[ʿAṭāʾ] heard Ibn ʿAbbās recite: {And upon those who are **burdened by it**, a compensation: feeding a poor person}. Ibn ʿAbbās said: **It is not abrogated.** It is for the elderly man and woman **who cannot fast** — let them feed a poor person in place of each day."

This version restricts the fidya to those who **cannot** fast — which is exactly the standard Sunni position today. Likewise in the **Nasāʾī, n° 2317** (via Warqāʾ ← ʿAmr ibn Dīnār ← ʿAṭāʾ):

> **لَيْسَتْ بِمَنْسُوخَةٍ** [...] لاَ يُرَخَّصُ فِي هَذَا إِلاَّ لِلَّذِي **لاَ يُطِيقُ** الصِّيَامَ أَوْ مَرِيضٍ لاَ يُشْفَى.

> "**It is not abrogated.** [...] The concession applies only to the one **who cannot** fast or the incurably ill."

These two versions do not prove optionality. They simply say the verse is not abrogated but applies to those incapable — which everyone accepts.

But a detail is remarkable: the Quran says يُطِيقُونَهُ — "those who **can** endure it." The Nasāʾī hadith rewrites it as لاَ يُطِيقُ الصِّيَامَ — "the one who **cannot** fast." It is the **same verb**, negated. The hadith does not comment on the verse — it **inverts** it. The sign of a deep unease: to make the verse compatible with the consensus, the word had to be turned against itself. See [[Concepts/Yuṭīqūnahu|يُطِيقُونَهُ — linguistic analysis]].

#### What proves the optionality — the version of Saʿīd ibn Jubayr

The decisive piece is **Abū Dāwūd, n° 2318** (Ibn al-Muthannā ← Ibn Abī ʿAdiyy ← Saʿīd ← Qatāda ← ʿAzra ← **Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** ← **Ibn ʿAbbās**):

> كَانَتْ رُخْصَةً لِلشَّيْخِ الْكَبِيرِ وَالْمَرْأَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ **وَهُمَا يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ** أَنْ يُفْطِرَا وَيُطْعِمَا مَكَانَ كُلِّ يَوْمٍ مِسْكِينًا. وَالْحُبْلَى وَالْمُرْضِعُ إِذَا خَافَتَا أَفْطَرَتَا وَأَطْعَمَتَا.

> "It was a **license** (rukhṣa) for the elderly man and woman — **while they could fast** — to break their fast and feed a poor person in place of each day. And the pregnant woman and the nursing mother, if they fear [for themselves or the child], break their fast and feed."

The text is explicit: **وَهُمَا يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ** — "while they **can** fast." The license does not address the incapable — it addresses people **capable of fasting** who choose not to do it in exchange for compensation. This is a position radically different from the modern consensus and from the Bukhārī 4505 version.

This version passes through an independent route (Saʿīd ibn Jubayr, one of the greatest students of Ibn ʿAbbās in Kūfa). Al-Albānī judged it **shādhdh** (anomalous) — which, in classical logic, means it contradicts more reliable versions. But from a historical standpoint, it is precisely this "anomaly" that is revealing: it preserves a trace of the **early** position of Ibn ʿAbbās, before the restricted version (fidya for the incapable only) became the norm.

#### The other routes — partial confirmations

**Abū Dāwūd, n° 2317** (Mūsā ibn Ismāʿīl ← Abān ← Qatāda ← **ʿIkrima** ← Ibn ʿAbbās):

> **أُثْبِتَتْ لِلْحُبْلَى وَالْمُرْضِعِ**

> "[The fidya] **remains established** for the pregnant woman and the nursing mother."

Judged ṣaḥīḥ by al-Albānī. The extension to pregnant and nursing women — who **can** fast but are granted the license — goes in the same direction as the version of Saʿīd ibn Jubayr.

**Ṭabarī** (via Mujāhid and ʿIkrima ← Ibn ʿAbbās):

> كان يقول: **هي للناس اليوم قائمة**

> "He used to say: **It is still in effect for people today.**"

"For people" (لِلنَّاسِ) — not "for incapable elderly." The formulation is **general**, without restriction to categories of incapacity.

#### Synthesis: two strata in Ibn ʿAbbās's position

| Stratum | Content | Sources | Scope |
|--------|---------|---------|--------|
| **Early** | Rukhṣa for those who **can** fast (يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ) | Abū Dāwūd 2318, Ṭabarī | **Optionality** |
| **Late** | Fidya reserved for those who **cannot** fast | Bukhārī 4505, Nasāʾī 2317 | Modern consensus |

The "early" version (fidya for those who can fast) was progressively replaced by the "restricted" version (fidya for the incapable only). This shift is itself an indicator of the process we are describing: the consensus on strict obligation took hold and **retrospectively reformulated** Ibn ʿAbbās's position to make it compatible with the new norm.

#### The position in secondary sources — known and fought

Ibn ʿAbbās's position has nothing obscure about it: it is **massively documented** in classical literature, and equally massively opposed.

| Scholar | Work | Position |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **Al-Ṭabarī** (d. 310) | *Jāmiʿ al-Bayān* | Presents three views, rules for **naskh**. Rejects the qirāʾa يُطَوَّقُونَهُ as "contrary to the muṣḥaf" |
| **Al-Jaṣṣāṣ** (d. 370) | *Aḥkām al-Qurʾān* | Ḥanafite — presents Ibn ʿAbbās's position **sympathetically**. Notes that ʿĀʾisha shared the qirāʾa يُطَوَّقُونَهُ. Gives three possible taʾwīlāt |
| **Al-Qurṭubī** (d. 671) | *al-Jāmiʿ li-Aḥkām al-Qurʾān* | Acknowledges the **solidity of the isnāds** of Ibn ʿAbbās (ثبت بالأسانيد الصحاح) |
| **Al-Rāzī** (d. 606) | *Mafātīḥ al-Ghayb* | Presents both camps without harsh ruling |
| **Ibn Kathīr** (d. 774) | *Tafsīr* | Compromise: naskh for the healthy resident, fidya maintained for the shaykh al-fānī. Cites the practice of Anas feeding a poor person after growing old |
| **Ibn Ḥajar** (d. 852) | *Fatḥ al-Bārī* | "هَذَا مَذْهَبُ ابْنِ عَبَّاسٍ **وَخَالَفَهُ الْأَكْثَرُ**" — this is Ibn ʿAbbās's madhhab, the majority opposed him |
| **Al-Albānī** (d. 1999) | *Taḥqīq Sunan Abī Dāwūd* | Classes Abū Dāwūd 2318 (وهما يطيقان الصيام) as **shādhdh** — neutralizing the most explicit version |

Two observations:

1. **The position is treated as a real problem**, not as a marginal curiosity. The greatest exegetes — Ṭabarī, Jaṣṣāṣ, Qurṭubī, Rāzī, Ibn Kathīr, Ibn Ḥajar — all devote a substantial discussion to it. This proves that the tradition never succeeded in eliminating it completely.

2. **The neutralization strategies are revealing**: rejecting the qirāʾa (Ṭabarī), classifying the hadith as shādhdh (Albānī), restricting the meaning of يُطِيقُونَهُ to "with difficulty," or simply saying "the majority opposed him" (Ibn Ḥajar). None of these responses addresses the fundamental problem: **the verb يُطِيقُ in the hadith of Ibn ʿAbbās himself means "be capable"** — and it is the same verb as in the verse.

Against this, the abrogation thesis rests on **only two Companions**: Ibn ʿUmar (Bukhārī 4506: هِيَ مَنْسُوخَةٌ — "it is abrogated") and Salama ibn al-Akwaʿ (Bukhārī 4507).

## The argument: why the abrogation thesis is problematic

### 1. The Quran does not say "abrogation"

The naskh thesis for 2:184 by 2:185 is not a makeshift argument — it is the majority position, supported by major authorities. Al-Ṭabarī himself, while rejecting the qirāʾa يُطَوَّقُونَهُ as contrary to the muṣḥaf, accepts a form of naskh. The weight of this position must be acknowledged.

However, this thesis presupposes the validity of the naskh mechanism itself — a concept whose foundations, criteria and uses will be the subject of a [[Concepts/Naskh|dedicated study]]. Within the framework of this dossier, we limit ourselves to observing the textual tensions:

Verse 2:185 does not say "we have abrogated the previous verse." It prescribes fasting for whoever is present during the month (فَمَن شَهِدَ مِنكُمُ الشَّهْرَ فَلْيَصُمْهُ). The two verses are **consecutive in the same sura** — one offers an option, the other indicates the preference. The most natural reading is **complementary**, not abrogatory.

The affirmation "it is abrogated" comes from the Companions and the tābiʿīn — not from the text itself.

**Ibn al-Jawzī** (d. 597 AH) provides a decisive textual argument in *Nawāsikh al-Qurʾān* ([p. 56](https://shamela.ws/book/38121/56)). After presenting the abrogation thesis (the "counted days" = 3 days/month, abrogated by "شهر رمضان"), he writes:

> وَقَدْ زَعَمَ أَرْبَابُ هَذَا الْقَوْلِ أَنَّ الْآيَةَ مَنْسُوخَةٌ بِقَوْلِهِ: شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ. **وَفِي هَذَا بُعْدٌ كَثِيرٌ**، لِأَنَّ قَوْلَهُ: شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ جَاءَ عَقِيبَ قَوْلِهِ: كُتِبَ عَلَيْكُمُ الصِّيَامُ، **فَهُوَ كَالتَّفْسِيرِ لِلصِّيَامِ وَالْبَيَانِ لَهُ**

> "The proponents of this position claim that the verse is abrogated by 'the month of Ramaḍān.' **This is a very far-fetched claim** (بُعْدٌ كَثِيرٌ), because 'the month of Ramaḍān' comes immediately after 'it has been prescribed for you to fast' — **it is an explanation and clarification of fasting**, [not a replacement]."

What to retain — and not retain — from this citation:

**The textual observation is unassailable**: "شهر رمضان" and "كتب عليكم الصيام" form a single continuous discourse, not two successive norms one of which annuls the other. The very mechanism of naskh is textually unjustifiable here — this is a **classical and mainstream** scholar, a recognized specialist on abrogation, who says it.

**But Ibn al-Jawzī's conclusion is not ours.** He infers that Ramaḍān was *always* the prescribed fast — not that there was an optional phase. His argument destroys the chronology of naskh (3 days/month → Ramaḍān), but does not challenge the obligation itself. What we retain is solely his demolition of the abrogation mechanism: if 2:185 cannot abrogate 2:184, then **the verse of the fidya remains in effect as is** — exactly what Ibn ʿAbbās says ("لَيْسَتْ بِمَنْسُوخَةٍ"). Ibn al-Jawzī provides the textual lever, Ibn ʿAbbās draws the normative consequence.

### 2. The practice contradicts the abrogation

If the abrogation were an established fact from the Prophet's era, one should not find:
- Companions freely choosing the fidya instead of fasting (Bukhārī, p. 3110)
- Ibn ʿAbbās maintaining the fidya **for those who can fast**: "وَهُمَا يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ" (Abū Dāwūd 2318), "هي للناس اليوم قائمة" (Ṭabarī)
- ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ, muftī of Mecca, drinking water during Ramaḍān citing Ibn ʿAbbās (Siyar, p. 3854)
- A need to **declare** the abrogation (Ibn ʿUmar, Salama ibn al-Akwaʿ) — which implies the opposite position still existed

The declaration of abrogation is a **normative** act, not a historical fact. It aims to close a debate that existed within the 1st-century community itself.

### 3. The recurring pattern of late hadiths

The same phenomenon is observed elsewhere: an ancient divergence among scholars → the appearance in the 2nd century of hadiths ruling in one direction → the retroactive qualification of the losing position as "abrogated." See [[Traditions/Jald shārib al-khamr]] (divergence on the wine ḥadd), [[Traditions/Prohibition of carnivores]] (prohibition unknown in the Ḥijāz).

It is not that the early scholars were unaware of abrogation — it is that **the abrogation did not exist** as a historical event. It is the **ex post rationalization** of a change in consensus.

## The kaffāra — the silence of the Companions

If the Ramaḍān fast were a strict obligation from the Prophet's era, the question of the **penalty for breaking the fast** (kaffāra) would have been settled by the Companions. Yet **no Companion speaks on this subject**. The debate only appears with the tābiʿīn — and their answers diverge radically.

### The kaffāra hadith — two sources

The typical narrative is that of a man who comes to see the Prophet after having had sexual relations during Ramaḍān. Two sources:

**1. Abū Hurayra** — marfūʿ (with a Companion in the chain)

Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, n° 7457 ([p. 8015](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/8015#p1)), via Maʿmar ← al-Zuhrī ← Ḥumayd ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ← Abū Hurayra:

> وَاقَعْتُ أَهْلِي فِي رَمَضَانَ. قَالَ: «أَتَجِدُ رَقَبَةً؟» قَالَ: لَا. قَالَ: «أَتَسْتَطِيعُ أَنْ تَصُومَ شَهْرَيْنِ مُتَتَابِعَيْنِ؟» قَالَ: لَا. قَالَ: «فَأَطْعِمْ سِتِّينَ مِسْكِينًا» [...] فَضَحِكَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ ﷺ، ثُمَّ قَالَ: «اذْهَبْ بِهِ إِلَى أَهْلِكَ»

> "'I had relations with my wife during Ramaḍān.' — 'Can you free a slave?' — No. — 'Can you fast two consecutive months?' — No. — 'Then feed sixty poor people.' [...] The Prophet laughed, then said: 'Take it to your family.'"

This is the canonical version (Bukhārī/Muslim). But **al-Zuhrī** himself adds a revealing comment:

> **وَإِنَّمَا كَانَ هَذَا رُخْصَةً لِلرَّجُلِ خَاصَّةً، وَلَوْ أَنَّ رَجُلًا فَعَلَ ذَلِكَ الْيَوْمَ لَمْ يَكُنْ بُدٌّ مِنَ التَّكْفِيرِ**

> "This was only a **license for this particular man**. If a man did this today, **there would be no alternative to expiation**."

Al-Zuhrī acknowledges the problem: the hadith results in **no effective sanction** (the Prophet ends up giving the dates to the offender for him to eat). He must neutralize this result by declaring it exceptional.

**2. Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyib** — mursal (without a Companion in the chain)

Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, n° 7459 ([p. 8017](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/8017#p1)):

A Bedouin comes to the Prophet, beating his chest and pulling out his hair: "The wretch is lost!" [...] "I had relations during Ramaḍān." — "Can you free a slave?" — No. — "Then offer a sacrifice." — You mean a camel? — "That is exactly it." — I cannot find one. [...] "Give it in alms." The man complained of poverty. — "Take it for yourself and your family."

Also in the Muṣannaf Ibn Abī Shayba, n° 12568 ([p. 14047](https://shamela.ws/book/9944/14047#p1)) via al-Muṭṭalib ibn al-Sāʾib ← Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyib — with a simpler version:

> جَاءَ رَجُلٌ إِلَى النَّبِيِّ ﷺ فَقَالَ: إِنِّي أَفْطَرْتُ يَوْمًا مِنْ رَمَضَانَ، فَقَالَ لَهُ النَّبِيُّ ﷺ: «**تَصَدَّقْ، وَاسْتَغْفِرِ اللَّهَ، وَصُمْ يَوْمًا مَكَانَهُ**»

> "A man came to the Prophet and said: I broke a day of Ramaḍān. The Prophet said: **'Give alms, ask God's forgiveness, and fast a day in its place.'**"

Here, no slave, no camel — just alms, istighfār and making up one day.

The two versions (Abū Hurayra and Ibn al-Musayyib) coexist in early literature with **incompatible** sanctions — and the tābiʿīn add their own variants.

And in the Muṣannaf Ibn Abī Shayba, n° 12568 ([p. 14047](https://shamela.ws/book/9944/14047#p1)), yet another version via Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyib: "**تَصَدَّقْ، وَاسْتَغْفِرِ اللَّهَ، وَصُمْ يَوْمًا مَكَانَهُ**" — "Give alms, ask God's forgiveness, and fast a day in its place." No slave, no months of fasting, no 60 poor people.

Five transmitters, five different sanctions. **No Companion** among them — only tābiʿīn and mursals.

#### ʿAṭāʾ's response — the most revealing

Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, n° 7464 ([p. 8021](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/8021#p1)):

> عَنِ ابْنِ جُرَيْجٍ، عَنْ عَطَاءٍ قَالَ: **الَّذِي يُصِيبُ أَهْلَهُ فِي رَمَضَانَ يَأْكُلُ، وَيَشْرَبُ إِنْ شَاءَ**

> "ʿAṭāʾ said: Whoever has relations with his wife during Ramaḍān **eats and drinks if he wishes.**"

The muftī of Mecca — the same one who drank water during Ramaḍān (Siyar, p. 3854) — simply says that if the fast of the day is broken, **the day is lost** — one can eat and drink the rest of the day. This text does **not explicitly** say there is no kaffāra — it mentions neither kaffāra nor its absence. But the silence is significant: he is asked what the offender must do, and his answer is limited to "he eats and drinks if he wishes."

#### The panorama of Ibn Ḥazm — normative chaos

Ibn Ḥazm surveys in the Muḥallā (masʾalat man afṭara fī Ramaḍān, [pp. 1557-1561](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1557)) **all the positions of the ancients** on the kaffāra — and the spectacle is striking:

| Scholar(s) | Location | Sanction | Source |
|-----------|------|----------|--------|
| **Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī, Ibn Sīrīn, al-Shaʿbī, Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** | Kūfa/Basra | **No kaffāra** — istighfār + fast one day | Muḥallā [p. 1557](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1557) |
| **Saʿīd ibn Jubayr** | Kūfa | "مَا نَدْرِي مَا يُكَفِّرُهُ" — "We do not know what expiates it" | Muḥallā [p. 1558](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1558) |
| **ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb** (attributed) | Medina | Fast one day + feed a poor person | Muḥallā [p. 1558](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1558) |
| **Ibn ʿAbbās** (via Saʿīd ibn Jubayr) | Mecca | Slave **or** one month **or** 30 poor people | Muḥallā [p. 1558](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1558) |
| **Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī** (via Thawrī) | Kūfa | **3000 days** of fasting | Muḥallā [p. 1559](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1559) |
| **al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī** | Basra | 4 slaves, or 4 camels, or 20 ṣāʿ/day, or 2 days/day | Muḥallā [p. 1559](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1559) |
| **Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyib** | Medina | One **month** per day broken | Muḥallā [p. 1560](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1560) |
| **Rabīʿa** | Medina | **12 days** per day broken | Muḥallā [p. 1561](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1561) |
| **[[Persons/ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ\|ʿAṭāʾ]]** | Mecca | **Nothing** — "يأكل ويشرب إن شاء" | ʿAbd al-Razzāq [7464](https://shamela.ws/book/13174/8021) |

Nine positions, nine different sanctions — from "nothing at all" (ʿAṭāʾ) to "3000 days" (Nakhaʿī via Thawrī). Ibn Ḥazm himself notes that the Ḥanafites, Mālikites and Shāfiʿites "clung to **nothing** of this hadith, nor to qiyās, nor to the statement of a single one of the ancients" (لَمْ يَتَعَلَّقُوا بِشَيْءٍ مِنْ هَذَا الْخَبَرِ أَصْلًا, [p. 1557](https://shamela.ws/book/767/1557)).

The fact that Saʿīd ibn Jubayr — **Ibn ʿAbbās's own student** — responds "we do not know what expiates it, it is a sin or fault, God does with it what He wills" shows that in the early 2nd century, **the very concept of kaffāra for breaking the fast did not exist** as an established norm.

### What the silence of the Companions proves

The fact that **no Companion** discusses the kaffāra for breaking the fast is the strongest indicator of the **late** character of the obligation:

1. **If fasting were strictly obligatory from the Prophet's time**, the question of the sanction would have been immediate — as it is for theft (ḥadd), adultery (ḥadd) or murder. Yet nobody speaks of it in the 1st century.

2. **The debate only appears with the tābiʿīn** (late 1st - early 2nd century) — and they diverge totally: Medina prescribes a graduated kaffāra, Mecca prescribes nothing, Basra demands a slave and a camel.

3. **The divergence of the tābiʿīn** is itself proof that they did not have a clear, established prophetic hadith on the subject. The hadith of Abū Hurayra, which became the canonical version, was clearly not known to ʿAṭāʾ, nor to Nāfiʿ ibn Jubayr, nor even to Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyib (who transmits a **different** version in mursal).

## Ka'b al-Ahbar and Ramaḍān — the Torah prescribes ten days

The paradox: [[Persons/Ka'b al-Ahbar]], the usual source of [[Concepts/Isrāʾīliyyāt]], here produces a text that **inadvertently confirms** the optionality thesis.

Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ (Abū Nuʿaym), [Ka'b section](https://shamela.ws/book/10495/8119):

> عَنْ كَعْبٍ قَالَ: إِنَّ اللَّهَ تَعَالَى قَالَ: يَا مُوسَى بْنَ عِمْرَانَ إِنِّي افْتَرَضْتُ الصِّيَامَ عَلَى عِبَادِي وَهُوَ شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ، يَا مُوسَى إِنَّهُ مَنْ وَافَى يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ فِي صَحِيفَتِهِ **صِيَامُ عَشْرِ رَمَضَانَ** فَهُوَ مِنَ **الْمُخْبِتِينَ**، وَمَنْ وَافَى بِعِشْرِينَ مِنْ رَمَضَانَ فَهُوَ مِنَ **الْأَبْرَارِ**، وَمَنْ وَافَى بِثَلَاثِينَ مِنْ رَمَضَانَ فَهُوَ **أَفْضَلُ مِنَ الشُّهَدَاءِ** عِنْدِي

> "Ka'b said: God said: O Moses son of ʿImrān, I have prescribed fasting upon My servants — it is the month of Ramaḍān. O Moses, whoever presents himself on the Day of Resurrection with in his record **the fast of ten [days] of Ramaḍān** is among the **humble**, whoever presents himself with twenty is among the **pious**, and whoever presents himself with thirty is **superior to the martyrs** before Me."

### Why this text is decisive

Ka'b speaks as a **propagandist of fasting** — he incentivizes fasting as much as possible. But the very **structure** of his statement betrays the early norm:

1. **Ten days suffice** to be counted among the "mukḥbitīn" (humble before God). If the complete month were a strict obligation from the Prophet's time, proposing ten days as a tier of merit would be absurd — it would be like saying "whoever prays two ṣalawāt out of five is among the pious."

2. **The gradation** (10 → 20 → 30) only makes sense if partial fasting is **lawful**. There is no gradation in the obligatory — one does not say "whoever completes half the ḥajj is among the pious."

3. **The source is "the Torah"** — this is an isrāʾīlī account explicitly presented as divine speech addressed to Moses. Ka'b projects onto the Torah the normative state he knows: a Ramaḍān where partial fasting is a recognized option, and where the complete month is an ideal, not a minimum.

## What this dossier reveals

For a single verse — 2:184 — and a single word — يُطِيقُونَهُ — the tradition mobilized several types of response: the abrogation thesis, reading variants affecting the verb, and reinterpretations of its meaning. A hadith attributed to Ibn ʿAbbās (Nasāʾī 2317) goes so far as to formulate the verb in the negative — يُطِيقُونَهُ ("those who can") becomes لاَ يُطِيقُ ("the one who cannot"). See [[Concepts/Yuṭīqūnahu|يُطِيقُونَهُ — linguistic analysis]].

The accumulation of these responses does not correspond to a simple occasional disagreement. It suggests a **structural difficulty** of interpretation: the apparent meaning of the verse — where the fidya is offered to those who can fast — conflicts with the later legal norm that makes fasting strictly obligatory.

The verb يُطِيقُ, in its other Quranic occurrences, in the hadith and in classical dictionaries, refers consistently to the idea of capacity (often at the limit of effort). It is precisely in this verse, and on this question, that its interpretation becomes problematic.

The early sources preserve the trace of this tension:

- The verse itself, which maintains an open formulation
- Accounts attributed to the Companions, where the fidya is practiced by persons capable of fasting
- Traditions linked to Ibn ʿAbbās, some of which describe a rukhṣa for capable individuals (وَهُمَا يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ)
- The practice attributed to ʿAṭāʾ, which shows the persistence of this reading in the earliest generations

Taken together, these elements suggest that the understanding and practice of Ramaḍān fasting was not immediately stabilized in the form of a uniform obligation, but underwent a process of interpretation and progressive fixation. The abrogation thesis then appears as one of the responses elaborated to resolve this tension.
