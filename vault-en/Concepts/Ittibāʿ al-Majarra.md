# Ittibāʿ al-Majarra (اتباع المجرّة) — following the Milky Way

When a transmitter receives a hadith through a **complex or unusual chain**, he unconsciously simplifies it by attaching it to a **famous chain** he knows better. The term is an astronomical metaphor: the *majarra* (المجرّة) is the **Milky Way** — the luminous, obvious path that crosses the sky. "Following the majarra" means taking the most visible road instead of the real path.

Al-Shāfiʿī glosses the expression thus:

> اتبع سفيان ابن عيينة المجرّة : يريد **لزم الطريق**

> "Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna followed the majarra — meaning he **took the straight road**"

— Manāqib al-Shāfiʿī, al-Bayhaqī ([shamela.ws/book/22797/596](https://shamela.ws/book/22797/596#p6))

## The mechanism

```
REAL CHAIN (complex, unusual)
    │
    └── The transmitter knows it poorly or has forgotten it
            │
            └── He REPLACES it with a FAMOUS chain
                he knows better
                    │
                    └── Result: a "clean" but FALSE isnād
```

The phenomenon rests on a cognitive bias: transmitters know by heart the **famous associations** (al-Zuhrī → ʿUrwa, [[Persons/Thābit al-Bunānī|Thābit]] → [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas]]). When they encounter a chain that begins the same way but diverges, their memory **corrects** toward the known path. This is not necessarily fraud — it is a **structural** memory error, a *wahm* driven by familiarity.

## The resulting rule

> When one finds for the same hadith a **famous and simple** chain and a **complex and unusual** chain, the more complex one is generally more reliable — because the simple one is probably the result of an ittibāʿ al-majarra.

A complicated isnād has no reason to be invented. A famous isnād, on the other hand, imposes itself on memory of its own accord.

## Case 1: Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna and al-Zuhrī

**The hadith**: ʿUmar prays the ṣubḥ in Mecca, performs the ṭawāf, then departs for Medina.

**What Sufyān says**:

```
al-Zuhrī → ʿUrwa → ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn ʿAbd al-Qārī → ʿUmar
```

**What Mālik, Yūnus and others say**:

```
al-Zuhrī → Ḥumayd ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān → ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn ʿAbd al-Qārī → ʿUmar
```

Al-Shāfiʿī diagnoses: Sufyān **followed the majarra**. The chain al-Zuhrī → ʿUrwa is so famous that Sufyān slipped into it naturally, when the real intermediary is **Ḥumayd ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān**. Mālik is right, Sufyān is wrong.

> قال يونس بن عبد الأعلى : قال لي الشافعي في هذا الحديث : اتبع سفيان ابن عيينة - في قوله الزهري عن عروة عن عبد الرحمن - **المجرّة** : يريد لزم الطريق

Source: Manāqib al-Shāfiʿī ([shamela.ws/book/22797/596](https://shamela.ws/book/22797/596#p6))

## Case 2: al-Mubārak ibn Faḍāla and Thābit al-Bunānī

**The hadith**: "When a man loves his brother, let him tell him" (*idhā aḥabba al-rajul akhāhu fa-l-yuʿlimhu*).

**What al-Mubārak ibn Faḍāla says**:

```
Thābit al-Bunānī → Anas ibn Mālik → the Prophet ﷺ
```

**What Ḥammād ibn Salama says** (the most reliable of Thābit's students):

```
Thābit al-Bunānī → Ḥabīb ibn Ṣubayʿa al-Ḍubaʿī → a man → the Prophet ﷺ (mursal)
```

Abū Ḥātim rules:

> قال أبي : هذا أشبه ، وهو الصحيح ، **وذاك لزم الطريق**

> "This [Ḥammād's version] is more plausible and is the correct one. The other [al-Mubārak] **took the straight road**."

— al-ʿIlal, Ibn Abī Ḥātim ([shamela.ws/book/1350/3315](https://shamela.ws/book/1350/3315))

**Why?** Because [[Persons/Thābit al-Bunānī|Thābit al-Bunānī]] transmits approximately **90% of his traditions** from [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]]. The association Thābit → Anas is so automatic that al-Mubārak plugged into Anas instead of the real link: Ḥabīb ibn Ṣubayʿa al-Ḍubaʿī → an anonymous man. The real chain is **weaker** (mursal, unknown intermediary) — which is precisely why one does not invent it.

Al-Nasāʾī and al-Dāraquṭnī confirm: [[Persons/Ḥammād ibn Salama|Ḥammād ibn Salama]], the most reliable of Thābit's transmitters, has the correct version.

## Case 3: al-Mundhir ibn ʿAbd Allāh and ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz ibn Abī Salama

**The hadith**: the opening invocation of the prayer — "subḥānaka Allāhumma tabāraka ismuka wa-taʿālā jadduka".

**What al-Mundhir says** (the majarra):

```
ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz ibn Abī Salama → ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār → Ibn ʿUmar → the Prophet ﷺ
```

**The real chain** (via Abū Ghassān Mālik ibn Ismāʿīl):

```
ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz ibn Abī Salama → ʿAbd Allāh ibn al-Faḍl → al-Aʿraj → ʿUbayd Allāh ibn Abī Rāfiʿ → ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib → the Prophet ﷺ
```

Al-Ḥākim diagnoses:

> لهذا الحديث علة صحيحة **والمنذر بن عبد الله أخذ طريق المجرة فيه**

> "This hadith has an authentic defect: **al-Mundhir ibn ʿAbd Allāh took the way of the majarra**."

— Maʿrifat ʿUlūm al-Ḥadīth, al-Ḥākim, al-jins al-tāsiʿ min ʿilal al-ḥadīth ([shamela.ws/book/13179/295](https://shamela.ws/book/13179/295#p1))

**The analysis**: ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz ibn Abī Salama is **Medinan**, but the real chain passes through **Kūfan** transmitters. Al-Mundhir saw ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz and automatically connected him to the famous Medinan chain ʿAbd Allāh ibn Dīnār → Ibn ʿUmar, instead of the real Kūfan chain — longer, more complex, and ending with **ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib** instead of Ibn ʿUmar. The hadith is in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim through the correct chain.

## The structural pattern

```
            FAMOUS CHAIN                       REAL CHAIN
            (the majarra)                      (complex)

Case 1:  Zuhrī → ʿUrwa → ʿAbd al-R.     Zuhrī → Ḥumayd → ʿAbd al-R.
                 ↑                                  ↑
                 famous                           less known

Case 2:  Thābit → Anas (90%)             Thābit → Ḥabīb → a man
                  ↑                                ↑
                  reflex                        weak chain (mursal)

Case 3:  ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz → Ibn Dīnār      ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz → Ibn al-Faḍl
        → Ibn ʿUmar                       → al-Aʿraj → Ibn Abī Rāfiʿ → ʿAlī
            ↑                                          ↑
        short path (Medinan)             long path (Kūfan → ʿAlī)
```

In all three cases, the **famous** chain is shorter, "cleaner", and connects to a **better-known** Companion. The **real** chain is longer, more fragile, and sometimes leads to a different Companion. No one invents a more complicated and weaker isnād — it is the simple and famous isnād that is suspect.

## Implications for criticism

Ittibāʿ al-majarra is a tool of **internal** criticism within the classical system — the muḥaddithūn themselves use it to demonstrate that apparently solid chains are in reality **memory errors**. It reveals:

1. **Memory is not neutral**: it is driven by familiarity. Famous chains function as **attractors** that distort the real chains.
2. **Simplicity is suspect**: an isnād that is "too clean" and perfectly matches a famous combination deserves suspicion.
3. **Complexity is a guarantee**: a complicated isnād, with unusual intermediaries, has no reason to be fabricated — it is precisely its strangeness that makes it credible.

It is the **mirror** of [[Concepts/Tafarrud|tafarrud]]: tafarrud detects chains that are **too isolated** (a single transmitter when there should be several), while the majarra detects chains that are **too familiar** (a famous combination when reality is more complex).

### The blind spot

The entire reasoning above assumes that an **alternative chain** is available for comparison. Al-Shāfiʿī spots Sufyān's error because Mālik preserved the correct version. Abū Ḥātim corrects al-Mubārak because Ḥammād ibn Salama has the real chain.

But what happens when the alternative chain **does not exist**? When only a single isnād has reached us, clean and famous — and no one preserved the complicated version? Or when **all** the transmitters made the same error, because the association was so obvious that no one thought to verify? Or again — the most delicate case — when a student received a *mursal* (interrupted) hadith and "completed" the chain in good faith, plugging into the most plausible master?

In those cases, ittibāʿ al-majarra is **undetectable**. The system can only diagnose errors for which an alternative witness has survived. The others are fossilised in the collections as authentic chains — and nothing allows one to distinguish them from the outside.
