# The Muṣannaf of ʿAbd al-Razzāq — three layers of corruption

The Muṣannaf of ʿAbd al-Razzāq al-Ṣanʿānī is one of the oldest preserved hadith collections (2nd century). It is also one of the most vulnerable: **three vectors of corruption** documented by the classical critics themselves — Maʿmar's nephew, ʿAbd al-Razzāq's blindness, and external forgers such as al-Kirmānī — converge on the same corpus.

---

## ʿAbd al-Razzāq — profile and transmission

ʿAbd al-Razzāq ibn Hammām al-Ṣanʿānī (d. 211 H), a Yemeni based in Ṣanʿāʾ, is the principal transmitter of **Maʿmar ibn Rāshid** (d. 153 H). Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal, Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn and others travelled all the way to Ṣanʿāʾ to hear him — proof of his rank in the network.

The **dominant chain** of the Muṣannaf is: ʿAbd al-Razzāq ← Maʿmar ← al-Zuhrī. It is a chain of prestige, and therefore a prime target for forgers.

**Evaluation**: thiqa by consensus, but with two major caveats:
- Recognised **Shiite tendencies** (Siyar al-Aʿlām) — aggravating for faḍāʾil ʿAlī
- Documented problems **after his blindness** (~200 H)

---

## Layer 1: Maʿmar's rāfiḍī nephew

The nephew of Maʿmar ibn Rāshid (ibn akh Maʿmar), described as **rāfiḍī**, inserted **faḍāʾil ʿAlī** (merits of ʿAlī) into the books of ʿAbd al-Razzāq — traditions that did not exist in Maʿmar's original transmission.

Abū Ḥāmid ibn al-Sharqī reports in the Siyar:

> كان معمر يمكنه من كتبه فأدخل عليه هذا الحديث

> "Maʿmar gave him access to his books, and he inserted this hadith."

The mechanism:
1. ʿAbd al-Razzāq possesses Maʿmar's books
2. The nephew, having access to the same corpus, **inserts** pro-ʿAlī traditions
3. ʿAbd al-Razzāq transmits the whole as coming from Maʿmar
4. The critics detect faḍāʾil unknown in his corpus

See: [[Concepts/Falsification of nusakh]] — case 2a.

---

## Layer 2: blindness and talqīn

ʿAbd al-Razzāq went **blind** towards the end of his life (~200 H). After his blindness, people would come and **prompt him with hadiths** (talqīn) he had never transmitted, and he accepted them without verifying, no longer able to consult his books.

Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal makes the distinction:

> هؤلاء سمعوا بعدما عمي كان يلقن فلقنوه وليس في كتبه

> "Those people heard [from him] after he went blind. He was prompted and he accepted — and it was not in his books."

| Period | Status | Reason |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Before blindness (~197 H) | **Reliable** — Aḥmad and others heard him in Ṣanʿāʾ | Books intact, memory active |
| After blindness | **Suspect** — talqīn | Can no longer verify his own books |

See: [[Concepts/Taḥammul al-ḥadīth]] — talqīn section; [[Concepts/Al-Ziyāda fī al-kutub]] — Aḥmad on the blind man.

---

## Layer 3: Muḥammad ibn ʿUkāsha al-Kirmānī — external forger

### Profile

**Muḥammad ibn ʿUkāsha ibn Muḥṣin**, Abū ʿAbd Allāh, known as **al-Kirmānī** (Persian nisba, from Kirmān). He is also identified under the name **Muḥammad ibn Muḥṣin al-ʿUkāshī** — attributed to his distant ancestor ʿUkāsha ibn Muḥṣin al-Asadī, Companion of the Prophet. The rijāl sources mention him under both names, which has sometimes created identity confusion.

### The jarḥ — critical consensus

| Critic | Judgement | Source |
|----------|----------|--------|
| Al-Bukhārī | **منكر الحديث** (munkar al-ḥadīth) | Mīzān [p. 1827](https://shamela.ws/book/1692/1827) |
| Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn | **كذاب** (kadhdhāb — liar) | Mīzān [p. 1827](https://shamela.ws/book/1692/1827) |
| Al-Dāraquṭnī | **يضع الحديث** (waḍḍāʿ — fabricates hadith) | Mīzān [p. 650](https://shamela.ws/book/1692/650) |
| Ibn ʿAdī | **كلها مناكير موضوعة** (all munkar and fabricated) | Al-Kāmil [p. 3578](https://shamela.ws/book/12579/3578) |

Four of the greatest classical critics converge: he is a **proven forger**.

### The method — usurpation of the Muṣannaf's chain

Al-Kirmānī fabricates hadiths using the Muṣannaf's prestige chain:

> **ʿAbd al-Razzāq ← Maʿmar ← al-Zuhrī**

Al-Dhahabī documents this in the Mīzān ([p. 650](https://shamela.ws/book/1692/650)) by citing an example:

> حدثنا محمد بن عكاشة الكرماني، حدثنا عبد الرزاق، حدثنا معمر، عن الزهري، عن سعيد، عن أبي هريرة

> "Muḥammad ibn ʿUkāsha al-Kirmānī told us, from ʿAbd al-Razzāq, from Maʿmar, from al-Zuhrī, from Saʿīd, from Abū Hurayra…"

There follows an absurd hadith about incense for pregnant women. Al-Dhahabī comments: **هو محمد بن محصن، دلسوه ونسبوه إلى جده البعيد** — "He is Muḥammad ibn Muḥṣin; they disguised him by attributing him to his distant ancestor."

### The Khorasanian trio — 10,000 fabricated hadiths

Al-Kirmānī does not act alone. Ibn al-Jawzī (al-Mawḍūʿāt) places him in a **trio of Khorasanian forgers**:

1. **Al-Juwaybārī** (Muḥammad ibn Saʿīd al-Juwaybārī)
2. **Al-Kirmānī** (Muḥammad ibn ʿUkāsha)
3. **Al-Fāriyānī** (Aḥmad ibn ʿAbd Allāh)

These three forgers are said to have fabricated between them **more than 10,000 hadiths**, injected into the most prestigious transmission circuits.

### Ibn ʿAdī — the final verdict

In al-Kāmil ([p. 3578](https://shamela.ws/book/12579/3578)), after citing a series of hadiths fabricated by al-ʿUkāshī via the chains of al-Awzāʿī, Ibrāhīm ibn Abī ʿAbla, Jaʿfar ibn Burqān and al-Aʿmash, Ibn ʿAdī concludes:

> هذه الأحاديث بأسانيدها مع غير هذا مما لم أذكره لمحمد بن إسحاق العكاشي كلها مناكير موضوعة

> "These hadiths, with their isnāds, along with others that I have not mentioned, attributed to Muḥammad ibn Isḥāq al-ʿUkāshī — **all are munkar and fabricated**."

## The counter-argument: "the criticism worked"

The objection is head-on: if al-Bukhārī, Ibn Maʿīn, al-Dāraquṭnī and Ibn ʿAdī **identified** al-Kirmānī as a forger, the nephew as an interpolator, and blindness as a risk factor — then the system **detected and neutralised** the threat. Where is the problem?

Four responses.

### 1. Survivorship bias

We know al-Kirmānī because he was **caught**. But the system can only document its successes, not its failures. How many more skilful forgers — who did not fabricate hadiths as grotesque as the one about incense for pregnant women — were never identified? The Khorasanian trio (al-Juwaybārī, al-Kirmānī, al-Fāriyānī) was spotted because of its **excess**: more than 10,000 hadiths. A modest forger, producing a few dozen plausible traditions, flies under the radar.

### 2. Detection does not equal cleanup

Al-Kirmānī was identified, but his hadiths were not **systematically removed** from the circulating corpus. Ibn ʿAdī himself admits the extent of the problem: after citing a series of examples, he adds "**مع غير هذا مما لم أذكره**" — "and others [hadiths] that I have not mentioned." The catalogue of fabrications is larger than what is documented. Identifying the forger is not the same as identifying — and eliminating — each of his fabrications.

### 3. Detection rests on the name, not the content

The critics spotted al-Kirmānī because transmitters **named** him in their isnāds. But al-Dhahabī notes himself (Mīzān, [p. 650](https://shamela.ws/book/1692/650)):

> دلسوه ونسبوه إلى جده البعيد

> "They disguised him (dallasūhu) by attributing him to his distant ancestor."

A hadith circulating under "Muḥammad ibn Muḥṣin" instead of "Muḥammad ibn ʿUkāsha" escapes the filter if the reader does not make the connection. Al-Dhahabī himself needs **two separate entries** in the Mīzān (7202 and 7956) to cover the two identities of the same man. How many readers, over the centuries, have made the link?

### 4. The convergence of layers — the real argument

Each layer taken in isolation is perhaps "manageable". But the Muṣannaf is the **only corpus** to simultaneously suffer:
- internal interpolation (nephew)
- corruption through loss of control (blindness + talqīn)
- external fabrication through isnād usurpation (al-Kirmānī and his associates)

Classical criticism identified each vector **separately**. But no one ever attempted an **audit of the Muṣannaf** tradition by tradition to distinguish the layers. And this is where the system reaches its limit: when a hadith appears in the Muṣannaf with the chain ʿAbd al-Razzāq ← Maʿmar ← al-Zuhrī, it is **structurally impossible** to determine whether it belongs to the authentic transmission, the nephew's interpolation, the post-blindness talqīn, or al-Kirmānī's fabrication. The jarḥ (negative criticism) of the transmitter resolves nothing here, because the reference transmitter — ʿAbd al-Razzāq — is **himself thiqa**.

---

## Critical awareness — the scholars who knew

The classical scholars were not unaware of these problems — and it is precisely this awareness that makes the situation striking:

- **Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal** distinguishes before/after blindness — what he heard from ʿAbd al-Razzāq in 197 H is reliable; the rest is suspect
- **Abū Ḥātim al-Rāzī**: "يكتب حديثه ولا يحتج به" — one writes down his hadith but does not use it as proof
- The systemic awareness of the risk of insertion into books is documented with **Mālik**, **Aḥmad**, **Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn** — see [[Concepts/Al-Ziyāda fī al-kutub]]

They **knew** — and despite this, the Muṣannaf continued to circulate, and its traditions to be integrated into later collections, often without the precautions recommended by Aḥmad.
