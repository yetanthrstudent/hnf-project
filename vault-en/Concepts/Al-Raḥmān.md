# Al-Raḥmān — a proper name, not an attribute

## The thesis

**Al-Raḥmān** (الرحمن) is not a divine attribute meaning "the Most Merciful" — it is a **proper name of God** borrowed from South Arabian monotheism, where it is attested in the form **Raḥmānān** (𐩧𐩢𐩣𐩬𐩬) in late Sabaic inscriptions (4th-6th century). If this identification is correct, then the Islamic tradition — tafsīr, hadith, theology — has **misunderstood** one of the most fundamental words of the Quran, and has retrospectively transformed it into an attribute of mercy.

## The linguistic coherence test — al-Raḥīm vs al-Raḥmān

Before any epigraphic or historical discussion, the Quran itself provides a decisive test. If al-Raḥmān and al-Raḥīm both derive from the root r-ḥ-m (mercy), they should appear in **comparable semantic contexts**. Yet this is not the case at all.

### Al-Raḥīm — an attribute of mercy, without exception

The word **al-Raḥīm** (الرحيم) appears **115 times** in the Quran. Its semantic field is **univocal**:

- **~70 occurrences** in the formula **غَفُورٌ رَحِيمٌ** (ghafūr raḥīm, "Forgiving, Merciful") — contexts of repentance, forgiveness, divine clemency
- Formulas of **duʿāʾ** and invocation: **وَتُبْ عَلَيْنَا إِنَّكَ أَنتَ التَّوَّابُ الرَّحِيمُ** (2:128) — "Accept our repentance, You are the Accepter of repentance, the Merciful"
- Paired with other attributes of gentleness: **رَءُوفٌ رَحِيمٌ** (raʾūf raḥīm, "Kind, Merciful") — 9:128, 22:65, 57:9
- In the divine "double aspect": **شَدِيدُ الْعِقَابِ** ... **الرَّحِيمُ** — power and mercy in balance (never mercy alone in a context of punishment)

Al-Raḥīm is **always** linked to mercy, forgiveness, benevolence. Not a single occurrence places it in a context of threat, punishment, or coercive sovereignty. It is a semantically **transparent** and **consistent** attribute: it means exactly what the root r-ḥ-m implies.

### Al-Raḥmān — a word that escapes mercy

If al-Raḥmān were an intensive of the same root ("the *Most* Merciful"), one would expect it to appear in contexts **at least as merciful** as al-Raḥīm — and even more so. Yet the Quran does exactly the opposite. Al-Raḥmān appears **57 times** (excluding the Basmala), and a striking number of these occurrences are found in contexts of **punishment**, **threat**, and **coercive sovereignty**:

**Punishment and threat:**

- **19:45**
  > إِنِّي أَخَافُ أَن يَمَسَّكَ **عَذَابٌ مِّنَ الرَّحْمَنِ**
  >
  > "I fear that a **punishment from al-Raḥmān** may touch you" — *ʿadhāb min al-Raḥmān*: punishment issued directly from al-Raḥmān

- **36:23**
  > إِنْ يُرِدْنِ **الرَّحْمَنُ بِضُرٍّ**
  >
  > "If **al-Raḥmān** wants to **harm** me (ḍurr)" — harm inflicted by al-Raḥmān

- **19:75**
  > حَتَّىٰ إِذَا رَأَوْا مَا يُوعَدُونَ إِمَّا الْعَذَابَ وَإِمَّا السَّاعَةَ فَسَيَعْلَمُونَ مَنْ هُوَ شَرٌّ مَّكَانًا وَأَضْعَفُ جُندًا
  >
  > Context of punishment — the preceding verse (19:74) contrasts these people with those whom "al-Raḥmān promises"

- **43:36**
  > وَمَن يَعْشُ عَن ذِكْرِ **الرَّحْمَنِ** نُقَيِّضْ لَهُ شَيْطَانًا
  >
  > "Whoever turns away from the remembrance of **al-Raḥmān**, We assign to him a **demon**" — al-Raḥmān as sovereign who punishes heedlessness

- **67:20**
  > أَمَّنْ هَٰذَا الَّذِي هُوَ جُندٌ لَّكُمْ يَنصُرُكُم مِّن دُونِ **الرَّحْمَنِ**
  >
  > "Who is this army that could help you against **al-Raḥmān**?" — direct military threat

**Rebellion and defiance:**

- **19:44**
  > يَا أَبَتِ لَا تَعْبُدِ الشَّيْطَانَ إِنَّ الشَّيْطَانَ كَانَ **لِلرَّحْمَنِ** عَصِيًّا
  >
  > "Satan is rebellious **against al-Raḥmān**" — one rebels against a sovereign, not against "mercy"

- **19:69**
  > ثُمَّ لَنَنزِعَنَّ مِن كُلِّ شِيعَةٍ أَيُّهُمْ أَشَدُّ عَلَى **الرَّحْمَنِ عِتِيًّا**
  >
  > "We shall drag from every group the one most **rebellious against al-Raḥmān**" — ʿitiyy ʿalā al-Raḥmān: defiance and rebellion

- **19:88-92**
  > وَقَالُوا اتَّخَذَ **الرَّحْمَنُ** وَلَدًا ... تَكَادُ السَّمَاوَاتُ يَتَفَطَّرْنَ مِنْهُ
  >
  > "They say: al-Raḥmān has taken a son... The heavens nearly burst, the earth nearly splits..." — cosmic wrath of al-Raḥmān

**Sovereignty and majesty:**

- **19:85**
  > يَوْمَ نَحْشُرُ الْمُتَّقِينَ إِلَى **الرَّحْمَنِ** وَفْدًا
  >
  > "The day We gather the pious **before al-Raḥmān** as a delegation" — royal audience

- **19:87**
  > لَّا يَمْلِكُونَ الشَّفَاعَةَ إِلَّا مَنِ اتَّخَذَ عِندَ **الرَّحْمَنِ** عَهْدًا
  >
  > "Only the one who has a covenant **with al-Raḥmān** possesses intercession" — royal power to grant or deny

- **19:93**
  > إِن كُلُّ مَن فِي السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضِ إِلَّا آتِي **الرَّحْمَنِ** عَبْدًا
  >
  > "All shall come **before al-Raḥmān** as servants" — universal submission

- **67:19**
  > أَوَلَمْ يَرَوْا إِلَى الطَّيْرِ فَوْقَهُمْ صَافَّاتٍ وَيَقْبِضْنَ مَا يُمْسِكُهُنَّ إِلَّا **الرَّحْمَنُ**
  >
  > "Only **al-Raḥmān** holds them [the birds] aloft" — cosmic power

### The case of Sūrat Maryam — 16 occurrences

Sūrat Maryam (19) is the **ideal laboratory** for this test. It alone contains **16 occurrences** of al-Raḥmān — the highest concentration in the Quran. Yet the majority of these occurrences have **nothing to do with mercy**: punishment (19:45), rebellion (19:44, 19:69), cosmic wrath (19:88-92), judicial sovereignty (19:85, 19:87, 19:93). If al-Raḥmān meant "the Most Merciful," Sūrat Maryam would be a **stylistic absurdity** — a word of mercy hammered in contexts of threat and overwhelming sovereignty.

**The expected objection**: a theologian will respond that a merciful God can also punish — "a punishment from the Merciful" would be an intentional oxymoron, not evidence of a proper name. But this objection does not account for the **asymmetry**: if al-Raḥmān and al-Raḥīm are two attributes of the same root, why does al-Raḥīm **never** appear in a punishment construction? Not a single "ʿadhāb min al-Raḥīm," not a single "ʿitiyy ʿalā al-Raḥīm." The Quran **distinguishes** the two words in their usage — it is the later tradition that flattens them.

On the other hand, if al-Raḥmān is a **proper name** — a theonym — the problem disappears. A proper name has no lexical meaning to respect: it designates an entity, period. One can perfectly say "a punishment from al-Raḥmān" just as one says "a punishment from God" without contradiction, because "God" is not an adjective.

### Synthesis: two words, two statuses

| | **Al-Raḥīm** (الرحيم) | **Al-Raḥmān** (الرحمن) |
|---|---|---|
| Occurrences | ~115 | ~57 (excl. Basmala) |
| Contexts | Forgiveness, repentance, duʿāʾ, clemency | Punishment, threat, sovereignty, rebellion |
| Typical pairings | ghafūr raḥīm, raʾūf raḥīm, tawwāb raḥīm | ʿadhāb min al-Raḥmān, ʿitiyy ʿalā al-Raḥmān |
| Coherence with r-ḥ-m | **Total** — always mercy | **Non-existent** — often the opposite |
| Linguistic status | **Attribute** (Arabic adjective, root r-ḥ-m) | **Proper name** (borrowed theonym) |

### The morphological argument — RḤMN is not faʿlān

The Arabic grammatical tradition analyzes al-Raḥmān as a derivative of the root **r-ḥ-m** on the pattern **faʿlān** (فعلان) — like ghaḍbān (furious) from gh-ḍ-b, ʿaṭshān (thirsty) from ʿ-ṭ-sh, or sakrān (drunk) from s-k-r. In this reading, the nūn belongs to the pattern, not to the root, and the word has three radicals.

This analysis poses **three problems**:

**1. The faʿlān pattern denotes a temporary state of excess**

Faʿlān adjectives in Arabic express a **passing, intense state**: one is sakrān (drunk) *right now*, ghaḍbān (furious) *at this moment*, ʿaṭshān (thirsty) *temporarily*. Applying this pattern to a **permanent** divine attribute — "eternally in a state of excess mercy" — is morphologically incongruous. No other Quranic divine attribute follows this pattern.

**2. The word does not behave like a faʿlān**

As demonstrated above, al-Raḥmān appears massively in contexts of punishment, threat, and sovereignty — exactly the opposite of what an "intensive of mercy" should produce. A faʿlān of r-ḥ-m should be **more** merciful than raḥīm, not less. Yet it is al-Raḥīm that monopolizes forgiveness contexts, never al-Raḥmān.

**3. The rule of Arabic roots: three consonants, not four**

In native Arabic, roots are **triconsonantal**. Words with four radical consonants (quadriliterals) fall into limited categories:

- **Phonosymbolic reduplication**: zalzala (ز-ل-ز-ل), waswasa (و-س-و-س), qalqala, ṣalṣala — doubling of a biconsonantal to express a sound or movement
- **Arabized borrowings**: istabraq (إستبرق, Persian), fusṭāṭ (فسطاط, Latin/Greek), qirṭās (قرطاس, Greek), barhana (برهن, Aramaic)
- Foreign **proper names**

If one considers RḤMN as a **quadriliteral** — that is, if the nūn is part of the word and not a suffix — then it falls into one of these categories: borrowing or proper name. This is exactly what South Arabian epigraphy confirms: **Raḥmānān** (𐩧𐩢𐩣𐩬𐩬) is a quadriliteral theonym, not an augmented triliteral adjective.

The faʿlān analysis of r-ḥ-m is a **retroprojection**: the tradition, having decided that the word meant "merciful," sought an Arabic root and a compatible pattern. But the word existed before this analysis — in Sabaic inscriptions, as a proper name.

### The silence of the tradition — and the exception of the grammarians

To our knowledge, **no classical scholar** noticed this semantic incoherence between al-Raḥmān and al-Raḥīm in Quranic usage. The exegetical tradition uniformly treated both words as attributes of the same root, distinguished only by intensity (al-Raḥmān = more intense).

The only known exceptions come from the **grammarians** — not from the exegetes:

**Thaʿlab** (Abū al-ʿAbbās Aḥmad ibn Yaḥyā, d. 291 AH), grammarian of Kūfa, and **al-Mubarrad** (Abū al-ʿAbbās Muḥammad ibn Yazīd, d. 286 AH), grammarian of Baṣra, both assert that al-Raḥmān is a **Hebrew** (عبراني) word, and that this is why al-Raḥīm (Arabic) was added alongside it in the Basmala.

The primary source is **Ibn al-Anbārī** (d. 328 AH) in *al-Zāhir fī maʿānī kalimāt al-nās*:

> وسمعت أبا العباس أيضاً يقول: إنما جمع بين الرحمن والرحيم، لأن **الرحمن عبراني**، فجاء معه بالرحيم العربي. وأنشد لجرير يهجو الأخطل:
> أو تتركون إلى القِسّينِ هجرتكم ... ومسحكم صُلبهم **رَحمان قربانا**

> "I heard Abū al-ʿAbbās [= Thaʿlab] say: al-Raḥmān and al-Raḥīm were combined because **al-Raḥmān is Hebrew**, and al-Raḥīm, the Arabic one, was added alongside it. And he cited Jarīr mocking al-Akhṭal: 'Or will you emigrate to the priests (qissīn)... and anoint their cross, raḥmān as offering (qurbān).'"

The verse of Jarīr is significant: he uses "raḥmān" in a **Christian** context (priests, cross, offering) — proof, for Thaʿlab, that the word belongs to the People of the Book, not to pure Arabic.

**Al-Māwardī** (d. 450 AH) in *al-Nukat wa-l-ʿUyūn* takes up and explicates:

> وأما الرحمن ففيه قولان: أحدهما: أنه **اسم عبراني معرب**, وليس بعربي, كالفسطاط رومي معرب, والإستبرق فارسي معرب, لأن قريشاً وهم فَطَنَةُ العرب وفُصَحَاؤهم, لم يعرفوهُ حتى ذكر لهم, وقالوا ما حكاه الله تعالى عنهم: وَمَا الرَّحْمنُ

> "Al-Raḥmān: two opinions. The first: it is an **Arabized Hebrew name**, not Arabic — like *fusṭāṭ* is Arabized Roman and *istabraq* is Arabized Persian. Because the Quraysh, who are the most perceptive and eloquent of the Arabs, did not know it until it was mentioned to them, and they said what God reports of them: وَمَا الرَّحْمَنُ."

Al-Māwardī's argument is exactly that of the Quran itself: if the Qurayshites — masters of the Arabic language — ask "what is al-Raḥmān?", the word **is not Arabic**. The majority tradition nonetheless rejected this position. Al-Zajjāj calls it "مرغوب عنه" (to be abandoned), and it remained **marginal** (shādhdh).

The fact that it was **grammarians** — and not exegetes — who perceived the problem is revealing: they are the ones who handled morphological structures and could sense that a word did not follow expected patterns. The exegetes, for their part, had a theological system to maintain — al-Raḥmān had to be an attribute of mercy for their reading of the Basmala to work.

## The Quranic evidence

The Quran itself suggests that "al-Raḥmān" is not a transparent word for the Meccans.

### 25:60 — "What is al-Raḥmān?"

> وَإِذَا قِيلَ لَهُمُ اسْجُدُوا لِلرَّحْمَنِ قَالُوا **وَمَا الرَّحْمَنُ** أَنَسْجُدُ لِمَا تَأْمُرُنَا وَزَادَهُمْ نُفُورًا

> "And when they are told: 'Prostrate before al-Raḥmān,' they say: '**And what is al-Raḥmān?** Shall we prostrate before what you command us?' And it increases their aversion."

The question "وَمَا الرَّحْمَنُ" is decisive. If al-Raḥmān were an ordinary Arabic word meaning "the Merciful" — derived from the root r-ḥ-m (رحم) that every Arabic speaker knows — the Meccans would not have asked "what is it?" One does not ask the meaning of an adjective formed on a common root of one's own language. The question implies that the word is **foreign** or **opaque** — a proper name they are not accustomed to using.

### 17:110 — Two distinct names

> قُلِ ادْعُوا **اللَّهَ** أَوِ ادْعُوا **الرَّحْمَنَ** أَيًّا مَّا تَدْعُوا فَلَهُ الْأَسْمَاءُ الْحُسْنَىٰ

> "Say: Invoke **Allāh** or invoke **al-Raḥmān** — whichever you invoke, to Him belong the most beautiful names."

The verse presents Allāh and al-Raḥmān as **two names** (not a name and an attribute) and affirms their equivalence. The structure "X or Y" implies that some use one name and others the other — exactly the situation of a community where two monotheistic traditions with different divine names coexist.

### 21:36 — Rejection by the Meccans

> وَإِذَا رَآكَ الَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا إِن يَتَّخِذُونَكَ إِلَّا هُزُوًا أَهَٰذَا الَّذِي يَذْكُرُ آلِهَتَكُمْ **وَهُم بِذِكْرِ الرَّحْمَنِ هُمْ كَافِرُونَ**

> "And when those who disbelieve see you, they take you only in mockery: 'Is this the one who speaks of your gods?' — **while they themselves deny al-Raḥmān.**"

The Meccans do not deny "mercy" — they deny **al-Raḥmān** as a divine entity. The verse opposes their mockery of the Prophet (who criticizes their gods) with their own kufr toward al-Raḥmān. It is a conflict of divine names, not a rejection of an abstract concept.

## The epigraphic evidence — Raḥmānān

### South Arabian inscriptions

In **late Sabaic** (Late Sabaic, 4th-6th century) inscriptions, the term **Raḥmānān** (𐩧𐩢𐩣𐩬𐩬) is attested as a **proper name of God** within the framework of South Arabian monotheism. This monotheism — whether of Jewish, Christian, or indigenous influence — uses Raḥmānān as the primary designation of the one deity.

Typical epigraphic formulas:

- **Raḥmānān** alone — as a divine name
- **Raḥmānān wa-Masīḥu-hu** — "Raḥmānān and His Messiah" (Christian formula)
- **Raḥmānān dh-b-smyn** — "Raḥmānān who is in the heavens"

The term is not a qualifier — it functions as a **theonym** in the same way as Allāh, Elohim, or Theos.

### The geography

The monotheism of al-Raḥmānān is primarily attested in **Yemen** — the kingdom of Ḥimyar (4th-6th century). But the theonym does not circulate only in the south: in **Syriac**, the liturgical language of Eastern Christianity, God is commonly designated as **Raḥmānā** (ܪܚܡܢܐ). The Ḥijāz thus stands at the intersection of two monotheistic circuits — South Arabian and Syriac — that independently use the same theonym. This convergence makes the hypothesis of a simple Arabic derivative of r-ḥ-m even less plausible: the word arrives in Mecca and Medina through circuits **external** to Arabic, not as a Quranic innovation.

### Musaylima "Raḥmān al-Yamāma"

The case of **Musaylima ibn Ḥabīb** is revealing. Islamic sources call him "Raḥmān al-Yamāma" — the Raḥmān of Yamāma. This is not a title that Musaylima would have given himself by derivation from the Arabic "merciful" — it is a **cultic title**: he presents himself as the prophet of al-Raḥmān in his region, just as Muḥammad presents himself as the messenger of Allāh/al-Raḥmān in his. Both figures claim the same God under the same name.

**Caution**: all sources on Musaylima are **hostile** — he is the archetypal false prophet in the Islamic tradition. What they report of his actual titles is therefore uncertain. The evidence remains consistent with the overall dossier, but it cannot be treated as independent proof.

## What the tradition did with the word

### The "attribute-turned-name" objection

Before examining the traditional reading, a serious counter-argument must be addressed: in any language, an attribute can **become** a proper name through usage. "The Almighty" in English, "El Shaddai" in Hebrew — so many attributes that function as names. Could al-Raḥmān not be an Arabic attribute that gradually solidified into a proper name?

The problem is the **direction of the trajectory**. In the "attribute to name" hypothesis, the word begins as transparent and later becomes lexicalized. But the epigraphic record shows the reverse: **Raḥmānān** is attested as a theonym in South Arabia **before** Islam, in a context where it is not a derivative of a local root but a name of God in its own right. The Quran receives it — the Meccans do not recognize it (25:60) — and the later tradition **reanalyzes** it as an attribute. The actual trajectory is therefore **proper name to retrojected attribute**, not the reverse.

### Normalization as attribute

Classical tafsīr and Islamic theology transformed al-Raḥmān into a **ṣifa** (divine attribute) derived from the root r-ḥ-m (raḥima = to have mercy). In this reading:

- **al-Raḥmān** = "the Most Merciful" (intensive form, universal mercy)
- **al-Raḥīm** = "the Merciful" (regular form, mercy particular to believers)

The distinction al-Raḥmān / al-Raḥīm is presented as a semantic gradation within the same root. The Basmala (بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم) is read as "in the name of God, the Most Merciful, the Merciful" — which is semantically redundant and grammatically forced.

It is not certain that this transformation is a "misunderstanding" in the strict sense. The mechanism is actually classic in linguistics: it is a **folk etymology** — when a language borrows a foreign word that phonetically resembles a local root, speakers end up rationalizing the word and attributing to it the meaning of that root. Al-Raḥmān, a borrowed theonym, resembles a derivative of r-ḥ-m — so it was reanalyzed as such. On top of this natural process, it is also possible that the tradition **deliberately normalized** the theonym to integrate it into the system of Asmāʾ al-Ḥusnā — exactly as religious traditions regularly absorb exogenous elements by reinterpreting them within their own framework. Spontaneous linguistic assimilation or conscious theological strategy — in both cases, the result is the same: the original meaning of the word is lost.

### The alternative reading

If al-Raḥmān is a **proper name** borrowed from South Arabian, the Basmala reads differently:

> بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم

> "In the name of **Allāh**, **al-Raḥmān**, the Merciful"

Three elements: a generic name (Allāh = the God), a proper name (al-Raḥmān = the South Arabian theonym), and an attribute (al-Raḥīm = the Merciful). The Basmala is no longer a redundancy — it is an **identification**: Allāh is al-Raḥmān, and He is merciful.

## Implications for the heritage

### If the word was normalized

If al-Raḥmān is a proper name — whether it was misunderstood or deliberately reinterpreted — then:

1. **The tafsīr** of dozens of verses rests on a false premise — wherever exegetes explain al-Raḥmān through mercy, they are projecting an Arabic etymology onto a foreign borrowing
2. **The theology of the Asmāʾ al-Ḥusnā** (the 99 names of God) incorporates a foreign theonym as if it were an attribute — al-Raḥmān is not the first qualifier in the list, it is a proper name from another monotheistic tradition
3. **The hadith** that explains the difference between al-Raḥmān and al-Raḥīm is a late rationalization of a problem the tradition resolved through grammar rather than history
4. **The question of the Meccans** (25:60) takes on a radically different meaning: they are not asking for the definition of an Arabic adjective — they are asking which foreign God they are being told about

### The proof from the Quran itself

The Quran itself provides the clues:
- The Meccans do not **know** the word (25:60)
- Al-Raḥmān and Allāh are presented as **two equivalent names** (17:110)
- The disbelievers **deny** al-Raḥmān specifically (21:36) — not as an attribute but as an entity

The later tradition resolved the tension by declaring that the Meccans "pretended not to understand" or "denied the attribute out of arrogance." But the text does not say that — it says they did not know what it was.

### The methodological stakes

If a word as central as al-Raḥmān — repeated **57 times** in the Quran, present in the Basmala that opens each sura — could be normalized by the tradition without provoking major resistance, then the question arises for the entire Quranic vocabulary: how many other terms have been retrospectively reinterpreted through the prism of the 2nd-3rd century, losing their original meaning?

This is the fundamental problem of HNF: **the interpretive heritage is posterior to the text**, and it does not always have the keys to the text it claims to explain.

### Personal note

Let us be clear about what this essay asserts.

The argument is not: "they made a forgivable error on an obscure point." The argument is: **the clues were in the very text they claimed to explain**. The Quran says وَمَا الرَّحْمَنُ — the Meccans do not recognize the word. The grammarians of Kūfa and Baṣra say عبراني — Hebrew, not Arabic. The Ḥimyarite inscriptions have been there since the 4th century, the Syriac Raḥmānā circulates throughout the Fertile Crescent. And despite all of that, the exegetical tradition flattened the word into a ṣifa of mercy and never looked back.

This is not some dark corner of the literature. This is the Basmala. This is 57 occurrences. This is the word the Meccans themselves say they do not recognize.

Above, we left open the possibility of a "deliberate normalization" — a conscious theological strategy. This is an honest concession, but the record argues against it. If the exegetes knew it was a foreign theonym and deliberately reinterpreted it, one would expect to find traces of that knowledge somewhere in the tradition — debates, refutations, justifications. What we find instead is **near-total silence**, except among a few grammarians whose position is labeled shādhdh and brushed aside. That looks more like people who did not see the problem than people who saw it and chose to cover it up.

The most probable scenario is the simplest: **they did not understand**. And if one can miss that — on *this* word, the most repeated, the most central, the one that opens every sura — then the question of what else was missed becomes dizzying.
