# Nabīdh, ṭilā', khamr — the beverages of early Islam

The absolute prohibition of every alcoholic beverage in Islam is a late consensus. The classical sources — the very ones that underpin orthodoxy — show that the first two centuries were traversed by a **regional debate** in which each camp had its beverage, its arguments, and its drinking transmitters. The Quran prohibits **khamr** (5:90). The question is: what counts as khamr and what does not?

The regions clashed over this question, instrumentalizing and creating hadiths in the process.

## Khamr — what the Quran prohibits

The Quran explicitly prohibits khamr (5:90-91). No one disputes this point. The problem begins afterward: does the word khamr designate **all alcohol** or only **pure grape wine**? The Kūfites defend the second reading — only khamr in the strict sense is ḥarām; the rest is a matter of quantity and intention.

### The Companions of Shām drinking khamr

The scene is reported by [[Persons/Ibn Jurayj|Ibn Jurayj]] ('Abd al-Razzāq, Muṣannaf, vol.9 p.244 no.17078): in Shām, Abū 'Ubayda finds three Companions of the Prophet — Abū Jandal b. Suhayl, Ḍirār b. al-Khaṭṭāb, and Abū al-Azwar — **who had been drinking**. Abū Jandal invokes the Quran in his defense:

> « لَيْسَ عَلَى الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا وَعَمِلُوا الصَّالِحَاتِ جُنَاحٌ فِيمَا طَعِمُوا » (5:93)
>
> "There is no blame upon those who believe and do good works for what they have consumed."

Abū 'Ubayda writes to 'Umar, who orders the flogging. Abū al-Azwar asks for a reprieve to fight the enemy — he falls as a martyr the next day. The other two are flogged.

The account is instructive: Companions of the Prophet, in Shām, drink — and defend themselves **from the Quran**. The flogging is ordered by 'Umar, not by an explicit prophetic text. The prohibition is being constructed in real time.

## Nabīdh — the beverage of Kūfa

Nabīdh (نبيذ) is a fermented beverage made from dates, honey, or soaked cereals. In Kūfa, **virtually all the muḥaddithīn drank it**. This was not a shameful secret — it was a proudly claimed doctrinal position.

### [[Sufyān al-Thawrī]] — he permits intoxicating nabīdh

Al-Dhahabī (Siyar, ṭ al-Ḥadīth vol.6 p.650; ṭ al-Risāla in the entry beginning at [p. 229](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/4893)) — with a comment that betrays his discomfort:

> « مع جلالة سفيان، كان يبيح النبيذ الذي كثيره مسكر »
>
> "Despite the majesty of Sufyān, he permitted the nabīdh which, **in large quantity, is intoxicating**."

Sufyān goes further — he drinks in public, deliberately:

> « إني لآتي الدعوة، وما أشتهي النبيذ، فأشربه لكي يراني الناس »
>
> "I come to the invitation, and I do not desire nabīdh, but **I drink it so that people see me**."

He makes it a militant act. The nabīdh is a Kūfite profession of faith.

### Wakī' b. al-Jarrāḥ — nabīdh more licit than water

Wakī' (d. 197H) is one of the greatest muḥaddithīn of his generation — student of [[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|al-Aʿmash]] and Sufyān, teacher of Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal. His relationship with nabīdh is documented by al-Dhahabī (Siyar, [vol.9 p.150](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/5816)) and Yaḥyā b. Ma'īn (Tārīkh, vol.3 p.484) in a series of converging anecdotes.

**He conditions the transmission on nabīdh.** Isḥāq ibn Buhlūl recounts: Wakī' asks for nabīdh, drinks while dictating hadiths, and when the beverage runs out, extinguishes the lamp:

> « لو زدتنا، زدناك »
>
> "If you had given us more, **we would have given you more** [hadiths]."

**He declares it more licit than water.** Nu'aym ibn Ḥammād recounts that Wakī' offers "the nabīdh of the elders or the nabīdh of the young?" and, faced with astonishment, decides:

> « هو عندي أحل من ماء الفرات »
>
> "To me, it is **more licit than the water of the Euphrates**."

**He refuses a wedding without nabīdh.** Abū Sa'īd al-Ashajj recounts (al-Dhahabī, Tārīkh al-Islām, vol.13 p.442): a man invites Wakī' to a wedding. Wakī' asks if there is nabīdh. No. He refuses to come. The man promises to bring some — Wakī' then accepts.

**He calls an innovator (mubtadi') anyone who refuses nabīdh.** Yaḥyā b. Ma'īn reports (Tārīkh, vol.3 p.484): a man asks to postpone the "book of beverages" (kitāb al-ashriba) to the end. Wakī' erupts:

> « ما لهذا الرجل لا يريد كتاب الأشربة ؟ هو صاحب بدعة ! »
>
> "What is wrong with this man? He doesn't want the book of beverages — **he is an innovator!**"

**He refuses to have nabīdh equated with khamr.** A man tells Wakī' that he dreamed someone was saying to him "you have drunk khamr." Wakī' decides (Siyar, vol.9 p.150):

> « ذلك الشيطان »
>
> "That was **the Devil**."

### Mass consumption in Kūfa

The phenomenon is not limited to a few figures. Wakī' himself formulates it as a general rule (Ibn Qutayba, al-Ashriba, vol.1 p.176):

> « النبيذ أحل من الماء ولم يكن أحد من الكوفيين يحرمه غير ابن إدريس وكان بذلك عندنا معيبا »
>
> "Nabīdh is more licit than water. **Nobody among the Kūfites prohibited it**, except Ibn Idrīs — and for that, **he was blamed among us**."

Abū Ya'lā confirms (Ibn 'Adī, Man rawā 'anhum al-Bukhārī, p.185): there was **nobody** among the muḥaddithīn of Kūfa who did not drink, except Ibn Idrīs, Hannād, and perhaps Ibn Abī Shayba and Ibn Numayr the younger.

The nabīdh is assimilated to other Kūfite identity markers. Sufyān al-Thawrī (via al-Faḍl ibn Dukayn, Tārīkh Ibn Ma'īn, vol.3 p.484):

> « من لم يأكل الجري ويمسح على الخفين ويشرب النبيذ فاتهموه »
>
> "Whoever does not eat jarī, does not wipe over his khuff, and **does not drink nabīdh** — accuse him."

The nabīdh, wiping over leather socks, and fish consumption form a **Kūfite identity triptych**.

### [[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|Al-Aʿmash]] — the colocynth joke

Al-Aʿmash, true to character (Tārīkh Ibn Ma'īn, vol.3 p.485):

> « قيل للأعمش : فلان لا يشرب النبيذ. قال : أبعده الله، دعه حتى يقتله القولنج »
>
> "Someone said to al-Aʿmash: 'So-and-so does not drink nabīdh.' He replied: 'May God distance him — **let him die of colic!**'"

### Abū Bakr b. 'Ayyāsh — five months without water

The famous Quran reciter (al-Khaṭīb, Tārīkh Baghdād, ṭ al-'Ilmiyya, vol.14 p.386):

> « مكثت خمسة أشهر ما أشرب ماء، ما أشرب إلا النبيذ »
>
> "I spent **five months without drinking water** — I drank only nabīdh."

### Muḥammad b. 'Abd al-Raḥmān b. Abī Laylā — judge of Kūfa

Judge (qāḍī) of Kūfa. His position (al-Dhahabī, Mīzān, vol.3 p.614):

> « كان ابن أبي ليلى لا يجيز قول من لا يشرب النبيذ »
>
> "Ibn Abī Laylā **would not accept the testimony** of one who did not drink nabīdh."

The refusal to drink becomes a ground for legal disqualification. The judge of Kūfa considers the non-drinker as suspect.

### Sharīk b. 'Abdallāh al-Nakha'ī — four raṭl before judging

Sharīk, judge in Kūfa (al-Khaṭīb, Tārīkh Baghdād, ṭ al-'Ilmiyya, vol.9 p.293):

> « كان شريك القاضي لا يجلس حتى يتغدى ويشرب أربعة أرطال نبيذ، ثم يأتي المسجد فيصلي ركعتين »
>
> "Judge Sharīk would not sit down [to judge] before having lunch and drinking **four raṭl of nabīdh**, then he would go to the mosque and pray two rak'āt."

In his sleeve, a parchment: "O Sharīk, remember the Bridge [al-ṣirāṭ] and its thinness. Remember the standing before God." — then he would call the litigants.

**'Umar instrumentalized in the debate.** Sharīk transmits from Abū Isḥāq, from 'Amr b. Maymūn, from 'Umar b. al-Khaṭṭāb:

> « إنا نأكل من لحوم هذه الإبل، ونشرب عليها من النبيذ ليقطعها في أجوافنا »
>
> "We eat the meat of these camels, and **we drink nabīdh** to dissolve it in our bellies."

Al-Ḥasan b. Zayd (descendant of 'Alī) protests: "We have never heard this — it is a fabrication!" Sharīk retorts: "By God, you haven't heard it because you spent your time sitting on cushions in the back of salons." Then, when asked to transmit more hadiths about nabīdh, he refuses: "The hadith is too precious to be exposed to denial."

## Ṭilā' — the beverage of Shām

Ṭilā' (طلاء) is grape must cooked until reduced by **two-thirds**. It is the beverage of Shām — the Syrian counterpart of the Kūfite nabīdh.

Ibn Sīrīn, when asked about the ṭilā' (al-Dhahabī, Siyar, [p. 284](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3902)):

> « نهى عنه إمام هدى — يعني عمر بن عبد العزيز »
>
> "A rightly-guided imam prohibited it — meaning 'Umar b. 'Abd al-'Azīz."

The implication is transparent: **before ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz, ṭilāʾ was permitted in Shām**. It was ʿUmar who prohibited it — and even then, the prohibition was not consensual.

### The context of the rescript — an earthquake, not a Quranic norm

The prohibition rescript is preserved in al-Nasāʾī (Sunan al-Kubrā, ḥadīth 5090, [p. 7115](https://shamela.ws/book/8361/7115)), via Ibn al-Mubārak ← ʿAbd al-Malik ibn al-Ṭufayl al-Jazarī:

> كَتَبَ إِلَيْنَا عُمَرُ بْنُ عَبْدِ العَزِيزِ: **أَلَا تَشْرَبُوا مِنَ الطِّلَاءِ حَتَّى يَذْهَبَ ثُلُثَاهُ، وَيَبْقَى ثُلُثُهُ وَكُلُّ مُسْكِرٍ حَرَامٌ**

> "ʿUmar wrote to us: **Do not drink ṭilāʾ until two-thirds have evaporated and only one-third remains. And every intoxicant is ḥarām.**"

The sources link this rescript to a **devastating earthquake in Syria**. Ibn Abī Shayba (ḥadīth 8328, [p. 9252](https://shamela.ws/book/9944/9252)) reports that ʿUmar wrote to the Syrians "**في زلزلة كانت بالشام**" (on the occasion of an earthquake in Shām), ordering prayers and alms. Al-Suyūṭī dates the earthquake to 97H: "it destroyed numerous places and fortresses, and **swallowed a village in the district of Buṣrā**." Christian chroniclers (Theophanes, AM 6210; Agapius of Hierapolis) explicitly link the earthquake to ʿUmar's prohibition of wine.

The prohibition of ṭilāʾ is therefore a **circumstantial eschatological act** — ʿUmar fears that the consumption of intoxicants may provoke a collective divine punishment — not the application of an obvious Quranic norm. And even this rescript is contested: **Sālim ibn ʿAbd Allāh**, son of Ibn ʿUmar, drinks ṭilāʾ in Shām in the presence of ʿUmar II and justifies himself: "**قد كان أبي يشربه**" — "my father used to drink it" (Muwaṭṭaʾ Ibn Wahb, [p. 142](https://shamela.ws/book/916/142)). The son of a major Companion invokes his father's Sunna against the caliph's rescript.

*(Harvey, "ʿUmar II and the Prohibition of Ṭilāʾ and Nabīdh", Islamic Law and Society 30/4, 2023 — academic synthesis outside the patrimony, useful for context.)*

'Umar b. al-Khaṭṭāb himself permits ṭilā' cooked by two-thirds (Mālik, Muwaṭṭa', 2/847): when the Shāmīs complain that only "this beverage" suits them, 'Umar accepts that it be cooked to eliminate "the Devil's portion." 'Ubāda b. al-Ṣāmit protests: "You have made it licit!" 'Umar swears he has not.

Abū Ṭalḥa, Mu'āḏ, and Abū 'Ubayda drank ṭilā' in Shām (Qatāda, from Anas — isnād authenticated by the muḥaqqiqīn).

**'Abd al-Malik b. Marwān** — the caliph who built the Dome of the Rock — drank ṭilā'. Umm al-Dardā' reproaches him:

> « بلغني أنك شربت الطلاء بعد النسك والعبادة ! »
>
> "It has reached me that you drank ṭilā' after the rites and worship!"

He replies:

> « إي والله، والدماء »
>
> "**Yes, by God — and blood.**"

## The position of Abū Ḥanīfa — the Quran, not the hadith

Faced with this reality — the Companions drank, the muḥaddithīn drank, the judges drank — [[Persons/Abū Ḥanīfa|Abū Ḥanīfa]] attempts to establish a coherent legal position.

His teacher Ibrāhīm al-Nakha'ī (Abū Yūsuf, Kitāb al-Āthār, vol.1 p.227):

> « قول الناس : كل مسكر حرام، خطأ منهم. إنما أرادوا : السُّكر حرام خاصة »
>
> "The people's saying — 'every intoxicant is ḥarām' — **is an error on their part**. They meant: it is **intoxication** that is specifically prohibited."

The position is that of [[Concepts/Kūfa contre le Chām|Kūfa]] (question 4): the Quran prohibits khamr (5:90) and intoxication (sukr). Everything else is an extension via the Sunna — that is, via hadith, that is, via the power that produces the hadith. The hadith "every intoxicant is ḥarām" (كل مسكر حرام) is not from the Quran — it is a hadith, and the Kūfites do not grant hadiths the power to extend Quranic prohibitions.

### "Kull muskir ḥarām" — from a popular saying to a prophetic hadith

The case of the formula **كل مسكر حرام** is a documented example of the trajectory by which a **common saying** becomes a **hadith of the Prophet**.

**Stage 1 — قول الناس (people's saying).** Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī (d. ~96H), the great faqīh of Kūfa from whom Abū Ḥanīfa inherits via Ḥammād, identifies the formula as a **قول الناس** — a saying circulating among the people, not a prophetic transmission. And he corrects it: it is an **error** (خطأ). The source is a contemporary, a direct witness to the state of knowledge at the turn of the 1st century.

**Stage 2 — caliphal rescript (~100H).** The same formula appears verbatim in the rescript of [[Persons/ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz|ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz]] prohibiting ṭilāʾ (al-Nasāʾī, Sunan al-Kubrā, [p. 7115](https://shamela.ws/book/8361/7115)): "**ألا تشربوا من الطلاء [...] وكل مسكر حرام**." The caliph takes up the popular formula and gives it the force of law.

**Stage 3 — prophetic hadith (~200-300H).** The formula enters the canonical collections as a saying of the Prophet: Muslim (Kitāb al-Ashriba), Abū Dāwūd, al-Tirmidhī, al-Nasāʾī, Ibn Māja — all transmit "كل مسكر حرام" or "كل مسكر خمر وكل مسكر حرام" as a marfūʿ hadith.

```
قول الناس               →    caliphal rescript    →    prophetic hadith
  Ibrāhīm (~96H):              ʿUmar II (~100H)          collections (~200-300H)
  « خطأ منهم »                   (official edict)           (ṣaḥīḥ)
  (it is an error)
```

Ibrāhīm is a first-hand witness: he lives in Kūfa, he knows the formula, he knows it circulates as **قول الناس**, and he contests it. Two generations later, the same formula has an isnād going back to the Prophet and appears in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim.

### Takhrīj — the Nāfiʿ knot and progressive elevation

The hadith "كل مسكر حرام" is attributed to at least **eight Companions** (Ibn ʿUmar, ʿĀʾisha, Abū Mūsā, Jābir, Ibn ʿAbbās, Abū Hurayra, Muʿāwiya, Qays ibn Saʿd). The most abundant chain — that of **Ibn ʿUmar** — systematically passes through a single pivot: **[[Persons/Nāfiʿ mawlā Ibn ʿUmar|Nāfiʿ]]**.

The decisive finding concerns the **status** of the hadith among Nāfiʿ's transmitters:

| Transmitter from Nāfiʿ | Status | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Mālik ibn Anas** (d. 179H) | **Mawqūf** — saying of Ibn ʿUmar | Unanimous among all his transmitters except Rawḥ |
| **Ayyūb al-Sakhtiyānī** | Marfūʿ via Ḥammād ibn Zayd / **mawqūf** via Sulaymān ibn Ḥarb ← Ḥammād | Internal divergence |
| **ʿUbayd Allāh ibn ʿUmar** | Marfūʿ — but dubitative formula | Muslim 2003d: "**ولا أعلمه إلا عن النبي**" (I only know it from the Prophet) |
| **Mūsā ibn ʿUqba** | Marfūʿ | Via Ibn Jurayj |
| **Muḥammad ibn ʿAjlān** | Marfūʿ | Al-Nasāʾī 5586 |

**Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr** (al-Tamhīd) confirms: "**هذا الحديث موقوف في الموطأ لم يختلف**" — "this hadith is mawqūf in the Muwaṭṭaʾ, without divergence." Ibn al-Turkumānī (al-Jawhar al-Naqī) adds: "**كذا رواه سائر أصحاب مالك عن مالك موقوفا غير روح فإنه رفعه**" — "all the transmitters of Mālik reported it as mawqūf, **except Rawḥ** who elevated it [to the Prophet]."

```
                    Prophet (?)
                        │
                   Ibn ʿUmar
                        │
                   *** NĀFIʿ ***
                  ╱     │     ╲         ╲
             Mālik   Ayyūb   ʿUbayd    Mūsā ibn
                              Allāh     ʿUqba
               │       │        │         │
            MAWQŪF   rafʿ/   rafʿ      rafʿ
           (unanim.)  waqf   (doubt.)
```

The oldest and most reliable of Nāfiʿ's transmitters — **Mālik** — reports this formula only as a **saying of Ibn ʿUmar**. It is the **later** transmitters who elevate it to the Prophet — and even among them, Ayyūb's chain is disputed (Abū Ḥātim al-Rāzī, ʿIlal), and ʿUbayd Allāh hesitates.

For the other Companions, each chain rests on a **single transmitter**:
- **ʿĀʾisha**: al-Zuhrī ← Abū Salama (single chain — different matn: **كل شراب أسكر فهو حرام**)
- **Abū Mūsā**: Saʿīd ibn Abī Burda ← Abū Burda (single family chain)
- **Jābir**: [[Persons/Abū al-Zubayr al-Makkī|Abū al-Zubayr]] (notorious mudallis — single chain)
- **Ibn ʿAbbās**: al-Nuʿmān ← Ṭāwūs (different matn: **كل مخمر خمر**)

### Voices against the rafʿ

Three ancient authorities explicitly contest the prophetic status:

**1. Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī** — accuses people of having **falsified the matn** by adding a mīm. Al-Raqīq al-Qayrawānī (Quṭb al-Surūr):

> **أفسد الناسُ النبيذَ بميمٍ زادوها في الحديث — قالوا: كل مُسكِر حرام، وإنما هو كل سُكْر حرام**

> "The people have **corrupted nabīdh by a mīm they added to the hadith** — they say *kull muskir ḥarām* (every **intoxicant** is ḥarām) whereas it is *kull sukr ḥarām* (every **intoxication** is ḥarām)."

The accusation is frontal: the change of **a single letter** (سُكْر → مُسكِر) transforms a prohibition of the state of intoxication into a prohibition of the substance itself.

**2. Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn** (d. 233H) — via ʿAbbās al-Dūrī (reported in Tanqīḥ al-Taḥqīq of Ibn ʿAbd al-Hādī and al-Talkhīṣ al-Ḥabīr of Ibn Ḥajar):

> **ثلاثة أحاديث لا تصح عن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم: كل مسكر حرام، ولا نكاح إلا بولي، ومن مس ذكره فليتوضأ**

> "Three hadiths are not authentic as sayings of the Prophet: *every intoxicant is ḥarām*, *no marriage without a guardian*, and *whoever touches his genitals must renew his ablutions*."

*(Al-Dhahabī contests the attribution to Ibn Maʿīn: "لا يثبت عن ابن معين." The chain passes through the Ḥanafites — making it an intra-school argument, not a consensus.)*

**3. Al-Nasāʾī himself** — in the Sunan al-Kubrā, he places alongside the marfūʿa versions two **mawqūfa** versions: one from **ʿAṭāʾ** (no. 5598) and another from **Ibn Sīrīn** (no. 5599), who say "كل مسكر حرام" as **their own statement**, not the Prophet's.

The current consensus — "every alcoholic beverage is ḥarām" — is a **historical product**, not a scriptural self-evidence. The first two centuries show a radically different landscape: the greatest hadith transmitters drank, the judges drank, and the prohibition was built progressively, through hadith (not through the Quran), against the documented practice of the Companions and the Tābi'ūn.
