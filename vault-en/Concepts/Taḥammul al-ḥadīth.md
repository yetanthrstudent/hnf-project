# Taḥammul al-ḥadīth (تحمل الحديث) — how one "receives" a hadith

Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ (d. 643/1245) codifies **eight methods** by which a transmitter receives a hadith. The uṣūl treatises present them as a hierarchy — from *samāʿ* (direct audition, the most reliable) to *wijāda* (finding in a book, the weakest). Al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī (al-Kifāya, [p. 141](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/141) sqq.) had laid the foundations two centuries earlier.

What this hierarchy reveals is that the system **knows its own flaws**: each inferior method is defined by what it lacks compared to the preceding one. And yet, in practice, the formulas of transmission blur these distinctions — a simple **عن** (ʿan) can mask any of the eight.

---

## The hierarchy and its cracks

```
DIRECT AUDITION                        NO ORAL CONTACT
(samāʿ)                               (wijāda)
   ◄────────────────────────────────────►
   1. Samāʿ        The shaykh speaks, the student listens
   2. ʿArḍ         The student reads, the shaykh listens
   3. Ijāza        The shaykh authorizes without reading
   4. Munāwala     The shaykh hands over the book
   5. Mukātaba     The shaykh writes from afar
   6. Iʿlām        The shaykh says "this is my samāʿ" (without authorizing)
   7. Waṣiyya      The shaykh bequeaths his books upon dying
   8. Wijāda       One finds a book — nobody has said anything

   ◄── DECREASING CONTROL ──────────────────►
```

Each descent in the hierarchy **removes a control**: first the shaykh's speech (ʿarḍ), then the reading itself (ijāza), then physical contact (mukātaba), then authorization (iʿlām), then the shaykh's life (waṣiyya), and finally all human connection (wijāda). The hadith passes from a living act to a **found object**.

## 1. Al-Samāʿ (السماع) — direct audition

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 141](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/141) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 130](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/130)

The highest method. The shaykh dictates from memory or from his book, the student listens. Al-Khaṭīb ranks the formulas in decreasing order:

| Formula | Usage | Rank |
|---|---|---|
| **سمعت** (*samiʿtu*) | Strict direct audition | The highest |
| **حدثنا** (*ḥaddathanā*) | Transmission in a group | High |
| **حدثني** (*ḥaddathanī*) | Private transmission | High |
| **أخبرنا** (*akhbaranā*) | Common formula | Common |
| **قال لنا** (*qāla lanā*) | Informal discussion (mudhākara) | Lower |
| **قال فلان** (*qāla fulān*) | No direct link mentioned | The lowest |

### The problem: phantom samāʿ

Samāʿ presupposes active attention. Yet the sources document the **concrete limitations** of this attention.

**Ibn Wahb falls asleep during samāʿ** — al-Kifāya ([p. 164](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/164)):

> كنت عند ابن وهب فنعس فقلت لأصحابي: **خرج الشيخ**

> "I was with Ibn Wahb. He dozed off. I said to my companions: **the shaykh has left** [= stop writing]."

If the shaykh can fall asleep during dictation and the student continues writing, what is recorded has no guarantee. Samāʿ is only as reliable as the **attention** of the moment — and nobody verifies.

**Al-Barqānī listens in secret** — Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 133](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/133)):

> فَكَانَ الْبَرْقَانِيُّ يَجْلِسُ بِحَيْثُ لَا يَرَاهُ أَبُو الْقَاسِمِ [...] فَلِذَلِكَ يَقُولُ: « سَمِعْتُ »، وَلَا يَقُولُ: « حَدَّثَنَا، وَلَا أَخْبَرَنَا »

> "Al-Barqānī would sit where Abū al-Qāsim could not see him [...] — that is why he said *samiʿtu* and not *ḥaddathanā*: because the shaykh was not addressing him."

Samāʿ can therefore be **non-intentional** on the shaykh's part. The student hears, but the shaykh does not know he is there. The distinction between formulas is supposed to capture this nuance — but in practice, how many transmitters respect it?

## 2. Al-ʿArḍ (العرض) — reading to the master

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 952](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/952) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 134](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/134)

The student reads to the shaykh, who listens and approves. The debate between samāʿ and ʿarḍ agitated the early centuries:

> وَالصَّحِيحُ: تَرْجِيحُ السَّمَاعِ مِنْ لَفْظِ الشَّيْخِ، وَالْحُكْمُ بِأَنَّ الْقِرَاءَةَ عَلَيْهِ مَرْتَبَةٌ ثَانِيَةٌ

> "The correct position is to prefer audition from the shaykh's own speech, and to judge that reading to him is a secondary rank."

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 135](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/135))

**The crucial convention** — the 3rd madhhab (al-Shāfiʿī, Muslim, the Easterners) distinguishes:
- **حدثنا** = reserved for direct samāʿ
- **أخبرنا** = for ʿarḍ

> الْفَرْقُ بَيْنَهُمَا صَارَ هُوَ الشَّائِعَ الْغَالِبَ عَلَى أَهْلِ الْحَدِيثِ، وَالِاحْتِجَاجُ لِذَلِكَ مِنْ حَيْثُ اللُّغَةُ عَنَاءٌ وَتَكَلُّفٌ، وَخَيْرُ مَا يُقَالُ فِيهِ: إِنَّهُ اصْطِلَاحٌ مِنْهُمْ أَرَادُوا بِهِ التَّمْيِيزَ بَيْنَ النَّوْعَيْنِ

> "The distinction has become dominant among hadith scholars. Seeking a linguistic foundation is forced. The best that can be said: it is a **iṣṭilāḥ** (convention) they gave themselves."

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 137](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/137))

Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ acknowledges it: the distinction has **no linguistic foundation**. It is a convention — and like every convention, it is only respected by those who know it. The early transmitters (Zuhrī, Mālik) had no knowledge of it.

### Al-Dāraquṭnī's memory test

> فَقَالَ الدَّارَقُطْنِيُّ: أَمْلَى ثَمَانِيَةَ عَشَرَ حَدِيثًا، فَعُدَّتِ الْأَحَادِيثُ فَوُجِدَتْ كَمَا قَالَ

> "Al-Dāraquṭnī said: [the shaykh] dictated 18 hadiths. They were counted: it was exact. Then Abū al-Ḥasan recited the first hadith — its isnād and its matn — from memory."

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 143](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/143))

This kind of mnemonic feat is exceptional — and that is precisely the problem: the treatises cite it because it is **remarkable**, not because it is **representative**.

## 3. Al-Ijāza (الإجازة) — license without reading

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 1165](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/1165) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 148](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/148)

The shaykh authorizes the student to transmit his traditions, **without reading or audition**. Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ distinguishes seven subtypes. The debate on its validity is fierce:

> وَالصَّحِيحُ - وَالْمُخْتَارُ الَّذِي عَلَيْهِ عَمَلُ الْجُمْهُورِ - الْمَنْعُ فِي ذَلِكَ مِنْ إِطْلَاقِ « حَدَّثَنَا، وَأَخْبَرَنَا »

> "The correct position — and the choice of the Jumhūr — is to **prohibit** the use of *ḥaddathanā* and *akhbaranā* [for ijāza], and to limit it to formulas that specify it."

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 167](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/167))

### The flaw: Abū Nuʿaym and the blurring of formulas

> أَنَا إِذَا قُلْتُ: « حَدَّثَنَا » فَهُوَ سَمَاعِي، وَإِذَا قُلْتُ: « أَخْبَرَنَا » عَلَى الْإِطْلَاقِ فَهُوَ إِجَازَةٌ مِنْ غَيْرِ أَنْ أَذْكُرَ فِيهِ إِجَازَةً، أَوْ كِتَابَةً

> "When I say *ḥaddathanā*, it is my audition. When I say *akhbaranā* without qualification, it is an **ijāza** — without my mentioning the word ijāza or kitāba."

— Abū Nuʿaym al-Iṣbahānī, Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 167](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/167))

Abū Nuʿaym uses **أخبرنا** for ijāza **without saying so**. A later reader who reads "akhbaranā" in his books cannot know whether it is an audition or a simple authorization. The Jumhūr prohibits this practice — but it existed, and the texts resulting from it are in the collections.

**The late convention of أنبأنا** — Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 168](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/168)):

> وَاصْطَلَحَ قَوْمٌ مِنَ الْمُتَأَخِّرِينَ عَلَى إِطْلَاقِ « أَنْبَأَنَا » فِي الْإِجَازَةِ

> "Later scholars agreed to use *anbaʾanā* for ijāza."

Yet another convention — and yet another convention posterior to the bulk of the canonical corpus.

## 4. Al-Munāwala (المناولة) — handing over the book

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 1219](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/1219) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 162](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/162)

The shaykh physically hands his book to the student. Two types:

1. **Munāwala + ijāza** (valid): "Here is my book. Transmit it from me." (ارْوِهِ عَنِّي)
2. **Munāwala alone** (invalid): he gives the book without authorization to transmit.

### Al-Zuhrī's analogy — the testament

Al-Kifāya ([p. 1228](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/1228)) reports al-Zuhrī's analogy:

> ناولني الزهري كتبا وأذن لي في روايتها وقال: **أشهد عليها كما يشهد الرجل على الوصية ثم لا يفتحها**

> "Al-Zuhrī handed me his books and authorized me to transmit them. He said: **Bear witness to them as one bears witness to a testament without opening it.**"

The analogy is revealing: al-Zuhrī compares transmission to a **formal legal act** — the witness attests to the existence of the document, not to its content. The transmitter in munāwala guarantees the **attribution**, not the **verification**. It is an admission: the book's content has not been read aloud — it is **sealed**, like a testament.

---

## 5. Al-Mukātaba (المكاتبة) — correspondence

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 1251](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/1251) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 170](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/170)

The shaykh writes to an absent student. Proper formula: **كتب إليّ فلان** (So-and-so wrote to me).

> وَهِيَ أَنْ يَكْتُبَ الشَّيْخُ إِلَى الطَّالِبِ، وَهُوَ غَائِبٌ شَيْئًا مِنْ حَدِيثِهِ بِخَطِّهِ

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 170](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/170))

The problem is obvious: **how does one verify the authenticity of the letter?** The manuscript handwriting is the only guarantee — and it is falsifiable. Mukātaba makes the entire system rest on the recognition of a calligraphy.

---

## 6. Al-Iʿlām (الإعلام) — notification without authorization

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 1286](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/1286) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 172](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/172)

The shaykh simply says: "This hadith is my audition." Without saying: "You may transmit it from me."

> إِعْلَامُ الرَّاوِي لِلطَّالِبِ بِأَنَّ هَذَا الْحَدِيثَ سَمَاعُهُ مِنْ فُلَانٍ، مُقْتَصِرًا عَلَى ذَلِكَ، مِنْ غَيْرِ أَنْ يَقُولَ: « ارْوِهِ عَنِّي »

The Jumhūr **rejects** transmission by simple iʿlām. And yet — how does one distinguish, in an isnād, a ʿan hiding an iʿlām from a ʿan hiding a samāʿ?

---

## 7. Al-Waṣiyya (الوصية) — testamentary bequest

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 1299](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/1299) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 174](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/174)

The transmitter bequeaths his books before dying or traveling.

> وَهَذَا بَعِيدٌ جِدًّا، وَهُوَ إِمَّا زَلَّةُ عَالِمٍ، أَوْ مُتَأَوَّلٌ

> "This is very far-fetched [as a valid mode of transmission] — it is either a scholar's error, or a forced interpretation."

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 174](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/174))

## 8. Al-Wijāda (الوجادة) — finding in a book

**Al-Kifāya** [p. 1307](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/1307) | **Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ** [p. 175](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/175)

One finds a shaykh's manuscript **without having heard him or received an ijāza**. The term itself is a neologism:

> وَهِيَ مَصْدَرٌ لِـ « وَجَدَ يَجِدُ »، مُوَلَّدٌ غَيْرُ مَسْمُوعٍ مِنَ الْعَرَبِ

> "[Al-wijāda] is a maṣdar of *wajada* — a **neologism**, not attested among the [classical] Arabs."

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 175](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/175))

Proper formulas: **وجدت بخط فلان** or **قرأت بخط فلان** (I found / read in the handwriting of So-and-so).

### Tadlīs through wijāda

> وَرُبَّمَا دَلَّسَ بَعْضُهُمْ، فَذَكَرَ الَّذِي وَجَدَ خَطَّهُ، وَقَالَ فِيهِ: « عَنْ فُلَانٍ »، وَذَلِكَ تَدْلِيسٌ قَبِيحٌ

> "Some **practiced tadlīs**: they mentioned the author of the manuscript and simply said *ʿan fulān* ("from So-and-so") — and this is a **repugnant tadlīs**."

— Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ ([p. 176](https://shamela.ws/book/22870/176))

### Project case: [[Persons/Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna|Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna]] and the Makhzumiyya

The paradigmatic case in our research. Sufyān goes to ask al-Zuhrī the hadith of the Makhzumiyya — Zuhrī **yells at him** and Sufyān falls silent. ʿAlī ibn al-Madīnī presses him:

> قلت لسفيان: فلم تحمله عن أحد؟ قال: **وجدته في كتاب**، قلت لسفيان: كتاب من؟ قال: **كان كتاب أيوب بن موسى**

> "I said to Sufyān: so you did not take it from anyone? He said: **I found it in a book**. I said: whose book? He said: **it was the book of Ayyūb ibn Mūsā.**"

— ʿIlal al-Dāraquṭnī, hadith 3466 ([p. 5605](https://shamela.ws/book/9082/5605))

Sufyān admits a wijāda — the weakest form of transmission. The book is that of a **third party** he does not control. And yet, in current isnāds (including in Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī), Sufyān appears as a direct transmitter from Zuhrī. See the complete entry: [[Persons/Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna]] (flaw 3).

### Another case: Makhrama ibn Bukayr

Makhrama ibn Bukayr ibn ʿAbd Allāh ibn al-Ashajj transmits his father's hadiths from his books — without ever having heard them from him. This is a systematic wijāda presented as samāʿ.

## Talqīn — the test and the flaw

**Talqīn** (التلقين) is prompting: one suggests to the transmitter a hadith he never said, to see if he accepts it. It is both a **testing tool** and a **weapon of corruption**.

### Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna tests a transmitter

Al-Kifāya ([p. 826](https://shamela.ws/book/13055/826)) — Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn:

> الرَّجُلُ يُلَقَّنُ حَدِيثَهُ؟ قَالَ: **إِذَا كَانَ يَعْرِفُ إِنْ أُدْخِلَ عَلَيْهِ** فَلَيْسَ بِحَدِيثِهِ بَأْسٌ وَإِنْ لَمْ يَكُنْ يَعْرِفُ إِذَا أُدْخِلَ عَلَيْهِ , فَكَأَنَّ يَحْيَى كَرِهَهُ

> "The one who is prompted (yulaqqan) his hadith? — **If he recognizes when something is inserted** (udkhila ʿalayhi), no problem; otherwise, Yaḥyā disapproved."

The term **أُدخل عليه** is technical: it is the same verb as in [[Concepts/Al-Ziyāda fī al-kutub|al-idkhāl]] — insertion into books.

### The case of ʿAbd al-Razzāq

[[Concepts/Falsification of nusakh|ʿAbd al-Razzāq al-Ṣanʿānī]] went blind at the end of his life. After his blindness, people would come to **prompt him with hadiths** he had never transmitted — and he would accept them. Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal distinguishes: what he heard from ʿAbd al-Razzāq **before** the blindness is reliable; what comes **after** is suspect. See [[Concepts/Muṣannaf de ʿAbd al-Razzāq]] for the complete analysis of the three layers of corruption.

## The ʿan — the universal mask

The particle **عن** (*ʿan*, "from") is the most common transmission formula in isnāds — and the most opaque. It says nothing about the **method** of reception:

```
عن فلان     can mean:

  ← samāʿ     (I heard it from his mouth)
  ← ʿarḍ      (I read to him and he approved)
  ← ijāza     (he authorized me to transmit)
  ← munāwala  (he handed me his book)
  ← wijāda    (I found it in his book)
  ← talqīn    (it was prompted to me)
  ← tadlīs    (I skipped an intermediary)
```

This is why critics classify transmitters as "mudallis" (those whose ʿan is suspect) — see [[Persons/Ibn Jurayj]] and [[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|al-Aʿmash]], known for their tadlīs. But this classification assumes that one **knows** the transmitter practices tadlīs. For undetected cases, the ʿan masks everything.

## What the system acknowledges

The taḥammul hierarchy is a **structural admission**: the classical scholars knew that not all methods are equal, and that the inferior ones opened real vulnerabilities.

1. **Samāʿ is not infallible**: Ibn Wahb falls asleep, the student continues writing. The formula *samiʿtu* covers highly variable realities.

2. **The formulas are conventions, not guarantees**: Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ acknowledges that the ḥaddathanā/akhbaranā distinction has "no linguistic foundation." Abū Nuʿaym uses *akhbaranā* for ijāza without signaling it.

3. **Wijāda is everywhere**: Sufyān with the book of Ayyūb ibn Mūsā, Makhrama with his father's books — the hadith "found in a book" is presented as direct audition.

4. **Talqīn exploits the same flaw as idkhāl**: prompting a hadith to a shaykh (talqīn) and inserting a hadith into his book ([[Concepts/Al-Ziyāda fī al-kutub|al-ziyāda fī al-kutub]]) are two faces of the same phenomenon — an external control that does not exist.

5. **The ʿan masks everything**: eight methods, but a single particle to designate all of them. The later reader cannot know what lies behind a عن.

The system knows its flaws — it documents them, names them, ranks them. What it cannot do is **eliminate them retroactively**: the isnāds in the canonical collections are already fixed, and most use the opaque عن.

## The Qatāda case — wijāda by a blind man and the phantom reader

[[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] is **blind from birth**. He cannot physically read a manuscript. Yet the sources document that he absorbed entire written corpora: the **ṣaḥīfa of Jābir**, the **notebooks of Khilās ibn ʿAmr**, and other written material. Someone had to read these to him — but the reader **never appears** in any isnād.

The sources use the **passive voice** systematically: **قُرِئَتْ عَلَيْهِ** ("it was read to him"), **ذَهَبُوا بصحيفة جابر** ("they brought the ṣaḥīfa"). The only named reader in Qatāda's entourage is **Saʿīd ibn Abī ʿArūba** — who served as secretary-reader before becoming his most prolific transmitter.

The structural flaw: in every isnād where Qatāda transmits from a written source, the real schema is:

```
What the isnād says:     Qatāda ← Khilās ← ʿAlī
What actually happened:  Qatāda ← [READER] ← [MANUSCRIPT] ← Khilās ← ʿAlī
```

The reader is a **necessary but invisible** link. The muṣṭalaḥ treatises discuss the hierarchy of reception methods at length but never ask: when a blind man receives a written text, **who is the reader, and why does he have no status in the isnād?** See the full analysis in [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma]].
