# Ighrāb (إغراب) — importing from the distant

A transmitter departs for a **distant land** (al-Andalus, Khurāsān, Sind), spends decades outside the hadith centres (Ḥijāz, Iraq, Shām), then returns with a corpus that **no one can verify through cross-checking**. The term is related to *gharīb* (isolated hadith) — but whereas *gharīb* designates an individual isolated hadith, ighrāb designates a **structural condition**: the foreignness of an entire corpus.

## The mechanism

```
CENTRE (Ḥijāz / Iraq / Shām)
    │
    └── Transmitter departs ──→ PERIPHERY (al-Andalus, ~30 years)
                                    │
                                    └── Accumulates an isolated corpus
                                            │
                                            └── Returns to the CENTRE
                                                    │
                                                    └── UNVERIFIABLE material
                                                        (no mutābaʿāt possible)
```

The hadith criticism system rests on **cross-checking chains**: if a master taught a text, several of his students would have transmitted it. A transmitter is verified through his *mutābaʿāt* (corroborations) and *shawāhid* (supporting witnesses). But when the transmitter returns from a civilisation where **no one else was collecting hadith**, this system collapses: there is **no parallel chain** to verify.

## The difference from tafarrud

**[[Concepts/Tafarrud|Tafarrud]]** is an **isolated point** in a chain: a Syrian alone transmitting from a Medinan master whom no one in Medina knows (e.g.: [[Persons/Al-Awzā'ī|al-Awzā'ī]] alone transmitting from [[Persons/Nāfiʿ mawlā Ibn ʿUmar|Nāfiʿ]]).

Ighrāb is **more radical**:

| | Tafarrud | Ighrāb |
|---|---|---|
| **Scale** | A single isolated hadith | An entire corpus (~200 hadiths) |
| **Geography** | Syrian ← Medinan | Return from another civilisation |
| **Verification** | Difficult (no one on site recognises him) | Impossible (no collectors on site) |
| **Mechanism** | Import of a single point | Import of a corpus |

Tafarrud is a **warning signal**; ighrāb is an **epistemological impossibility**.

## Paradigmatic case: Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ

**[[Persons/Muʿāwiya ibn Ṣāliḥ]]** (d. ~158H) — Ḥimṣī who departed around 125H, qāḍī of al-Andalus under ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Dākhil, returned to the East around 154H with **~200 unique hadiths** (*ifrādāt*).

Critical points:
- **~30 years** outside the hadith centres
- **Shāmī** material (pro-Umayyad?) and **ṣifāt** (divine attributes)
- Manuscripts **lost** in al-Andalus — no return to sources possible
- Carries a **tafsīr ṣaḥīfa** whose chain is **interrupted** (ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭalḥa never met Ibn ʿAbbās)
- Former **Umayyad qāḍī** returning to Abbasid territory

Ibn ʿAdī qualifies his material as *ṣadūq* but explicitly notes the *ifrādāt*. Yaḥyā al-Qaṭṭān rejected him entirely.

## The epistemological problem

In a system founded on cross-checking chains (*common link*, *mutābaʿāt*), a transmitter whose material cannot be cross-checked is **structurally unverifiable**. Trust then rests solely on the **person** — his *ʿadāla* (probity) and his *ḍabṭ* (precision) — and not on the **network**.

Yet it is precisely the network that distinguishes the science of hadith from mere individual testimony. Without a network, one is left with **faith in the individual** — which is, paradoxically, exactly what the isnād system claimed to transcend.
