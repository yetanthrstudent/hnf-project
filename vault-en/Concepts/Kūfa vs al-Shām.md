# Kūfa vs al-Shām — five questions, two Islams

Two camps clash in first-century Islam. On one side, **al-Shām** — Umayyad Syria, the land of power, the army and the regime's Sunna. On the other, **Kūfa** — the Iraqi garrison, land of 'Alī, of the school of [[Persons/Ibn Mas'ūd|Ibn Mas'ūd]] and of the Quran as sole authority. This is not a minor quarrel: the two camps diverge on **five fundamental questions** that structure primitive Islam.

Examination of the classical sources reveals a clear fracture: there is a "Muhammad's Islam" — that of the Book — and a "men's Islam" — that of power and fabricated Sunna.

## Question 1: What solution to conflict?

The same Companion — [[Persons/Hudhayfah ibn al-Yamān|Hudhayfah ibn al-Yamān]] — asks the Prophet the same question: "What to do in times of fitna?" Two transmission circuits deliver two contradictory answers.

**Al-Shām answers: obey the ruler.** The Shāmī version (Muslim 1847):

> « الزم جماعة المسلمين وإمامهم [...] فاسمع وأطع الأمير وإن ضرب ظهرك وأخذ مالك »
>
> "Stay with the group of Muslims and their leader. [...] Listen and obey the emir, even if he strikes your back and takes your property."

**Kūfa answers: study the Quran.** The Kūfan version (Bazzār 2799, Ibn Ḥibbān 117):

> « تعلّم كتاب الله واعمل بما فيه »
>
> "Study the Book of God and act upon what it contains."

'Alī gives the same answer as the Kūfan version: "The Book" (Bazzār).

The two versions cannot coexist. One commands submission to political power, the other commands autonomy through the Quran. Al-Shām canonised the first — Muslim retains it in his Ṣaḥīḥ. The second survives in secondary collections.

## Question 2: The victorious group?

**Al-Shām names itself: Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamā'a.** The appellation is political, not theological — it originates with 'Umar b. 'Abd al-'Azīz in his epistles (see [[Concepts/Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamā'a]]). The hadiths of al-'Irbāḍ b. Sāriya and of Mu'āwiya propagate it: "Hold fast to my Tradition and that of the rightly-guided Caliphs."

**Kūfa names itself: Ahl al-Qur'ān** — the party of the Quran. They refuse the Witr to the Shāmīs because the latter do not recite the Quran as they should. [[Persons/Hudhayfah ibn al-Yamān|Hudhayfah]]:

> « لا وتر لمن لم يقرأ بأم القرآن »
>
> "No Witr for whoever does not recite the Mother of the Book."

The rejection is concrete: the Kūfites consider that the Shāmīs do not know how to pray (al-Bayhaqī, Sunan, no. 4660; Muṣannaf Ibn Abī Shayba, no. 6964, 6965, 6989).

## Question 3: The sacred capital?

**Al-Shām sacralises al-Quds (Jerusalem).** The Dome of the Rock, built by 'Abd al-Malik under the supervision of [[Rajā' ibn Ḥaywa]], is the monument of this sacralisation. Faḍā'il (merits) works on Jerusalem multiply. Hadiths are fabricated to magnify al-Quds — al-Zuhrī and the regime's transmitters propagate them.

**Kūfa sacralises the great mosque of Kūfa.** Hudhayfah refuses Bayt al-Maqdis (Ibn Abī Shayba, Muṣannaf, no. 7617):

> « لو أعملت ناقتي حتى أنيخها عند باب بيت المقدس ما دخلته »
>
> "If I rode my camel all the way to the gate of Bayt al-Maqdis, I would not enter it."

'Alī says to a man who wants to go pray in Jerusalem:

> « بع راحلتك وكل ثمنها وصلّ ها هنا »
>
> "Sell your camel, eat its price, and pray here [in Kūfa]."

Abū Dharr settles:

> « لَرَكْعتان بهذا الرمل الأحمر أحبّ إليّ من أن آتي بيت المقدس »
>
> "Two rak'āt on this red sand are dearer to me than going to Bayt al-Maqdis."

The sacralisation of al-Quds is an imperial policy. The Dome of the Rock is a political act before being a religious one — it rivals the Ka'ba during Ibn al-Zubayr's revolt (see also [[Traditions/Hijra to al-Shām]]).

## Question 4: What sources of Islam?

**Al-Shām places the Sunna above the Quran.** [[Makhūl al-Shāmī]] (via [[Al-Awzā'ī]]):

> « القرآن أحوج إلى السنة من السنة إلى القرآن »
>
> "The Quran needs the Sunna more than the Sunna needs the Quran."

This doctrine is propagated under 'Abd al-Malik through relays such as Khālid b. Ma'dān and Muslim b. Mishkam. The "Sunna" in question is not necessarily the Prophet's — it is the Sunna of the Caliphs, the regime's practice elevated to a norm.

**Kūfa posits the Quran as sole source.** [[Persons/Abū Ḥanīfa|Abū Ḥanīfa]] rejects the hadiths of al-Shām wholesale. When the tradition on obedience to the governor is reported to him, he responds: "خرافة!" — "Fables!" (Khaṭīb, Tārīkh, vol. 15, p. 532). The Kūfites have a principle:

> « ما لم يحرّم عليك القرآن فهو حلال »
>
> "What the Quran does not forbid you is lawful."

And further:

> « بيني وبينهم كتاب الله »
>
> "Between me and them stands the Book of God."

The conflict is not theoretical: it concerns meat, dietary prohibitions, ritual practices. Kūfa limits prohibitions to the strict Quran; al-Shām extends them through the Sunna — that is, through hadith, that is, through the power that produces and disseminates hadith (see [[Concepts/ʿAmal al-Sulṭān]]).

## Question 5: Which Quran?

**Al-Shām reads the Quran of Zayd b. Thābit** — the 'Uthmānic muṣḥaf, imposed by central authority.

**Kūfa reads the Quran of [[Persons/Ibn Mas'ūd|Ibn Mas'ūd]]** — the Companion sent by 'Umar to Kūfa, among the earliest converts, present at the last presentation of the Quran to the Prophet. Ibn 'Abbās defends him (Bazzār 4923):

> « كان القرآن يُعرض على النبي في كل رمضان مرة وعُرض عليه في آخر عمره مرتين، وكان ابن مسعود حاضرا »
>
> "The Quran was presented to the Prophet every Ramadan. In his last year, it was presented twice — and Ibn Mas'ūd was present."

The differences in reading are not cosmetic. They produce **doctrinal divergences**:

| Verse | Al-Shām reading (Zayd) | Kūfa reading (Ibn Mas'ūd) | Stakes |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------------|-------|
| 1.4 | مَلِكِ يوم الدين (Malik) | مَالِكِ يوم الدين (Mālik) | King vs Owner — magnifying royalty or denying it |
| 21.96 | حَدَب (ḥadab) | جَدَث (jadath) | Gog and Magog — hill vs tomb |
| 2.221 | حتى يؤمنّ / يؤمنوا | Active/passive variant | Status of women in marriage |
| 36.38 | لِمُسْتَقَرٍّ لها | لا مستقرّ لها | Does the sun have an assigned resting place? |

The policy of diacritical points (naqṭ), imposed by Marwān and 'Abd al-Malik, fixes the readings — and with them the doctrines. The Kūfites recite differently to preserve their reading and their doctrine.

## Synthesis — two Islams

```
                     AL-SHĀM (Umayyad Syria)              KŪFA (Iraq — party of 'Alī)
                     ═══════════════════════             ══════════════════════════════
 Q1. Conflict?       Obey the ruler                      Study the Quran
                     [Muslim 1847]                        [Bazzār 2799]

 Q2. Group?          Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamā'a             Ahl al-Qur'ān
                     ['Umar b. 'Abd al-'Azīz]            [Hudhayfah, 'Alī]

 Q3. Capital?        Al-Quds (Dome of the Rock)           Mosque of Kūfa
                     [Rajā' ibn Ḥaywa]                   [Hudhayfah, 'Alī, Abū Dharr]

 Q4. Sources?        The Sunna overrides the Quran        The Quran suffices
                     [Makhūl, al-Awzā'ī]                 [Abū Ḥanīfa]

 Q5. Which Quran?    Reading of Zayd b. Thābit           Reading of Ibn Mas'ūd
                     ['Uthmānic muṣḥaf]                  [Ibn 'Abbās defends it]
                     ─────────────────────                ──────────────────────
                     WINNER                               LOSER
                     (force + power)                      (resistance + Quran)
```

The Shām camp prevails — not by the strength of its arguments, but by the strength of its armies. Canonical Islam is the Islam of al-Shām: Muslim's Ṣaḥīḥ retains the Shāmī version of Hudhayfah, not the Kūfan. "Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamā'a" becomes the name of orthodoxy. Al-Quds is sacralised. The Sunna overrides the Quran. The muṣḥaf of Zayd is the only Quran.

Kūfa survives through Abū Ḥanīfa — the largest living madhhab carries the Kūfan heritage. But the heritage is domesticated: later Ḥanafism accepts hadith, accepts the Sunna, accepts the single muṣḥaf. The victory of al-Shām is so complete that it has made people forget there was ever a battle.
