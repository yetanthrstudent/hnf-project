# ʿAmal al-Sulṭān (عمل السلطان)

**ʿAmal al-sulṭān** ("work for the ruler") is a **criterion of jarḥ** (moral disqualification) in the science of jarḥ wa-l-taʿdīl. It does not concern memory or transmission technique: a man can be **thiqa fī al-ḥadīth** (reliable in hadith) and nonetheless be disapproved for his proximity to power. This criterion takes on its full meaning in the opposition between [[Concepts/Kūfa vs al-Shām|the two camps of early Islam]]: the Kūfites who refuse power (including [[Persons/Abū Ḥanīfa|Abū Ḥanīfa]]) against the patronized transmitters of al-Shām.

This criterion reveals a **structural tension**: the most prolific scholars are also the closest to power — while those who refuse transmit little or nothing.

## A. The named jarḥ: عمل السلطان

### Ḥumayd ibn Hilāl al-ʿAdawī — the paradigmatic case

Basran tābiʿī, classified thiqa by Ibn Maʿīn and al-Nasāʾī. Abū Hilāl al-Rāsibī (via Qatāda) says of him that he was the most learned man of Basra, without excluding either al-Ḥasan or Ibn Sīrīn. Yet, **Ibn Sīrīn did not approve of him**.

**Al-Qaṭṭān** via ʿAlī ibn al-Madīnī (Tahdhīb al-Kamāl, [p. 3628](https://shamela.ws/book/3722/3628)):

> « كان ابن سيرين لا يرضى حميد بن هلال »
>
> "Ibn Sīrīn did not approve of Ḥumayd ibn Hilāl."

**Abū Ḥātim** explains the reason (Tahdhīb al-Tahdhīb, [p. 495](https://shamela.ws/book/1293/495)):

> « دخل في شيء من عمل السلطان، فلهذا كان يرضاه، وكان في الحديث ثقة »
>
> "He got involved in the **work for the ruler** — that is why [Ibn Sīrīn] did not approve of him. Yet he was **reliable in hadith**."

**Ibn Saʿd** dates his death « في ولاية خالد بن عبد الله على العراق » — under the governorship of [[Persons/Khālid ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Qasrī]]. The same governor who patronized the transmitters of Anas.

Abū Ḥātim's formula is the key: **عمل السلطان** is not a technical defect (he is thiqa), it is a **moral flaw** that, in the eyes of the ancients, sufficed to disqualify a transmitter.

### [[Persons/Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl|Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl]] — the criterion explicitly named

**Zāʾida** ceases to transmit from Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl (al-ʿUqaylī, al-Ḍuʿafāʾ, [p. 770](https://shamela.ws/book/13041/770)):

> « لتدخله في عمل السلطان »
>
> "Because he got involved in the **work for the ruler**."

Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl transmits directly from [[Persons/Khālid ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Qasrī]] (Tārīkh al-Islām, [p. 2468](https://shamela.ws/book/22771/2468)). He is both a mudallis and a man of the governor. But **Bukhārī and Muslim retain him** — the criticism for عمل السلطان is treated as non-disqualifying.

### The paradox

Two men of Basra, both linked to the governor Khālid, both criticized for عمل السلطان, both thiqa nonetheless. The criterion exists but **produces no effect**: it is noted, documented, then set aside. The moral jarḥ is recorded without technical consequence.

## B. The victims of power

### [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]] — branded by al-Ḥajjāj

Al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf brands Anas physically (ختم — seal on the hand), a gesture of humiliation reserved for slaves and dhimmīs. But Anas is also **governor (ʿāmil) of Naysābūr** for the Umayyads, wears the **khazz** of emirs, and sits on the **emir's seat** (Yazīd al-Ḍabbī incident). The victim is also a cog in the machine.

### Saʿīd ibn Jubayr — the counterpoint

Tābiʿī executed by al-Ḥajjāj in 95 AH. Unlike Anas, he refuses to submit and pays with his life. His execution shows the **cost** of refusal — and, in contrast, what obedience yields.

The dynamic is twofold: power **punishes** the recalcitrant AND **uses** the compliant. Both mechanisms work together.

## C. Hand in hand: patronized scholars

### [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Diʿāma|Qatāda]] with [[Persons/Khālid ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Qasrī|Khālid al-Qasrī]]

Qatāda dies in **117 AH at Wāsiṭ**, at the governor's residence, in debt, sustained by Khālid's provisions. [[Concepts/Quṣṣāṣ|Qāṣṣ]] (preacher-storyteller), not a muḥaddith — Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal: « كان قتادة يقص ». Blind from birth, **notorious mudallis** according to [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]] and [[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]].

### [[Persons/al-Zuhrī|Al-Zuhrī]] — the instrumentalized scholar

Muḥammad ibn Muslim ibn Shihāb al-Zuhrī (d. 124 AH): the greatest hadith scholar of his era. His relationship with Umayyad power is **ambivalent**, not servile.

**What he endures** — Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik compels him to put hadith in writing. Al-Zuhrī complains (Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ, [p. 1131](https://shamela.ws/book/10495/1131)):

> « كنا نكره الكتب حتى أكرهنا عليه السلطان فكرهنا أن نمنعه الناس »
>
> "We used to dislike writing [hadith] — until **the ruler compelled us**. Then we were reluctant to deny it to people."

And in the same source:

> « أكره هشام الزهري فكتب لبنيه فكتب الناس الحديث »
>
> "Hishām compelled al-Zuhrī [to write], and he wrote for his sons — then people [began to] write hadith."

**What he resists** — Al-Zuhrī is not a submissive man. At the court of al-Walīd ibn ʿAbd al-Malik, when the caliph claims that the verse « والذي تولى كبره » refers to ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, al-Zuhrī corrects him to his face: « أصلح الله الأمير، ليس كذا » — "May God reform the emir, that is not so" (Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ, [p. 1137](https://shamela.ws/book/10495/1137)). He cites ʿUrwa from ʿĀʾisha: the verse refers to ʿAbd Allāh ibn Ubayy, not ʿAlī.

More seriously: al-Zuhrī transmits the hadith (via Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab ← ʿUmar) about a man from Banū Umayya named al-Walīd who is « أشد على هذه الأمة من فرعون على قومه » — "harsher on this community than Pharaoh on his people" (Musnad Aḥmad). He dies in **124 AH** — one year before the accession of al-Walīd ibn Yazīd (125 AH), the libertine caliph who would be killed in 126 AH.

**What he loses** — the structural problem is not al-Zuhrī's submission, it is the **loss of control** over his material. Once written for the caliph's sons, the hadith enters the **dīwān** — the Umayyad administrative machinery. Al-Zuhrī no longer controls the circulation, copying, or use of what he produced. The codification of hadith is born from a **political compulsion**, and the material immediately escapes the one who produced it.

### [[Concepts/Quṣṣāṣ|The Quṣṣāṣ]] — state-salaried preachers

Under [[Persons/Muʿāwiya ibn Abī Sufyān|Muʿāwiya]], the qaṣṣ becomes a **state institution**. The hadith « لا يقص إلا أمير أو مأمور أو مختال » (none preaches except an emir, a mandated agent, or an impostor) codifies the monopoly. [[Persons/Kaʿb al-Ahbar|Kaʿb al-Ahbar]] is authorized by Muʿāwiya to preach; the quṣṣāṣ are the cultural agents of the caliphate.

The **three principal transmitters** of Anas are all linked to the same governor. This is not an independent network of free scholars: it is a patronized network whose hadith production is inseparable from its political anchorage.

## D. Those who WERE the power

### [[Persons/Muʿāwiya ibn Abī Sufyān|Muʿāwiya ibn Abī Sufyān]] — caliph AND transmitter

First Umayyad caliph. **Protects** [[Persons/Kaʿb al-Ahbar|Kaʿb al-Ahbar]], publicly certifies him, while admitting that he caught him lying. **Pays** [[Persons/Abū Hurayra|Abū Hurayra]] — Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab: "when Muʿāwiya paid him, he fell silent." Muʿāwiya does not merely transmit hadith: he **controls the conditions** under which hadith is produced.

### [[Persons/Abū Hurayra|Abū Hurayra]] — governor of Bahrayn

The most prolific of the Companions (+5,000 hadiths). Governor (ʿāmil) of Bahrayn under ʿUmar, who recalls him calling him "enemy of God" (عدو الله) for embezzlement. Paid by [[Persons/Muʿāwiya ibn Abī Sufyān|Muʿāwiya]] for his silence. Systematically confuses the material of [[Persons/Kaʿb al-Ahbar|Kaʿb al-Ahbar]] with the words of the Prophet (Muslim's diagnosis, al-Tamyīz no. 10).

### [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]] — governor of Naysābūr

Companion AND Umayyad governor. He himself claims his proximity to power in his letter to ʿAbd al-Malik. Wears the luxurious garments of emirs (khazz). Documented late appearance — after 75 AH, under al-Ḥajjāj.

### ʿAmr ibn al-ʿĀṣ — conqueror and governor of Egypt

Companion, general, governor. Transmits hadiths while administering a province. His son [[Persons/Ibn ʿAmr ibn al-ʿĀṣ|ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAmr]] meets [[Persons/Kaʿb al-Ahbar|Kaʿb al-Ahbar]] in Mecca and transmits his materials.

### The protective asymmetry

All these figures are protected by the dogma of [[Concepts/ʿAdālat al-Ṣaḥāba|ʿadālat al-ṣaḥāba]] (justice of the Companions). This dogma holds that **every Companion is ʿadl** (just, reliable by default) — which immunizes precisely those who combine political power and hadith authority. Tābiʿīn like Ḥumayd ibn Hilāl are criticized for عمل السلطان; the Companion-governors are above this criticism.

## E. Those who refused — the counter-examples

### [[Persons/Abū Ḥanīfa|Abū Ḥanīfa]] (d. 150 AH)

Imprisoned in Baghdad for refusing the post of **qāḍī** (judge) offered by al-Manṣūr. Dies in prison. Founder of the largest legal school in Islam — but his hadiths are few. Sheikh of the Kūfites, he rejects en bloc the Sunna of al-Shām (see [[Concepts/Kūfa vs al-Shām]]).

### Sufyān al-Thawrī (d. 161 AH)

Fugitive. Refuses all official function. Flees from city to city to escape summons from the authorities. His general condemnation:

> « أفسد الناس صنفان : علماء السوء وعُبَّاد الجهل »
>
> "Two categories have corrupted people: **evil scholars** and **ignorant devotees**."

### Saʿīd ibn al-Musayyab (d. 94 AH)

Medinan tābiʿī. **Beaten** (lashed) for refusing to pledge allegiance to al-Walīd and Sulaymān. Refuses to marry his daughter to the caliph. Pays the physical price of refusal.

### Mālik ibn Anas (d. 179 AH)

Tortured (arm dislocated) for a fatwa displeasing the governor of Medina. The author of the Muwaṭṭaʾ bears in his body the mark of resistance to power.

## Systemic consequence: structural bias

The canonical hadith corpus presents a **selection bias** in favor of transmitters linked to power:

1. **Those who resist transmit little** — whoever refuses power lives on the margins, flees, is imprisoned or killed. He produces fiqh (Abū Ḥanīfa) or spirituality (Sufyān), rarely massive hadith.

2. **Those close to power transmit massively** — patronized, salaried, protected, they have the time, resources, and audience to produce and diffuse an abundant corpus.

3. **The criterion of عمل السلطان is documented but without effect** — Ḥumayd ibn Hilāl is thiqa despite Ibn Sīrīn's jarḥ; Ḥumayd al-Ṭawīl is in the Ṣaḥīḥayn despite Zāʾida; al-Zuhrī is the supreme reference despite his admission of compulsion.

4. **The dogma of ʿadālat al-ṣaḥāba immunizes the most exposed** — the Companion-governors (Anas, Abū Hurayra, Muʿāwiya) are protected by a dogma that places them above criticism. Yet they are the ones who most intensely combine political power and hadith production.

The result: canonical hadith systematically overrepresents material produced in the orbit of Umayyad power, and underrepresents that of independent scholars. This is not a conspiracy — it is a **structural effect**: power creates the material conditions of transmission, and the science of hadith does not have the tools to correct this bias.
