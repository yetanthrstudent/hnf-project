# Al-Naskh — abrogation, or how to erase the Quran

Naskh is the mechanism by which the Islamic tradition justifies the replacement of one Quranic norm by another. In theory, it is a sober hermeneutical tool: when two verses contradict each other, the later one abrogates the earlier. In practice, naskh has become a **machine for neutralizing the Quranic text** — through hadith, through consensus, and ultimately through the mere decision of a caliph.

The escalation is documented:

| Level | Mechanism | Example | Acceptance |
|-------|-----------|---------|------------|
| **1** | The Quran abrogates the Quran | Direction of prayer (qibla): Q 2:144 abrogates Q 2:115 | **Consensus** |
| **2** | The hadith abrogates the Quran | Stoning (rajm): the Sunna abrogates the flogging of Q 24:2 | **Majority** (al-Shāfiʿī, al-Sarakhsī) |
| **3** | A Companion's practice abrogates the Quran | Al-muʾallafa (Q 9:60): ʿUmar suppresses a Quranic category | **Ḥanafites** — contested by the others |

Each level is bolder than the preceding one. At the end of the chain, a verse of the Quran can be neutralized by one man's decision — provided that man is ʿUmar.

---

## The three types of naskh

The classical uṣūliyyūn (al-Sarakhsī, al-Āmidī, Ibn al-Ḥājib) distinguish three forms:

### 1. Naskh al-tilāwa wa-l-ḥukm — text and ruling disappear

The verse is removed from the Quran and its legal norm ceases to apply. This is the least problematic case — but also the most unverifiable, since nothing remains of it.

### 2. Naskh al-ḥukm dūna al-tilāwa — the ruling is abrogated, the text remains

The verse is still recited in the Quran, but its legal norm is considered obsolete. This is the most commonly invoked type by the fuqahāʾ — and the most destructive to the integrity of the text as a source of law.

**Alleged examples:** the fidya of fasting (Q 2:184 → see [[Traditions/Ṣawm Ramaḍān]]), the bequest to parents (Q 2:180), the mutʿa (Q 4:24).

### 3. Naskh al-tilāwa dūna al-ḥukm — the text disappears, the ruling remains

The most extraordinary case: a verse **is no longer in the Quran** — it has been "removed from recitation" — but the legal norm it contained continues to apply. Muslims are asked to obey a text that no longer exists.

It is in this category that the **stoning verse** is classified.

## Case 1: The "stoning verse" — a phantom text

### The alleged text

> الشَّيْخُ وَالشَّيْخَةُ إِذَا زَنَيَا فَارْجُمُوهُمَا الْبَتَّةَ نَكَالًا مِنَ اللَّهِ وَاللَّهُ عَزِيزٌ حَكِيمٌ

> "The old man and the old woman, if they commit adultery, stone them absolutely, as punishment from God — and God is Mighty and Wise."

This text **does not appear in the Quran**. But according to the tradition, it was once part of it, it was "abrogated in recitation" (nuskhat tilāwatuhu), and yet its legal norm — the stoning of the married adulterer — continues to apply.

### ʿUmar's khuṭba — Bukhārī no. 6830

Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, Kitāb al-Ḥudūd ([p. 10202](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/10202)):

> فَكَانَ فِيمَا أَنْزَلَ اللهُ **آيَةُ الرَّجْمِ**، فَقَرَأْنَاهَا وَعَقَلْنَاهَا وَوَعَيْنَاهَا، رَجَمَ رَسُولُ اللهِ ﷺ وَرَجَمْنَا بَعْدَهُ، فَأَخْشَى إِنْ طَالَ بِالنَّاسِ زَمَانٌ أَنْ يَقُولَ قَائِلٌ: **وَاللهِ مَا نَجِدُ آيَةَ الرَّجْمِ فِي كِتَابِ اللهِ**، فَيَضِلُّوا بِتَرْكِ فَرِيضَةٍ أَنْزَلَهَا اللهُ، وَالرَّجْمُ فِي كِتَابِ اللهِ حَقٌّ عَلَى مَنْ زَنَى إِذَا أُحْصِنَ مِنَ الرِّجَالِ وَالنِّسَاءِ، إِذَا قَامَتِ الْبَيِّنَةُ أَوْ كَانَ الْحَبَلُ أَوِ الِاعْتِرَافُ

> "Among what God revealed was the **stoning verse**. We read it, understood it, and memorized it. The Prophet ﷺ stoned, and we stoned after him. I fear that if time passes, someone will say: **'By God, we do not find the stoning verse in the Book of God!'** — and they will go astray by abandoning an obligation that God revealed."

And in the shorter version (Bukhārī no. 6829, [p. 10200](https://shamela.ws/book/1681/10200)), ʿUmar adds the decisive admission:

> وَالَّذِي نَفْسِي بِيَدِهِ لَوْلَا أَنْ يَقُولَ النَّاسُ: **زَادَ عُمَرُ فِي كِتَابِ اللهِ** لَأَثْبَتُّهَا

> "By Him who holds my soul, were it not that people would say: **'ʿUmar has added to the Book of God,'** I would have inscribed it."

### What the text says about itself

Let us measure what we are asked to believe:

1. A **Quranic verse** existed — revealed, recited, memorized
2. This verse was **removed from the Quran** — without the tradition clearly explaining when, how, or by whom
3. Its **legal norm remains in force** — stoning is obligatory
4. **ʿUmar himself** acknowledges that the text is no longer in the muṣḥaf and that he **would have liked to restore it** but feared the accusation of falsification (ziyāda)
5. A second "lost verse" is mentioned in the same khuṭba: **"لَا تَرْغَبُوا عَنْ آبَائِكُمْ فَإِنَّهُ كُفْرٌ بِكُمْ"** ("Do not disavow your fathers, for that is ingratitude toward you")

We are in a situation where the caliph declares that the Quran as it exists is **incomplete** — that a verse is missing — but that he cannot restore it for fear of being accused of [[Concepts/Al-Ziyāda fī al-kutub|falsification]]. And the canonical collections present this admission as proof of the **reliability** of the tradition.

### Al-Shāfiʿī — the Sunna abrogates the Quran

Al-Shāfiʿī addresses the question in the Risāla (chapter "Ibtidāʾ al-nāsikh wa-l-mansūkh," [p. 198](https://shamela.ws/book/8180/198)). His **theoretical** position is that each type of source only abrogates its own type:

> أَبَانَ اللهُ لَهُمْ أَنَّهُ إِنَّمَا نَسَخَ مَا نَسَخَ مِنَ الكِتَابِ **بِالكِتَابِ** وَأَنَّ السُّنَّةَ لَا نَاسِخَةٌ لِلْكِتَابِ وَإِنَّمَا هِيَ تَبَعٌ لِلكِتَابِ

> "God made clear to them that He only abrogated what He abrogated from the Book **by the Book**, and that the Sunna does not abrogate the Book — it only follows it."

— Al-Risāla, §314 ([p. 198](https://shamela.ws/book/8180/198))

And the symmetry rule:

> وَهَكَذَا سُنَّةُ رَسُولِ اللهِ لَا يَنْسَخُهَا إِلَّا سُنَّةٌ لِرَسُولِ اللهِ

> "And likewise, the Sunna of the Prophet is only abrogated by a Sunna of the Prophet."

— Al-Risāla, §324 ([p. 200](https://shamela.ws/book/8180/200))

But **in practice**, a few pages later ([p. 337](https://shamela.ws/book/8180/337)), al-Shāfiʿī uses the Sunna to abrogate the Quran in the case of rajm:

> ثم رَجَمَ رسولُ الله مَاعِزاً وَلَمْ يَجْلِدهُ وَامرأةَ الأسلميِّ وَلَمْ يَجْلِدهَا **فَدَلَّتْ سُنَّةُ رَسُولِ اللهِ عَلَى أَنَّ الجَلْدَ مَنْسُوخٌ** عَنِ الزَّانِيَيْنِ الثَّيِّبَيْنِ

> "Then the Prophet stoned Māʿiz **without flogging him**, and the Aslamiyya woman **without flogging her** — **the Sunna therefore showed that the flogging is abrogated** for the two married adulterers."

— Al-Risāla, §688 ([p. 337](https://shamela.ws/book/8180/337))

The contradiction is structural: al-Shāfiʿī posits the principle that the Sunna does not abrogate the Quran, then uses the Sunna to abrogate the flogging prescribed by Q 24:2. He works around the problem by saying that the "stoning verse" existed and that it is therefore the Quran abrogating the Quran — but that verse is **not in the Quran**.

## Case 2: Al-muʾallafatu qulūbuhum — ʿUmar abrogates the Quran

### The verse

Q 9:60 — the verse of the eight categories of zakāt:

> إِنَّمَا الصَّدَقَاتُ لِلْفُقَرَاءِ وَالْمَسَاكِينِ وَالْعَامِلِينَ عَلَيْهَا **وَالْمُؤَلَّفَةِ قُلُوبُهُمْ** وَفِي الرِّقَابِ وَالْغَارِمِينَ وَفِي سَبِيلِ اللَّهِ وَابْنِ السَّبِيلِ ۖ فَرِيضَةً مِّنَ اللَّهِ

> "Alms are for the poor, the needy, those who collect them, **those whose hearts are to be won**, slaves, debtors, in the way of God, and the traveler — **an obligation from God.**"

The text is categorical: **فريضة من الله** — obligation from God. Eight categories, including "those whose hearts are to be won" (al-muʾallafatu qulūbuhum) — non-Muslims or recent converts who are attracted to Islam through gifts.

### ʿUmar's decision

At the Prophet's death, al-Aqraʿ ibn Ḥābis and ʿUyayna ibn Ḥiṣn — two tribal chiefs who had received their share as muʾallafa under the Prophet — present themselves before Abū Bakr with a written document (kitāb) from the Prophet allocating this share. Abū Bakr validates it. Then **ʿUmar tears up the document** and tells them:

> إِنَّ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ ﷺ كَانَ يَتَأَلَّفُكُمْ وَالإِسْلَامُ يَوْمَئِذٍ قَلِيلٌ، وَإِنَّ اللَّهَ قَدْ أَغْنَى الإِسْلَامَ، اذْهَبَا فَاجْهَدَا جَهْدَكُمَا

> "The Prophet ﷺ used to win you over [through gifts] when Islam was still weak. God has now strengthened Islam. **Go and do your best.**"

— Tafsīr al-Ṭabarī, on Q 9:60 ([p. 7897](https://shamela.ws/book/7798/7897))

And via al-Shaʿbī (same page):

> إِنَّمَا كَانَتِ الْمُؤَلَّفَةُ قُلُوبُهُمْ عَلَى عَهْدِ النَّبِيِّ ﷺ، فَلَمَّا وَلِيَ أَبُو بَكْرٍ **انْقَطَعَتِ الرِّشَا**

> "The muʾallafa qulūbuhum only existed during the Prophet's time ﷺ. When Abū Bakr took power, **the bribes ceased** (انقطعت الرشا)."

The word **الرشا** (bribes) is remarkable: al-Shaʿbī describes the Quranic gifts to the muʾallafa as **bribes** that ʿUmar had the merit of stopping.

### The theoretical escalation

The problem is vertiginous. The Quran prescribes eight categories of zakāt — **فريضة من الله**. ʿUmar suppresses one. How do the jurists justify this?

| Position | Proponents | Argument |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| The category is **abrogated** (mansūkha) | **Ḥanafites** (Abū Ḥanīfa, al-Jaṣṣāṣ) | The consensus (ijmāʿ) of the Companions — neither Abū Bakr nor ʿUmar applied it |
| It is a **circumstantial ijtihād** | **Shāfiʿites, Mālikites, Ḥanbalites** | ʿUmar judged that the need had ceased; if the need returns, the category applies again |
| The category **still exists** | **Al-Ṭabarī, Abū Jaʿfar al-Bāqir** | One cannot suspend a فريضة من الله by one man's decision |

Al-Ṭabarī's position is the most textually coherent. He concludes ([p. 7898](https://shamela.ws/book/7798/7898)):

> فَلَا حُجَّةَ لِمُحْتَجٍّ بِأَنْ يَقُولَ: لَا يُتَأَلَّفُ اليَوْمَ عَلَى الإِسْلَامِ أَحَدٌ

> "There is no argument for anyone who claims that hearts can no longer be won today."

But the Ḥanafites went further than anyone: a Quranic verse containing **فريضة من الله** is neutralized — not by another verse, not by a prophetic hadith, but by **ʿUmar's practice**. The justification? If ʿUmar did it, he must have had a good reason — perhaps a hadith that only he knew.

This is the summit of the escalation: **the Quran is abrogated by an inference about what ʿUmar might have heard**.

## Case 3: The bequest to parents — a hadith abrogates a verse

### The verse

Q 2:180:

> كُتِبَ عَلَيْكُمْ إِذَا حَضَرَ أَحَدَكُمُ الْمَوْتُ إِن تَرَكَ خَيْرًا **الْوَصِيَّةُ لِلْوَالِدَيْنِ وَالْأَقْرَبِينَ** بِالْمَعْرُوفِ ۖ حَقًّا عَلَى الْمُتَّقِينَ

> "It is prescribed for you, when death approaches one of you — if he leaves wealth — **to make a bequest in favor of parents and close relatives**, according to custom — **an obligation** upon the pious."

The text is explicit: **كُتب عليكم** (it is prescribed for you) + **حقًّا على المتقين** (an obligation upon the pious). The bequest in favor of parents is a **Quranic obligation**.

### The abrogating hadith

> إِنَّ اللَّهَ قَدْ أَعْطَى كُلَّ ذِي حَقٍّ حَقَّهُ، **فَلَا وَصِيَّةَ لِوَارِثٍ**

> "God has given every rightful claimant his right — **therefore no bequest for an heir.**"

— Tirmidhī no. 2120; Abū Dāwūd no. 2870; Ibn Mājah no. 2713; classified ṣaḥīḥ by al-Albānī (Irwāʾ al-Ghalīl no. 1655, [p. 1973](https://shamela.ws/book/22592/1973))

A prophetic hadith — transmitted as aḥad (non-mutawātir) — annuls a Quranic verse containing **كتب عليكم**. The argument: the inheritance verses (Q 4:11-12), revealed after Q 2:180, made the bequest to parents superfluous since they already receive a fixed share. The hadith merely **confirms** this abrogation.

Al-Shāfiʿī, aware of the theoretical problem, prefers to say that it is the inheritance verses (Q 4:11-12) that abrogate Q 2:180, and that the hadith is simply **mubayyinun** (explanatory). But in legal practice, it is indeed the hadith that is cited as the source of the rule — not the inheritance verses.

Al-Naḥḥās documents five different positions on this abrogation in his *Nāsikh wa-l-mansūkh* ([p. 46](https://shamela.ws/book/13073/46)).

## Case 4: Mutʿa — abrogated, restored, re-abrogated, re-restored...

### The verse

Q 4:24:

> **فَمَا اسْتَمْتَعْتُم بِهِ مِنْهُنَّ فَآتُوهُنَّ أُجُورَهُنَّ فَرِيضَةً**

> "Those [women] from whom you have derived pleasure (istamtaʿtum), give them their compensation — **an obligation.**"

For the Shiites, this verse explicitly authorizes temporary marriage (mutʿa). For the Sunnis, it is abrogated.

### The hadith of prohibition

Via ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib — Bukhārī no. 5115; Muslim no. 1407 ([p. 3335](https://shamela.ws/book/1727/3335)):

> إِنَّ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ ﷺ **نَهَى عَنْ مُتْعَةِ النِّسَاءِ** يَوْمَ خَيْبَرَ

> "The Prophet ﷺ **prohibited the mutʿa of women** on the day of Khaybar."

### The chronological chaos

The chapter title in Muslim is itself an admission:

> **بَابُ نِكَاحِ الْمُتْعَةِ وَبَيَانُ أَنَّهُ أُبِيحَ ثُمَّ نُسِخَ ثُمَّ أُبِيحَ ثُمَّ نُسِخَ**

> "Chapter on temporary marriage — explanation that it was **permitted, then abrogated, then permitted, then abrogated** — and that its prohibition is definitive until the Day of Resurrection."

Muslim needs **four stages** to explain the chronology of the abrogation. Permitted, then prohibited, then permitted, then prohibited. The traditions contradict each other on the date of prohibition: Khaybar? The conquest of Mecca? Ḥunayn? The ʿumra of al-Qaḍāʾ? Each chain gives a different date.

And above all: it is **ʿAlī** who transmits the prohibition — the same ʿAlī whose partisans (Shiites) consider mutʿa as licit. The Shiites respond that the prohibition comes from **ʿUmar**, not from the Prophet, and cite Ibn ʿUmar's own son who practiced it.

The naskh here does not resolve a contradiction — **it creates new ones**.

## Case 5: The fidya of fasting — the abrogation that never happened

Q 2:184 and Ramaḍān. The tradition claims that the fidya verse ("Those who can endure it: a compensation — feeding a poor person") was abrogated by Q 2:185 ("Whoever among you is present in this month, let him fast it").

But **Ibn ʿAbbās** — the first exegete — says: **لَيْسَتْ بِمَنْسُوخَةٍ** ("it is not abrogated"). And **ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ** — the muftī of Mecca — ate during Ramaḍān by invoking this verse. See [[Traditions/Ṣawm Ramaḍān]] and [[Persons/ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ]].

## The balance sheet — a tool out of control

### What naskh reveals about the system

Naskh is supposed to resolve the contradictions of the corpus. In reality, it **multiplies** them:

1. **The stoning verse** creates a precedent where the Quran can be **incomplete** — revealed verses are missing from the muṣḥaf, but their norms apply. This contradicts Q 15:9 ("It is We who sent down the Reminder, and it is We who are its guardians").

2. **Al-muʾallafatu qulūbuhum** creates a precedent where a **caliph** can neutralize a Quranic category containing **فريضة من الله** — on the basis of his own judgment (ijtihād). If ʿUmar can do it for Q 9:60, who prevents another caliph from doing it for another category?

3. **The waṣiyya** creates a precedent where an **aḥad hadith** (non-mutawātir) abrogates a **كتب عليكم** in the Quran. If the aḥad hadith can abrogate the Quran, the integrity of the text as the primary source of law is **structurally compromised**.

4. **The mutʿa** shows that the system does not even know **when** the abrogation took place — four different dates, and Muslim must invent a "permitted-abrogated-permitted-abrogated" scheme to reconcile the traditions.

5. **The fidya** shows that the abrogation is **contested from the very origin** by the most ancient authorities (Ibn ʿAbbās, ʿAṭāʾ) — meaning that the "consensus" on the abrogation is a late product, not a datum of the first century.

### The paradox of naskh

Naskh is presented as a **science** (ʿilm al-nāsikh wa-l-mansūkh) — a discipline that every mujtahid must master. But its concrete application reveals that it functions as a **mechanism of retrospective control**: the jurists of the 2nd-3rd centuries use naskh to impose their doctrinal conclusions, then project the abrogation into the past by attributing it to the Prophet, the Companions, or the text itself.

The Quran — which presents itself as **حكيم** (wise) and **مبين** (clear) — is subjected to a system that declares it **incomplete** (āyat al-rajm), **partially obsolete** (fidya, waṣiyya, mutʿa), and **modifiable by men** (al-muʾallafa). The tool meant to preserve the coherence of the text undermines it from within.
