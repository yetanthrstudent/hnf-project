# "In certain of the Books" (في بعض الكتب)

## The formula

The expression **في بعض الكتب** ("in certain of the Books") and its variants — **يقرأ الكتب** ("he used to read the Books"), **أجد في بعض الكتب** ("I find in certain of the Books") — are a **technical marker** in early Islamic sources. They systematically designate the consultation of a **corpus foreign to Islam**: Torah (توراة), Gospel (إنجيل), Psalms (زبور), or other biblical and para-biblical writings.

These are not "books" in the general sense. The word **الكتب** (al-kutub, "the Books") with the definite article refers specifically to the **earlier revealed Scriptures** — what the Islamic tradition calls the "books of God" (كتب الله) sent down before the Quran.

## How it works

The formula operates as a **legitimation mechanism**: it invokes the authority of earlier Scriptures without citing them precisely, making verification impossible. The speaker positions himself as an **exclusive intermediary** between an inaccessible corpus (in Hebrew, Syriac or Greek) and an Arabic-speaking audience that can neither read nor verify the source.

The circuit is always the same:
1. The "reader of the Books" **claims** to find information in the earlier Scriptures
2. He **formulates** it in Arabic, in an Islamic context (mosque, study circle, caliphal court)
3. The content is **absorbed** into the emerging Islamic corpus — ḥadīth, tafsīr, faḍā'il
4. The **original source** (Torah, Gospel, etc.) is never quoted verbatim nor verified

## The blurring of boundaries — "the Torah or the Book"

The most revealing case is that of **Wahb ibn Munabbih** (al-Zuhd of Aḥmad [p. 189](https://shamela.ws/book/11333/189)):

> « **أجد في التوراة أو في الكتاب** : أنا الله لا إله إلا أنا خالق الخلق خلقت الخير وخلقت من يكون الخير على يديه فطوبى لمن خلقته ليكون الخير على يديه أنا الله لا إله إلا أنا خلقت الشر وخلقت من يكون الشر على يديه فويل لمن خلقته ليكون الشر على يديه »

> "I find **in the Torah, or in the Book**: I am Allah, there is no deity but Me, the creator of all creatures. I created good and those through whose hands good shall come… I created evil and those through whose hands evil shall come…"

The hesitation "**في التوراة أو في الكتاب**" (in the Torah **or** in the Book) is crucial. Wahb no longer knows — or does not wish to specify — in which "Book" he found this text. The content is presented as a **divine speech in the first person** ("I am Allah"), with a theology of predestination (God creates both good and evil) that is not Quranic but **resembles** Quranic discourse. This is exactly the confusion mechanism: a foreign text, formulated in the register of Islamic revelation, injected into the corpus under the cover of scriptural authority.

The isnād is short: **Kulthūm ibn Jubayr → Wahb** — a single intermediary.

## "The revealed Book of God" — maximum confusion

The most ambiguous form of blurring is the expression "**في كتاب الله المنزل**" (in the revealed Book of God). In Islamic theology, the Torah and the Gospel are also "revealed Books of God" — so the speaker is not necessarily claiming to cite the Quran. But the wording is **problematic**: for an Arabic-speaking Muslim audience, "the revealed Book of God" spontaneously evokes the Quran, and the cited content does not appear in it. The ambiguity — intentional or not — blurs the boundary between corpora.

### 'Abd Allāh ibn 'Amr — "two categories in Hellfire"

Muṣannaf Ibn Abī Shayba ([p. 42550](https://shamela.ws/book/9944/42550)), via al-A'mash → Sālim ibn Abī al-Ja'd → Ibn 'Amr:

> « إنا لنجد **في كتاب الله المنزل** صنفين في النار : قوم يكونون في آخر الزمان معهم سياط كأنها أذناب البقر يضربون بها الناس على غير جرم... ونساء كاسيات عاريات مائلات مميلات لا يدخلن الجنة ولا يجدن ريحها »

> "We find **in the revealed Book of God** two categories in Hellfire: people at the end of times with whips like the tails of cows, striking people without cause… and women clothed yet naked, swaying and making others sway, who shall not enter Paradise."

This text does not exist in the Quran. Ibn 'Amr may designate by "كتاب الله المنزل" another revealed Book (Torah, Gospel) — which is consistent with his access to the zamālatain. But the wording is ambiguous for a Muslim audience, and the content is absorbed into the Islamic corpus without a clear marker of exteriority. Ibn Ḥajar discusses this passage in al-Qawl al-Musaddad ([p. 31](https://shamela.ws/book/6035/31)).

### Nawf al-Bikālī — "sheepskins, hearts of wolves"

Tafsīr al-Ṭabarī ([p. 2226](https://shamela.ws/book/7798/2226)), via Sa'īd ibn Abī Hilāl → al-Quraẓī → Nawf:

> « عن نوف، **وكان يقرأ الكتب**، قال: إني لأجد صفة ناس من هذه الأمة **في كتاب الله المنزل** : قوم يحتالون الدنيا بالدين ألسنتهم أحلى من العسل وقلوبهم أمر من الصبر **يلبسون لباس مسوك الضأن وقلوبهم قلوب الذئاب** »

> "Nawf — **and he used to read the Books** — said: I find the description of people of this community **in the revealed Book of God**: people who cheat the world through religion, their tongues sweeter than honey, their hearts more bitter than aloe, **they wear sheepskins but their hearts are the hearts of wolves**."

The image of "**sheepskins / hearts of wolves**" is a direct echo of **Matthew 7:15**: "Beware of false prophets, who come to you **in sheep's clothing**, but inwardly are **ravenous wolves**." Nawf — a disciple of Ka'b, who "used to read the Books" — may designate the Gospel by "كتاب الله المنزل", but the wording is ambiguous and the content enters the tafsīr without any marker of exteriority.

Al-Quraẓī (Muḥammad ibn Ka'b, descendant of the Jewish tribe of Qurayẓa) adds: "I checked in the Quran, and they are the hypocrites" — he seeks *a posteriori* to connect Nawf's citation to the Quranic text.

### The mechanism

The progression is clear:
1. **في بعض الكتب** ("in certain Books") — cautious formula, acknowledges the otherness of the corpus
2. **في التوراة أو في الكتاب** ("in the Torah or the Book") — hesitation, vagueness about the source
3. **في كتاب الله المنزل** ("in the revealed Book of God") — **maximally ambiguous** wording: can designate the Torah or the Gospel (which are also "revealed Books of God" in Islamic theology), but for a Muslim audience the expression spontaneously evokes the Quran, and the cited content does not appear in it

This gradation shows how the **marker of exteriority** progressively fades. The speaker moves from honest admission ("I find in the Torah") to a formulation that no longer clearly distinguishes the source. The result is the same: foreign scriptural material is absorbed into the emerging Islamic corpus — tafsīr, hadith, faḍā'il — without the listener being able to identify its provenance.

## What the formula reveals

When a figure is described as "reading the Books", the sources implicitly acknowledge that he draws from a **non-Islamic corpus**. It is an admission: the transmitted material does not come from the Prophet or the Companions, but from an earlier scriptural tradition.

The fact that **Khālid ibn Ma'dān** — chief of police under Yazīd, ascetic of the ribāṭ, future abdāl of Sufism — is described with the same formula as Ka'b al-Ahbar shows that this network of "readers of the Books" is broader than the immediate circle Ka'b → Nawf → Shahr → Wahb. It also reaches the military and ascetic circles of Homs.
