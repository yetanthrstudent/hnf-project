# Al-Qadar — free will as political crime

Qadar is not an abstract theological question. It is **the first political debate of Islam** — and the bodies of the executed prove it.

The question is simple: when an Umayyad governor massacres, confiscates, tortures — **did God will it?** If yes, contesting the governor is contesting God. If no, man is free and the governor is a tyrant who can be overthrown.

The Umayyads chose the first answer — **jabr** (absolute predestination) — and imposed it as state ideology. Those who chose the second — **qadar** (free will) — were crucified.

---

## The Umayyad thesis: "God willed us"

### The mechanism

The schema is structurally simple:

```
Premise 1:  Everything that happens is willed by God (jabr)
Premise 2:  The Umayyads are in power
──────────────────────────────────────────────
Conclusion: Umayyad power is willed by God
            → opposing it = opposing God
```

Crone and Hinds (*God's Caliph*, Cambridge, 1986) showed that the Umayyads used the title of **khalīfat Allāh** ("God's vicegerent") — and not khalīfat rasūl Allāh ("successor of the Prophet") — in its most literal sense. The caliph is God's direct representative on earth. His authority is both political and religious, and he is accountable only to God.

Within this framework, predestination is not a theological luxury — it is the **foundation of legitimacy**. If men are free, they can choose another caliph. If everything is predetermined, the sitting caliph is irreplaceable.

### The apparatus of coercion

| Caliph / Governor | Action |
|-------------------|--------|
| **Muʿāwiya** | Founds Umayyad legitimacy on power and qadar ("God gave me power") |
| **ʿAbd al-Malik ibn Marwān** | Writes to al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī demanding clarifications on qadar — the debate threatens him |
| **Al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf** | Crucifies **Maʿbad al-Juhanī** (~80H) — first Qadarite executed |
| **Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik** | Has the hands and feet of **Ghaylān al-Dimashqī** cut off, then crucifies him (~105H) |

The violence is not collateral — it is **programmatic**. Executing a Qadarite is defending the dogma that supports the throne.

## The martyrs of free will

### Maʿbad al-Juhanī (d. ~80H) — the first

Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ ([p. 3339](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3339)). Basran, from the tribe of Juhayna. First to "speak of qadar in Baṣra" — the sources systematically present him as the origin of the movement.

**His thesis**: men possess free will (ikhtiyār) and cannot be compelled to accept a predetermined destiny. Direct implication: if men are free, Umayyad power **is not ordained by God**, and people have the capacity to determine their own political future.

**His engagement**: he did not remain in theological abstraction. He took up arms in the **revolt of Ibn al-Ashʿath** against al-Ḥajjāj (81-83H) — one of the most important uprisings against Umayyad power in Iraq. Mālik ibn Dīnār testifies: "I met Maʿbad in Mecca after the fitna of Ibn al-Ashʿath, wounded, **having fought al-Ḥajjāj in every battle**."

**His execution**: crucified by order of ʿAbd al-Malik (or al-Ḥajjāj directly — the sources diverge). The context is the repression following the defeat of Ibn al-Ashʿath.

**The crucial point for hadith criticism**: the critics of transmitters (rijāl) evaluate Maʿbad as **thiqa** (Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn) and **ṣadūq fī al-ḥadīth** (Abū Ḥātim). He is technically reliable — but theologically condemned. The tension between technical competence and doctrinal orthodoxy is the heart of the problem: **the "science of hadith" is not neutral — it serves a doctrinal framework that was itself imposed by violence**.

### Ghaylān al-Dimashqī (d. ~105H) — the Damascene

Tārīkh Dimashq ([p. 22355](https://shamela.ws/book/71/22355)). Coptic father converted to Islam, mawlā of ʿUthmān. Trained under al-Ḥasan ibn Muḥammad ibn al-Ḥanafiyya (Medina) and [[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī]] (Basra). Resided in Damascus, near Bāb al-Farādīs.

**His double doctrine**:
1. **On qadar**: man is **mukhtār** (choosing) and will be judged for his choice
2. **On the caliphate**: whoever fulfills the conditions of command deserves it, **even if he is not of Quraysh** — and he is established only by the community's consensus

The second thesis is a **frontal attack** on Umayyad legitimacy — and against the Qurashite principle that supports it.

**The ʿUmar II parenthesis**: the reformist caliph ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz (r. 99-101H) initially favors him, charging him with **distributing the assets confiscated from corrupt governors**. Ghaylān proclaims in the markets:

> **هَلُمُّوا إِلَى مَتَاعِ الظَّلَمَةِ، هَلُمُّوا إِلَى مَتَاعِ الخَوَنَةِ**

> "Come get the possessions of the **tyrants**! Come get the possessions of the **traitors**!"

These words reached **Hishām ibn ʿAbd al-Malik**, then crown prince, who swore: **"If I catch him, I will cut off his hands and feet."**

When ʿUmar II learned of Ghaylān's Qadarite views, he summoned him for a debate with **Iyās ibn Muʿāwiya**. Ghaylān retracted in appearance. But after ʿUmar's death (101H), he returned to his doctrines.

**The debate with al-Awzāʿī**: Hishām, having become caliph, ordered a public debate. [[Persons/Al-Awzāʿī|Al-Awzāʿī]] posed four questions on the divine decree. To each, Ghaylān answered: **"لَا عِلْمَ لِي بِهَذَا"** — "I have no knowledge of this." Al-Awzāʿī declared: **"القدرية خصماء الله عز وجل في الأرض"** — "The Qadarites are God's adversaries on earth."

**The execution**: his hands and feet were cut off. He was crucified at **Bāb Kaysān** (Damascus). Mutilated and crucified, **he continued to harangue the crowd** — Hishām ordered that his tongue be cut out, after which he died.

**The reactions**: Numayr ibn Aws (qāḍī of Damascus) congratulated Hishām. Pro-Umayyads declared: **"His execution surpasses the killing of two thousand Romans in value!"**

His teacher al-Ḥasan ibn Muḥammad ibn al-Ḥanafiyya had predicted: "Do you see that man? He is God's proof against the people of Shām... but **the young man will be killed**!"

## The suspects — spared by their usefulness

### Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī — the Risāla to the caliph

[[Persons/al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī|Al-Ḥasan]] is the **greatest tābiʿī** — sayyid al-tābiʿīn. And he is suspected of qadarism.

The **Risālat al-qadar ilā ʿAbd al-Malik** is the oldest document associated with the Qadarite movement — an epistle addressed to the caliph defending free will and human responsibility on the basis of the Quran. Al-Ḥasan argues therein that **God did not compel men to sin** and that the Quran systematically attributes bad actions to men and good ones to God.

Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal decides: "Qatāda and Saʿīd spoke of qadar while hiding it. Both repented, **as their master repented**" — the "master" being al-Ḥasan. Repentance (tawba) is the escape hatch that allows al-Ḥasan to be preserved in the Sunni tradition.

Authenticity problem: some scholars (including Suleiman Mourad, *Early Islam between Myth and History*, Brill, 2006) consider that the complete manuscripts of the Risāla are **pseudepigraphic** — possibly composed in Muʿtazilite circles in the 3rd-4th century. But even if the Risāla is late, the **accusation** of qadarism against al-Ḥasan is ancient and multiple.

**Why al-Ḥasan was not executed**: his prestige was too great. Maʿbad was an armed militant who could be crucified. Ghaylān was an eccentric mawlā. Al-Ḥasan was the intellectual pole of Basra — killing him would have provoked a revolt. The system preferred to absorb him, then invent his retraction.

### Qatāda ibn Diʿāma — the canonical Qadarite

[[Persons/Qatāda ibn Diʿāma|Qatāda]] is unanimously recognized as **thiqa thabt** (reliable and solid) — and accused of qadarism. The two facts coexist in the same biographical notices.

**Abū Dāwūd** tries to save the file: "Nothing is authentic concerning Qatāda and qadar" (لَيْسَ يَصِحُّ عَنْ قَتَادَةَ فِي القَدَرِ شَيْءٌ). Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal invokes, once again, **tawba**: he repented, like his master al-Ḥasan.

The Qatāda case is the most revealing: a transmitter whose hadiths populate the Ṣaḥīḥayn, whose memory is legendary, whose exegesis is authoritative — and who shared the doctrine for which Maʿbad was crucified. The only difference: Qatāda did not take up arms. His usefulness for the isnād system protected him.

## The hadith of Jibrīl — the anti-Qadarite weapon

The [[Traditions/Ḥadīth Jibrīl|hadith of Jibrīl]] (Muslim no. 8, [p. 80](https://shamela.ws/book/711/80)) is the textbook case of instrumentalization. The hadith does **not** begin with the scene of the angel — it begins with a **political preamble**:

> كَانَ أَوَّلَ مَنْ قَالَ فِي القَدَرِ بِالبَصْرَةِ **مَعْبَدٌ الجُهَنِيُّ** [...] فَقُلْنَا: لَوْ لَقِينَا أَحَداً مِنْ أَصْحَابِ رَسُولِ اللهِ ﷺ **فَسَأَلْنَاهُ عَمَّا يَقُولُ هَؤُلَاءِ فِي القَدَرِ**

> "The first to speak of qadar in Baṣra was **Maʿbad al-Juhanī** [...] We said to ourselves: if we could meet a Companion to ask him about **what these people say about qadar**."

Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur and Ḥumayd ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān find Ibn ʿUmar, who first condemns them — **"tell them that I disassociate from them"** — then, and only after, recounts the scene of the angel. In the list of pillars of faith dictated by Jibrīl, qadar features prominently: **"أَنْ تُؤْمِنَ بِالقَدَرِ خَيْرِهِ وَشَرِّهِ"** — "that you believe in qadar, its good and its evil."

The hadith of Jibrīl is transmitted through a **double tafarrud** (see [[Traditions/Ḥadīth Jibrīl]]): Yaḥyā ibn Yaʿmur is the only one to transmit from Ibn ʿUmar, and ʿAbd Allāh ibn Burayda is the only one to transmit from Yaḥyā. A cosmic event — the visit of an angel — that only emerges in the context of a specific political debate, in Baṣra, at the turn of the 1st century.

## The founding hadiths of qadar — ʿUbāda and the Pen

Before the "majūs" hadith, we must examine the **founding hadith** of jabriyya doctrine: that of the **Pen** (al-qalam), attributed to ʿUbāda ibn al-Ṣāmit.

### The framing narrative — ʿUbāda on his deathbed

Kitāb al-Sunna of Ibn Abī ʿĀṣim ([p. 517](https://shamela.ws/book/13035/517)):

> عُبَادَةُ بْنُ الصَّامِتِ لَمَّا احْتُضِرَ سَأَلَهُ ابْنُهُ فَقَالَ: يَا أَبَتِ أَوْصِنِي [...] يَا بُنَيَّ، اتَّقِ اللَّهَ، وَلَنْ تَتَّقِيَ اللَّهَ حَتَّى تُؤْمِنَ بِاللَّهِ، وَلَنْ تُؤْمِنَ بِاللَّهِ حَتَّى **تُؤْمِنَ بِالْقَدَرِ خَيْرِهِ وَشَرِّهِ** [...] سَمِعْتُ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ يَقُولُ: **القَدَرُ عَلَى هَذَا — مَنْ مَاتَ عَلَى غَيْرِ هَذَا دَخَلَ النَّارَ**

> "ʿUbāda, on his deathbed, said to his son: 'O my son, fear God — and you will not fear God until you believe in Him, and you will not believe in God until you **believe in qadar, its good and its evil** [...] I heard the Prophet ﷺ say: **Qadar is thus — whoever dies without believing in this will enter the Fire.**'"

The chain passes through **Sulaymān ibn Ḥabīb** (judge in Damascus) ← **ʿUthmān ibn Abī al-ʿĀtika** ← **al-Walīd ibn Muslim** — an **entirely Damascene** circuit.

### The hadith of the Pen — "Write the destinies of all things"

The most developed version: Sunan Abī Dāwūd no. 4700 ([p. 3975](https://shamela.ws/book/117359/3975)):

> سَمِعْتُ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ ﷺ يَقُولُ: **إِنَّ أَوَّلَ مَا خَلَقَ اللهُ الْقَلَمُ، فَقَالَ لَهُ: اكْتُبْ، قَالَ: رَبِّ، وَمَاذَا أَكْتُبُ؟ قَالَ: اكْتُبْ مَقَادِيرَ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ حَتَّى تَقُومَ السَّاعَةُ**

> "I heard the Prophet ﷺ say: **'The first thing God created is the Pen. He said to it: Write. It asked: Lord, what shall I write? He said: Write the destinies of all things until the establishment of the Hour.'**"

Chain: **al-Walīd ibn Rabāḥ** ← **Ibrāhīm ibn Abī ʿAbla** (treasurer of al-Walīd ibn ʿAbd al-Malik — the governor) ← **Abū Ḥafṣa** (**unknown**) ← ʿUbāda.

Another route ([p. 202](https://shamela.ws/book/13035/202)): **Muʿāwiya ibn Yaḥyā** (bayt al-māl) ← **al-Zuhrī** ← **Muḥammad ibn ʿUbāda ibn al-Ṣāmit**. Problems: no other student of al-Zuhrī transmits this hadith; Muḥammad ibn ʿUbāda **does not exist** in the repertoires — a phantom son.

### Second founding hadith — Jābir and the Pen

The same doctrine (the Pen writes everything in advance) is also attributed to **Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh**, via a direct prophetic hadith in the Matālib al-ʿĀliya ([p. 3986](https://shamela.ws/book/22804/3986)):

> إِنَّ فِي بَعْضِ مَا أَنْزَلَ اللهُ تَعَالَى مِنَ الكُتُبِ: **إِنِّي أَنَا اللهُ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا أَنَا قَدَّرْتُ الْخَيْرَ وَالشَّرَّ**

> "It is written in some of the Books that God revealed: **'I am God, there is no deity but Me — I have decreed the good and the evil.'**"

Chain: **Sālim al-Khurāsānī** ← Nāfiʿ ← al-Qāsim ← Muḥammad ibn ʿAlī ← Jābir. The Prophet cites **the ancient Books** as source — the same material that [[Persons/Wahb ibn Munabbih|Wahb]] attributes to the "Torah."

### The cosmogonic variant — the Nūn and the inkwell

Ibn Abī ʿĀṣim, Kitāb al-Sunna ([p. 200](https://shamela.ws/book/13035/200)), via **al-Ḥasan ibn Yaḥyā al-Khushanī** (Damascus) ← Abū ʿAbd Allāh mawlā Banī Umayya ← Abū Ṣāliḥ ← Abū Hurayra:

> أَوَّلُ شَيْءٍ خَلَقَ اللهُ **الْقَلَمُ**، ثُمَّ خَلَقَ بَعْدَهُ **النُّونَ وَهِيَ الدَّوَاةُ** [...] اكْتُبْ مَا يَكُونُ وَمَا هُوَ كَائِنٌ مِنْ عَمَلٍ أَوْ أَثَرٍ أَوْ رِزْقٍ [...] ثُمَّ **خَتَمَ عَلَى الْقَلَمِ فَلَمْ يَنْطِقْ وَلَا يَنْطِقُ إِلَى يَوْمِ الْقِيَامَةِ**

> "The first thing God created is the **Pen**, then He created after it the **Nūn — which is the inkwell** [...] Write all that will be [...] then He **sealed the Pen, and it spoke no more and will speak no more until the Day of Resurrection**."

The **mawlā of Banū Umayya** — literally "the freedman of the Umayyads" — is in the chain. The hadith even explains Q 68:1 ("Nūn. By the Pen") as a cosmogonic reference to predestination.

### What the chains reveal

All routes of the Pen hadith converge on **Umayyad functionaries**:

| Transmitter | Function / Milieu |
|-------------|------------------|
| Sulaymān ibn Ḥabīb | **Judge** in Damascus |
| Ibrāhīm ibn Abī ʿAbla | **Treasurer** of governor al-Walīd |
| Muʿāwiya ibn Yaḥyā | **Bayt al-māl** (treasury) |
| Al-Walīd ibn Muslim | Damascene, notorious mudallis |

The hadith that founds jabriyya doctrine — "everything is written in advance" — circulates in the **Umayyad administrative circles of Damascus**. The "unknown intermediaries" (Abū Ḥafṣa, Abū Yazīd al-Azdī) between ʿUbāda and these functionaries are unverifiable.

## "The Qadarites are the Zoroastrians of this community"

### The hadith — seven routes, all weak

The complete corpus of the "majūs" hadith can be reconstituted from several collections. Seven main routes, none solid:

**Route 1** — Abū Dāwūd 4691 ([p. 3966](https://shamela.ws/book/117359/3966)), via **ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz ibn Abī Ḥāzim** ← his father ← Ibn ʿUmar:

> **القَدَرِيَّةُ مَجُوسُ هَذِهِ الأُمَّةِ : إِنْ مَرِضُوا فَلَا تَعُودُوهُمْ، وَإِنْ مَاتُوا فَلَا تَشْهَدُوهُمْ**

Problem: Abū Ḥāzim Salama ibn Dīnār — his own son declares: "Whoever claims that my father heard from a Companion other than Sahl ibn Saʿd **has lied**" (Ibn Ḥajar, Tahdhīb 4/126). **Munqaṭiʿ**.

**Route 2** — Aḥmad 5584 ([p. 2360](https://shamela.ws/book/98139/2360)), via **ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra** ← Ibn ʿUmar:

> **لِكُلِّ أُمَّةٍ مَجُوسٌ، وَمَجُوسُ أُمَّتِي الَّذِينَ يَقُولُونَ: لَا قَدَرَ**

**ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra** is the pivot of several anti-Qadarite chains — see the dedicated section below.

**Route 3** — Abū Dāwūd 4692 ([p. 3966](https://shamela.ws/book/117359/3966)), via **ʿUmar ibn Muḥammad** ← **ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra** ← an anonymous Anṣārī ← **Ḥudhayfa** — with the explosive addition:

> **وَهُمْ شِيعَةُ الدَّجَّالِ، وَحَقٌّ عَلَى اللهِ أَنْ يُلْحِقَهُمْ بِالدَّجَّالِ**

> "They are the **partisans of the Dajjāl**, and it is God's right to join them with the Dajjāl."

Chain: anonymous intermediary ("a man of the Anṣār"), ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra again.

**Route 4** — Ibn Abī ʿĀṣim, Kitāb al-Sunna ([p. 105](https://shamela.ws/book/1198/105)), via **Baqiyya ibn al-Walīd** ← al-Awzāʿī ← Ibn Jurayj ← Abū al-Zubayr ← **Jābir**:

> **إِنَّ مَجُوسَ هَذِهِ الأُمَّةِ الْمُكَذِّبُونَ بِأَقْدَارِ اللَّهِ**

**Baqiyya** is a notorious Shāmī mudallis who practices "tadlīs al-taswiya."

**Route 5** — Ṭabarānī al-Kabīr ([p. 12565](https://shamela.ws/book/1733/12565)), via **ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra** ← Abū al-Aswad al-Duʾalī ← ʿImrān ibn Ḥuṣayn:

> **لَوْ كَانَتِ الجِبَالُ لِأَحَدِكُمْ ذَهَباً فَأَنْفَقَهَا فِي سَبِيلِ اللهِ، ثُمَّ لَمْ يُؤْمِنْ بِالقَدَرِ خَيْرِهِ وَشَرِّهِ، لَمْ يَنْتَفِعْ بِذَلِكَ**

Again ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra — with an isnād featuring **three Companions** (ʿImrān, Ibn Masʿūd, Ubayy ibn Kaʿb) relaying the same hadith, giving an impression of mass.

**Route 6** — Ṭabarānī al-Awsaṭ ([p. 3270](https://shamela.ws/book/13162/3270)), via ʿAṭāʾ al-Khurāsānī ← **[[Persons/Makhūl al-Shāmī|Makhūl]]** ← Abū Hurayra. Problem: Makhūl **never met Abū Hurayra** — munqaṭiʿa chain.

**Route 7** — Ṭabarānī al-Kabīr ([p. 924](https://shamela.ws/book/13162/924)), via **Nuʿaym ibn Ḥammād** ← **Baqiyya ibn al-Walīd** ← Arṭāʾa ibn al-Mundhir ← Mujāhid ← Ibn ʿUmar. Problem: Nuʿaym (controversial) + Baqiyya (mudallis) = cumulative chain of weakness.

### ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra — the recurring pivot

ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd Allāh mawlā Ghufra is the **central figure** of the anti-Qadarite network: three of the seven routes pass through him. Profile:

- **Ḍaʿīf** (weak) according to the consensus of critics
- **Qāṣṣ** — preacher-storyteller, like [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Diʿāma|Qatāda]]
- **Massive irsāl** — cannot have heard directly from the Companions he cites
- His associate is **Abū Khālid al-Khushanī** ([p. 6037](https://shamela.ws/book/71/6037)), who transmits hadiths about the plague (al-Jābiya = Damascus) and innovation — same Damascene milieu
- **Mālik** knew him personally but **refuses to take knowledge from him** — "I did not take knowledge from him" (evasion)

ʿUmar mawlā Ghufra is a **Damascene qāṣṣ** who mass-produces anti-Qadarite hadiths, attributing them to various Companions via disconnected chains. He is the local equivalent of what [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Diʿāma|Qatāda]] does in Basra for other themes — the qaṣṣ generates the hadith.

### The structural irony

The comparison with Zoroastrians (majūs) rests on an argument: the Majūs believe in two principles (Good/Evil, Ahura Mazdā/Ahriman), and the Qadarites "attribute evil to other than God." But the accusation is **reversible**: if God predetermines everything, including evil, it is the **jabriyya** doctrine that attributes evil to God — a far more problematic theological position.

The hadith directly serves the Umayyad political agenda: it transforms opponents of jabr into **heretics assimilated to infidels** (the Majūs). The fact that all its chains are weak and converge on **Damascene circles** makes it a serious candidate for **political fabrication**.

## Wahb ibn Munabbih — the "72 Books" in the service of jabr

[[Persons/Wahb ibn Munabbih|Wahb]] is not a spectator of the debate. He is the **champion of jabr** — the one who supplies the Umayyads with a corpus of "scriptural proofs" drawn from the "ancient Books."

### The material

1. **96 unanimous books**: "I have read 72 books and 24 others — and not a single one fails to contain: 'When the servant attributes something of the will to himself, he has committed unbelief'" (al-Bayhaqī, al-Qaḍāʾ wa-l-qadar, [p. 150](https://shamela.ws/book/13053/150))

2. **Preaching from the minbar**: "I found in the Book of God: good comes from Me and I have decreed it for the best of My servants [...] and evil comes from Me and I have decreed it for the worst of My creatures" ([p. 132](https://shamela.ws/book/13053/132))

3. **Citing the "Torah"**: "I am God — I created good and I created the one through whose hands good will come [...] I created evil and I created the one through whose hands evil will come" (Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, [p. 189](https://shamela.ws/book/11333/189))

Wahb mobilizes [[Concepts/Isrā'īliyyāt]] — unverifiable biblical traditions — to found jabr. It is the same mechanism as [[Ka'b al-Ahbar]] for the faḍāʾil of Shām: the "Books" say whatever one needs them to say.

### The propaganda hadith — "Wahb the wisdom, Ghaylān the fitna"

Tārīkh Dimashq ([p. 22339](https://shamela.ws/book/71/22339)):

> عَنْ مَرْوَانَ بْنِ سَالِمٍ، عَنْ خَالِدِ بْنِ مَعْدَانَ، عَنْ عُبَادَةَ بْنِ الصَّامِتِ: **يَكُونُ فِي أُمَّتِي رَجُلَانِ أَحَدُهُمَا وَهْبٌ يَهَبُ اللهُ لَهُ الحِكْمَةَ وَالآخَرُ غَيْلَانُ فِتْنَتُهُ عَلَى هَذِهِ الأُمَّةِ أَشَدُّ مِنْ فِتْنَةِ الشَّيْطَانِ**

> "There will be in my community two men: one is **Wahb**, to whom God will grant wisdom — and the other is **Ghaylān**, whose temptation will be **worse than Satan's**."

Chain: **Marwān ibn Sālim** (weak) ← **[[Persons/Khālid ibn Maʿdān|Khālid ibn Maʿdān]]** (chief of police of Homs) ← ʿUbāda. A hadith in which the Prophet **predicts by name** the opposition between the champion of jabr (Wahb) and the champion of qadar (Ghaylān) — fabricated after the fact, transmitted by the chief of police.

### The direct political use — ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz

Even the "reformist" caliph uses qadar as an excuse. Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq via Maʿmar ([p. 744](https://shamela.ws/book/20879/744)):

> كَتَبَ عُمَرُ بْنُ عَبْدِ العَزِيزِ إِلَى عَدِيِّ بْنِ أَرْطَاةَ: أَمَّا بَعْدُ: فَإِنَّ اسْتِعْمَالَكَ سَعْدَ بْنَ مَسْعُودٍ عَلَى عُمَانَ **مِنَ الخَطَايَا الَّتِي قَدَّرَ اللهُ عَزَّ وَجَلَّ عَلَيْكَ وَقَدَّرَ أَنْ تُبْتَلَى بِهَا**

> "ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz writes to ʿAdī ibn Arṭāʾa: your appointment of Saʿd ibn Masʿūd as governor of Oman is among **the sins that God decreed for you and decreed that you be tested with**."

Who is ʿAdī ibn Arṭāʾa? A **Damascene**, former chief of the shurṭa (police) under al-Walīd, whom ʿUmar II appoints governor of Basra in 99H. Primary sources document his abuses:

**The dream interpreted by Ibn Sīrīn** — Tārīkh Dimashq ([p. 17985](https://shamela.ws/book/71/17985)): ʿAdī dreams he is milking a she-camel — first milk, then blood. Ibn Sīrīn interprets:

> أَمَّا البُخْتِيَّةُ فَهَؤُلَاءِ قَوْمٌ مِنَ العَجَمِ وَالحَلْبُ جِبَايَةٌ وَاللَّبَنُ حَلَالٌ [...] ثُمَّ تَعَدَّيْتَ فَجَبَيْتَهُمْ حَرَامًا — الدَّمُ **تَجَاوَزْتَ مَا أَحَلَّ اللهُ لَكَ إِلَى مَا حَرَّمَ عَلَيْكَ**

> "The she-camel represents non-Arabs. Milking = levying tax. The milk = licit: you first levied licitly. Then **you crossed the line and levied illicitly** — the blood: **you exceeded what God permitted you toward what He forbade you**."

**The black turban letter** — Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq via Maʿmar ([p. 5387](https://shamela.ws/book/84/5387)), and Tārīkh Dimashq ([p. 17986](https://shamela.ws/book/71/17986)):

> إِنَّكَ غَرَرْتَنِي بِعِمَامَتِكَ السَّوْدَاءِ وَمُجَالَسَتِكَ القُرَّاءَ وَإِرْسَالِكَ العِمَامَةَ مِنْ وَرَائِكَ وَإِنَّكَ أَظْهَرْتَ لِي الخَيْرَ فَأَحْسَنْتُ بِكَ الظَّنَّ **وَقَدْ أَظْهَرَنَا اللهُ عَلَى كَثِيرٍ مِمَّا كُنْتُمْ تَكْتُمُونَ**

> "You deceived me with your **black turban**, your frequenting of the reciters, and your turban trailing behind [signs of piety]. You showed me good and I thought well of you — but **God has revealed to us much of what you were concealing**."

ʿUmar II **knows** that ʿAdī is a deceiver, a tax collector who crosses from licit to illicit. And yet, in the same correspondence, he **excuses his crimes through qadar**: the appointment of a corrupt governor is among the sins God "decreed." The "reformist" caliph uses predestination exactly like his Umayyad predecessors: divine will absorbs political responsibility. ʿUmar II **is** an Umayyad — he never claimed to overthrow the dynasty, only to moralize it. And yet, at the moment of accounting for a corrupt governor, he resorts to the same ideological tool as the caliphs he denounces.

The letter is attested by at least two independent chains: Maʿmar (Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq / Sunna of ʿAbd Allāh ibn Aḥmad, [p. 744](https://shamela.ws/book/20879/744)) and al-Awzāʿī ← Yaḥyā ibn Kathīr (Tārīkh Dimashq, [p. 9195](https://shamela.ws/book/71/9195)) — in this second version, the "خطايا" (sins) become "ذنوب" (faults), with no change in meaning.

**ʿAdī contests the reforms** — Tārīkh Dimashq ([p. 17987](https://shamela.ws/book/71/17987)): ʿAdī asks Bakr ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Muzanī:

> أَفِي حَقِّ اللهِ مَا يَصْنَعُ هَذَا الرَّجُلُ — يَعْنِي عُمَرَ بْنَ عَبْدِ العَزِيزِ — **يَرُدُّ أَعْمَالَ الخُلَفَاءِ قَبْلَهُ وَيُسَمِّيهَا المَظَالِمَ**

> "Is it God's right, what **this man** does — meaning ʿUmar II — **who overturns the decisions of the caliphs before him and calls them 'injustices'**?"

The governor appointed by the "pious caliph" openly contests his reforms. Predestination functions as **automatic absolution** of power — and power knows it.

## From qadar to the Muʿtazila — systematization

**Wāṣil ibn ʿAṭāʾ** (d. 131H/748) and **ʿAmr ibn ʿUbayd** — two students of al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī — transformed diffuse qadarism into an organized theological system. The Muʿtazila inherits from qadar its central thesis — man is free and responsible — and integrates it into the five principles (al-uṣūl al-khamsa):

| Principle | Link to qadar |
|----------|--------------|
| **Al-tawḥīd** (divine unity) | God is one — He does not cause evil |
| **Al-ʿadl** (divine justice) | God is just — man is free, otherwise punishment is unjust |
| **Al-waʿd wa-l-waʿīd** (promise and threat) | Rewards and punishments are **deserved** — they presuppose freedom |
| **Al-manzila bayn al-manzilatayn** | The grave sinner is neither believer nor unbeliever |
| **Al-amr bi-l-maʿrūf** | The obligation to command good **implies the capacity to act** |

The Muʿtazila systematizes what qadarism had begun in a more diffuse and more political manner. Many historians struggle to distinguish the first Muʿtazilites from the last Qadarites — the boundary is chronological rather than doctrinal.

## The Khārijite link

The Khārijites and the Qadarites share a **fundamental opposition to Umayyad power**, even if their means differ:

| | Khārijites | Qadarites |
|-|------------|-----------|
| **Means** | Arms | Theology |
| **Thesis** | The caliph must be morally irreproachable — otherwise, deposition | Man is free — therefore the tyrant is responsible for his tyranny |
| **On Quraysh** | Anyone can be caliph, even a slave | Ghaylān: same position |
| **Violence** | Open war against the caliphs | Maʿbad takes up arms; Ghaylān harangues the crowd |

The link is **political** rather than strictly theological: any doctrine that attributes free will and moral responsibility to man **de facto delegitimizes** a power that claims divine destiny.

## The balance sheet — qadar as a test of the system

The qadar dossier is a **decisive test** for the claimed neutrality of hadith science:

1. **The anti-Qadarite hadiths** ("majūs hādhihi al-umma") have weak or disconnected chains — but they are widely disseminated because they serve power

2. **The hadith of Jibrīl** — a cosmic event — only emerges in the context of a specific political debate, transmitted through a double tafarrud, and its preamble is explicitly anti-Qadarite

3. **The Qadarite transmitters** (Qatāda, al-Ḥasan) are maintained in the system **because they are too useful** for the isnāds — but their retraction (tawba) is invented to neutralize the problem

4. **The executed Qadarites** (Maʿbad, Ghaylān) are technically reliable (Yaḥyā ibn Maʿīn: "thiqa") — but doctrinally condemned. The criterion is not competence, it is **political alignment**

5. **The violence** — crucifixions, mutilations, exiles — is not an accident: it is the **mechanism of production of the "consensus."** The ijmāʿ on jabr is not the fruit of free debate — it is the result of systematic repression of dissenting voices

The Sunni "consensus" on predestination — the one later codified as "believe in qadar, its good and its evil" — is not a neutral intellectual achievement. It is the **product of an Umayyad political victory**, consolidated by violence, sanctuarized by hadiths, and maintained by the excommunication of those who contest it.
