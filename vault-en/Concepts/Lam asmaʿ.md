# Lam asmaʿ — « لم أسمع »

The formula **« لم أسمع »** (lam asmaʿ — "I have not heard") recurs frequently among jurists of the first and second centuries. It is found extensively in the Muṣannaf collections (ʿAbd al-Razzāq, Ibn Abī Shayba), where it serves as a response to legal questions. Its exact meaning and epistemological scope are subject to debate.

## The formula in the practice of the early scholars

### Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī (d. ~96H) — the mechanism made explicit

Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī openly describes how he operates when he has no tradition:

> أَكُلُّ مَا تُفْتِي بِهِ النَّاسُ سَمِعْتَهُ؟ قَالَ: لَا وَلَكِنْ **بَعْضَهُ سَمِعْتُ وَقِسْتُ مَا لَمْ أَسْمَعْ عَلَى مَا سَمِعْتُ**

> "Everything you give as fatwa to the people — did you hear it? — No, but **I heard part of it, and I measured by analogy what I had not heard against what I had heard.**"

— Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr, Jāmiʿ bayān al-ʿilm, no. 1646 ([p. 1103](https://shamela.ws/book/22367/1103))

This is the model of the pre-hadith jurist: he reasons from what he has received, and extends by qiyās what he has not received. The "لم أسمع" is not an admission of ignorance — it is the **starting point** of legal reasoning.

### ʿUrwa and Abū Bakr ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān — when the hadith comes afterwards

> الْتَقَى أَبِي وَعُرْوَةُ فَذَكَرَا مَسَّ الذَّكَرِ، فَقَالَ أَبِي: **لَمْ أَسْمَعْ بِشَيْءٍ**، قَالَ عُرْوَةُ: **وَأَنَا لَمْ أَسْمَعْ فِيهِ بِشَيْءٍ**، **فَأَرْسَلَ إِلَى بُسْرَةَ** فَأَخْبَرَتْ أَنَّ رَسُولَ اللَّهِ ﷺ قَالَ: «مَنْ مَسَّ فَرْجَهُ فَلْيَتَوَضَّأْ»

> "My father [Abū Bakr ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān] and ʿUrwa met and discussed touching the genitals. My father said: **'I have heard nothing.'** ʿUrwa said: **'I too have heard nothing on this matter.'** **So they sent for Busra** [bint Ṣafwān], who informed them that the Prophet ﷺ said: 'Whoever touches his genitals, let him perform ablution.'"

— Musnad Isḥāq ibn Rāhawayh, vol. 5 ([p. 2034](https://shamela.ws/book/13159/2034))

Two of the greatest Medinan jurists — ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr and Abū Bakr ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān, both among the "seven jurists of Medina" — say "لم أسمع" on a question. Yet the hadith existed, transmitted by a female Companion living in the same city. Their "لم أسمع" was a subjective observation, not proof of non-existence.

But the sequel is significant: **someone corrects them**. Busra is there, the hadith circulates, one only needs to ask.

### Anas ibn Mālik and the Basmala — "لم أسمع" does not mean "it does not exist"

Anas says:

> **لَمْ أَسْمَعْ أَحَدًا مِنْهُمْ يَقْرَأُ: بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَنِ الرَّحِيمِ**

> "I did not hear any of them recite: 'In the name of God, the Merciful, the Compassionate.'"

Ibn Khuzayma devotes an entire chapter to explaining that this "لم أسمع" means: **"I did not hear them recite aloud"** (جهرا), not that they omitted the Basmala.

— Al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī, Dhikr al-Jahr bi-l-Basmala, no. 69, citing Ibn Khuzayma ([p. 61](https://shamela.ws/book/29892/61))

## The principle of the uṣūliyyūn

### عدم العلم ليس علما بالعدم

Ibn Taymiyya formulates the central principle:

> فَإِنَّ **عَدَمَ الْعِلْمِ لَيْسَ عِلْمًا بِالْعَدَمِ** لَا سِيَّمَا فِي أَقْوَالِ عُلَمَاءِ أُمَّةِ مُحَمَّدٍ ﷺ **الَّتِي لَا يُحْصِيهَا إِلَّا رَبُّ الْعَالَمِينَ**

> "**The absence of knowledge is not knowledge of absence** — especially regarding the sayings of the scholars of the community of Muḥammad ﷺ, **which only the Lord of the Worlds can enumerate.**"

— Majmūʿ al-Fatāwā, vol. 19, p. 271 ([p. 9788](https://shamela.ws/book/7289/9788))

And in Rafʿ al-malām ʿan al-aʾimma al-aʿlām, he details the first reason why an imam contradicts a hadith:

> السبب الأول: **أن لا يكون الحديث قد بلغه**، ومن لم يبلغه الحديث لم يكلف أن يكون عالما بموجبه. فإن **الإحاطة بحديث رسول الله ﷺ لم تكن لأحد من الأمة**.

> "First cause: **the hadith did not reach him.** Whoever has not received a hadith is not required to know its implications. For **exhaustive mastery of the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ was given to no one in the community.**"

— Rafʿ al-malām ([p. 7](https://shamela.ws/book/4118/7))

Ibn Taymiyya illustrates with examples of major Companions:
- **Abū Bakr** was unaware of the sunna on the grandmother's inheritance — al-Mughīra and Muḥammad ibn Maslama knew it
- **ʿUmar** was unaware of the hadith of isti'dhān — Abū Mūsā knew it

### المثبت مقدم على النافي

The corollary: between a scholar who affirms the existence of a tradition and another who denies it, **the one who affirms takes precedence**, because the negation may stem from ignorance.

## Two registers of "لم أسمع"

Examination of the occurrences shows that the formula operates on two distinct registers:

**1. Limit of personal reception** — "I have not received authoritative information on this question." The scholar marks the boundary of what he received (samāʿ) and shifts to reasoning (raʾy, qiyās). This is the most frequent meaning in the Muṣannaf collections.

**2. Distinction between direct hearing and indirect transmission** — [[Persons/ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ|ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ]] carefully distinguishes "لم أسمع منه" (I did not hear it from him directly) from "بلغني عنه" (it reached me from him). Visible in the case of the Īlāʾ (Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq, no. 11610) and the forgotten prayer: "لم أسمع منه في ذلك **غير ذلك**، ولكن **بلغني** عنه" (I heard from him on this only that, but it **reached me** from him...).

In neither case does the formula constitute proof of non-existence of the tradition in question. It bears on the state of knowledge **of the speaker**, not on the state of the corpus.

## Motzki and Meccan jurisprudence of the first century

Harald Motzki (*The Muṣannaf of ʿAbd al-Razzāq al-Ṣanʿānī as a Source of Authentic Aḥādīth of the First Century A.H.*) analysed the responses of ʿAṭāʾ transmitted by Ibn Jurayj and notes that approximately **70% of his opinions** are pure raʾy, **~15%** cite Companions, **~10%** the Quran, and only **~5%** prophetic hadiths.

These proportions do not mean that ʿAṭāʾ "was unaware of" the hadiths in the sense that the corpus did not exist. They reflect a **mode of jurisprudence** where raʾy, ʿamal (living practice) and the athar of the Companions constituted the normal toolkit of the faqīh. The formalised prophetic hadith — with a fixed matn and complete isnād — did not yet have the normative status it would acquire with the ahl al-ḥadīth movement in the course of the second century.
