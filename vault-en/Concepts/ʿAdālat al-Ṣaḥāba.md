# ʿAdālat al-Ṣaḥāba (عدالة الصحابة) — the dogma vs the sources

The dogma: **all ṣaḥāba are ʿudūl** — just, reliable, above all criticism. They are not evaluated as later transmitters are. They are not subjected to jarḥ wa-l-taʿdīl. A late construction elevated to an inviolable principle.

The documented reality: the tābiʿīn — and even the ṣaḥāba among themselves — **criticize, contradict, and depreciate** their peers. Not at the margins, but **in the canonical sources themselves**. The muḥaddithīn compile these criticisms, then decree that they do not count.

## ʿUrwa and Ibn ʿAbbās — a ṣaḥābī with dubious sources

Main case study. Double criticism: a tābiʿī criticizes a ṣaḥābī **and** identifies the dubious source — a poet, not the Prophet.

### The disagreement

How many years did the Prophet spend in Mecca after the revelation?

- **ʿUrwa**: **ten years** (عشر سنين)
- **Ibn ʿAbbās**: **بضع عشرة سنة** ("a little more than ten" = 13 years)

### ʿUrwa's criticism

Reported in the Istīʿāb of Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr ([p. 33](https://shamela.ws/book/12288/33)) and the Tārīkh of Abū Zurʿa al-Dimashqī ([p. 6](https://shamela.ws/book/30741/6)).

**Isnād**: Sufyān ibn ʿUyayna ← ʿAmr ibn Dīnār ← [[Persons/ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr|ʿUrwa ibn al-Zubayr]]

> « إنما أخذه من قول الشاعر »
>
> "He merely took that from the words of the poet."

ʿUrwa does not say: "Ibn ʿAbbās was mistaken." He says: his **source** is a poem, not a prophetic tradition. The tābiʿī performs jarḥ on the ṣaḥābī — and a methodological jarḥ, not a simple difference of opinion.

Abū Zurʿa uses the verb **يزعم** ("claims," pejorative) to introduce Ibn ʿAbbās's position — a ṣaḥābī treated with the vocabulary of doubt.

### Identification of the source

Yaḥyā ibn Saʿīd al-Anṣārī reports (Istīʿāb, [p. 33](https://shamela.ws/book/12288/33)):

> « سمعت عجوزًا من الأنصار يقول: رأيت ابن عباس يختلف إلى صرمة بن قيس يتعلّم منه هذه الأبيات »
>
> "I heard an old man from the Anṣār say: I saw Ibn ʿAbbās **regularly visiting** Ṣurma ibn Qays to **learn** these verses from him."

Ibn ʿAbbās does not transmit from the Prophet. He **frequents a poet** and transforms a verse into chronological data.

The verse in question:

> « ثوى في قريش بضع عشرة حجة / يذكر لو يلقى صديقا مواتيا »
>
> "He dwelt among Quraysh a little more than ten years / recalling [his message], hoping to find a favorable friend."

The poet is identified as **Ṣurma ibn Abī Anas ibn Qays** (Sharḥ al-Zarqānī, [p. 614](https://shamela.ws/book/26568/614)).

### Ibn Kathīr's confirmation

Ibn Kathīr (Sīra, [p. 812](https://shamela.ws/book/930/812)) confirms unambiguously:

> « كتب أبيات صرمة بن أبي أنس بن قيس »
>
> "He **wrote down** the verses of Ṣurma ibn Abī Anas ibn Qays."

Ibn ʿAbbās **copied** the verses. His Meccan chronology does not rest on prophetic teaching but on a poem he learned and transcribed. ʿUrwa, a tābiʿī, says so plainly. The dogma of universal ʿadāla does not yet exist.

## The ṣaḥāba admit they do not transmit directly from the Prophet

The dogma of ʿadāla presupposes that the ṣaḥāba transmit **what they heard from the Prophet**. Yet the ṣaḥāba themselves say the opposite.

### Al-Barāʾ ibn ʿĀzib

Al-Barāʾ acknowledges (al-Sunna qabla l-tadwīn, [p. 67](https://shamela.ws/book/96178/67#p3)):

> « ما كل الحديث سمعناه من رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم، كان يحدثنا أصحابنا، وكنا مشتغلين في رعاية الإبل »
>
> "We did not hear all the hadiths from the Prophet. Our companions would report them to us, and we were busy tending camels."

And in a second riwāya:

> « ليس كلنا كان يسمع حديث رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم، كانت لنا ضيعة وأشغال، ولكن الناس لم يكونوا يكذبون يومئذ، فيحدث الشاهد الغائب »
>
> "Not all of us heard the hadiths of the Prophet — we had lands and occupations. But people did not lie in those days: the one present would report to the one absent."

The formula "from the Prophet" (عن النبي) therefore does not necessarily mean direct hearing — it is often **hearsay among ṣaḥāba**.

### Anas ibn Mālik

Anas says exactly the same thing. When someone asks him whether he **heard** a hadith directly from the Prophet, Anas **becomes angry** and responds (Mustadrak, no. 6458, [p. 7079](https://shamela.ws/book/2266/7079)):

> « والله ما كل ما نحدثكم به سمعناه من رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم، ولكن كان يحدث بعضنا بعضا، ولا يتهم بعضنا بعضا »
>
> "By God, we did not hear from the Prophet everything we report to you. But we used to report to one another, and **no one suspected anyone**."

Anas's anger is revealing: the question bothers him, because the honest answer undermines the authority of his own traditions.

## Abū Hurayra — tadlīs and retraction

### The hadith of janāba — a ṣaḥābī corrected by the Prophet's wives

Abū Hurayra teaches publicly in his qaṣaṣ:

> « من أدركه الفجر جنبا فلا يصم »
>
> "Whoever is reached by dawn in a state of janāba, let him not fast."

**Abū Bakr ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn al-Ḥārith al-Makhzūmī** hears this fatwa and goes to verify with **ʿĀʾisha** and **Umm Salama** — both respond:

> « كان النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم يصبح جنبا من غير حلم ثم يصوم »
>
> "The Prophet would wake up in a state of janāba — without a dream — and then fast."

A head-on contradiction. **Marwān ibn al-Ḥakam**, governor of Medina, then orders a direct confrontation: he adjures ʿAbd al-Raḥmān to go find Abū Hurayra and **confront him with what he says**. A governor fact-checking a ṣaḥābī.

### The admission

Confronted, Abū Hurayra capitulates:

> « هما أعلم … سمعت ذلك من الفضل ولم أسمعه من النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم »
>
> "They know better than I do... I heard that from **al-Faḍl ibn al-ʿAbbās**, and I did **not hear it from the Prophet**."

A ṣaḥābī was teaching as prophetic speech what he had received from an **intermediary** — al-Faḍl, who died shortly after the Farewell Pilgrimage, unverifiable. Then he **retracts**: « فرجع أبو هريرة عما كان يقول في ذلك ».

### Shuʿba: "Abū Hurayra yudallis"

This is not an isolated case. **[[Persons/Shuʿba ibn al-Ḥajjāj|Shuʿba ibn al-Ḥajjāj]]** — the greatest critic of his era, nicknamed "amīr al-muʾminīn fī l-ḥadīth" — says plainly (Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ, [p. 2588](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/2588)):

> « كان أبو هريرة يدلّس »
>
> "Abū Hurayra **practiced tadlīs**."

Isnād: Yazīd ibn Hārūn ← Shuʿba.

Tadlīs means **concealing one's real source** — reporting "from the Prophet" what one actually received from an intermediary. The hadith of janāba is the perfect illustration.

Al-Dhahabī, confronted with this statement, tries to neutralize it:

> « تدليس الصحابة كثير، ولا عيب فيه … والصحابة كلهم عدول »
>
> "The tadlīs of the Companions is frequent, and there is no blame in it... and the Companions are all ʿudūl."

The defense is circular: tadlīs is a vice — except among the ṣaḥāba, because they are ʿudūl. And they are ʿudūl because... that is the dogma. **The people of Kūfa**, for their part, did not bother with this circularity: they did not accept all of Abū Hurayra's hadiths.

Ibn Kathīr moreover confirms in al-Bidāya (8/109) that Shuʿba was **probably** alluding to the hadith of janāba when he spoke of Abū Hurayra's tadlīs.

## Mujāhid and Anas — "leave him"

[[Persons/Mujāhid|Mujāhid ibn Jabr]] (d. 104 AH), a first-rank tābiʿī, student of Ibn ʿAbbās, major exegete. About [[Persons/Anas ibn Mālik|Anas ibn Mālik]] (Qabūl al-Akhbār, [1/191](https://shamela.ws/book/14538/191)):

> « دعه فإنه يشرب الطلاء »
>
> "**Leave him**, he drinks ṭilāʾ."

The gesture is a **pure and simple rejection**: Anas does not deserve to be listened to. A tābiʿī dismisses a ṣaḥābī with a wave of the hand — in a work on hadith methodology.

## The dogma vs the sources

The paradox is structural: the doctrine of the universal ʿadāla of the ṣaḥāba is defended **by the very same corpora that document its violation**. The muḥaddithīn compile the criticisms of ʿUrwa, Mujāhid, ʿĀʾisha — then decree that the ṣaḥāba must not be criticized.

Al-Bayhaqī, confronted with the rejection of the hadith of ʿUkl by ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn, rules (Maʿrifat al-Sunan, [p. 6827](https://shamela.ws/book/2863/6827)):

> « فلا معنى للإنكار بعد صحة الإسناد »
>
> "Rejection has no meaning once the isnād is authenticated."

The formula is the keystone: the isnād takes precedence over the content, and the ṣaḥābī at the end of the chain is **by definition** reliable. Circular, but effective.

**The chronology of the dogma**: the tābiʿīn (1st-2nd century) criticize freely. The lockdown occurs in the 3rd-4th century with the canonization of the collections. The principle "all ṣaḥāba are ʿudūl" is not a primitive datum — it is a construction **posterior** to the very criticisms it is supposed to forbid.
