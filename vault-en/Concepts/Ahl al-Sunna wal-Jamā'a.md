# Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamā'a (أهل السنة والجماعة)

The expression **Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamā'a** is not an original theological concept. It is a **political appellation** born in the Umayyad context, coined to distinguish the camp of al-Shām — partisan of obedience to power and of the Sunna of the regime — from its adversaries, notably the Kūfites who called themselves **Ahl al-Qur'ān**.

## Origin — 'Umar b. 'Abd al-'Azīz

The first significant attestation comes from the **epistles of 'Umar b. 'Abd al-'Azīz** (r. 99-101 AH), the only Umayyad caliph that the Sunni tradition considers "just." Abū Nu'aym (Ḥilyat al-Awliyā', vol.5 p.346) and al-Marwazī (al-Sunna, p.27 no.72; p.31 no.94-95) preserve these letters in which 'Umar uses the expression to rally his partisans.

Al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī and his successors adopt the term — it spreads through the Basran milieu before becoming the label of orthodoxy.

## Propagation — the hadiths of al-Shām

The Shāmī camp produces hadiths that self-certify the group:

**Al-'Irbāḍ b. Sāriya** (Shāmī Companion) reports:

> « عليكم بسنتي وسنة الخلفاء الراشدين المهديين، عضّوا عليها بالنواجذ »
>
> "Hold fast to my Tradition and to that of the Rightly Guided Caliphs — bite onto it with your molars."

The hadith deliberately associates the Sunna of the Prophet with the **Sunna of the Caliphs**. This is not a detail: it is the heart of the system. The "Sunna" in question is not only the prophetic practice — it is the practice of power.

**Mu'āwiya** reports a hadith about the victorious group that "will remain on the truth." This group is identified with the "people of al-Shām" in the Shāmī versions.

## The "Sunna" in question — which Sunna?

The semantic trap lies in the word "Sunna." For the Kūfites, the Sunna is the practice of the Prophet as attested by the Quran. For al-Shām, the Sunna is a corpus of oral traditions (the hadith) that **takes precedence over the Quran** — [[Persons/Makhūl al-Shāmī|Makhūl al-Shāmī]]: "the Quran needs the Sunna more than the Sunna needs the Quran."

When al-'Irbāḍ adds "and the Sunna of the Caliphs," the word shifts: the Sunna is no longer solely prophetic, it is **state-sanctioned**. The practices of the Umayyad regime — the codification of hadith under Hishām, the primacy of the Sunna under 'Abd al-Malik, the fabrication of the faḍā'il of al-Shām — themselves become "Sunna."

## The counter-term: Ahl al-Qur'ān

The Kūfites call themselves **Ahl al-Qur'ān** — the party of the Quran. They do not recognize the Sunna of al-Shām as authority. [[Persons/Abū Ḥanīfa|Abū Ḥanīfa]] treats it as "fables" (khurāfa). The Kūfites reject the primacy of the Sunna over the Quran and posit the Book as the sole source.

The rejection is also ritual: the Kūfites **exclude the Shāmīs from the Witr** — they consider that those who do not correctly recite the Quran have no right to this prayer. [[Persons/Hudhayfah ibn al-Yamān|Hudhayfah]]:

> « لا وتر لمن لم يقرأ بأم القرآن »
>
> "No Witr for whoever does not recite the Mother of the Book."

Al-Bayhaqī (Sunan, no. 4660), Ibn Abī Shayba (Muṣannaf, no. 6964, 6965, 6989) — the sources are multiple. The Witr becomes an **identity marker**: praying the Witr "correctly" means belonging to the party of the Quran, not to the Sunna of the regime.

## The victory of a label

The expression "Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamā'a" prevailed historically because the camp that coined it won — politically, militarily, institutionally. It passed from the Umayyad political vocabulary to the Abbasid theological vocabulary, then became the name of Muslim orthodoxy itself. Its political origin was erased; it was retrojected onto the Prophet and his Companions.

The "Jamā'a" (the group) is the group of those who obey the ruler — that is what the hadith of Hudhayfah says in its Shāmī version (Muslim 1847). The "Sunna" is the Sunna of the Caliphs as much as that of the Prophet. The label carries within it the political program of al-Shām.
