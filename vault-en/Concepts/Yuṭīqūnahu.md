# يُطِيقُونَهُ — "those who endure it"

## The word

Verse 2:184 says:

> وَعَلَى الَّذِينَ **يُطِيقُونَهُ** فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِينٍ

> "And for those who **endure it** — a compensation: feeding a poor person."

يُطِيقُونَهُ comes from the root ط-و-ق, form IV (أَطَاقَ). The word simply means **"to endure"** — that is, to be capable of doing something, with the idea that it requires effort. The foundational image of the root is the طَوْق (collar, yoke): carrying something around the neck, at the limit of one's capacity. *To endure* — nothing more, nothing less.

This meaning is transparent, and it suffices to create the problem: if the verse addresses those who **can** fast (even with difficulty), then it offers them an **option** — to fast or to pay the fidya. Fasting is "better" (وَأَن تَصُومُوا خَيْرٌ لَّكُمْ), but the fidya is **licit**.

This is unacceptable for the later consensus, which requires fasting of every able Muslim. The word يُطِيقُونَهُ has therefore become a battlefield — and **nine variant readings** attest to this discomfort.

## Nine variants — a single discomfort

The Quranic variants database (erquran) records **nine alternative readings** for this single word — a remarkably high number:

### Shādhdhah-type variants (recognized anomalies)

| Variant | Type | Effect on meaning |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| **يُطَوَّقُونَهُ** | ibdāl | Form II passive: "those on whom it is imposed as a yoke" — **reverses the meaning** |
| **يَتَطَوَّقُونَهُ** | verb form | Form V: "those who take it upon themselves" — reflexive |
| **يَطَّوَّقُونَهُ** | assimilation | Phonetic variant of يَتَطَوَّقُونَهُ |
| **يُطْوِقُونَهُ** | verb form | Form IV with apparent wāw — same root, same meaning |
| **يُطَوَّفُونَهُ** | dotting (fāʾ instead of qāf) | Root ط-و-ف (to go around) — root change |

### Irregular-type variants

| Variant | Type |
|---------|------|
| **يُطَيِّقُونَهُ** | verb form |
| **يُطَّيَّقُونَهُ** | internal vowels |
| **يُطَّيِّقُونَهُ** | assimilation |
| **يَطَّيَّقُونَهُ** | internal vowels |

### What these variants prove

Nine attempts at rereading for a single word. The most annotated variant — يُطَوَّقُونَهُ (15 annotations in the erquran database) — is also the most radical: it shifts the verb from form IV (capacity) to form II passive (imposed burden), reversing the meaning from "those who can" to "those who are burdened by it."

The multiplication of variants is in itself an **admission**: one does not produce nine alternative readings for a word whose meaning poses no problem.

## The root ط-و-ق — what the patrimony says about it

The meaning of يُطِيقُ is stable throughout the entire patrimony — Quran, hadith, dictionaries. It means "to endure, to be capable of bearing."

### In the Quran — 4 occurrences

| Verse | Form | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| 2:184 | يُطِيقُونَهُ (IV) | "those who endure it" |
| 2:249 | لَا طَاقَةَ لَنَا (noun) | "we do not have the strength [to fight Goliath]" |
| 2:286 | لَا طَاقَةَ لَنَا بِهِ (noun) | "we do not have the strength for that" |
| 3:180 | سَيُطَوَّقُونَ (II) | "they will be encircled at the neck" — physical sense of the طَوْق |

The two occurrences of طَاقَة mean **pure capacity**: "we do not have the strength to fight Goliath" does not mean "it's hard" — it means "we cannot."

### In the hadith

The hadith corpus uses يُطِيقُ / أَطَاقَ / طَاقَة dozens of times. The meaning is constant — "to endure, to be capable of":

**Bukhārī 1987**:

> وَأَيُّكُمْ **يُطِيقُ** مَا كَانَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ **يُطِيقُ**

> "And who among you **can endure** what the Prophet ﷺ **endured**?"

**Muslim 1662**:

> لَا يُكَلَّفُ مِنَ الْعَمَلِ إِلَّا مَا **يُطِيقُ**

> "[The slave] should only be tasked with what he **can endure**."

**Muwaṭṭaʾ, Mālik 1807**:

> فَإِذَا وَجَدَ عَبْدًا فِي عَمَلٍ **لَا يُطِيقُهُ** وَضَعَ عَنْهُ

> "ʿUmar, if he found a slave in work he **could not endure**, would relieve him of it."

**Abū Dāwūd (vows)**:

> مَنْ نَذَرَ نَذْرًا **لَا يُطِيقُهُ** [...] وَمَنْ نَذَرَ نَذْرًا **أَطَاقَهُ** فَلْيَفِ بِهِ

> "Whoever makes a vow he **cannot endure** [...] and whoever makes a vow he **can endure**, let him fulfill it."

**Musnad Aḥmad 1242**:

> وَأَيُّكُمْ **يُطِيقُهُ** [...] نَأْخُذُ مِنْهُ مَا **أَطَقْنَا**

> "Who **can endure** that? [...] We will take from it what we **can endure**."

Not a single occurrence in the hadith where يُطِيقُ shifts from "capable of enduring" to "incapable."

### In the classical Arabic dictionaries

**Lisān al-ʿArab** (Ibn Manẓūr):

> الطَّوْقُ : الطَّاقَةُ أَي **أَقْصَى غَايَتِه** — وهو اسم لمقدار ما يمكنه أن يفعله من ذلك **بمشقة**

> "The ṭawq is the ṭāqa, that is **the extreme limit** [of what one can do] — a name for the measure of what one can do **with difficulty**."

**Maqāyīs al-Lugha** (Ibn Fāris):

> طوق — **أصلٌ واحدٌ يدلُّ على إحاطة الشيء بالشيء**

> "ṭ-w-q — **a single root indicating the encirclement of one thing by another**."

The foundational image is physical: the طَوْق (collar, yoke) encircling the neck. From there: ṭāqa = the measure of what one can bear, at the limit of effort. The nuance of difficulty is in the root's DNA — but it reinforces the argument rather than weakening it: *to endure* implies that it is hard, **not that one cannot**. Someone who يُطِيقُ fasting **can** fast, even if it is painful.

## Ibn ʿAbbās's own hadith

**Abū Dāwūd 2318** — Ibn ʿAbbās via Saʿīd ibn Jubayr:

> كَانَتْ رُخْصَةً لِلشَّيْخِ الْكَبِيرِ وَالْمَرْأَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ **وَهُمَا يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ** أَنْ يُفْطِرَا وَيُطْعِمَا مَكَانَ كُلِّ يَوْمٍ مِسْكِينًا

> "It was a license for the elderly man and the elderly woman — **while they are capable of enduring the fast** — to break their fast and feed a poor person for each day instead."

The same verb in the same form: يُطِيقَانِ الصِّيَامَ. The meaning is limpid — they **can** fast. This is not a partisan reading: it is the word as used everywhere, by everyone, including by Ibn ʿAbbās himself.

## ʿAṭāʾ ate during Ramaḍān — and cited Ibn ʿAbbās

The argument does not remain theoretical. [[Persons/ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ|ʿAṭāʾ ibn Abī Rabāḥ]] — Meccan tābiʿī, muftī of Mecca, student of Ibn ʿAbbās — **applied** this reading. The episode is attested by **three independent chains**, all via Ibn Abī Laylā (Muḥammad ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān).

**Version 1 — al-Dhahabī (Siyar, [p. 3854](https://shamela.ws/book/10906/3854))**: the most concise.

> رَأَيْتُهُ يَشْرَبُ المَاءَ فِي رَمَضَانَ، وَيَقُوْلُ: قَالَ ابْنُ عَبَّاسٍ:
> {وَعَلَى الَّذِيْنَ يُطِيْقُوْنَهُ فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِيْنٍ}:
> إِنِّي أُطْعِمُ أَكْثَرَ مِنْ مِسْكِيْنٍ.

> "I saw him **drinking water during Ramaḍān** while saying: Ibn ʿAbbās said: *'And for those who endure it, a compensation: feeding a poor person'* — I feed more than one poor person."

No justification by old age or incapacity — just the verse, Ibn ʿAbbās's statement, and the fidya. ʿAṭāʾ **can** fast — he يُطِيقُ — and he chooses the compensation, exactly as the verse permits.

This is the living proof that يُطِيقُونَهُ was understood in its plain sense — "those who are capable" — and that the fidya was a **licit option**, not a last resort for invalids.

## The three neutralization strategies

The tradition needed **three simultaneous strategies** to neutralize this word:

1. **Naskh** — the verse is "abrogated" by 2:185. This is the majority position, carried by major authorities — al-Ṭabarī himself, while rejecting the qirāʾa يُطَوَّقُونَهُ, accepts a form of naskh. On the other side, Ibn ʿAbbās says explicitly لَيْسَتْ بِمَنْسُوخَةٍ (Bukhārī 4505), and Ibn al-Jawzī qualifies this naskh as بُعْدٌ كَثِيرٌ — very far-fetched. This question presupposes acceptance of the naskh mechanism itself, which is the subject of a [[Concepts/Naskh|dedicated study]]. See also [[Traditions/Ṣawm Ramaḍān#1. Le Coran ne dit pas « abrogation »|Ṣawm Ramaḍān]].

2. **The qirāʾa** — يُطَوَّقُونَهُ (form II passive) instead of يُطِيقُونَهُ (form IV). This is the most annotated variant (15 annotations in erquran) among the nine. Al-Ṭabarī rejects it as contrary to the muṣḥaf: "فَقِرَاءَةٌ لِمَصَاحِفِ أَهْلِ الْإِسْلَامِ خِلَافٌ." It is itself an indicator of the problem: one does not produce a variant for a word that causes no discomfort.

3. **Reinterpretation** — Ibn Masʿūd glosses يُطِيقُونَهُ as يَتَجَشَّمُونَهُ ("they accomplish it with difficulty"). But this is an interpretive gloss, not a translation. The nuance of difficulty exists in the root — but it does not transform "capable of enduring" into "incapable."

Three strategies for a single word. The fact that none sufficed — that all three were needed simultaneously — is an admission.

And the rewriting extends to the hadith itself. Al-Nasāʾī (no. 2317) reports Ibn ʿAbbās saying:

> لاَ يُرَخَّصُ فِي هَذَا إِلاَّ لِلَّذِي **لاَ يُطِيقُ** الصِّيَامَ

> "The concession applies only to one who **cannot** fast."

The Quran says يُطِيقُونَهُ — "those who **can**." The hadith says لاَ يُطِيقُ — "one who **cannot**." It is the **same verb, negated**. The hadith does not comment on the verse — it inverts it. Compare with the version in Abū Dāwūd 2318 (via Saʿīd ibn Jubayr) where Ibn ʿAbbās says وَهُمَا **يُطِيقَانِ** الصِّيَامَ — "while they **can** fast." Two hadiths attributed to the same man, with the same verb — one affirms, the other negates.

## Conclusion

يُطِيقُونَهُ means "those who endure it" — those who are capable, even if it is hard. This is what the classical Arabic dictionaries say, what the Quran shows in every use of the root, what dozens of hadiths confirm, the meaning Ibn ʿAbbās gives the word in his own explanation of the verse — and that his student ʿAṭāʾ applies by drinking water during Ramaḍān.

The problem is not the word. The problem is what the word implies. For the consequences of this analysis on the fasting dossier, see [[Traditions/Ṣawm Ramaḍān#Ce que ce dossier révèle|Ṣawm Ramaḍān — conclusion]].
