# Arkān al-Islām — the "pillars" of Islam

## The canonical position

Every Muslim learns that Islam rests on **five pillars**: the shahāda, the ṣalāt, the zakāt, the ṣawm of Ramaḍān, and the ḥajj. This number five is considered self-evident — *maʿlūm min al-dīn bi-l-ḍarūra*. It rests primarily on two hadiths:

1. The **hadith of Ibn ʿUmar**: « بُنِيَ الإسلامُ على خَمْسٍ » — Bukhārī and Muslim. See [[Traditions/Ḥadīth Buniya al-Islām|dedicated analysis]].
2. The **hadith of Jibrīl**: ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb reports that the angel Jibrīl questioned the Prophet about Islam, īmān, and iḥsān — Muslim. See [[Traditions/Ḥadīth Jibrīl|dedicated analysis]].

Yet the early sources do not agree on this number. Depending on the transmitter and the collection, Islam rests on **three**, **four**, **five**, or **eight** pillars — and one version gives **no number at all**.

## The texts

### Three pillars — Ibn ʿAbbās

Al-Lālikāʾī, via Ibn Rajab (*Jāmiʿ al-ʿUlūm wal-Ḥikam*, [p. 146](https://shamela.ws/book/10443/146)):

> **عُرَى الإسْلامِ وقَواعدُ الدين ثلاثةٌ**: عَلَيْهن أُسِّسَ الإسْلامُ: شَهادةُ أن لا إله إلا الله **والصَّلاةُ المكتوبة وَصَومُ رَمَضان**، مَنْ تَرك مِنْهنَّ وَاحدةً فَهو بِهَا كَافرٌ حَلَالُ الدَّم
>
> "**The bonds of Islam and the foundations of the religion are three** — upon them Islam is built: the shahāda, **the prescribed ṣalāt, and the fast of Ramaḍān**. Whoever abandons one of them is a disbeliever, licit of blood."

Three pillars only: shahāda, ṣalāt, ṣiyām. **Neither zakāt, nor ḥajj.** Ibn ʿAbbās then adds (as mawqūf) that refusal of ḥajj and zakāt is also kufr — but he separates them from the "foundations."

### Three pillars — ʿĀʾisha

Musnad Aḥmad 6/145, Abū Yaʿlā 4566 (al-Haythamī: « rijāluhu thiqāt »):

> ثَلاَثٌ أَحْلِفُ عَلَيْهِنَّ [...] وَ**أَسْهُمُ الإسْلاَمِ الثلاَثَةُ: الصَّلاَةُ، وَالصَّوْمُ، وَالزَّكَاةُ**
>
> "Three things upon which I swear [...] and **the three shares of Islam: ṣalāt, ṣawm, and zakāt**."

Neither shahāda, nor ḥajj. Al-Haythamī qualifies the rijāl as thiqāt.

### Five — says "five," names three — Ibn ʿAbbās

Ṭabarānī, al-Muʿjam al-Kabīr no. 12800 ([p. 14923](https://shamela.ws/book/1733/14923)):

> بُنِيَ الْإِسْلَامُ عَلَى خَمْسٍ: شَهَادَةِ أَنْ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ، **وَالصَّلَاةِ، وَصِيَامِ رَمَضَانَ** فَمَنْ تَرَكَ وَاحِدَةً مِنْهُنَّ كَانَ **كَافِرًا حَلَالَ الدَّمِ**
>
> "Islam is built upon five: the shahāda, **ṣalāt, and the fast of Ramaḍān**. Whoever abandons one of them is a disbeliever, licit of blood."

The text says "five" but **names only three**. The editor of al-Suyūṭī notes: « واقتصر على ثلاثة منها ». Al-Haythamī: isnāduhu ḥasan.

### Four — without shahāda

Musnad Aḥmad 4/200, cited by Ibn Rajab ([p. 148](https://shamela.ws/book/10443/148)):

> أربعٌ فَرضَهُنَّ الله في الإسْلَام فَمن أَتى بِثَلاثٍ لَمْ يُغنينَ عنه شَيئًا حَتَّى يَأتي بِهنَّ جَميعًا: **الصَّلاةُ والزَّكاةُ وَصَومُ رمضانَ وَحَجُّ البيت**
>
> "Four things God has prescribed in Islam — whoever fulfills three, they avail him nothing until he fulfills all of them: **ṣalāt, zakāt, the fast of Ramaḍān, and the ḥajj.**"

Four — the shahāda is not mentioned.

### Eight pillars — Ḥudhayfa ibn al-Yamān

Muṣannaf ʿAbd al-Razzāq no. 9280:

> بُنِيَ الْإِسْلَامُ عَلَى **ثَمَانِيَةِ أَسْهُمٍ**: شَهَادَةُ أَنْ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ، وَأَنَّ مُحَمَّدًا رَسُولُ اللَّهِ، وَإِقَامُ الصَّلَاةِ، وَإِيتَاءُ الزَّكَاةِ، وَحَجُّ الْبَيْتِ، وَصَوْمُ رَمَضَانَ، **وَالْجِهَادُ، وَالْأَمْرُ بِالْمَعْرُوفِ، وَالنَّهْيُ عَنِ الْمُنْكَرِ**، وَقَدْ خَابَ مَنْ لَا سَهْمَ لَهُ
>
> "Islam is built upon **eight shares**: the shahāda, ṣalāt, zakāt, ḥajj, the fast of Ramaḍān, **jihād, commanding the good, and forbidding the evil** — and he has failed who has no share."

Mawqūf (attributed to Ḥudhayfa, not to the Prophet), but with a ṣaḥīḥ isnād (ʿAbd al-Razzāq ← Maʿmar + al-Thawrī ← Abū Isḥāq ← Ṣila ibn Zufar ← Ḥudhayfa). Al-Jaṣṣāṣ notes in his *Aḥkām al-Qurʾān* that this hadith « يُعَارِضُ قَوْلَ ابْنِ عُمَرَ » — **contradicts that of Ibn ʿUmar**.

### No number — the "khiṣāl"

Suyūṭī, *Jamʿ al-Jawāmiʿ* no. 12360:

> بُنِى الإسلام على **خصال**: على شهادة أن لا إله إلا الله, وأن محمدا رسول الله, **والإقرار بما جاء من عند الله, والجهاد ماض منذ بعث الله رسله إلى آخر عصابة تكون من المسلمين يقاتلون الدجال** [...] **والقدر خيره وشره من الله**
>
> "Islam is built upon **traits** (khiṣāl): the shahāda, **adherence to what comes from God, permanent jihād** [...] **and qadar, its good and evil coming from God**."

No number. And above all: **neither ṣalāt, nor ṣawm, nor zakāt, nor ḥajj** are mentioned. The content is radically different — it includes permanent jihād and qadar (predestination).

### Jihād refused by Ibn ʿUmar

Musnad Aḥmad no. 4798 ([p. 3773](https://shamela.ws/book/25794/3773)):

> فَقَالَ لَهُ رَجُلٌ: **وَالْجِهَادُ فِي سَبِيلِ اللهِ؟**
> قَالَ ابْنُ عُمَرَ: **الْجِهَادُ حَسَنٌ، هَكَذَا حَدَّثَنَا رَسُولُ اللهِ**
>
> "A man said to him: **And jihād in the path of God?** Ibn ʿUmar replied: **Jihād is good — but this is what the Prophet transmitted to us.**"

The dialogue is revealing: someone challenges the list of five by proposing to add jihād. Ibn ʿUmar refuses — not because jihād is unimportant, but because he holds to the prophetic formula as he heard it. This dialogue only makes sense in a context where **the list is not yet fixed** and is under debate.

## What these variants reveal

### The instability of the number

Three major Companions — Ibn ʿUmar, Ibn ʿAbbās, and Ḥudhayfa — transmit **incompatible** versions on the number of pillars. ʿĀʾisha gives three, Ḥudhayfa eight. Ibn ʿAbbās says "five" in one text but names only three. A version attributed to Ibn ʿUmar himself gives no number and includes qadar.

If the Prophet had stated a fixed formula memorized by all — as the status of *maʿlūm min al-dīn bi-l-ḍarūra* suggests — such dispersion should not exist. One does not disagree on the number of pillars in a building everyone has seen.

### Jihād — inside or outside?

The question of jihād as a pillar crystallizes the debate:
- **Ḥudhayfa** includes it (8 pillars), along with amr bi-l-maʿrūf and nahy ʿan al-munkar
- **An anonymous man** proposes it to Ibn ʿUmar, who refuses
- **The "khiṣāl" version** makes it a central and permanent element

The exclusion of jihād from the canonical list of five pillars is not an original fact — it is the result of a selection process whose traces are still visible in the texts.

### The internal order

Even within the canonical five-pillar version, **the order varies**: ḥajj and ṣawm are permuted depending on the version. Ibn ʿUmar corrects a man who reverses the two (*Taʿẓīm Qadr al-Ṣalāt* no. 418, [p. 483](https://shamela.ws/book/13087/483)) — which shows that the order was still debated in his time.

### The classical analysis — al-Marwazī

Muḥammad ibn Naṣr al-Marwazī (d. 294 AH), in *Taʿẓīm Qadr al-Ṣalāt* ([p. 487-488](https://shamela.ws/book/13087/487)), proposes the classical explanation of these divergences:

> فَقَدْ دَلَّ ذَلِكَ مَنْ عَقَلَ عَلَى أَنَّ الْإِسْلَامَ كَثِيرٌ، لِأَنَّ الْبُنْيَانَ أَكْثَرُ مِنَ الْأَصْلِ [...] وَقَدْ حُفِظَ فِي بَعْضِ هَذِهِ الْأَحَادِيثِ مِنْ شَرَائِعِ الْإِسْلَامِ مَا لَمْ يُحْفَظْ فِي بَعْضٍ
>
> "This proves to the intelligent person that Islam is vast, for the building is larger than its foundations [...] Some of these hadiths preserve elements of the sharīʿa that others have not preserved."

Al-Marwazī attempts to reconcile: each narrator would have retained only part of the prophetic teaching, the five pillars being the foundations and the rest the building. It is an ingenious harmonization — but it implicitly concedes that **the formula was not fixed** to the point of being uniformly memorized.
