# Kūfa — the dissident garrison

Founded as a **miṣr** (permanent military camp) under ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb around 17H, Kūfa is the second Iraqi garrison alongside [[Places/Basra|Basra]]. But the two cities do not share the same intellectual DNA. Basra is the city of the quṣṣāṣ and of tadlīs, the Basran relay of Umayyad power. Kūfa is the city of the **Quran** — and of resistance to the regime's hadith.

## Ibn Masʿūd, not ʿAlī — the true founder of the Kūfan school

The canonical narrative presents Kūfa as "the city of ʿAlī." This is an anachronism. ʿAlī settles in Kūfa in **36H** for the duration of his caliphate (~4 years), and dies in **40H**. But **the intellectual school of Kūfa is founded twenty years earlier** by another man.

**[[Persons/Ibn Mas'ūd|ʿAbd Allāh ibn Masʿūd]]** is sent to Kūfa by ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb as **teacher and treasurer** from the city's foundation. He trains the first generation of Kūfan scholars:

| Direct student of Ibn Masʿūd | Role |
|-------------------------------|------|
| **ʿAlqama ibn Qays al-Nakhaʿī** | Principal transmitter, "the most resembling Ibn Masʿūd" |
| **al-Aswad ibn Yazīd** | Jurist, companion of ʿAlqama |
| **Masrūq ibn al-Ajdaʿ** | Great Kūfan tābiʿī |
| **Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī** (d. ~96H) | Heir of ʿAlqama, master of Abū Ḥanīfa via Ḥammād |
| **ʿĀmir al-Shaʿbī** (d. ~106H) | Jurist and muḥaddith, participates in the revolt of [[Persons/Ibn al-Ashʿath|Ibn al-Ashʿath]] |

The intellectual chain of transmission is clear:

```
Ibn Masʿūd (d. ~32H)
    └── ʿAlqama (d. ~62H)
            └── Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī (d. ~96H)
                    └── Ḥammād ibn Abī Sulaymān (d. ~120H)
                            └── Abū Ḥanīfa (d. 150H)
```

It is the school of **Ibn Masʿūd** — not ʿAlī — that produces the greatest living madhhab. ʿAlī has almost **no Kūfan student** who founds an enduring school. His direct transmitters (Kumayl ibn Ziyād, ʿAbīda al-Salmānī) do not produce a comparable lineage. The material attributed to ʿAlī in the collections arrives late, often through chains external to Kūfa — via Khilās ibn ʿAmr in Basra (and [[Persons/Qatāda ibn Di'āma|Qatāda]] who transmits his ṣaḥīfas without having heard him), or via the compilers of the 3rd century.

The image of ʿAlī as the intellectual founder of Kūfa is a **retrospective construction**. The early sources show that the real master of Kūfa, the one whose teaching structures the city for generations, is Ibn Masʿūd. The "Kūfanisation" of ʿAlī is a phenomenon posterior to his death — a projection serving the identity needs of later partisans.

## What Kūfa teaches — the Quran against the Sunna

The Kūfan school is defined by five documented positions in [[Concepts/Kūfa vs al-Shām]]:

1. **The Quran suffices** — in case of fitna, "study the Book of God" (Bazzār 2799), not "obey the ruler" (Muslim 1847)
2. **The reading of Ibn Masʿūd** — the Kūfan muṣḥaf diverges from the ʿUthmānic muṣḥaf on doctrinal points
3. **The rejection of al-Quds** — Hudhayfah, ʿAlī and Abū Dharr refuse the sacralization of Jerusalem
4. **The rejection of the regime's Sunna** — Abū Ḥanīfa: "خرافة!" in the face of Shāmī obedience hadiths
5. **Ahl al-Qurʾān** against Ahl al-Sunna wal-Jamāʿa

## The decimation — al-Ḥajjāj and Ibn al-Ashʿath

The revolt of **[[Persons/Ibn al-Ashʿath|Ibn al-Ashʿath]]** (81-83H) is the turning point. Hundreds of Kūfan scholars take up arms against al-Ḥajjāj. The **katībat al-qurrāʾ** (battalion of reciters) is at the front line at the battle of **Dayr al-Jamājim** (83H / 702).

The toll is documented in the Ibn al-Ashʿath entry:

| Scholar | Fate |
|---------|------|
| Saʿīd ibn Jubayr | Flees for 12 years, **executed** (95H) |
| ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Abī Laylā | **Drowned** in the Euphrates |
| al-Shaʿbī | Captured, confesses, **pardoned** |
| Kumayl ibn Ziyād | **Executed** |
| Muḥammad ibn Saʿd ibn Abī Waqqāṣ | **Captured and executed** |

The Kūfan transmission network emerges **physically broken** from the episode. Al-Ḥajjāj pardons the Qurayshites and Syrians but massacres the mawālī and Iraqi tribes. The survivors are marked as political suspects.

## After the decimation — what remains

The resistant voices are eliminated. What survives:

- **Al-Shaʿbī** — pardoned after confession, falls in line
- **Ibrāhīm al-Nakhaʿī** — did not participate in the revolt, went into hiding, reproached Saʿīd ibn Jubayr for his participation
- **[[Persons/Al-Aʿmash|al-Aʿmash]]** (d. 148H) — pivot of Kūfan transmission, but notorious mudallis
- **Abū Ḥanīfa** (d. 150H) — rebuilds the Kūfan school in the following generation, imprisoned by the authorities

The canonical corpus that has reached us was transmitted by the **survivors** — those who kept silent, who confessed, or who came after. The voices of the dead (Saʿīd, Ibn Abī Laylā) are absent from the corpus, except through indirect channels.

## Kūfa vs Basra — two Iraqs

| | **Kūfa** | **[[Places/Basra|Basra]]** |
|---|---|---|
| **Intellectual founder** | Ibn Masʿūd | Anas ibn Mālik (declared source) |
| **Orientation** | Quran, dissidence | Hadith, accommodation |
| **Method** | Fiqh, raʾy, qiyās | Qaṣṣ, tadlīs, massive transmission |
| **Relationship to power** | Resistance → decimation | Patronage → survival |
| **Emblematic figure** | Abū Ḥanīfa (imprisoned) | al-Ḥasan al-Baṣrī (qāḍī) |
| **Transmission network** | Broken after 83H | Intact, controlled by the regime |
| **What survives** | The Ḥanafī madhhab | The canonical isnāds |

The two cities share a mistrust of al-Shām, but Basra accommodates power where Kūfa resists — and pays the price.
